# Anthropic Hits $30 Billion Revenue Run Rate, Surpassing OpenAI for the First Time

The company that most people outside the tech industry could not name two years ago is now generating more revenue than the one that defined the AI era. Anthropic disclosed at its Code with Claude developer conference this week that it has crossed a $30 billion annualized revenue run rate, leapfrogging OpenAI's $24 billion figure and marking the first time the ChatGPT maker has trailed a rival on top-line revenue.

The milestone caps what CEO Dario Amodei called a period of growth so intense it strained the company's own infrastructure. "We tried to plan very well for a world of 10x growth per year. And yet we saw 80x," Amodei said. "And so that is the reason we have had difficulties with compute." He described the surge as "just crazy" and "too hard to handle," a rare admission from a chief executive that demand was outpacing the company's ability to serve it.

## The Numbers Behind the Surge

Anthropic's revenue trajectory reads less like a software company and more like a vertical launch. The company sat at an $87 million run rate in January 2024. By December of that year it had hit $1 billion. It closed 2025 at $9 billion. Then the acceleration truly began: $14 billion in February 2026, $19 billion in March, and $30 billion by April. That final leg -- from $14 billion to $30 billion in roughly eight weeks -- represents a 114 percent increase in 60 days, a pace that defies comparison in the history of enterprise software.

For context, Salesforce required approximately 20 years to reach $30 billion in annual revenue. Anthropic achieved it in under three years from what was essentially a standing start.

OpenAI, meanwhile, confirmed $2 billion in monthly revenue alongside a $122 billion fundraise at an $852 billion valuation. Its $24 billion annualized run rate is itself a staggering figure -- representing roughly 3x year-over-year growth sustained at massive scale. OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar called it "never-before-seen growth at such scale," and she was not exaggerating. But for the first time, the company founded by former OpenAI research leaders is ahead on revenue.

## Enterprise API: The Engine Room

The divergence in business models tells much of the story. OpenAI built its brand on ChatGPT, a consumer product with roughly 900 million weekly users. Anthropic bypassed the consumer market almost entirely. Approximately 80 percent of its revenue flows through enterprise API contracts and cloud provider partnerships with Amazon Web Services and Google Cloud.

The enterprise traction is substantial. More than 1,000 businesses now spend over $1 million per year on Claude, doubling from 500 companies in February alone. Eight of the Fortune 10 are Claude customers. The company has roughly 5 percent of ChatGPT's consumer user base, yet it has pulled ahead on revenue -- a stark illustration that consumer scale and revenue scale are not the same thing.

Claude Code, Anthropic's AI-powered coding tool that launched publicly in May 2025, has emerged as a particular growth engine. By February 2026 it was generating $2.5 billion in annualized revenue on its own, with business subscriptions quadrupling in a single quarter. The tool now authors an estimated 4 percent of all public GitHub commits, with projections suggesting 20 percent or more by year-end.

## The Accounting Asterisk

OpenAI has pushed back on direct comparisons, arguing that Anthropic's reported figures overstate actual revenue by approximately $8 billion due to differences in how the two companies account for revenue flowing through cloud partners. Anthropic books gross revenue from deals brokered through AWS and Google Cloud; OpenAI contends that a net-revenue comparison would narrow the gap considerably.

The dispute is not trivial. If OpenAI's framing is correct, the two companies may be closer to parity than the headline numbers suggest. But even adjusting for the accounting difference, Anthropic's growth rate remains extraordinary, and the direction of the trend line is unmistakable.

## Analysis: Capital Efficiency as Competitive Weapon

Perhaps the most underappreciated dimension of this story is what it costs each company to compete. Confidential financial documents published by the Wall Street Journal ahead of both companies' anticipated IPOs reveal a striking gap in training expenditures. OpenAI projects spending $121 billion on compute in 2028 alone and does not expect to reach profitability until after 2030. Anthropic's training costs peak at roughly $30 billion over the same period -- approximately four times less -- and the company projects reaching profitability in 2028 or 2029.

As SaaStr founder Jason Lemkin observed, the fact that Anthropic is this competitive "at a fraction of training costs" represents something close to a "narrative violation" -- an outcome that challenges the prevailing assumption that the AI race will be won by whoever spends the most on model training.

Neither company is profitable today. OpenAI is burning an estimated $17 billion in cash this year against projected losses of $14 billion. Anthropic's burn is proportionally significant against its $18 billion-plus in total funding. The investors behind both -- SoftBank, Amazon, Nvidia, Google, Andreessen Horowitz -- are making a bet that compute costs will continue to fall per unit of intelligence and that revenue will compound faster than burn.

But the two companies are making fundamentally different versions of that bet. OpenAI is wagering that massive training spend will yield model supremacy that justifies the cost by the early 2030s. Anthropic is wagering that enterprise revenue density can outrun costs sooner. The market just gave the first definitive signal about which approach is generating more revenue today.

The crossover that most analysts had penciled in for August 2026 arrived four months early. In this market, four months is a very long time.
