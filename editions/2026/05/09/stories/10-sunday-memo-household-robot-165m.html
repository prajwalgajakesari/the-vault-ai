Sunday Raises $165 Million for Household Robot Memo at $1.15 Billion Valuation

3 min read 774 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Sunday raised $165M Series B led by Coatue at $1.15B valuation to launch household robot Memo in beta to 50 homes by Thanksgiving 2026.

The dream of a robot that folds your laundry, loads your dishwasher, and tidies your kitchen counter just got a massive vote of confidence from Wall Street. Sunday, a Mountain View, California-based robotics startup founded by two Stanford PhD researchers, has closed an oversubscribed $165 million Series B round led by Coatue Management, vaulting the two-year-old company to a $1.15 billion valuation and into the unicorn club.

The round drew a murderer\u2019s row of institutional backers. Bain Capital Ventures, Fidelity Management & Research, Tiger Global Management, Benchmark Capital, Conviction Capital, and Xtal Ventures all participated. Combined with the $35 million seed round Sunday closed from Benchmark and Conviction in November 2025, the company has now raised a total of $200 million.

From Demos to Deployment

Sunday\u2019s pitch is deceptively simple: build a robot that actually works in real homes, not just on conference stages. CEO Tony Zhao framed the fundraise in blunt terms: \u201cWe raised our Series B to stop giving demos. Now, we\u2019re focusing entirely on deployment, with Beta deliveries starting in just months.\u201d

The startup\u2019s flagship product, Memo, is a wheeled humanoid robot designed to handle the repetitive chores that consume hours of household time each week\u2014dishes, laundry, clearing tables, and general tidying. Unlike legged humanoid prototypes that dominate viral social media clips, Memo rides on a rolling base, a deliberate engineering decision that reduces weight, improves balance, and eliminates the risk of the robot toppling over during a power loss.

What sets Memo apart technically is the sheer volume of real-world training data behind it. Sunday developed a proprietary wearable called the Skill Capture Glove, which records how people move, clean, and organize in their own homes. Data from more than 500 households has produced roughly 10 million episodes of genuine household routines\u2014an unmatched dataset in consumer robotics. The robot\u2019s behavior is governed by ACT-1, a foundation model built in-house at Sunday that translates those captured motions into autonomous actions.

Coatue\u2019s Bet

Thomas Laffont, co-founder of Coatue Management, pointed to Sunday\u2019s execution speed as the decisive factor. \u201cAt Coatue, we look for founders who can maintain a very high velocity of excellence as they scale. Tony fits that profile,\u201d Laffont said. \u201cSunday\u2019s velocity is the best signal we have that they will be the first to ship truly helpful, autonomous home robots at scale.\u201d

The investment lands Coatue squarely in one of the hottest sectors in venture capital. Robotics funding has surged as breakthroughs in large language models, transformer architectures, and sim-to-real transfer learning have collectively narrowed the gap between what robots can do in controlled lab environments and what they can handle in the messy, unpredictable reality of a family kitchen.

The Road to Thanksgiving

Sunday\u2019s immediate plan is to begin shipping Memo units to a select group of beta participants\u2014the company\u2019s \u201cFounding Family Beta\u201d program\u2014by late 2026, with a target of getting robots into real homes by Thanksgiving. Fifty households have been selected as early adopters, giving Sunday a critical feedback loop to refine Memo\u2019s capabilities before any broader commercial rollout.

CTO Cheng Chi framed the company\u2019s long-term ambition in characteristically direct fashion: \u201cWe believe that if the robot is cheap, safe, and capable, everyone will want our robots.\u201d

The Bigger Picture

Sunday is far from the only company chasing the household robotics market. Figure AI, 1X Technologies, and Agility Robotics have all raised substantial rounds in the past eighteen months, while Tesla continues to develop its Optimus humanoid. But most competitors have focused on industrial and warehouse applications first. Sunday\u2019s decision to go directly after the consumer home market\u2014arguably the hardest environment for a robot to navigate\u2014is both its biggest risk and its most compelling differentiator.

The data advantage may prove decisive. While competitors rely heavily on simulation and teleoperation, Sunday\u2019s Skill Capture Glove pipeline generates training data from real human behavior in real domestic settings. As Zhao has noted, \u201cData has always been the biggest bottleneck in robotics. We built the only pipeline that turns the complexity of real-world homes into autonomous intelligence at scale.\u201d

With $200 million in the bank, a unicorn valuation, and a Thanksgiving deadline, Sunday has placed a very public bet that the age of the household robot is not a decade away\u2014it is months away. Whether Memo can survive the chaos of fifty real American kitchens will determine if that bet pays off.

"This is the GPT moment for physical AI."
