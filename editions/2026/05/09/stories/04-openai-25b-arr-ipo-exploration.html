OpenAI Tops $25 Billion Annualized Revenue as Company Explores Late 2026 Public Listing

4 min read 876 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

OpenAI hit $25B ARR in February 2026 generating $2B monthly, while weighing a late 2026 IPO despite projecting $14B in losses.

OpenAI is generating money at a pace that would have seemed fantastical even eighteen months ago — and now the question confronting Sam Altman is whether the world's most valuable startup is ready to let public markets in on the ride.

The company crossed $25 billion in annualized revenue at the end of February 2026, pulling in roughly $2 billion every month, according to a report from The Information. That figure is up from $21.4 billion at year-end 2025 and a comparatively modest $6 billion at the close of 2024 — a fourfold leap in just fourteen months. Internally, leadership has set a 2026 full-year revenue target of $29.4 billion, a number that would require sustained acceleration through the second half of the year.

The growth engine runs on two cylinders. Consumer products, led by ChatGPT and its 400-million-plus weekly user base, account for approximately 60 percent of revenue. Enterprise contracts make up the remaining 40 percent — a share that CFO Sarah Friar has said is "on track to reach parity with consumer by the end of 2026." The enterprise pipeline has been buoyed by multi-million-dollar deals with Fortune 500 companies embedding OpenAI models into customer service, legal research, and software development workflows.

The IPO Question

Those numbers have supercharged speculation about an initial public offering. The Wall Street Journal reported in January that OpenAI is laying groundwork for a listing as early as the fourth quarter of this year, with Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley advising the company. A successful debut could value OpenAI at or above $1 trillion, making it one of the largest IPOs in history.

But the path to the trading floor is far from settled. Altman and Friar appear to be pulling in different directions. Altman has pushed for a 2026 listing, while Friar has privately told colleagues she does not believe the company will be ready this year, citing the procedural and organizational work still needed to meet the rigorous reporting standards demanded of public companies, according to reporting from The Information and Tech Times.

The tension extends beyond timing. Friar has questioned whether OpenAI's slowing revenue growth rate can support Altman's $600 billion in infrastructure spending commitments — a staggering sum earmarked for AI server capacity over the coming years.

Altman himself has been characteristically candid about the trade-offs. "Am I excited to be a public company CEO? Zero percent," he said on the Big Technology Podcast late last year. "In some ways I think it'd be really annoying." But he acknowledged the inevitability: "We need lots of capital. We're going to cross all of the shareholder limits and stuff at some point."

Record-Breaking Capital

OpenAI is not short on private capital. On March 31, the company closed a $122 billion funding round — the largest private technology financing in history — at a post-money valuation of $852 billion. Amazon committed up to $50 billion, Nvidia invested $30 billion, and SoftBank put in another $30 billion. For the first time, OpenAI also extended participation to retail investors through bank channels, raising $3 billion from individuals.

The war chest is enormous, but so is the burn rate. OpenAI generated $13.1 billion in revenue in 2025 while spending approximately $22 billion to operate, and the company projects losses of $14 billion in 2026. Profitability is not expected until 2030, according to internal projections reported by multiple outlets.

The Anthropic Shadow

Complicating the narrative is a public spat with rival Anthropic over revenue accounting. In April, OpenAI's chief revenue officer Denise Dresser circulated an internal memo alleging that Anthropic's reported $30 billion annualized run rate is inflated by roughly $8 billion. The crux of the dispute: when a customer buys tokens through a cloud partner like Amazon or Google, Anthropic allegedly books the full dollar as revenue, while OpenAI records only its net share after the platform's cut. If the allegation holds, Anthropic's comparable run rate would sit closer to $22 billion — below OpenAI's $25 billion figure. Anthropic has not publicly responded.

The accounting fight is more than corporate sniping. Both companies are positioning for public listings, and the revenue crown carries enormous weight with prospective investors parsing which AI lab truly leads the market.

What Comes Next

OpenAI sits at an inflection point. Its revenue trajectory is extraordinary by any historical measure — the company went from near-zero to $25 billion in annualized revenue in roughly 39 months. But the gap between revenue and profitability remains vast, the competitive landscape is intensifying, and the internal debate over IPO timing signals that even OpenAI's own leadership is not fully aligned on the next chapter.

For investors, the calculus is straightforward but high-stakes: bet that the company generating $2 billion a month can eventually turn that into profit before the capital runs dry — or wait on the sidelines while what may become the defining technology company of the decade makes its public market debut without them.
