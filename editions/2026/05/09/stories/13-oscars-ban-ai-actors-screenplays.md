# Academy Bans AI-Generated Actors and AI-Written Scripts From Oscar Eligibility

The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences drew its sharpest line yet against generative AI on May 1, announcing that AI-generated performances and AI-written screenplays are ineligible for Academy Awards, effective immediately for the 99th ceremony covering films released in 2026.

Under the new rules, only acting roles that are credited in a film\u2019s legal billing and \u201cdemonstrably performed by humans with their consent\u201d can compete for Oscars. On the writing side, the language is equally unambiguous: screenplays must be \u201chuman-authored\u201d to qualify. The Academy also reserved the right to request detailed information about any submission\u2019s AI usage and human authorship, giving the organization an enforcement lever it has never before possessed.

\u201cHumans have to be at the center of the creative process,\u201d Academy President Lynette Howell Taylor said in remarks accompanying the announcement. \u201cAs AI continues to evolve, our conversations around AI will do so along with that. But for the Academy, we are always going to put human authorship at the center of our awards eligibility process.\u201d

## What the rules cover\u2014and what they don\u2019t

The prohibitions target two specific categories: acting and screenwriting. Digital recreations of deceased actors, synthetic performers generated entirely by AI, and scripts produced or substantially generated by machine-learning tools are all ruled out. The consent clause is notable\u2014it addresses not just AI-fabricated performers but also the unauthorized digital cloning of real actors, a practice that became a flashpoint during the 2023 Hollywood strikes.

Crucially, the Academy did not extend the ban to every corner of filmmaking. AI tools remain eligible in visual effects, sound design, editing, and other production categories. The distinction signals that the Academy views AI as an acceptable craft tool but draws the line at crediting it as creative author or performer.

\u201cThey can\u2019t stop productions from using genAI, but those works just won\u2019t be rewarded for that,\u201d Gizmodo\u2019s Justin Carter noted, capturing the pragmatic boundary the Academy is trying to set.

## Why now

The timing is no accident. In recent months, the AI entertainment space has pushed several provocations into public view. An independent film featuring an AI-generated version of the late Val Kilmer is currently in production. AI \u201cactress\u201d Tilly Norwood\u2014a fully synthetic performer created by a tech startup\u2014has generated headlines and controversy, including a widely panned music video released ahead of the 98th Oscars. Meanwhile, rapid advances in video-generation models like SeedAnce 2.0 have prompted some filmmakers to make sweeping declarations about the future of the craft.

The Academy\u2019s decision also lands in a year when both SAG-AFTRA and the Writers Guild of America are navigating new contract cycles with the major studios. The 2023 strikes\u2014which shut down Hollywood production for over four months\u2014were driven in large part by AI concerns. SAG-AFTRA\u2019s 118-day walkout specifically targeted the unauthorized use of AI replicas of actors\u2019 likenesses and voices. The WGA\u2019s resulting contract established that AI cannot be credited as a writer and cannot be used to undermine writers\u2019 compensation or credit.

By codifying similar principles into its eligibility rules, the Academy is essentially stamping the union positions with the industry\u2019s most prestigious institutional seal. The move may also strengthen the guilds\u2019 bargaining positions as they head into fresh negotiations this year.

## Enforcement questions

The rules raise practical questions that the Academy has not yet fully answered. How will voters and administrators verify that a performance was \u201cdemonstrably\u201d human? What threshold of AI assistance in a screenplay crosses the line from tool to author? The Academy\u2019s stated right to request AI-usage information suggests a disclosure-based model\u2014producers will need to certify, under penalty of disqualification, that credited performances were performed by humans and credited screenplays were written by them.

That approach mirrors a broader trend. Science-fiction and literary awards organizations have already begun requiring similar declarations, with some introducing formal affidavits of human origin. The Hugo Awards added AI-disclosure requirements in 2024 after controversy over suspected AI-generated nominees.

## The bigger picture

The Oscar rules do not exist in a vacuum. They arrive alongside a wave of institutional responses to generative AI across the creative industries. Publishers have pulled novels over suspected AI authorship. Comic conventions have barred AI-generated art from competitions. The Emmy Awards have reportedly been studying their own AI policies.

For the film industry specifically, the Academy\u2019s move establishes a de facto standard. Studios seeking Oscar-qualifying releases\u2014which account for the vast majority of prestige productions\u2014now have a concrete incentive to keep human performers and writers at the center of their projects, at least in the categories that matter most for awards campaigns.

The 99th Academy Awards ceremony is scheduled for March 2027, covering films released during calendar year 2026. Between now and then, the industry will be watching to see whether the Academy\u2019s new guardrails hold\u2014and whether other institutions follow its lead.
