Washington Governor Signs Two AI Safety Bills Into Law Amid National Wave of State Regulation

4 min read 872 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Washington Governor Bob Ferguson signed HB 2225 (chatbot safety) and HB 1170 (AI disclosure/provenance) into law, joining a national wave of state AI regulation.

OLYMPIA, Wash. — Washington Governor Bob Ferguson on March 24 signed two artificial intelligence safety bills into law before a crowd of parents, child safety advocates, and state lawmakers, making Washington one of the latest states to act on AI governance as a national patchwork of regulation continues to expand.

The two measures — House Bill 2225, targeting AI companion chatbot safety, and House Bill 1170, requiring disclosure when content is generated or modified by AI — reflect growing urgency among state legislators to address risks posed by rapidly advancing AI systems, particularly to young users.

"I speak partly as a governor, but also as a father of teenage twins, who grapples with this as a lot of parents do every single day," Ferguson said at the signing ceremony in the state capital. "We've all read news stories about the harm AI chatbots can do to our kids when these safeguards are not in place."

What the Laws Require

HB 2225, sponsored by Rep. Lisa Callan (D-Issaquah) and Sen. Lisa Wellman (D-Mercer Island), imposes new obligations on companies operating AI chatbots. Under the law, chatbot operators must clearly disclose to users that they are interacting with an artificial system, not a human. For users known to be minors, operators must repeat that disclosure every hour rather than the previously proposed three-hour interval. The bill also bans AI companions from engaging in sexually explicit conversations with minors and prohibits the use of manipulative engagement techniques designed to keep young users on the platform.

Critically, HB 2225 extends beyond minors: it requires chatbot operators to direct any user expressing thoughts of self-harm or suicidal ideation to crisis resources. Ferguson had personally requested the bill late last year after a series of widely reported incidents in which teenagers turned to AI chatbots during mental health crises with tragic outcomes.

HB 1170, authored by Rep. Clyde Shavers (D-Whidbey Island), takes aim at AI-generated misinformation by requiring that video, photo, and audio recordings created or enhanced through artificial intelligence carry distinguishable provenance data. The goal is to give consumers a clear signal when the content they encounter has been produced or altered by AI.

"By making it clear when AI generates media, Washingtonians are better protected against confusion, deception, and misinformation," Ferguson said. "I am confident I'm not the only Washingtonian who will often see something on my phone and wonder to myself, is that AI or is it real?"

A Staggered Rollout

The two laws will take effect on a staggered timeline. Provisions related to AI impersonation protections begin this June. The chatbot safety rules under HB 2225 take effect in January 2027. The full AI-generated media labeling requirements under HB 1170 follow in February 2027, giving companies time to build the necessary provenance infrastructure.

Part of a Nationwide Surge

Washington's action does not exist in a vacuum. According to tracking by Baker Botts, 78 chatbot-specific safety bills have been filed across 27 states in 2026 alone, virtually all targeting protections for minors. Oregon passed a similar chatbot safety bill just days before Washington, and Georgia has also advanced its own chatbot legislation this year.

The surge at the state level has intensified a parallel debate in Washington, D.C., over whether federal law should preempt the growing quilt of state regulations. In March 2026, the Trump administration released a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence that outlined seven pillars of proposed federal regulation, with limiting state influence through preemption as a central goal. The administration directed the Secretary of Commerce to identify state AI laws deemed "burdensome" and tasked the FTC with issuing guidance on when federal rules might override state statutes.

Yet the push for preemption has hit resistance in Congress. The Senate voted 99–1 to strip a proposed 10-year moratorium on state AI regulation from a reconciliation bill, signaling broad bipartisan support for preserving state authority. Legal experts note that executive orders cannot themselves preempt state law — only Congress or a federal court can do so — meaning the current wave of state legislation remains enforceable for now.

Advocates See Momentum

For organizations like the Transparency Coalition, which provided policy and technical expertise on both Washington bills, the signing ceremony marked a milestone after years of work. The coalition partnered with Rep. Shavers over two legislative sessions to move HB 1170 to passage.

Ferguson acknowledged the role of affected families in driving the legislation forward. "The most persuasive testimony at any hearing is someone directly impacted by whatever the issue is," he said. "We really appreciate individuals being willing to do that, which is not easy."

The question now is whether the emerging state-by-state approach will produce a durable regulatory framework or whether federal action — legislative or judicial — will ultimately reshape the landscape. For the parents and advocates who gathered in Olympia, the answer cannot come soon enough.

"Our children deserve to know when they are talking to a machine."
— Rep. Lisa Callan, Washington State Representative
