Nature Study Finds Human Scientists Still Outperform Best AI Agents on Complex Research Tasks

3 min read 838 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

AI agents score below 20% on ReplicationBench for full-scale paper replication while exceeding human baselines on narrow benchmarks like GPQA at 93%.

AI systems can win math olympiads and outperform chemists on standardized benchmarks, but when it comes to replicating real scientific research, humans still dominate -- and it is not even close.

The annual Stanford AI Index Report for 2026, highlighted in a Nature news article by journalist Nicola Jones, has delivered a paradox that should give both AI evangelists and skeptics plenty to chew on. While artificial intelligence has made extraordinary strides across narrow scientific benchmarks, the best AI agents still score below 20 percent on tasks that require replicating full-scale astrophysics research papers -- work that human scientists handle routinely.

The findings underscore what Stanford researchers are calling a "jagged frontier" of AI capability: a landscape where the same model that earns gold at the International Mathematical Olympiad can read an analog clock correctly only 50.1 percent of the time.

The Numbers Tell a Split Story

The scale of AI adoption in science is staggering. Publications in the natural sciences mentioning AI grew almost 30-fold from 2010 to 2025, according to the report. AI-related publications in the natural, physical, and life sciences all increased 26 to 28 percent year over year. Scientists who use AI-augmented research workflows publish 3.02 times more papers, receive 4.84 times more citations, and become research project leaders 1.37 years earlier than their peers.

But adoption does not equal mastery. On ReplicationBench, a benchmark developed by Stanford and University of Toronto researchers that tests whether AI agents can replicate entire astrophysics research papers from start to finish -- including experimental setup, data analysis, and code -- frontier models score below 20 percent. The benchmark requires agents to reproduce a paper's core contributions, with each task co-developed alongside the original authors to ensure objective evaluation.

Meanwhile, on narrower benchmarks, AI is crushing it. Model accuracy on GPQA, a graduate-level question-answering benchmark, surged past the expert human validator baseline of 81.2 percent to hit 93 percent. Frontier systems now outperform human chemists on average on ChemBench. On OSWorld, which tests agents on computer tasks across operating systems, accuracy rose from roughly 12 percent to 66.3 percent, within six percentage points of human performance at 72.35 percent.

"AI continues to expand its capabilities, hitting higher scores on benchmarks across types," the Stanford HAI report notes, while also documenting the stark reality that telling time, managing multi-step planning, and conducting financial analysis remain persistent weak spots.

The Paradox of Narrow Excellence

The disconnect between benchmark performance and real-world scientific capability is not just academic. It strikes at the heart of the question keeping research institutions up at night: can AI agents actually do science, or can they only pass science tests?

Raymond Perrault, co-chair of the AI Index, has framed the challenge in terms that make the gap visceral. The same systems scoring gold-medal performances on the hardest mathematical competitions humanity has devised cannot reliably interpret an analog clock face -- a task most children master by age seven.

The report's science chapter paints a nuanced picture. AI ran a full weather forecasting pipeline end-to-end for the first time, taking raw meteorological observations and directly outputting predictions for temperature, wind, and humidity. Astronomy built its first foundation model, automating observations across 10 telescopes. A 111-million-parameter protein language model beat previous leading methods on ProteinGym.

Yet when asked to replicate the kind of multi-step, judgment-heavy work that constitutes actual scientific research -- designing experiments, interpreting ambiguous data, writing coherent analyses -- AI agents fall dramatically short. The success rate of agents handling real-world tasks improved from 20 percent in 2025 to 77.3 percent on Terminal-Bench, but those tasks remain far simpler than conducting original research.

The Collaboration Question

The findings are already reshaping how institutions think about AI in the lab. A separate Nature correspondence published in May 2026 warned that over-reliance on AI agents in research risks undermining the apprenticeship model that trains the next generation of scientists. If junior researchers delegate complex analytical work to AI systems that cannot actually handle it reliably, the skills gap could compound over time.

The Stanford report also flags a troubling concentration effect. While AI-augmented researchers individually produce more, AI collectively narrows research diversity and reduces collaboration, concentrating work in data-rich areas. The risk is a scientific ecosystem that is individually more productive but collectively less creative.

For now, the data points to a clear interim conclusion: AI is an extraordinarily powerful research assistant but a mediocre research scientist. The gap between acing a standardized test and conducting original inquiry remains wide -- and closing it will require advances that go well beyond scaling up existing models.

The jagged frontier, it turns out, is jagged for a reason. Science is not a benchmark.
