Utah Governor Signs All Nine AI Bills Including Deepfake Protections and Provenance Requirements

4 min read 920 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Utah Governor Spencer Cox signed all nine AI-related bills from the 2026 session, including HB 276 requiring deepfake protections and AI provenance data.

Governor Spencer Cox has signed all nine artificial intelligence bills passed during Utah's 2026 legislative session, cementing the state's position as one of the most aggressive AI regulators in the country. The package, which cleared the legislature before it adjourned on March 7 after one of the nation's shortest sessions at just under seven weeks, spans deepfake protections, AI content provenance standards, school device bans, healthcare AI guardrails, and online age verification.

The Centerpiece: HB 276 and Digital Content Provenance

The most consequential measure in the package is HB 276, the Digital Voyeurism Prevention Act, sponsored by Rep. Ariel Defay (R) and Sen. Kirk Cullimore (R). The law attacks the problem of nonconsensual AI-generated intimate imagery from two angles: it bans AI generation services and platforms from distributing counterfeit intimate images without verified consent from the depicted individual, and it mandates that large online platforms detect, disclose, and preserve provenance data in distributed content.

Under the new law, websites and social media platforms operating in Utah must provide a mechanism for individuals to report when their likeness is used to create intimate photos or videos without permission. Platforms are required to remove flagged content within 48 hours and make reasonable efforts to identify and remove identical copies. Violations carry civil liability, including actual damages, punitive damages, attorney fees, and injunctive relief, with enforcement handled by the Utah Division of Consumer Protection.

The provenance provisions are equally significant. HB 276 requires AI providers to embed digital markers in AI-generated content, effectively creating a digital fingerprint that allows users to determine whether an image was captured by a real camera or generated by a computer. The requirement applies to generative AI systems with more than one million monthly users that are publicly accessible within Utah's borders.

Bill sponsor Defay said she worked closely with almost every large tech company operating in Utah to ensure protections for both the industry and its consumers. She described the provenance data requirements as showing the history of how an image or video has been changed, a transparency layer that the bill's backers argue is essential in an era of increasingly realistic synthetic media.

Nine Bills, Four Pillars

Beyond HB 276, the remaining eight bills organize around three additional pillars. Three measures target AI and digital devices in schools: SB 69 establishes one of the nation's most sweeping bell-to-bell bans on smartphones and smartwatches during school hours, effective July 1, 2026; HB 273 requires school districts to create model policies for AI use in classrooms; and HB 218 adds AI literacy to the required digital skills curriculum for seventh and eighth graders.

Two bills address AI in healthcare. SB 319 requires health insurers to disclose whether they use AI in preauthorization decisions and mandates that adverse determinations involve independent medical judgment rather than sole reliance on algorithmic recommendations. SB 150 restricts AI-driven healthcare innovations by specifying that technology upgrades cannot solely function to provide advice or treatment to patients without practitioner interaction.

SB 256 extends existing defamation law to cover AI-generated content, clarifying that libel and slander statutes apply to deepfakes and digitally manipulated material. HB 289 updates the legal definition of Child Sexual Abuse Material to include AI-generated depictions. And SB 73 enacts online age verification requirements for publishers or distributors of digital material harmful to minors.

Cox's National Stance on AI Regulation

Governor Cox has positioned himself as a leading voice for state-level AI regulation, directly challenging the federal government's preference for a lighter touch. "Let's use this technology to benefit humankind, and let's regulate it to make sure they don't destroy humankind," Cox said in a February address asserting states' right to legislate AI. "I don't think that's a contradiction. I think that's common sense."

That stance has drawn friction with the White House, which in February sent a letter to Utah legislators calling the state's AI Transparency Act "an unfixable bill that goes against the Administration's AI Agenda." But the administration's own executive order includes a critical carve-out: child safety protections are expressly exempt from federal preemption. Since the majority of Utah's AI bills focus on protecting minors and individuals from AI-generated harm, the carve-out likely shields their core provisions from federal challenge.

A Bellwether for a National Wave

Utah's nine-bill package arrives amid an unprecedented surge in state-level AI legislation. According to the Transparency Coalition, 78 chatbot-related bills are currently alive in 27 states, with proposals clustering around six themes: transparency, age assurance, content safety, professional licensure, data protection, and liability. Nineteen new AI bills were signed into law across various states in April alone, according to Plural Policy.

The breadth of Utah's approach, covering schools, healthcare, deepfakes, provenance, defamation, child safety, and age verification in a single session, offers a template that other states are likely to study. Whether the federal government will continue to defer to this state-by-state patchwork or move toward preemption remains the defining tension in American AI policy. For now, Utah has staked its position: regulate first, negotiate later.

"Utah is proving that pro-innovation and pro-safety are not mutually exclusive."
— Governor Spencer Cox, Governor of Utah
