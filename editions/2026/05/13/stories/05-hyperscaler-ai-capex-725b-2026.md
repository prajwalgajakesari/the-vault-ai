The four largest hyperscalers are on track to pour $725 billion into capital expenditure in 2026, a 77 percent increase over last year's record $410 billion and a sum that exceeds the entire annual GDP of Switzerland. The spending surge, confirmed across first-quarter earnings from Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft, represents the largest single-year infrastructure commitment in corporate history and signals that the AI arms race is accelerating, not plateauing.

The numbers from individual companies are staggering. Amazon is expected to approach $200 billion in capital expenditures for the year. Alphabet's CFO Anat Ashkenazi confirmed full-year 2026 capex guidance of $180 to $190 billion, with 2027 expected to increase further still. Meta raised its forecast to between $125 billion and $145 billion. Microsoft's annualized spending rate is tracking near $150 billion. Combined quarterly spending for the group topped $130 billion in Q1 alone.

The rationale is demand, pure and simple. Alphabet CEO Sundar Pichai put it bluntly during the company's earnings call: "We are compute constrained in the near term." Google Cloud revenue surged 63 percent year-over-year in Q1 to $20 billion, with operating income tripling to $6.6 billion. More telling still, Google Cloud's backlog nearly doubled quarter-over-quarter to more than $460 billion in contracted future revenue, a sign that enterprise customers are locking in AI infrastructure access years in advance rather than waiting to see whether the buildout pays off.

## Where the Money Goes

Roughly 75 percent of aggregate hyperscaler capex in 2026 is flowing directly into AI-related infrastructure, translating to approximately $545 billion earmarked specifically for the chips, data centers, and networking equipment that power modern AI systems. A single high-end Nvidia GPU can cost up to $40,000, but hyperscalers buy them by the tens of thousands. An eight-GPU server runs into the hundreds of thousands of dollars, and the clusters needed for frontier model training can cost billions on their own.

Then there are the data centers housing all that silicon. Meta's $27 billion Hyperion complex in northeastern Louisiana, which some estimates suggest will use millions of GPUs, offers a glimpse of the scale involved. These facilities consume electricity equivalent to small cities and require vast tracts of land, specialized cooling systems, and redundant power supplies.

As Fortune's Sharon Goldman wrote in her Eye on AI newsletter, the AI spending race "is now in its third year and still shows no signs of slowing." In 2024, the combined capex of the four biggest hyperscalers was just over $200 billion. Two years later, it has more than tripled.

## The Power Problem

The most significant constraint on this buildout is not capital or demand but electricity. Goldman Sachs research documents an 11-gigawatt shortfall in US data center power capacity today, a gap projected to widen to more than 40 gigawatts by 2028. Morgan Stanley Research forecasts US data center power demand could reach 74 GW by 2028, with a projected shortfall of roughly 49 GW in available power access.

The consequences are already visible. Microsoft has disclosed an $80 billion backlog of Azure orders that cannot be fulfilled because of power constraints. CEO Satya Nadella has acknowledged that GPUs sit idle in inventory because the company cannot find enough electricity to run them. With only about 5 GW under active construction against 12 to 16 GW that operators committed to deliver this year, the resulting 7 GW shortfall has become the defining bottleneck of the current cycle.

US utilities have responded with a $1.4 trillion capital spending plan through 2030 aimed primarily at meeting AI data center demand. Texas, Virginia, and Arizona are absorbing the largest share of new builds, but permitting delays and grid interconnection timelines mean that power availability will likely constrain AI expansion for years to come.

## The Bull and Bear Cases

Not everyone is convinced the spending spree is sustainable. Some investors warn of a potential overbuild scenario in which infrastructure investment runs too far ahead of revenue. AI hardware depreciates rapidly, meaning the true cost of this buildout extends well beyond the initial capital outlay. And the market reaction to earnings has been mixed: Meta shares fell sharply after its report as investors focused on spending scale, while Alphabet and Amazon rose on strong cloud growth.

But the bull case rests on numbers that are difficult to argue with. McKinsey research projects that global AI capex will require $6.7 trillion by 2030 to keep pace with compute demand. Enterprise customers are not merely experimenting with AI; they are signing multi-year, multi-billion-dollar cloud commitments that create durable, sticky revenue streams for the hyperscalers. The contracted backlog at Google Cloud alone suggests demand is outpacing even aggressive supply expansion.

## What It Means

The $725 billion year marks a structural inflection point. Four American corporations are now deploying infrastructure budgets that rival the output of mid-sized nations, building the compute layer that will underpin productivity gains across every industry for the next decade. Whether the returns justify the investment remains the central question, but one thing is clear: the hyperscalers are betting that the AI era is not a cycle to be timed but a transformation to be built. The only real constraint they face is not money or conviction. It is the grid.
