# Google and SpaceX in Talks to Launch AI Data Centers Into Orbit

The AI infrastructure race just left the atmosphere.

Google and SpaceX are in advanced discussions to launch data centers into orbit, according to a Wall Street Journal report published Monday. The partnership would see SpaceX provide rocket launch services for Google's ambitious Project Suncatcher initiative, which envisions constellations of AI-equipped satellites circling the Earth and powered by uninterrupted solar energy. If the deal moves forward, it would mark one of the most audacious bets yet in Big Tech's escalating scramble for compute capacity.

The talks come at a pivotal moment for both companies. SpaceX is preparing for an initial public offering later this year at a reported valuation of $1.75 trillion, and orbital AI infrastructure has become a central plank of the company's pitch to prospective investors. Google, meanwhile, owns a 6.1% stake in SpaceX, dating back to a $900 million investment in 2015, meaning it stands to benefit from the IPO whether or not the orbital venture succeeds.

## Project Suncatcher: TPUs in the Sky

Google first unveiled Project Suncatcher in November 2025 as a research initiative exploring space-based AI compute. The vision is striking in its scale: an eventual constellation of 81 satellites spanning a one-kilometer radius, forming an in-orbit compute cluster equipped with Google's custom Tensor Processing Units (TPUs). These satellites would communicate via laser-based free-space optical links, sharing massive AI workloads across the constellation.

Two prototype satellites, built in partnership with satellite manufacturer Planet Labs, are targeted for launch by early 2027. Those test units will serve as proof of concept, validating whether the hardware can survive radiation exposure and thermal extremes while performing meaningful AI inference and training tasks. Google's Trillium TPUs have already passed radiation testing in a particle accelerator simulating low-Earth orbit conditions, a critical early milestone.

"A launch deal would put the two companies in partnership as they gear up to compete on orbital data centers, an unproven technology that SpaceX Chief Executive Elon Musk has said is the next frontier for his rocket company," the Wall Street Journal reported.

## The Economics of Escape Velocity

The pitch for orbital data centers rests on a simple premise: AI models are consuming electricity faster than grid operators can supply it, and space offers virtually unlimited solar power with no land costs, no cooling water bills, and no local planning disputes. On the ground, new data center projects across the United States and Europe face mounting resistance tied to power consumption, water usage, zoning battles, and environmental concerns.

But the economics remain brutally challenging. Google's own internal models put the break-even point at $200 per kilogram of payload to orbit, a figure that only becomes realistic if SpaceX's Starship rocket flies around 180 times per year and drives launch prices down roughly 90% from current rates. Google's projection places that cost threshold at 2035, meaning nearly a decade of investment before orbital compute could compete with terrestrial alternatives on price.

As TechCrunch noted in a February analysis, today's ground-based data centers remain significantly cheaper than orbital ones once satellite construction, launch, and maintenance costs are factored in. Radiation exposure, thermal management in vacuum, and ultra-fast communication between orbital systems and Earth-based networks all present engineering challenges that remain largely unsolved at commercial scale.

## A Crowded Orbit

Google is not the only company eyeing the stars. Anthropic struck a deal with SpaceX last week to use computing resources from xAI's massive Memphis supercluster, which houses more than 220,000 Nvidia GPUs, and expressed interest in collaborating on orbital data center development. SpaceX acquired xAI in February, consolidating Elon Musk's AI and space ambitions under a single corporate umbrella.

The startup ecosystem is mobilizing as well. Cowboy Space Company recently raised $275 million to build rockets specifically designed for launching orbital compute payloads, a sign that venture capital sees a real market forming. If orbital infrastructure proves viable, the ripple effects could extend across satellite manufacturing, optical communications, in-orbit servicing, and space logistics.

Musk has been the most vocal champion of the concept, framing orbital data centers as SpaceX's next major commercial product beyond satellite internet. "Orbital data centers are going to be the lowest-cost path for AI compute within the next few years," Musk has argued, positioning the technology as a natural extension of the Starship program's promise to dramatically reduce the cost of reaching space.

## The Bottom Line

The Google-SpaceX talks represent a fascinating collision of two of the most capital-intensive industries on Earth: hyperscale cloud computing and commercial spaceflight. The deal would be symbolically powerful, pairing the world's largest AI cloud provider with the world's most prolific rocket company. But symbolism alone does not close a business case.

The real test is whether Starship can deliver on its cost promises, whether TPUs can function reliably in orbit, and whether the $200-per-kilogram threshold can be reached before competitors find cheaper ways to expand compute on the ground. Industry analysts caution that orbital data centers are unlikely to replace terrestrial mega-clusters anytime soon, with early deployments more likely serving as overflow infrastructure for the most compute-intensive frontier AI systems.

Still, the trajectory is unmistakable. Big Tech's AI race is no longer just about models and chips. It is becoming a contest over physical infrastructure, energy access, and who controls the next generation of compute. Google's willingness to look 400 kilometers straight up for answers suggests the company believes the ceiling on terrestrial AI growth is closer than most people think.
