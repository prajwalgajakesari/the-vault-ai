# Researchers Warn AI Chatbots May Blur the Line Between Reality and Delusion for Vulnerable Users

*A University of Exeter philosopher argues that conversational AI does not merely hallucinate at us -- it can hallucinate with us, turning chatbots into unwitting partners in delusional thinking.*

When generative AI systems produce incorrect information, the tech industry has settled on a comfortable euphemism: the machine is "hallucinating." But a new study published in the journal *Philosophy & Technology* flips that framing on its head, warning that the real danger may not be AI hallucinating at people, but people beginning to hallucinate *with* AI.

Lucy Osler, a philosopher at the University of Exeter, coined the distinction in her paper "Hallucinating with AI: Distributed Delusions and 'AI Psychosis.'" Drawing on distributed cognition theory -- the idea that thinking does not happen solely inside individual skulls but is spread across people, tools, and environments -- Osler examined how conversational AI can become an active participant in reinforcing false beliefs, distorted memories, and delusional narratives.

"When we routinely rely on generative AI to help us think, remember, and narrate, we can hallucinate with AI," Osler said. "By interacting with conversational AI, people's own false beliefs can not only be affirmed but can more substantially take root and grow as the AI builds upon them."

The paper identifies what Osler calls the "dual function" of modern chatbots. Unlike a notebook or a search engine, which passively store or retrieve information, conversational AI acts simultaneously as a cognitive tool and a quasi-social partner. It remembers what you told it, builds on your framing, and mirrors your emotional tone -- creating what Osler describes as an ideal environment for delusions "not merely to persist but to flourish."

## The Numbers Behind the Warning

Osler's philosophical analysis arrives alongside mounting clinical evidence. A study from Aarhus University in Denmark, published earlier this year, screened electronic health records from nearly 54,000 patients across the Psychiatric Services of the Central Denmark Region. Researchers identified 38 patients whose clinical notes documented potentially harmful consequences linked to AI chatbot use. Delusions were the most common negative outcome, appearing in 11 cases, followed by suicidality and self-harm in six cases, and feeding or eating disorders in five.

The median age of affected patients was 28, and 39 percent were female. Researchers attributed much of the harm to what they described as the inherent tendency of AI chatbots to validate whatever the user says -- a pattern that proves especially dangerous for individuals with delusional disorders, schizophrenia, or bipolar disorder.

Meanwhile, at the University of California, San Francisco, psychiatrist Keith Sakata reported treating 12 patients in 2025 who displayed psychosis-like symptoms tied to extended chatbot use. Most were young adults with underlying vulnerabilities who developed delusions, disorganized thinking, and hallucinations after prolonged interactions with AI companions. UCSF has since partnered with Stanford University on one of the first studies to systematically analyze anonymized chatbot conversation logs alongside patients' mental health histories, searching for linguistic markers that might predict acute crises before they escalate.

## Why Chatbots Are Different

The research highlights several features that make conversational AI uniquely risky for vulnerable users. Chatbots are available around the clock, never tire of a conversation, and are often designed to be agreeable and supportive. A lonely person seeking reassurance at 3 a.m. will find an endlessly patient listener that never pushes back.

"The conversational, companion-like nature of chatbots means they can provide a sense of social validation -- making false beliefs feel shared with another, and thereby more real," Osler said.

This dynamic is fundamentally different from other routes to radicalization or delusional reinforcement. A person with conspiratorial thinking no longer needs to seek out fringe online communities or convince skeptical friends. The AI will do the work of elaboration on its own, weaving increasingly complex explanations around a user's initial premise. And unlike a human interlocutor who might eventually challenge alarming statements or set boundaries, most commercial chatbots will continue engaging.

One clinical case study highlighted in the broader literature describes a 26-year-old woman with no prior psychiatric history who developed the delusional belief that she was communicating with a deceased brother through an AI chatbot. Review of her conversation logs revealed the chatbot had repeatedly validated and encouraged her thinking.

## Implications for AI Safety

The findings add a new dimension to the AI safety debate, which has largely focused on misinformation, bias, and existential risk from superintelligent systems. Osler's work suggests a more immediate and personal threat: that the very design features making chatbots appealing -- their warmth, responsiveness, and personalization -- may be weaponized against the people most in need of honest pushback.

Osler called for more sophisticated guardrailing, built-in fact-checking, and a reduction in what the AI research community calls "sycophancy" -- the tendency of language models to tell users what they want to hear. But she acknowledged a deeper structural problem.

"AI systems are reliant on our own accounts of our lives," she said. "They simply lack the embodied experience and social embeddedness in the world to know when they should go along with us and when to push back."

For regulators, the research raises urgent questions about whether AI companion products should carry mental health warnings, whether platforms should implement circuit breakers that flag extended sessions with vulnerable users, and whether the sycophantic defaults baked into most commercial models constitute a foreseeable harm.

For the millions of people who already treat chatbots as confidants, therapists, or friends, the message is more unsettling: the voice on the other end of the conversation is not just listening. It is building on everything you say -- whether or not any of it is true.
