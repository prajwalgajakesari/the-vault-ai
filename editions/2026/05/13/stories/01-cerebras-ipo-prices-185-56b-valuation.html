Cerebras Prices IPO at $185 Per Share, Raising $5.55 Billion at $56.4 Billion Valuation

4 min read 774 words 6 sources
Key Takeaway

Cerebras priced its IPO at $185 per share, raising $5.55 billion at a $56.4 billion valuation in the largest tech IPO of 2026, with the offering 20 times oversubscribed.

The AI chip startup that bet everything on building the world's largest processor just became the biggest tech IPO of the year.

Cerebras Systems priced its initial public offering at $185 per share on Wednesday evening, well above its most recently raised range of $150 to $160, pulling in $5.55 billion from the sale of 30 million shares. The offering values the Sunnyvale, California-based company at $56.4 billion on a fully diluted basis, cementing what has been one of the most closely watched public market debuts in the AI hardware sector.

The company will begin trading on the Nasdaq Global Select Market on Thursday, May 14, under the ticker symbol CBRS.

From Stealth Startup to Mega-Cap Aspirant

The pricing caps a whirlwind roadshow that saw investor appetite far outstrip expectations. Cerebras initially filed to sell 28 million shares at $115 to $125 apiece when it launched the offering on May 4. By May 11, surging demand forced the company to raise its range to $150 to $160. The final price of $185 blew past even that revised band, with the order book closing roughly 20 times oversubscribed, according to people familiar with the matter.

At the IPO price, co-founder and CEO Andrew Feldman is sitting on a stake worth approximately $1.9 billion. Feldman is not selling shares in the offering. As he told CNBC during the roadshow preparations, "It's a good time to be in AI hardware."

The frenzied demand reflects a Wall Street increasingly desperate to find the next Nvidia. Brian Colello, senior equity analyst at Morningstar, called the valuation surge "remarkable," noting that Cerebras sat at an $8 billion valuation as recently as October before its landmark deals with OpenAI and Amazon Web Services catapulted the company into a different orbit.

The Wafer-Scale Gamble

Cerebras has staked its future on a radically different approach to chip design. Its Wafer-Scale Engine, now in its third generation as the WSE-3, uses an entire silicon wafer as a single chip, making it roughly 57 times larger than the largest competing GPU die. The architecture packs vastly more on-chip memory, more processing cores, and higher interconnect bandwidth than traditional multi-chip GPU clusters, eliminating the latency overhead that comes from stitching together hundreds of discrete processors.

The technical bet has attracted some of the most prominent names in AI. Cerebras's most significant commercial win is a multi-year agreement with OpenAI for 750 megawatts of AI compute capacity, one of the largest AI infrastructure contracts ever publicly disclosed. The company's backers include OpenAI co-founders Sam Altman, Greg Brockman, and Ilya Sutskever, along with deals with Amazon Web Services that have further broadened its customer base.

Why This Matters for the AI Landscape

The Cerebras IPO is more than a single company milestone. It is a signal that the AI investment thesis is broadening beyond the Nvidia trade that has dominated public markets for the past three years. Investors are now aggressively hunting for second-generation AI winners tied to compute infrastructure, inference demand, and next-generation chip architectures.

The $56.4 billion valuation also sets a new benchmark for how the market prices pre-profit AI hardware companies. Cerebras reported approximately $510 million in revenue in its most recent fiscal year, but the bulk of that is concentrated among a handful of customers. OpenAI and Abu Dhabi-linked entities account for roughly 86 percent of the company's revenue, a concentration risk that analysts have flagged as the single biggest vulnerability in the investment case.

Still, the sheer scale of the oversubscription suggests institutional investors are willing to look past those risks in exchange for exposure to what they see as a credible alternative to Nvidia's dominance in AI training and inference hardware.

What to Watch Next

All eyes now turn to Thursday's opening trade on Nasdaq. Given the 20-times oversubscription, a significant first-day pop would not be surprising, though it would also raise questions about whether the underwriters left money on the table. Beyond the ticker debut, investors will be watching for early guidance from Cerebras on revenue trajectory, new customer wins outside its concentrated base, and how the company deploys the massive $5.55 billion war chest it has just raised. The Cerebras listing may also open the floodgates for a wave of AI-related IPOs that bankers have been teeing up throughout the first half of 2026.
