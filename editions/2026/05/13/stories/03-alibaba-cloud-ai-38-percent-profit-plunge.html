Alibaba Cloud Revenue Jumps 38 Percent as AI Investment Sends Profits Plunging 84 Percent

4 min read 830 words 5 sources
Key Takeaway

Alibabas Cloud Intelligence Group grew revenue 38% driven by AI products now at 30% of cloud revenue, but massive investment crushed adjusted EBITA by 84%.

Alibaba Group is betting that the future of artificial intelligence is worth more than the present comfort of its profit margins — and the numbers released Wednesday make that gamble impossible to ignore.

The Chinese tech giant reported that its Cloud Intelligence Group posted revenue growth of 38 percent year over year for the quarter ended March 31, reaching approximately 41.6 billion yuan ($6.04 billion). AI-related product revenue within that division has now grown at triple-digit rates for 11 consecutive quarters and accounts for 30 percent of the cloud unit's external customer revenue. But the cost of chasing that growth was staggering: adjusted EBITA plunged 84 percent to 740 million yuan, while adjusted net income cratered to just $12 million — a decline of nearly 100 percent. Alibaba also posted its first operating loss since 2021, recording a loss from operations of 848 million yuan ($123 million).

The results lay bare a tension at the heart of every major technology company's strategy right now. Growth in AI demands enormous upfront capital. The returns, if they come at all, arrive years later. Alibaba is placing one of the largest bets in the industry that the payoff will justify the pain.

The Bull Case From the C-Suite

CEO Eddie Wu used the earnings call to frame the spending not as a sacrifice but as an inevitability. He described the current moment in AI as a turning point, arguing that the industry is shifting from simple chatbots to autonomous AI agents — a transition that demands massive new infrastructure.

"We are at a pivotal inflection point in the evolution from conversational chatbots to autonomous AI agents, which is directly driving explosive growth across three core workload categories: training, inference and agent orchestration," Wu told analysts.

When pressed by analysts on how management balances aggressive AI spending against earnings stability, Wu compared the investment to building factories in order to profit from future manufacturing. "We see the ROI on this investment in the next three- to five-year period as being extremely clear," he said.

Wu laid out an ambitious target: surpassing $100 billion in combined cloud and AI external revenue within five years, powered by three growth pillars — model-as-a-service (MaaS) as the primary engine, private enterprise AI infrastructure deployment, and the transformation of traditional CPU-based cloud into agent-optimized infrastructure. He indicated that capital expenditure would likely exceed the previously stated 380 billion yuan figure, projecting a need for roughly ten times the data center capacity Alibaba had in 2022.

Revenue Growth Masks Broader Softness

The cloud division's surge contrasted sharply with the rest of Alibaba's business. Total group revenue came in at 243.38 billion yuan ($35.28 billion), up just 3 percent year over year and short of analyst expectations. Stripping out revenue from Sun Art and Intime — two businesses Alibaba divested — like-for-like revenue growth was a more respectable 11 percent.

Alibaba's two core e-commerce segments, which the company calls its "consumption businesses," each grew revenue by 6 percent. The Alibaba China E-commerce Group and Alibaba International Digital Commerce Group both showed steady but unspectacular performance. Meanwhile, the company's "all others" segment saw revenue decline 21 percent, driven primarily by the Sun Art and Intime disposals.

Group-wide EBITDA fell 61 percent year over year, a decline the company attributed to technology investments, the ramp-up of its Taobao Instant Commerce quick-delivery service, and spending on user experience improvements.

Market Reaction and the Bigger Picture

Investors were not immediately persuaded by the growth-over-profits narrative. Alibaba shares fell more than 2.5 percent in premarket trading following the report, reflecting Wall Street's discomfort with the margin compression even as AI revenue accelerated.

The tension is not unique to Alibaba. Across the global technology sector, companies from Microsoft to Amazon to Alphabet have signaled that AI infrastructure spending will remain elevated for years. But Alibaba's case is particularly striking because of the sheer magnitude of its margin contraction and the competitive dynamics of the Chinese cloud market, where rivals Huawei Cloud, Tencent Cloud, and Baidu AI Cloud are all pursuing similar strategies.

What separates Alibaba from some of its Western counterparts is the pace of its AI revenue ramp. Growing AI product revenue at triple-digit rates for nearly three years straight suggests genuine commercial traction, not just speculative capital deployment. Wu signaled that AI products will account for more than 50 percent of cloud revenue within about a year — a milestone that would fundamentally reshape the division's profile.

The question hanging over Alibaba's results is the same one facing every hyperscaler in 2026: can AI revenue scale fast enough to backfill the profits being consumed by infrastructure spending? Alibaba's leadership is clearly betting that it can. Whether investors will have the patience to wait for that bet to pay off remains the open question heading into the back half of the year.
