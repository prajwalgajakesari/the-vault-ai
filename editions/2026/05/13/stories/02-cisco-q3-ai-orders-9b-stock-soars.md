# Cisco Stock Soars 17 Percent as AI Infrastructure Orders Hit $9 Billion and Company Cuts 4,000 Jobs

*The networking giant posted record quarterly revenue of $15.8 billion, nearly doubled its full-year AI order forecast from $5 billion to $9 billion, and announced a restructuring plan that will eliminate fewer than 4,000 positions as it pivots aggressively toward silicon, optics, and enterprise AI.*

---

Cisco Systems delivered a third-quarter earnings report on Tuesday that sent shares surging as much as 20% in after-hours trading, driven by an explosive acceleration in AI infrastructure demand from hyperscale cloud providers and a decisive management bet that the networking giant's future runs through artificial intelligence.

The San Jose-based company reported record revenue of $15.84 billion for the quarter ended April 25, up 12% year over year and ahead of Wall Street's consensus estimate of $15.56 billion. Non-GAAP earnings per share came in at $1.06, topping the $1.04 analyst target. GAAP net income surged 35% to $3.37 billion, while non-GAAP operating margin reached 34.2%, reflecting what management described as strong execution and operational discipline across all regions.

The real headline, however, was Cisco's staggering revision to its AI infrastructure outlook. The company disclosed that it has already booked $5.3 billion in orders from hyperscalers year to date and now expects full-year AI infrastructure orders to reach $9 billion—nearly double the $5 billion target it set just months ago. Expected full-year AI revenue was raised from $3 billion to $4 billion.

## Hyperscaler Demand Reshapes the Business

The networking segment was the undeniable growth engine, with revenue climbing 25% year over year to $8.82 billion and exceeding the Street's $8.47 billion forecast. Total product orders surged 35% year over year, while networking product orders accelerated to more than 50% growth, powered by triple-digit gains in service provider routing and compute alongside double-digit growth in data center switching.

"Cisco delivered record quarterly revenue in Q3 and we saw very strong, broad-based demand for our products, demonstrating the relevance of our technology for connecting and securing AI," said CEO Chuck Robbins. "Cisco is well-positioned as the critical infrastructure for the AI era, building on our technology leadership and customer trust."

CFO Mark Patterson reinforced the momentum, noting that Cisco "once again delivered double-digit growth on both the top and bottom lines which exceeded the high end of our guidance, coupled with record non-GAAP operating income."

Fourth-quarter guidance underscored management's confidence: Cisco expects revenue of $16.7 billion to $16.9 billion and non-GAAP EPS of $1.16 to $1.18, far above the analyst consensus of $15.82 billion and $1.07 per share. Full-year revenue guidance was raised to $62.8 billion to $63.0 billion, with non-GAAP EPS of $4.27 to $4.29.

## 4,000 Jobs Cut to Fund the AI Pivot

Alongside the earnings beat, Cisco announced a restructuring plan that will eliminate fewer than 4,000 positions—less than 5% of its workforce—beginning immediately. The company said the cuts are designed to free up investment capacity in four priority areas: silicon, optics, security, and AI. The restructuring will carry pre-tax charges of up to $1 billion, with roughly $450 million expected to be recognized in the fourth quarter and the remainder in fiscal 2027.

In a blog post published to coincide with the results, Robbins framed the decision as an inevitable consequence of the AI transition. He wrote that winning in the AI era requires "focus, urgency, and the discipline to continuously shift investment toward the areas where demand and long-term value creation are strongest."

The layoffs add to a brutal year for tech workers. Industry tracker Layoffs.fyi reports that the technology sector has slashed more than 100,000 jobs in 2026 so far, rapidly approaching last year's full-year total. Cisco itself cut approximately 5,500 roles in 2024, and the latest round signals that the AI-driven reallocation of corporate resources shows no signs of slowing.

The company said it would provide affected employees with one year of access to Cisco U, its certification platform covering AI and security skills, along with internal placement services that it claims have a 75% success rate.

## Analysis: A Late Mover Making Up Ground

Cisco's results mark a decisive chapter in the company's AI transformation story. After years of sluggish growth that earned it a reputation as a legacy networking vendor, Cisco has leveraged its massive installed base and deep relationships with hyperscalers to position itself as a critical supplier of AI data center connectivity.

The $9 billion AI order revision is particularly significant. It suggests that hyperscaler spending on networking infrastructure is accelerating faster than even bullish forecasts anticipated, and that Cisco is capturing a meaningful share of that wallet. Campus networking orders growing more than 25% year over year, with next-generation products ramping faster than any prior product launch, indicate that enterprise demand is broadening beyond the cloud giants.

Analyst Holger Mueller of Constellation Research said the results confirm Cisco's resurgence after a multi-year slump. "Technology companies running AI need to invest in networks, and that's why it's showing strong growth in a part of its business that had not grown for some time," Mueller said. He noted, however, that the networking boom is masking continued stagnation in Cisco's security, collaboration, and observability segments, all of which were essentially flat in the quarter.

That mixed picture raises an important strategic question. Cisco's decision to cut 4,000 jobs while booking record orders suggests management recognizes that not all parts of the business will benefit equally from the AI wave. The restructuring is effectively a rebalancing—shrinking areas of flat growth to pour resources into the segments where demand is exploding.

For investors, the stock's 32% year-to-date rally reflects growing conviction that Cisco has successfully reinvented itself for the AI era. Whether that conviction is fully justified will depend on whether the company can translate its networking dominance into durable positions in silicon design and AI security—the very areas it is now betting billions on.
