US Intelligence Agencies Seek Greater Power Over AI Model Evaluations in Sharp Policy Battle

4 min read 870 words 5 sources
Key Takeaway

The Trump administration is deadlocked over whether the Commerce Department or intelligence community should lead pre-release evaluations of frontier AI models.

A turf war inside the Trump administration over who gets to vet America's most powerful AI models is stalling what could become the most significant federal intervention in the artificial intelligence industry to date.

The White House has spent the past several weeks drafting an executive order that would formalize government oversight of frontier AI systems before they reach the public, but a bitter dispute between the Commerce Department and the intelligence community over which arm of the federal government should lead that process has ground progress to a halt. The debate, first reported in detail by the Washington Post on May 11, has exposed a fundamental tension at the heart of the administration's AI strategy: whether frontier model evaluation is primarily a standards and innovation challenge or a national security imperative.

Mythos Changed the Calculus

The urgency behind the push can be traced directly to Anthropic's Mythos, the AI model that upended Washington's hands-off approach to the technology almost overnight. Mythos identified tens of thousands of software vulnerabilities across widely used cryptographic libraries, protocols like TLS and SSH, and consumer software including nearly 300 bugs in Firefox alone. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei warned in early May of a six- to 12-month window to patch the flaws before Chinese AI capabilities catch up, describing the situation as "a moment of danger."

The model's extraordinary cyber capabilities forced the administration into an uncomfortable reversal. On his first day in office, President Trump revoked the Biden administration's AI executive order, signaling that the government would take a permissive stance toward the technology. Now, barely 16 months later, many of the same oversight mechanisms the administration dismantled are being rebuilt under different branding, driven by the realization that unchecked frontier models pose tangible national security risks.

The Turf Battle

At the center of the dispute is a deceptively simple question: who should sit in the room when the next Mythos-class model is ready for evaluation?

The Commerce Department argues that its Center for AI Standards and Innovation, known as CAISI, already has the infrastructure and relationships to do the job. CAISI, housed under NIST, has conducted 40 pre-deployment evaluations to date and recently signed agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI to test their frontier models before public release, adding to existing partnerships with OpenAI and Anthropic. Congress backed the effort in the latest appropriations cycle, directing $55 million to NIST's AI research and measurement programs, with up to $10 million earmarked specifically for CAISI expansion.

Intelligence officials counter that CAISI's mandate is too narrow. The NSA has already been testing Mythos internally, and national security hawks argue that spy agencies' expertise in threat assessment makes them better equipped to evaluate models that could be weaponized for cyberattacks, disinformation, or biological and chemical threats. Their proposal would house a new AI evaluation center within the intelligence community, giving classified agencies direct access to frontier models before any commercial deployment.

Commerce officials have pushed back hard, warning that routing model evaluations through the intelligence apparatus could chill voluntary cooperation with AI developers. The existing CAISI framework relies on companies voluntarily submitting their models for testing. Introducing spy agencies into that pipeline, critics argue, risks turning a collaborative standards process into something closer to a security clearance review.

Congressional Pressure Builds

The stalemate has not gone unnoticed on Capitol Hill. Rep. Jim Himes of Connecticut, the top Democrat on the House Intelligence Committee, delivered a blunt assessment in remarks reported by Government Executive, calling it "insane" for intelligence agencies to lack early access to advanced AI models. His comments reflect a growing bipartisan consensus that the current ad hoc approach is insufficient, even as lawmakers disagree on the specifics.

Meanwhile, the White House's own messaging has been inconsistent. National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett floated the idea of an "FDA for AI" that would require safety reviews of future models, only for White House chief of staff Susie Wiles and former AI and crypto czar David Sacks to publicly walk back his remarks. The episode, reported by Axios, underscored the lack of internal cohesion on what the government should actually do next.

What Comes Next

The executive order had been expected as early as May 13, but administration officials told Axios that the China summit and internal disagreements have pushed the timeline back indefinitely. One tech industry source suggested the White House may wait for the outcome of Trump's China trip before making final decisions, recognizing that any AI oversight framework will have significant implications for the broader technology competition between Washington and Beijing.

For the AI industry, the stakes are enormous. Five of the world's leading frontier AI developers have already agreed to voluntary pre-deployment evaluations through CAISI. A shift toward intelligence-led oversight could fundamentally alter that dynamic, replacing a transparent, standards-based process with one shrouded in classification and national security secrecy.

The irony is hard to miss. An administration that entered office promising to unleash AI innovation by stripping away government guardrails is now engaged in an internal battle over which part of the government should have the most power over the technology's future. The answer, whenever it arrives, will shape how America's most consequential technology is developed, tested, and deployed for years to come.
