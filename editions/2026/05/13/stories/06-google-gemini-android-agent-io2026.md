Google wants your Android phone to stop being a tool you operate and start being an agent that operates on your behalf. At The Android Show: I/O Edition on Monday, the company unveiled Gemini Intelligence, an agentic AI layer baked into Android that can read your screen, move across apps, and execute multi-step tasks without requiring users to hop between services manually. The announcement lands one week before Google I/O 2026 on May 19-20 and sets the stage for what the company calls a fundamental shift in how smartphones work.

"We're transitioning from an operating system to an intelligence system," Sameer Samat, president of the Android Ecosystem, told CNBC. The framing is deliberate: Google is not pitching Gemini Intelligence as another chatbot skin laid over existing features. Instead, the company is rebuilding core parts of Android 17 around a model that can understand context, plan actions, and carry them out across first- and third-party applications.

## From assistant to agent

The demo that drew the most attention showed Gemini handling a barbecue planning workflow end to end. A user asked the assistant to check a guest list in one app, generate a menu, add the required ingredients to an Instacart shopping cart, and then pause for human approval before checkout. Each step involved a different application, yet Gemini navigated between them autonomously, only returning control to the user at the moment of purchase.

Google also demonstrated Gemini building a delivery cart from a grocery list stored in a notes app and booking restaurant reservations by pulling details from a conversation thread. The common thread: tasks that currently require five or six app switches are collapsed into a single voice or text request, with the AI handling the plumbing in between.

Samat emphasized that Android is being designed to transform "intention into action," with the goal of reducing screen dependence by allowing devices to act as proactive assistants rather than passive tools waiting for tap-by-tap instructions.

## How it works, and where the guardrails are

Gemini Intelligence sits at the system level of Android 17, giving it access to on-screen content and the ability to invoke actions inside apps that opt into the framework. The feature set announced Monday includes three pillars: cross-app task automation, an upgraded autofill system that pulls data from connected apps to complete forms across Android and Chrome, and Rambler, a speech-to-text tool that strips filler words from dictated messages in real time.

Privacy controls are central to the pitch. Google says every Gemini Intelligence feature will require explicit user permission before acting, and sensitive operations such as finalizing a purchase or submitting a form will demand manual confirmation or a biometric check like a fingerprint scan. The system is designed to pause and ask the user before any data is permanently committed, a safeguard that addresses one of the loudest criticisms leveled at agentic AI systems.

## Rollout and device scope

The initial rollout this summer will target select Samsung Galaxy and Google Pixel devices, with broader expansion planned for later in 2026. Google signaled significant ambition for the platform's reach: Gemini Intelligence is slated to extend beyond phones to Android watches, cars via Android Auto, laptops through the new Googlebook hardware line, and smart glasses running Android XR.

On the XR front, Google confirmed that Android XR glasses will be previewed at I/O 2026. Samsung is expected to unveil its Galaxy Glasses at a London event on July 22, running Android XR with Gemini as the built-in assistant. Warby Parker and Gentle Monster are also confirmed as eyewear partners, suggesting Google envisions AI-powered glasses across a range of price points rather than a single flagship device.

## The competitive picture

The timing is strategic. Apple is widely expected to announce a major AI overhaul for iOS at WWDC in June 2026, and Google appears determined to establish the narrative around agentic mobile AI before Cupertino takes the stage. By moving Android-specific announcements to May 12 and reserving the broader I/O keynote for May 19, Google gave Gemini Intelligence a full week of uncontested attention.

The stakes are high. Agentic AI on mobile devices represents the next major interface paradigm after the touchscreen, and the company that nails the experience first stands to define user expectations for years. Google's advantage is its control over both the operating system and the underlying model, but execution will matter more than positioning. If Gemini Intelligence ships this summer and actually handles multi-app workflows reliably, it could mark the most significant change to how people use Android since the introduction of Material Design. If the guardrails feel too restrictive or the automation too brittle, it risks joining the long list of Google demos that looked better on stage than in practice.

Either way, the line Google drew on Monday was unmistakable: the era of the phone as a passive rectangle of apps is ending, and the era of the phone as an autonomous agent is beginning.
