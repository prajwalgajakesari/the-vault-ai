# 65 Percent of US Doctors Now Use OpenEvidence AI Chatbot Across 27 Million Monthly Encounters

A three-year-old medical AI startup has quietly become the most widely adopted technology platform in American medicine, and most patients have no idea it exists.

OpenEvidence, a Miami-based AI-powered clinical search engine, is now used by roughly 650,000 physicians in the United States, representing approximately 65 percent of all practicing doctors in the country, the company disclosed to NBC News this week. In April alone, those clinicians generated nearly 27 million encounters on the platform, asking it to weigh in on drug interactions, treatment protocols, diagnostic uncertainties, and clinical decisions that directly shape patient care.

On March 10, the platform crossed yet another threshold: one million clinical consultations between verified doctors and the AI system in a single day. That milestone, announced in a press release, underscored the velocity at which OpenEvidence has moved from niche experiment to something approaching essential infrastructure for American medicine.

## From Zero to Twelve Billion Dollars

The financial trajectory has been just as staggering. OpenEvidence has raised approximately $700 million in total funding across four rounds completed in under a year. In January, a $250 million Series D co-led by Thrive Capital and DST Global doubled the company's valuation to $12 billion, up from $1 billion in early 2025. Sequoia Capital, Google Ventures, Nvidia, Andreessen Horowitz, Kleiner Perkins, and Coatue are also on the cap table. The company reached $100 million in annualized revenue in January, powered primarily by advertising from pharmaceutical and medical device companies.

CEO Daniel Nadler framed the accomplishment in characteristically bold terms. "We did the hardest thing in the history of American health care," Nadler told NBC News. "We got the majority of American doctors to all voluntarily adopt a single technology platform."

## What Sets It Apart

Unlike general-purpose chatbots that scrape the open internet, OpenEvidence draws its answers from licensed partnerships with elite medical publishers. The platform has secured content agreements with the New England Journal of Medicine, JAMA, the National Comprehensive Cancer Network, and the American Diabetes Association, among others. When a physician poses a clinical question, the system synthesizes relevant peer-reviewed literature and returns a sourced summary with links to the underlying papers.

Dr. Paul Sax, an infectious disease specialist at Brigham and Women's Hospital in Boston, described the experience as bordering on "miraculous," noting that OpenEvidence provides customized, source-linked answers far more efficiently than UpToDate, the legacy reference tool that has dominated clinical decision support for decades.

The platform has also scored a perfect 100 percent on the United States Medical Licensing Examination, a benchmark it prominently advertises. But independent scrutiny tells a more complicated story. An academic study released in December found that OpenEvidence accurately answered complex medical questions less than 45 percent of the time, though the study has not yet been peer reviewed.

## The Shadow AI Problem

Perhaps the most revealing detail in this week's reporting is how many physicians adopted the tool informally, on personal devices, outside the oversight of their hospital systems. Several doctors interviewed by NBC News requested anonymity because their employers had not authorized them to discuss tools that were never officially approved.

Dr. Girish Nadkarni, head of AI at Mount Sinai Health System in New York, put it bluntly, describing a growing phenomenon of "shadow AI" in which clinicians quietly use tools on personal computers while institutions focus on the visible surface of approved technology. In March, Mount Sinai moved to formalize the relationship, announcing an enterprise partnership that links OpenEvidence directly to the hospital system's electronic health record portal for use by doctors, nurses, and pharmacists.

Dr. Anupam Jena, an internal medicine physician at Massachusetts General Hospital and a professor at Harvard, is now studying 90 million OpenEvidence queries submitted since 2024. His early analysis reveals a telling pattern: "Sixty percent of all the searches are about how to make clinical decisions," Jena said. Surgeons ask about blood pressure management. Internists look up rare fracture diagnostics. Physicians are most active when stepping outside their core specialty, using the tool as a real-time extension of their training.

## Concerns and Competition

Not everyone is celebrating without caveats. Several doctors told NBC News that OpenEvidence occasionally draws overly strong conclusions from small-sample studies, and Dr. Hannah Galvin, chief health information officer at the Cambridge Health Alliance, cautioned that few rigorous studies have yet examined how the tool affects patient outcomes. "We want to make sure that we have explored and understood how these tools are performing for our population," Galvin said, adding that her team is currently conducting research comparing OpenEvidence answers against those from general-purpose chatbots.

There are also concerns about skill erosion. One midcareer physician in Missouri warned that introducing an AI tool that handles core reasoning tasks from the earliest stages of medical training could degrade students' ability to sort meaningful clinical signals from noise, a worry that gains weight as medical schools have not yet widely integrated AI literacy into their curricula.

Meanwhile, the competitive landscape is intensifying. UpToDate is rushing out its own AI product called Expert AI, claiming roughly 2,000 hospitals signed up by the end of April. In April, OpenAI launched ChatGPT for Clinicians, though it currently lacks the premium journal licensing deals that give OpenEvidence its authoritative edge. Other entrants like Doximity and iatroX are also vying for clinician attention.

## What Comes Next

OpenEvidence plans to expand into AI notetaking, medical billing, and deeper integration with clinical visit workflows. With 1.2 million additional users internationally and a war chest approaching three-quarters of a billion dollars, the company is positioning itself not merely as a search tool but as the operating layer for AI-assisted medicine.

The question that looms over the entire enterprise is whether adoption speed has outpaced the evidence base. American medicine now has a tool used by a supermajority of its physicians, generating tens of millions of consultations per month, whose impact on patient outcomes remains largely unmeasured. That gap between adoption and validation may be the most important story in healthcare AI this year.
