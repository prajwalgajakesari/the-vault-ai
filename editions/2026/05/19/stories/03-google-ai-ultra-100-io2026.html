Google Halves AI Ultra Price to $100 Per Month at I/O 2026

3 min read 748 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Google cut its AI Ultra entry price from $249.99 to $100/month at I/O 2026, undercutting ChatGPT Pro by half and bundling Gemini Spark as the primary value driver.

Google just made the most aggressive pricing move in the premium AI subscription war. At Google I/O 2026 on Tuesday, the company sliced the entry price of its AI Ultra tier in half — from $249.99 to $100 per month — while simultaneously cutting its top-end plan from $250 to $200, reshaping a competitive landscape where every dollar now signals strategic intent.

The price drop arrives alongside the debut of Gemini Spark, a 24/7 agentic personal assistant that may be the most consequential product Google announced all week. Together, they represent Google's sharpest pitch yet to developers, knowledge workers, and power users who have been watching the AI subscription market with growing frustration over what their money actually buys.

A New Tier, a New Agent, a New Playbook

The restructured lineup is built around three tiers. Google AI Pro remains at $19.99 per month — squarely in line with ChatGPT Plus and Claude Pro, where the standard market has now settled. The new $100 AI Ultra tier targets what Google describes as "developers, technical leads, knowledge workers, and advanced creators," offering a 5x higher usage limit in the Gemini app compared to Pro, 20 TB of cloud storage, a bundled YouTube Premium subscription, priority access to Google Antigravity, and early access to Gemini Spark. The $200 top-end Ultra plan retains a 20x usage ceiling and is aimed at the heaviest professional workloads.

The most significant structural change, however, is not purely about price. Google is replacing its daily prompt caps with a compute-based metering system — charging by the actual computational cost of a query rather than counting messages. Heavier workloads can be extended with paid top-up credits. It is a model borrowed from cloud infrastructure pricing, and it signals that Google is treating its AI products less like consumer apps and more like developer platforms.

Gemini Spark: The Agent Behind the Pricing Story

If the $100 price tag is the headline, Gemini Spark is the substance behind it. Announced as a native agentic assistant built on Gemini base models and the Google Antigravity infrastructure, Spark is designed to take on long-horizon tasks autonomously — continuing to work even when a user has closed their laptop or put down their phone.

Alphabet CEO Sundar Pichai framed the product in terms that deliberately distinguish it from the question-answering paradigm that has defined AI assistants to date. "Gemini Spark is your personal AI agent that helps you navigate your digital life, taking action on your behalf and under your direction," Pichai said at the keynote. "It is an active partner that does real work."

At launch, Spark integrates with Gmail — users can email the agent directly at a dedicated address — as well as Google Docs, Slides, and Sheets. Third-party integrations with Canva, OpenTable, and Instacart are confirmed for launch, with Google signaling that the list will expand throughout the year. The agent can also interact with the web through Chrome, extending its reach beyond the Google ecosystem.

Gemini Spark is currently in beta and will roll out first to trusted testers and AI Ultra subscribers starting this week. The Gmail integration alone positions Spark as something qualitatively different from prior Google Assistant products: it enables persistent task delegation rather than one-shot commands, a distinction that matters enormously for anyone who has tried to hand off a multi-step research task to an AI only to have it lose the thread five minutes later.

The Competitive Math

The pricing restructure carries an unmistakable competitive logic. OpenAI's ChatGPT Pro sits at $200 per month and includes access to the o1 Pro extended reasoning model. Anthropic has not yet fielded a comparable ultra-tier consumer product at scale. Google's new $100 AI Ultra slot undercuts ChatGPT Pro by half while bundling cloud storage, YouTube Premium, and agentic capability in a single package.

For enterprise and prosumer buyers doing the arithmetic, the value calculus shifts meaningfully. Twenty terabytes of cloud storage alone carries real standalone cost. A YouTube Premium individual subscription adds roughly $14 per month in saved spend. Add Gemini Spark's agentic functionality — which could plausibly reclaim hours of manual work per week for a knowledge worker — and the $100 figure becomes considerably harder to dismiss.

The compute-based usage model also matters here. Prompt-count limits have been a persistent frustration for power users who hit daily ceilings mid-workflow. Moving to computational cost metering aligns Google's incentives with actual usage patterns rather than penalizing users for asking complex, multi-step questions. "The move to compute-based pricing is arguably the most interesting part of this announcement," noted one analyst covering the release. "It shifts the conversation from 'how many questions can I ask' to 'how much work can I get done.'"

What It Means for the Market

Google's move at I/O 2026 is less a defensive play than a structural bet that the AI subscription market will stratify permanently. The $20 tier is becoming a commodity — ChatGPT Plus, Claude Pro, and Gemini AI Pro are now essentially price-identical. The real competition is playing out above that ceiling, where companies are racing to justify premium price points with genuinely differentiated capability.

Gemini Spark is Google's answer to that challenge. Whether it delivers on the promise of autonomous, long-horizon task execution will determine whether the $100 price point holds beyond early adopters. If it does, Google will have set a new benchmark that OpenAI and Anthropic will be under pressure to match. If it does not, the halved price reads as a concession rather than a confidence move.

The broader signal from I/O 2026 is that Google is no longer content to compete at the margins of a market it helped create. The company arrived at its developer conference this year with a restructured pricing model, a new agentic product, and a direct challenge to the incumbent at $200 per month. The AI race, it turns out, just got considerably more expensive to sit out.
