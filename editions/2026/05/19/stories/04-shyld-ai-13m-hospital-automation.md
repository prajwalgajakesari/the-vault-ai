# Shyld AI Raises $13.4 Million to Bring Agentic AI Inside Hospital Operations

Every year, roughly 72,000 people in the United States die from infections they contracted inside hospitals -- the very facilities meant to heal them. Shyld AI, a startup deploying autonomous disinfection robots and agentic AI agents inside operating rooms, just raised $13.4 million in seed funding to prove that AI can do something the healthcare industry has failed to accomplish through human effort alone: make hospitals consistently clean.

The round, led by Aulis Capital and announced this week, is one of the largest early-stage checks written into the hospital operations AI space this year. It caps a period of rapid growth for the San Francisco-based company, which now has its hardware and AI platform running inside more than 30 hospitals across the country.

## Not Another Dashboard

Most hospital AI today is what the industry calls ambient -- it watches, it listens, it documents. Shyld AI is betting on a different model. The company calls its approach Active AI: systems that perceive conditions in a room in real time and then physically act on what they find, without waiting for a human to intervene.

"We are not building another dashboard for clinicians to check," said Mohammad Noshad, co-founder and CEO of Shyld AI. "We are building AI that acts -- that owns a workflow end to end and closes the loop between perception and physical response."

That distinction matters in a market crowded with AI tools that have been criticized for adding alert fatigue rather than reducing clinical burden. Shyld AI's hardware mounts inside patient rooms and operating suites. It monitors movement, identifies when a high-contact surface has been touched or when a room has been vacated, and automatically deploys a directional UV-C beam to sanitize the area. The system completes targeted decontamination in seconds, without requiring a staff member to set it up or trigger it.

## The Vertex Model: Edge AI Built for Hospital Floors

The technical backbone of the platform is Vertex, Shyld AI's proprietary foundation model. Unlike cloud-dependent AI systems that route data through remote servers -- creating latency and raising patient privacy concerns -- Vertex runs directly on the company's edge devices installed inside hospital rooms. The model enables continuous perception, reasoning, and action without relying on hospital IT infrastructure, a significant practical advantage given how fragmented and legacy-laden hospital networks tend to be.

"Hospitals cannot hand their operational trust to a system that needs a strong Wi-Fi connection and a data center in another state," Morteza Noshad, co-founder and CTO, told HIT Consultant. "Vertex was designed from the ground up to be autonomous at the edge."

That architecture also sidesteps the data-sharing and HIPAA compliance hurdles that slow many healthcare AI deployments. Because no patient data leaves the device, Shyld AI has been able to deploy more quickly inside health systems that would otherwise spend months in IT security review.

## The Numbers Behind the Pitch

The clinical case for the technology rests on a study conducted at Stanford University and published in the American Journal of Infection Control. The research found that Shyld AI's autonomous disinfection system reduced contamination by more than 93 percent compared to a control room using standard cleaning protocols. That is not a marginal improvement -- it represents a near-elimination of the microbial load that conventional environmental services teams struggle to achieve consistently, particularly during high-turnover periods between surgeries.

The broader market problem the company is attacking is significant. Healthcare-associated infections affect an estimated 1.7 million patients annually in the United States, according to the CDC, contributing to tens of thousands of deaths and adding billions in excess treatment costs. Infection prevention has historically relied almost entirely on human compliance: staff remembering to wipe down surfaces, wearing protective gear correctly, and following turnover protocols under time pressure. Shyld AI's argument is that agentic AI removes the compliance variable entirely.

The $13.4 million seed round will fund expansion across additional U.S. health systems and support development of Active AI capabilities beyond infection control -- including operating room workflow optimization, compliance monitoring, and eventual expansion into regulated pharmaceutical cleanroom environments.

## The Agentic Shift in Healthcare Operations

Shyld AI's raise arrives at an inflection point for agentic AI broadly. After years of large language model demos and ambient documentation tools, investors and health systems alike are beginning to ask what it looks like when AI does more than assist -- when it executes. The company's positioning as Active AI is a direct response to that question, and its focus on the physical environment of care distinguishes it from the cohort of AI scribes and clinical decision support tools that have dominated healthcare AI investment over the past three years.

The brothers Noshad, who founded the company in 2022, came from engineering and robotics backgrounds rather than healthcare administration, which may explain why they approached the infection control problem as a systems automation challenge rather than a workflow improvement initiative. The difference in framing has produced a product that operates independently of clinician behavior -- a key selling point for hospital administrators exhausted by change management.

What makes the Shyld AI story worth watching is not just the funding round or the 93 percent contamination stat. It is the underlying proposition: that the most dangerous problems in healthcare operations may yield not to better training or better software, but to machines that simply never forget to do their job.
