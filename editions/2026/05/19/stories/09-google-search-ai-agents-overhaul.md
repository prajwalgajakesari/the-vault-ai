# Google Brings AI Agents to Search in Its Biggest Overhaul in 25 Years

The search box that has greeted billions of internet users for more than a quarter century got its most dramatic reinvention yet on Tuesday, as Google unveiled a sweeping transformation of its flagship product at Google I/O 2026 — replacing the familiar keyword bar with an AI-powered interface capable of accepting text, images, files, video, and open browser tabs, while deploying autonomous agents to monitor the web continuously on users' behalf.

The announcements, made by Elizabeth Reid, Google's VP of Search, mark what the company calls the dawn of "the era of Search agents" — a fundamental shift from a tool that retrieves information to one that reasons, plans, executes tasks, and proactively delivers answers before users think to ask. For Google, the timing is both an assertion of dominance and an acknowledgment of existential pressure: the company that invented modern web search is now racing to reinvent it before AI-native challengers do.

## What's Actually Changing

The centerpiece of Tuesday's rollout is a reimagined Search box powered by Gemini 3.5 Flash, Google's newest frontier model. The interface dynamically expands to accommodate complex, nuanced queries, surfaces AI-powered suggestions that go well beyond traditional autocomplete, and accepts multimodal inputs across text, images, video, files, and even open Chrome tabs. The upgrade ships globally today in every country and language where AI Mode is available.

The new box feeds into AI Mode, which hit a milestone Google was eager to announce: one billion monthly users, just one year after its debut. Queries in AI Mode have more than doubled every quarter since launch, and Google says total search volume reached an all-time high last quarter — a counter-narrative to fears that AI chatbots were cannibalizing search traffic.

But the interface change is almost secondary to the structural shift beneath it. Google is introducing what it calls "information agents" — persistent AI systems that monitor the web around the clock on a user's behalf, scanning blogs, news sites, social posts, financial data, sports scores, and shopping platforms, then delivering synthesized updates when something relevant changes. The demo scenarios offered a clear picture of the target use case: a user hunting for an apartment can describe their exact requirements in plain language, and an agent will continuously scan listings, notifying them when a match appears. A sneaker enthusiast can ask an agent to watch for athlete-branded product drops and get an alert the moment inventory goes live.

Information agents will launch first for Google AI Pro and Ultra subscribers this summer in the United States.

Reid framed the shift in characteristically expansive terms: "We're entering the era of Search agents, where you can easily create, customize and manage multiple AI agents for your many tasks, right in Search."

## From Retrieval to Execution

Beyond passive monitoring, Google is also expanding what it calls "agentic booking capabilities" — the ability for Search to take action, not just surface results. Users can describe a specific need (a private karaoke room for six on a Friday night that serves food late, for example), and Search will assemble real-time pricing and availability data with direct links to complete the booking. For categories like home repair, beauty, and pet care, Google will call businesses on a user's behalf — a feature that rolls out to all U.S. users this summer.

The third major pillar is agentic coding, powered by Google's Antigravity platform and Gemini 3.5 Flash. Search can now generate custom generative UIs — interactive visualizations, tables, simulations — on the fly in response to queries. For users with ongoing, recurring tasks, Search can build full custom dashboards or "mini apps": a fitness tracker that pulls real-time weather and local gym data, a wedding planning dashboard that aggregates vendor availability and reviews, a project timeline tool that updates as circumstances change. These generative UI capabilities will be available free to all users this summer, with the more powerful mini-app builder launching first for AI Pro and Ultra subscribers.

Sundar Pichai, speaking at the I/O keynote, described the cumulative effect plainly: "Search has turned into an execution engine."

## The SEO Reckoning

For the publishing industry and the broader ecosystem of businesses that have built their digital presence around Google search rankings, Tuesday's announcements accelerate a reckoning that has been building since the introduction of AI Overviews in 2024.

The data already tells a difficult story. Sites have reported search traffic declines ranging from 20% to 40% since AI-generated summaries began appearing at the top of results pages. Zero-click searches — queries that end without a user visiting any website — now account for 58% of all Google searches. When AI Overviews are present, that figure climbs to 83%.

The shift from retrieval to execution deepens the problem. When Google's agents book the karaoke room, monitor the apartment listings, and call the home repair service directly, the websites and directories that previously sat in the middle of those transactions lose their role as essential stops in the user journey. The question for publishers is no longer just how to rank for a keyword — it's whether ranking for a keyword still reliably delivers a visitor at all.

The emerging response within the SEO industry is a reorientation around what practitioners are calling Generative Engine Optimization, or GEO. Rather than competing purely for blue-link ranking positions, the strategic goal is to earn citations inside AI Overviews and agent-generated responses. Sites that achieve citation status can see click-through rates increase by as much as 35%, according to current data — but it requires a different kind of optimization: structured data, authoritative sourcing, and content that AI models are likely to surface as reference material.

Google has signaled, at least implicitly, that the web remains necessary to its model. Information agents draw their intelligence from "everything on the web," as Reid's post emphasized — blogs, news sites, social posts, live data feeds. The underlying bargain between Google and the open web has not been formally renegotiated. But the terms are clearly shifting.

## What Comes Next

The rollout cadence for Tuesday's announcements is staggered across the summer, with the most powerful agentic features reserved initially for paying AI Pro and Ultra subscribers. That subscription tier is becoming an increasingly important revenue vector for Google as it absorbs the infrastructure costs of Gemini-scale inference at search volume — a number that, by Google's own account, is growing faster than it ever has.

For competitors, the picture is equally clarifying. Microsoft's Copilot integration in Bing, Perplexity's AI-native search experience, and a growing field of vertical AI search tools have all been betting that Google's incumbency would make it slow to cannibalize its own business model. Tuesday's I/O announcements are Google's most direct answer yet to that bet: the company is willing to break the blue-link paradigm it built, on its own terms, before someone else does it for them.

The search box is 25 years old. As of this week, it is something else entirely.
