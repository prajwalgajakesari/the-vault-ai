# Amazon Custom Silicon Business Crosses $20 Billion Annual Run Rate as Trainium Demand Soars

Amazon has quietly built one of the largest semiconductor businesses on the planet. During the company's Q1 2026 earnings call in late April, CEO Andy Jassy revealed that Amazon's custom silicon operation -- spanning Trainium AI accelerators, Graviton CPUs, and Nitro virtualization chips -- has crossed a $20 billion annual revenue run rate, growing at triple-digit percentages year over year and nearly 40 percent quarter over quarter. The numbers position Amazon alongside NVIDIA and AMD as one of the top three data center chip businesses in the world, a status that would have been unthinkable just three years ago when the company was still proving its first-generation Trainium chips could compete with off-the-shelf GPUs.

The Scale of the Empire

Jassy put the true dimensions of the business into stark competitive terms on the earnings call. "If our chips business was a standalone business and sold chips produced this year to AWS and other third parties as other leading chip companies do, our annual revenue run rate would be $50 billion," he said. "As best as we can tell, our custom silicon business is now one of the top three datacenter chip businesses in the world."

The business doubled from a $10 billion annual run rate reported alongside Q4 2025 earnings. The demand pipeline is even more striking. Amazon holds over $225 billion in revenue commitments for Trainium alone, secured through multi-year, multi-gigawatt contracts with the world's leading AI labs. Anthropic has committed to up to five gigawatts of Trainium capacity, representing the largest single customer commitment Amazon has disclosed. OpenAI has committed to roughly two gigawatts, with the agreement set to ramp in 2027. Uber has signed on for Graviton4 and Trainium3 across its ride and delivery platform, and Meta Platforms is deploying tens of millions of Graviton cores for agentic AI workloads.

Sold Out Across Generations

The supply picture tells the story of a business that cannot build chips fast enough to meet demand. Trainium2, which Jassy said delivers approximately 30 percent better price-performance than comparable GPUs, has largely sold out. Trainium3, which began shipping in early 2026 with 30 to 40 percent better price-performance than its predecessor, is nearly fully subscribed despite being only months into production. Even Trainium4, still roughly 18 months from broad availability, has already been significantly reserved.

"The speed at which we've gotten here is extraordinary, and we have momentum for our custom AI silicon," Jassy said on the call. "We've recently shared very large, multi-year, multi-gigawatt training commitments from the two leading AI labs in the world, Anthropic and OpenAI, as well as an increasing number of companies like Uber betting on Trainium."

Graviton, Amazon's custom CPU line, is reinforcing the same trajectory. Graviton processors are now used by 98 percent of the top 1,000 AWS EC2 customers, delivering up to 40 percent better price-performance than x86 alternatives. Meta's decision to deploy Graviton at massive scale for agentic AI workloads validates the chip's position as a credible alternative to Intel and AMD server processors in AI-adjacent compute.

Why This Matters

Amazon's chip business matters for the AI industry far beyond AWS's balance sheet. The company is fundamentally restructuring the economics of cloud AI infrastructure. Every time a customer runs AI workloads on Trainium instead of renting NVIDIA GPU instances, Amazon pays near-cost for the compute rather than NVIDIA's retail price. Jassy stated on the earnings call that at scale, Trainium is expected to save Amazon tens of billions of dollars in capital expenditure annually and deliver several hundred basis points of operating margin advantage for inference versus relying on external chips.

This margin advantage is the mechanism behind AWS's expanding profitability. AWS posted $37.6 billion in Q1 2026 revenue, growing 28 percent year over year -- its fastest growth rate in 15 quarters. Operating margins hit 38 percent for the segment. Analysts project Amazon's EBITDA margins could approach 35 percent by 2030, driven primarily by Trainium's cost displacement effect rather than retail efficiency gains.

The competitive implications extend to NVIDIA, which has dominated AI training infrastructure for years. By offering a 30 percent price-performance advantage on Trainium and bundling it with the broader AWS ecosystem, Amazon is capturing an increasing share of AI compute spending that would otherwise flow to NVIDIA's data center business. The $225 billion in Trainium commitments represents revenue structurally locked away from NVIDIA's addressable market. For Marvell Technology, which designs Amazon's custom chips under a five-year partnership extended in December 2024, the backlog is a growth engine -- Marvell's revenue grew 42 percent year over year in fiscal 2026 to $8.2 billion, with 40 percent data center revenue growth projected for the current fiscal year.

What to Watch Next

The critical metric to track is AWS segment operating margin at the Q2 2026 earnings report, expected in late July. If margins hold at or above 38 percent, it will confirm that Trainium's cost advantages are flowing through the income statement ahead of schedule. Watch also for any announcement of Trainium chip sales to third parties outside the AWS ecosystem -- Jassy's framing of the $50 billion standalone business figure and recent reports that Amazon is considering selling Trainium rack systems suggest the company may be preparing to compete directly with NVIDIA in the merchant chip market. With $225 billion in committed revenue and three generations of chips either sold out or nearly subscribed, Amazon's silicon ambitions have moved well past the experimental phase. The question is no longer whether Amazon can build competitive AI chips. It is whether anyone can build them fast enough.
