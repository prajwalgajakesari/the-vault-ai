# Anthropic Commits 5 Gigawatts of Trainium Capacity Outpacing OpenAI Two and a Half to One

Anthropic is not just racing to build the best AI models. It is racing to secure the electricity to run them. The Claude maker has committed to up to five gigawatts of Trainium compute capacity from Amazon Web Services, a figure that outpaces OpenAIs roughly two-gigawatt commitment by a ratio of two and a half to one. To put that number in perspective, five gigawatts is roughly equivalent to five large nuclear power plants and represents approximately two-thirds the power capacity of Googles entire global data center fleet, which industry estimates place at seven to eight gigawatts. No single AI company has ever locked in infrastructure at this scale.

## The AWS Mega-Deal

The commitment is anchored in a sweeping ten-year agreement announced in April, under which Anthropic will spend more than 100 billion dollars on AWS technologies. Amazon, in turn, invested an additional five billion dollars in Anthropic immediately, with up to 20 billion more tied to milestones, bringing Amazons total investment in the company to roughly 29 billion dollars. The deal encompasses current and future generations of AWS custom silicon -- Trainium2, Trainium3, Trainium4, and subsequent iterations -- alongside tens of millions of Graviton CPU cores.

AWS CEO Andy Jassy framed the significance in competitive terms during the Q1 2026 earnings call. "We have recently shared very large, multi-year, multi-gigawatt training commitments from the two leading AI labs in the world, Anthropic and OpenAI," Jassy said. "As best as we can tell, our custom silicon business is now one of the top three datacenter chip businesses in the world." Trainium2 capacity is already largely sold out, with nearly one gigawatt of combined Trainium2 and Trainium3 capacity scheduled to come online by the end of 2026.

## A Compute Portfolio Unlike Any Other

What makes Anthropics strategy distinctive is not just the size of the AWS deal but the breadth of its compute portfolio. In a matter of weeks during May 2026, Anthropic assembled a multi-vendor infrastructure backbone that no other AI company has attempted at comparable scale.

The most headline-grabbing move was the partnership with SpaceX to take over all compute capacity at the Colossus 1 data center in Memphis, Tennessee. The facility, originally built by Elon Musks xAI, provides over 300 megawatts and houses more than 220,000 NVIDIA GPUs, including H100, H200, and next-generation GB200 accelerators. "We are partnering with SpaceX to use all the capacity of their Colossus One data center," Anthropic head of product Ami Vora announced at the companys developer conference in San Francisco. The deal was driven by immediate necessity -- Anthropic needed more GPUs to double Claude Code rate limits and expand API capacity for Pro, Max, and enterprise tiers.

Then came the Akamai deal: 1.8 billion dollars over seven years for cloud infrastructure services, the largest contract in Akamais 28-year history. Akamai brings something the hyperscalers cannot easily replicate -- a distributed edge network spanning 4,300 locations across 700 cities in 130 countries. For inference workloads, which require low latency at high volume, that geographic footprint offers a structural advantage. Akamai claims it can reduce latency and cut inference costs by up to 86 percent compared to traditional centralized data centers.

Add to this Anthropics existing Google Cloud partnership, which provides access to up to one million TPU chips, and the company is running AI workloads across at least four major providers simultaneously. A separate five-gigawatt agreement with Google Cloud and Broadcom is set to begin ramping in 2027, along with a Microsoft and NVIDIA strategic partnership that includes 30 billion dollars of Azure capacity.

## The Revenue Engine Behind the Spending

The infrastructure binge is backed by revenue growth that has stunned even Anthropics own leadership. At the developer conference in early May, CEO Dario Amodei disclosed that the company had experienced 80-fold annualized growth in revenue and usage during the first quarter of 2026. "That is the reason we have had difficulties with compute," Amodei told the crowd, describing the growth as "just crazy" and "too hard to handle." Anthropic had planned for tenfold growth and got eight times that.

The numbers bear it out. Anthropic crossed a 30 billion dollar annualized revenue run rate in Q1 2026, up from roughly nine billion at the end of 2025. For Q2 2026, the company projects 10.9 billion dollars in quarterly revenue -- a figure that would exceed its total revenue for all of last year in a single quarter -- and a 559 million dollar operating profit, its first ever. Compute spending per revenue dollar fell from 71 cents to 56 cents between Q1 and Q2, suggesting that economies of scale are beginning to kick in even as infrastructure commitments expand.

The company is in talks to raise capital at a 900 billion dollar-plus valuation and is eyeing a 2026 IPO, moves that would give it the financial firepower to sustain multi-hundred-billion-dollar infrastructure commitments over the coming decade.

## What It Means

Anthropics compute strategy represents a bet that in the AI era, infrastructure access matters as much as model architecture. Every frontier lab can build competitive models. Not every lab can secure the electricity, chips, and data center capacity to serve those models at global scale when demand spikes eightyfold in a single quarter.

The multi-vendor approach is also a hedge. OpenAIs deep dependence on Microsoft Azure has created friction as both companies compete for the same customers. By spreading commitments across AWS, Google Cloud, SpaceX, Akamai, and Microsoft, Anthropic reduces single-provider risk while gaining access to specialized infrastructure -- Trainium for cost-efficient training, TPUs for complementary workloads, Colossus GPUs for burst capacity, and Akamais edge network for low-latency inference.

The question is whether the spending can be sustained. More than 100 billion committed to AWS alone, plus billions more across other providers, requires revenue growth to continue at extraordinary rates. If demand for Claude plateaus, those fixed commitments become anchors rather than engines. For now, the trajectory says otherwise. When your revenue grows eightyfold and your biggest problem is finding enough power plants to keep the servers running, locking in five gigawatts looks less like a gamble and more like the minimum viable bet.
