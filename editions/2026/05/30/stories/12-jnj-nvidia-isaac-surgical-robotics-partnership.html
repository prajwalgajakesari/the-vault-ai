# Johnson and Johnson Partners With NVIDIA Isaac to Advance AI-Powered Surgical Robotics

The medtech giant is building physics-based digital twins of its MONARCH surgical robot using NVIDIA Omniverse, as the global surgical robotics market races toward a projected \$49 billion valuation by 2034.

The operating room of the future will not simply feature a surgeon and a scalpel. It will feature a surgeon, an AI-powered robotic platform, and a digital twin that has already rehearsed the procedure thousands of times in a physics-accurate virtual world. Johnson & Johnson MedTech is betting heavily on that vision, announcing a deepening partnership with NVIDIA to leverage the Isaac for Healthcare platform in developing its next generation of surgical robotic systems.

The collaboration centers on J&J MedTech’s MONARCH Platform for Urology, a robotic-assisted system for endourologic procedures that is set to become commercially available in the United States in 2026. By integrating NVIDIA’s Omniverse libraries and Cosmos World Foundation models, J&J’s engineering teams can now construct high-fidelity digital twins of the entire MONARCH system—including its tower, cart, fluid management system, and surgical instruments—and simulate how they behave inside a virtual operating room and within simulated patient anatomy.

“Simulation is the next frontier in surgical robotics,” said Neda Cvijetic, Senior Vice President and Global Head of Robotics & Digital R&D at Johnson & Johnson MedTech. “With AI-driven simulation, we can create high-fidelity digital twins that adhere to the laws of physics, can simulate the real world, and ultimately unlocks physical AI capabilities. As we launch new robotics technologies and continue accelerating our innovation pipeline, these simulations have the potential to enhance our differentiated technology development in support of our mission to deliver better patient outcomes.”

What NVIDIA Isaac Brings to the Table

NVIDIA Isaac for Healthcare is a purpose-built platform for developing AI-powered medical robotics. At its core, the platform provides tools for creating physics-based simulations using NVIDIA Omniverse, the company’s industrial metaverse engine. For surgical applications, this means researchers and engineers can build anatomically accurate digital twins from CT scan data, simulate soft-tissue interactions, and generate massive volumes of synthetic training data using NVIDIA Cosmos world foundation models—all without requiring access to real patients or cadaveric specimens.

For J&J, the practical implications are significant. Virtual operating room environments allow clinical teams to rehearse robotic system setup before touching a real patient. Simulated kidney stone procedures let surgeons practice instrument navigation through the urinary tract in a risk-free setting. And the synthetic data generated by these simulations can be used to train machine learning models that improve the robot’s autonomous capabilities over time.

The MONARCH Platform: From Bronchoscopy to Urology

The MONARCH Platform is already a first-to-market innovation in robotic-assisted bronchoscopy, where its scope-in-sheath architecture enables clinicians to navigate deep into the lung to locate and biopsy small nodules. The urology extension represents J&J’s push to make MONARCH the first and only multispecialty flexible robotic solution spanning both bronchoscopy and urology.

“Our teams are convening the right technologies to capture value from data and shape new experiences with our systems before, during, and after surgery,” said Aleksandra Popovic, President of MONARCH at Johnson & Johnson MedTech. The system’s ureteroscope and endourology accessories are designed to provide endoscopic visualization and access throughout the urinary tract, allowing a single operator to maintain precise instrument control during kidney stone procedures while performing multiple tasks simultaneously.

A Market in Overdrive

J&J’s investment comes as the global surgical robotics market enters a period of explosive growth. The market, currently valued at approximately €14.3 billion, is projected to reach €49.1 billion by 2034, according to industry analyses—a compound annual growth rate that reflects both expanding clinical applications and accelerating hospital adoption. An estimated 60 percent of large hospitals have now adopted robotic surgical systems, with over 6,700 systems installed worldwide.

The clinical evidence supporting robotic and AI-assisted surgery continues to strengthen. A systematic review and meta-analysis published in the Journal of Robotic Surgery, synthesizing findings from 25 peer-reviewed studies, found that AI-assisted robotic surgeries demonstrated a 25 percent reduction in operative time and a 30 percent decrease in intraoperative complications compared to conventional manual methods. The same body of research reported a 40 percent improvement in surgical precision and a 15 percent reduction in patient recovery times.

These numbers are driving institutional investment. North America dominates the market with over 50 percent of global revenue share, and competitors are racing to match J&J’s ambitions. Intuitive Surgical placed 493 da Vinci systems in a single quarter of 2024, including 174 units of its newest da Vinci 5 platform. The competitive pressure makes J&J’s NVIDIA partnership not merely a research exercise but a strategic imperative.

The Bigger Picture

What makes the J&J-NVIDIA collaboration notable is not just the technology itself but the methodology it represents. The shift from physical prototyping to simulation-first development could fundamentally reshape how surgical robots are designed, tested, and brought to market. By training AI models in photorealistic simulated environments before deploying them in clinical settings, companies can iterate faster, reduce development costs, and—most critically—improve patient safety by identifying failure modes that would be impossible to discover through traditional bench testing alone.

NVIDIA’s open-source ORBIT-Surgical framework, which underpins much of the Isaac for Healthcare platform, provides the research community with photorealistic human organ models and tools for training robotic surgical assistants using reinforcement learning and behavior cloning techniques. This democratization of surgical AI development tools could accelerate innovation well beyond the walls of J&J’s R&D labs.

As surgical robotics moves from a premium technology reserved for academic medical centers to a standard of care across community hospitals, the companies that can most effectively integrate AI simulation into their development pipelines will hold a decisive advantage. With the MONARCH Platform for Urology approaching its U.S. commercial launch and a growing library of digital twin capabilities, Johnson & Johnson is positioning itself at the leading edge of that transition.
