# AI Washing Intensifies as Companies Rebrand Legacy Tech to Ride the Hype Wave

The artificial intelligence gold rush has produced a predictable side effect: companies across every sector are slapping the "AI" label onto products and services that bear little resemblance to genuine machine learning. Regulators are now calling it what it is -- AI washing -- and the enforcement machinery is accelerating fast enough to make the practice genuinely dangerous for the companies doing it.

The term mirrors "greenwashing," the practice of exaggerating environmental credentials that has already triggered billions in fines globally. But AI washing carries its own unique risks: companies are not merely overstating sustainability goals, they are making specific technical claims about product capabilities that can be tested, audited, and disproven. Regression models, statistical analyses, and even Excel-based automation tools are being repackaged under the AI banner, despite lacking the adaptive learning capabilities that define actual machine learning systems.

## The SEC Draws a Line

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission has made AI washing an explicit enforcement priority. In February 2025, the agency created a dedicated Cybersecurity and Emerging Technologies Unit (CETU) focused specifically on AI-related misconduct. The unit's mandate is blunt: scrutinize how companies describe their AI technology to investors and determine whether they are genuinely using machine learning or merely rebranding old automation.

The enforcement record is already building. In March 2024, the SEC charged two investment advisory firms -- Delphia (USA) Inc. and Global Predictions Inc. -- for making false and misleading statements about their use of AI. Delphia paid $225,000 and Global Predictions paid $175,000 in penalties. One firm had claimed to be "the first regulated AI financial advisor," a statement regulators found unsubstantiated.

In January 2025, the SEC brought its first AI washing action against a public company, charging Presto Automation Inc. for misleading statements about its AI product, Presto Voice. The company had claimed the technology eliminated the need for human drive-thru order-taking at fast food restaurants. The SEC alleged that the vast majority of orders still required human intervention. Then in April 2025, the SEC and the U.S. Attorney's Office filed parallel actions against Albert Saniger, founder of Nate Inc., alleging he raised over $42 million through misleading statements about the company's AI capabilities -- when transactions were allegedly being processed manually by contract workers in foreign countries.

"The era of 'fake it until you make it' in AI is over," SEC officials warned at a May 2025 enforcement forum, stressing that staff are now scrutinizing whether companies are genuinely deploying machine learning or merely repackaging old automation under a new label.

## The Litigation Wave Builds

The enforcement actions are just the beginning. According to securities litigation data compiled by the consulting firm Secretariat, 51 AI-related securities class actions have been filed in the last five years, with a significant majority including allegations that companies overstated or misrepresented their AI capabilities. Baker McKenzie's 2026 analysis warns that the litigation wave is entering a new phase, with private plaintiff class actions now joining regulatory enforcement.

Perrie M. Weiner, Head of Baker McKenzie's Securities Litigation Practice Group in North America, wrote in Fortune that the disputes have evolved beyond simple fraud. "The more notable trend today is that many disputes no longer hinge on whether AI exists at all," he observed. "They also revolve around more nuanced questions: Does the AI meaningfully change the economics of the business?" A company may deploy machine learning models while investors question whether those systems materially improve margins, increase revenue, or create defensible advantages.

The pattern has drawn comparisons to the dot-com bubble, when appending ".com" to a company name could trigger immediate valuation spikes. Congress responded with the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. History suggests a similar regulatory tightening is coming for AI claims.

## Investors Wise Up

On the investor side, the mood has shifted sharply. Beazley, the specialty insurer, published a May 2026 report identifying AI washing as a growing directors and officers liability risk, noting that "where companies could make sweeping claims about 'transformative' AI and still secure strong IPO valuations -- the mood has shifted." The EU AI Act already provides for fines of up to 35 million euros or 7% of global annual revenue for misrepresenting AI capabilities.

The Institute of Sovereign Investors warned in a recent report that the pace of AI development has outpaced most due diligence frameworks. Kristian Flyvholm, the institute's chief executive, said that within a few years, AI due diligence will become a market standard. "The due diligence checklist in fund selection is clearly going to change," he told Top1000funds.com. "In a few more years, there's going to be a market standard of AI due diligence that everybody incorporates into their investment and external manager framework."

Private equity faces particular exposure. The pressure to deploy capital during compressed deal timelines can create incentives to accept ambitious AI narratives without the specialized technical expertise needed to verify them.

## Collateral Damage for Legitimate AI

The crackdown carries unintended consequences for companies genuinely building AI products. Legitimate AI firms now face heightened investor skepticism, longer due diligence cycles, and the burden of proving their technology works as advertised. The FTC has launched "Operation AI Comply" to crack down on deceptive AI marketing, meaning even accurate claims invite scrutiny.

## What Comes Next

The regulatory framework is still taking shape. In December 2025, the SEC's investor advisory committee voted to recommend that public companies disclose information about AI's impact on their businesses. Canada's proposed AI regulation remains fragmented, while the EU AI Act sets the most aggressive penalties.

The strongest defense against AI washing, as Beazley's report concludes, is to treat AI claims like financial ones: if use, data, governance, and outcomes cannot be evidenced, the risk falls on everyone in the chain. For an industry built on the promise of intelligence, the irony is pointed -- the companies most eager to claim AI capabilities are often the ones least capable of delivering them.
