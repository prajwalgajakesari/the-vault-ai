# Meta Develops AI Pendant Wearable That Listens All Day From Your Neck

Meta is building a device that hangs around your neck and listens to everything you say -- all day, every day. According to an internal memo obtained by The Information, the company plans to begin internal testing of an AI-powered pendant by spring 2027, marking its most ambitious push yet into always-on wearable hardware and raising fresh questions about the boundaries between personal AI assistance and ambient surveillance.

The memo, written by Alex Himel, Meta's Vice President of Wearables, lays out a three-pillar strategy: an AI pendant built on technology from the startup Limitless, which Meta acquired in late 2025; a dramatically expanded lineup of AI-powered smart glasses; and a new enterprise subscription service called "Wearables for Work." Together, these initiatives represent Meta's boldest bet that AI hardware -- not just software -- will drive the next era of consumer technology adoption.

## From Acquisition to Ambition

The pendant concept traces directly to Limitless, which made a clip-on Bluetooth microphone that recorded conversations throughout the day, generating summaries, transcripts, and a searchable database of everything its wearer heard or said. When Meta completed the acquisition, Limitless CEO Dan Siroker framed it as validation: "Meta recently announced a new vision to bring personal superintelligence to everyone and a key part of that vision is building incredible AI-enabled wearables," he said at the time. To his own users, Siroker was more blunt: "We're no longer working on a weird fringe idea."

The internal memo does not include detailed hardware specifications, according to The Information, though the device may include a camera in addition to its microphone array. The pendant will run on Meta's latest AI model, Muse Spark, along with an unreleased consumer AI agent codenamed "Hatch" that is still under development.

## A $19 Billion Hole to Fill

The hardware push comes with enormous financial pressure. Meta's Reality Labs division -- home to all its hardware efforts -- lost $19.1 billion in 2025 and another $4.03 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone, against just $402 million in quarterly revenue. Zuckerberg has told investors that 2026 will represent the "peak" of these losses before they begin to subside, and the company has already cut roughly 1,000 Reality Labs employees -- about 10% of the division's workforce.

To stanch the bleeding, Meta is now directing approximately 70% of Reality Labs' operating expenses toward wearables projects like AI glasses, with only 30% going to VR and its Horizon metaverse platform. Himel's memo sets aggressive targets: 10 million wearable devices sold in the second half of 2026 and 6.8 million monthly active wearable users by year's end. On the enterprise side, Meta is targeting at least 10 companies for its Wearables for Work service, including deployments to two large organizations requiring 100 devices each.

The glasses expansion is already well underway. Beyond existing Ray-Ban and Oakley collaborations with EssilorLuxottica -- which reported more than 7 million Meta-powered smart glasses sold in 2025 -- the company plans to debut up to four new models before year's end, with codenames including "Modelo," "Luna," and "Mojito VIP." Some future models, dubbed "supersensing" glasses, would keep cameras and sensors running for hours, letting Meta AI track events throughout the user's day.

## Why This Matters

Meta's pendant ambitions arrive at a peculiar inflection point for AI hardware. The category has been littered with high-profile failures: Humane's AI Pin was effectively dead within a year, its assets acquired by HP for $116 million. The startup Friend spent more than $1 million on subway ads before fading into obscurity. Yet the biggest companies in technology remain convinced that AI needs a body -- or at least a perch.

OpenAI acquired io Products, co-founded by former Apple designer Jony Ive, for $6.5 billion and now has more than 200 people working on a family of AI devices. Google has announced smart glasses in partnership with Samsung and Qualcomm for this fall. The logic driving all of them is the same: if AI assistants are going to be truly useful, they need persistent access to the context of your life -- what you see, hear, and do throughout the day.

Mark Zuckerberg has called smart glasses "one of the fastest-growing categories of consumer electronics ever," noting that daily use tripled year over year in the first quarter of 2026. The comparison he reaches for is telling: "It was clearly only a matter of time until all those flip phones became smartphones."

But a pendant that listens all day occupies different territory than glasses you can take off. Privacy advocates have already raised concerns about always-on recording in shared spaces -- offices, restaurants, public transit -- where bystanders cannot consent. Meta's track record on data privacy, from Cambridge Analytica to repeated FTC settlements, makes it a particularly fraught messenger for a device whose core function is continuous ambient listening.

## What to Watch

The spring 2027 internal testing timeline gives Meta roughly a year to refine the hardware before any consumer launch, but the real test will be whether the company can articulate a privacy framework convincing enough to overcome deep public skepticism. Meanwhile, keep an eye on Meta's subscription strategy: the company this week launched Meta One, a two-tier subscription at $7.99 and $19.99 per month for its AI chatbot across Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp. If Meta can convert even a fraction of its 3.3 billion monthly users into paying AI subscribers -- and then give them a wearable reason to stay -- the pendant might not need to succeed on its own. It just needs to keep you listening.
