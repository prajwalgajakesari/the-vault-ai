# DuckDuckGo Installs Surge 30 Percent as Users Reject Google AI Search Overhaul

Google told the world it was replacing its iconic blue links with AI agents. Within a week, DuckDuckGo had its best stretch of growth in years. The privacy-focused search engine reported that U.S. app installs rose an average of 18.1 percent week-over-week during the May 20 to May 25 period, peaking at 30.5 percent on May 25 -- a sustained six-day run that held strong even through Memorial Day weekend, when the company typically sees traffic dip.

The catalyst was unmistakable. At Google I/O 2026, held May 19-20, the company announced what it called the biggest upgrade to Search in 25 years. Traditional search results would give way to AI-powered conversational responses, an Intelligent Search Box capable of processing videos and files alongside queries, and background monitoring agents for paying subscribers. Google framed it as the future. A significant portion of its user base heard something else: the end of search as they knew it.

The Numbers Tell a Sharp Story

DuckDuckGo's data paints a picture of user flight that is particularly pronounced on mobile. On iOS, week-over-week install growth averaged 33 percent and peaked at a remarkable 69.9 percent -- meaning that on the best single day, nearly 70 percent more iPhone users downloaded DuckDuckGo than in the equivalent period the week before. Third-party analytics firm Apptopia independently confirmed the trend, estimating 29 percent higher daily downloads in the U.S. and 12 percent globally.

The company's AI-free search page, noai.duckduckgo.com, saw even more telling activity. Visits averaged 22.7 percent week-over-week growth, peaking at 27.7 percent on May 24. That page strips out every AI feature -- no AI-assisted answers, no AI-generated images -- offering what amounts to a time capsule of pre-AI search. Its growth suggests that many new users are not just switching search engines; they are actively seeking an experience that AI has not touched.

Meanwhile, DuckDuckGo's public traffic dashboard shows the company has crossed a milestone: more than 145 million daily search queries, up from a long-running plateau around 95 to 105 million -- roughly a 40 to 50 percent lift over 12 months and the steepest sustained acceleration in the company's history outside of the post-2020 privacy surge.

Force-Feeding Versus Freedom

DuckDuckGo CEO Gabriel Weinberg did not mince words about what is driving the shift. "Google is force-feeding AI with no way to opt out," Weinberg said. "As a result, their results are getting worse, not better. We want to be the place that puts users in charge and allows them to decide how much or how little AI they want."

The irony at the center of DuckDuckGo's surge is that the company is not anti-AI. It operates Duck.ai, a free chat product that routes queries to models from Anthropic, Meta, Mistral, and OpenAI while stripping IP addresses and deleting conversations within 30 days. It also offers Search Assist, its own version of AI-generated overviews. But every one of these features is optional, toggled on or off at the user's discretion.

"Not only do we respect user choice, but also user privacy," Weinberg added. "Everything you do in DuckDuckGo is private. We don't collect search histories or chats, and nothing is used for AI training."

Kamyl Bazbaz, the company's chief communications and policy officer, put it more simply: "People just want a choice."

A Pew-Sized Problem for Google

The backlash extends well beyond DuckDuckGo's install numbers. A Pew Research survey from the first quarter of 2026 found that 62 percent of U.S. internet users prefer traditional links over AI-generated overviews. Zero-click searches -- queries where Google's AI answer eliminates the need to visit any website -- now account for roughly 60 percent of all Google queries, frustrating publishers and power users alike. Reddit threads filled with users announcing their switch to DuckDuckGo began proliferating within hours of the I/O keynote.

Google's AI Mode has crossed one billion monthly users, and queries through it are more than doubling every quarter. But reaching a billion users is straightforward when the feature is embedded in the default experience of the world's dominant search engine. The question Google now faces is whether those billion users chose AI Mode or simply had no alternative.

Scale Versus Signal

None of this threatens Google's dominance in the near term. DuckDuckGo commands roughly 2 percent of the U.S. search market; Google holds approximately 89 percent. A 30 percent install spike for a two-percent player does not redraw the competitive map. Even combined with gains at Brave Search, Kagi, and AI-native tools from OpenAI and Perplexity, the collective defection remains a rounding error on Google's balance sheet.

But the signal matters more than the scale. This is the first time Google's AI push has produced a visible, measurable user-loss event that shows up in third-party data. It arrives alongside an ongoing antitrust trial in which DuckDuckGo's own CEO previously testified that Google's exclusive default search contracts have systematically limited competition. If regulators are looking for evidence that users want alternatives but struggle to access them, DuckDuckGo just handed them a data point.

What Comes Next

The real test is durability. If DuckDuckGo sustains 140 million or more daily queries past the I/O news cycle and into the summer, the growth is structural -- a genuine shift in user behavior. If it reverts to 110 or 120 million, it was a moment of frustration, not a movement.

Either way, the message to Google is clear: there is a ceiling on how aggressively you can push AI into search before users push back. The companies that let people decide how much AI they want may end up earning more trust than those that assume more is always better. DuckDuckGo is still small. But for the first time in years, it is growing because Google gave people a reason to leave.
