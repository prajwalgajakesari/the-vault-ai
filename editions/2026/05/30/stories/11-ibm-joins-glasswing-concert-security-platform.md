# IBM Joins Project Glasswing, Bringing Concert Security Platform to Vulnerability Consortium

*Big Blue adds its AI-driven vulnerability management tools and decades of mainframe security expertise to Anthropic's expanding defensive AI coalition.*

---

When Anthropic launched Project Glasswing on April 7, it assembled a who's-who of technology titans committed to finding and fixing software vulnerabilities before attackers could exploit them. Six weeks later, IBM made that roster even heavier, announcing on May 19 that it had joined the consortium and would bring its full AI-powered security portfolio -- headlined by the IBM Concert platform -- into the collaborative fold.

The addition is significant not just for the name on the letterhead, but for what IBM carries through the door: more than 175 countries worth of enterprise clients, sensitive mainframe and hybrid cloud environments underpinning global financial transactions, and a research organization that has been quietly hardening open-source software for decades.

## A Growing Coalition Against AI-Powered Threats

Project Glasswing now counts roughly 50 organizations among its participants, including launch partners Amazon Web Services, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorganChase, the Linux Foundation, Microsoft, NVIDIA, and Palo Alto Networks. Anthropic has extended access to its restricted frontier model, Claude Mythos Preview, along with up to $100 million in usage credits and $4 million in donations to open-source security groups. Within weeks of the program's launch, security teams reported that Mythos Preview had surfaced thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities across every major operating system and web browser.

IBM's entry brings a distinct operational perspective. Where many Glasswing participants are cloud-native or security-vendor specialists, IBM straddles the legacy and modern worlds -- managing mainframes that still process the majority of global credit card transactions alongside sprawling hybrid cloud deployments built on Red Hat OpenShift. That breadth gives the company unusual visibility into the full attack surface that adversaries are now probing with AI-assisted tools.

"AI-powered attacks have already moved beyond what traditional defenses can match," said Rob Thomas, Senior Vice President of Software and Chief Commercial Officer at IBM. "We're helping clients assess their exposure and putting tools like IBM Concert to work in more environments. Separately, as part of Project Glasswing, we've been hardening our own products and contributing fixes back to the open-source community. The collaboration makes the entire ecosystem stronger."

## IBM Concert: From Passive Monitoring to Coordinated Response

At the center of IBM's contribution is Concert, an agentic IT operations platform that unifies application, infrastructure, and network signals into a single operational view. Its security module, Concert Protect, uses AI-driven prioritization to identify, rank, and auto-remediate vulnerabilities -- cutting the median time to resolve critical CVEs by up to 90 percent, according to IBM. That speed matters: the company's 2026 Threat Intelligence Index found a 44 percent year-over-year increase in the exploitation of public-facing applications, with adversaries using AI to compress the window between vulnerability discovery and active exploitation from months to minutes.

Concert's capabilities extend into the developer workflow through Concert Secure Coder, currently in public preview, which embeds security scanning directly into integrated development environments. The tool detects risks, prioritizes them by business impact, and generates automatic code remediations before vulnerable software ever reaches production -- a textbook "shift left" approach accelerated by generative AI.

IBM is also deploying Autonomous Security, a multi-agent service announced in April that delivers coordinated detection, decision-making, and incident response at machine speed. Combined with Red Hat's open-source stewardship -- proactively contributing patches and maintaining enterprise-grade versions of widely used components -- the portfolio represents a layered defense strategy that spans from code commit to production runtime.

## What IBM Brings to Glasswing

As a Glasswing participant, IBM Research has been identifying and remediating vulnerabilities in widely used software and sharing those findings through coordinated disclosure and upstream open-source patches. The company frames its participation as an extension of a long-held philosophy: openness and external scrutiny are prerequisites for security at scale.

That philosophy aligns with the consortium's founding premise. CrowdStrike CTO Elia Zaitsev, one of the original launch partners, captured the urgency at Glasswing's unveiling: "The window between a vulnerability being discovered and being exploited by an adversary has collapsed -- what once took months now happens in minutes with AI. That is not a reason to slow down; it's a reason to move together, faster."

## Analysis: The Consolidation of Defensive AI

IBM's membership underscores a broader trend: the rapid consolidation of defensive AI capabilities under collaborative umbrellas. When the largest enterprise technology companies -- competitors in nearly every other market -- agree to share vulnerability findings and pool resources around a single frontier model, it signals a collective recognition that the threat landscape has shifted faster than any single organization can handle alone.

The question going forward is whether Glasswing's gated model approach -- restricting Claude Mythos Preview to vetted consortium members rather than releasing it broadly -- can scale fast enough to match the pace of attackers who face no such coordination overhead. IBM's global footprint and deep enterprise relationships may help answer that question by extending Glasswing's defensive findings into the mainframe halls and hybrid cloud environments where much of the world's most sensitive data still lives.

For now, the consortium's growth from a dozen launch partners to approximately 50 organizations in under two months suggests the industry is voting with its feet. The race between AI-powered offense and AI-powered defense is well underway, and the defenders are choosing to run it together.
