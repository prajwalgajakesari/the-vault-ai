# Korean Intelligence Agencies Explore Mythos Cybersecurity Access Through Anthropic Partnership

South Korea is moving aggressively to secure access to Anthropic's restricted Mythos cybersecurity model, with six government agencies -- including the National Intelligence Service -- engaging the AI company in formal cooperation talks that could reshape how the country defends against an escalating wave of North Korean cyberattacks.

The discussions, which took place in May 2026, represent one of the most comprehensive government-level engagements with a frontier AI company on defensive cybersecurity to date. They also signal that the geopolitical stakes of AI-powered vulnerability discovery are pushing allied nations to seek direct partnerships with the companies building these systems.

A six-agency push

The Ministry of Science and ICT (MSIT) convened the meeting with Michael Sellitto, Anthropic's Head of Global Policy, on May 11. But the scope of participation went far beyond a single ministry. The Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the National Intelligence Service, the Financial Services Commission, the AI Safety Institute (AISI), the Korea Internet and Security Agency (KISA), and the Financial Security Institute all sent representatives.

The breadth of attendance reflects the cross-cutting nature of the threat. North Korean state-sponsored hacking groups have targeted financial institutions, nuclear infrastructure, and defense contractors, making cybersecurity a concern that spans intelligence, diplomacy, finance, and technology policy.

At the center of the talks was Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview -- the frontier model that has already surfaced more than 10,000 high- or critical-severity vulnerabilities through Project Glasswing, the company's gated security consortium. AISI and KISA formally requested to join Glasswing as Korea's institutional representatives, seeking access to Mythos for defensive scanning of critical national infrastructure.

Ryu Je-myung, Second Vice Minister of Science and ICT, stated after the meeting: "We will actively cooperate with leading global AI companies to strengthen the prevention and response systems for AI risks, including securing the safety of AI models and enhancing cybersecurity capabilities."

The North Korean threat

The urgency behind Seoul's outreach is not abstract. According to data compiled by blockchain analytics firms and intelligence agencies, North Korea-linked cyber groups stole a combined $2.02 billion in 2025, a 51 percent year-over-year increase. Over the past three years, Pyongyang-affiliated cybercriminals have stolen more than $3 billion, primarily in cryptocurrency, with the proceeds funneled into the regime's nuclear weapons and ballistic missile programs.

The NIS has documented a steady expansion of DPRK hacking capacity, reporting that North Korea now fields approximately 8,400 cybersecurity personnel, up from 6,800 in 2022. The agency logged 1.62 million hacking attempts in 2023 alone, a 36 percent increase from the prior year. These operations target financial systems, energy infrastructure, defense supply chains, and increasingly, the software supply chain itself -- as demonstrated by a major 2026 attack in which suspected North Korean hackers compromised a software package used by thousands of U.S. companies.

Mythos's demonstrated ability to find decades-old vulnerabilities in legacy code -- including a 27-year-old flaw in the OpenBSD kernel and a 16-year-old bug in FFmpeg -- makes it particularly relevant to South Korea's defense posture. Much of the country's critical infrastructure, from banking to power generation, runs on legacy systems that have never been subjected to AI-scale vulnerability scanning.

Financial sector mobilization

The Financial Services Commission has moved in parallel, holding six rounds of discussions on advanced AI security threats since the "Mythos shock" first surfaced in April. On May 22, Vice Chairman Kwon Dae-young chaired a roundtable bringing together AI experts, security specialists, and chief information security officers from major financial firms.

The FSC announced it will ease longstanding network separation rules -- which have required Korean financial institutions to isolate business systems from the internet -- to allow deployment of security-focused AI. Forty-nine firms with total assets exceeding 10 trillion won will be eligible, with the first cohort of up to 10 companies to be selected in June or July.

"Advanced AI security threats are like a cold virus -- they cannot be completely blocked but must be managed as we live alongside them," Vice Chairman Kwon said. "Just as we wear masks, building AI defense systems as routine cyber hygiene is the security habit the financial sector must adopt."

Industry access and the conglomerate factor

The talks also addressed cooperation between Anthropic and Korean companies and institutions in the cybersecurity field. South Korea's major conglomerates -- Samsung, SK, Hyundai, and LG among them -- operate sprawling technology stacks across semiconductors, shipbuilding, automotive, and telecommunications. Each represents a potential high-value target for state-sponsored attackers, and each stands to benefit from Mythos-class vulnerability scanning.

KISA, meanwhile, has already launched its own $8.3 million AI cybersecurity program, selecting 50 companies to develop next-generation security products across four domains: AI-based security commercialization, AI security company development, Korean-model integrated security, and zero-trust adoption.

"High-performance AI models present a dual nature -- an opportunity to raise security standards and a potential vector for large-scale cyber threats," a KISA official said.

What remains unresolved

Participation in Project Glasswing has not been confirmed. The MSIT acknowledged that the meeting produced an agreement to "continue active cooperation and working-level discussions on the use of AI models in cybersecurity," but no formal membership was announced.

The meeting was a follow-up to discussions between MSIT Minister Bae Kyung-hoon and Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei at the 2026 India AI Impact Summit in February, suggesting the partnership has been building over several months. Korea has since expanded its AI security outreach to include OpenAI as well.

For South Korea, the strategic calculus is straightforward. The country faces one of the most sophisticated state-sponsored cyber threats in the world, operating against infrastructure that includes legacy systems never designed to withstand AI-augmented attacks. Gaining access to the tools that can find those vulnerabilities before Pyongyang does is not a matter of technology policy alone -- it is a national security imperative.
