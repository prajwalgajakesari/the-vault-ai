# OpenAI Plans Confidential IPO Filing With September 2026 Listing in Sight

OpenAI has filed a confidential S-1 registration with the Securities and Exchange Commission, setting the stage for what could become the largest technology IPO in history and marking the moment the artificial intelligence boom formally collides with Wall Street.

The filing, submitted on May 22, positions OpenAI for a public listing as early as September 2026, according to multiple people familiar with the matter. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are leading the deal as underwriters for a company last valued at $852 billion following its record-breaking $122 billion private funding round in March -- itself the largest private fundraise ever completed. OpenAI is now targeting a public market valuation above $1 trillion.

## Revenue Engine Running Hot

The numbers underpinning the IPO ambitions are staggering by any measure. OpenAI surpassed $25 billion in annualized revenue by early 2026, generating roughly $2 billion per month. The company counts more than 50 million consumer subscribers and 9 million business users across its ChatGPT and API platforms. Its nascent advertising business, the ChatGPT Ads Manager launched on May 5, crossed $100 million in annualized revenue in under six weeks.

But growth and profitability remain two very different stories. For full-year 2025, OpenAI generated $13.1 billion in revenue against approximately $22 billion in total costs, producing a net loss of roughly $9 billion. Internal projections suggest a $14 billion operating loss for 2026, and cumulative losses could reach $44 billion before profitability arrives, potentially in 2029. The company has pledged over $600 billion toward cloud server capacity over the next five years, and estimates suggest it will need an additional $207 billion in capital through 2030 just to honor those compute commitments.

Public markets, in other words, are not a victory lap. They are a lifeline.

## Altman Pushes Forward, CFO Urges Caution

The IPO timeline has exposed a rare fault line inside OpenAI between ambition and prudence. CEO Sam Altman has been candid about his complicated feelings toward the public markets, even as he pushes aggressively for a September debut.

"Am I excited to be a public company CEO? Zero percent," Altman said on the Big Technology podcast. "In some ways I think it will be really annoying."

Yet Altman views the IPO as strategically essential -- a chance to capitalize on intense investor appetite for AI before the window shifts. His urgency has put him at odds with CFO Sarah Friar, who has warned colleagues that OpenAI may not be fully IPO-ready by the fall. Friar has flagged gaps in financial reporting systems, compliance frameworks, and governance structures, and has privately targeted a more conservative 2027 listing.

"AI-powered attacks have already moved beyond what traditional defenses can match," observed Rob Thomas, SVP of Software and Chief Commercial Officer at IBM, commenting on the broader AI infrastructure race that companies like OpenAI are at the center of. The scale of investment required to compete at the frontier level is precisely what is driving OpenAI toward public capital markets.

The internal tension reflects a broader question facing every high-growth AI company: does the urgency of the market opportunity justify the risk of going public before the financial house is fully in order?

## Part of a Larger Wave

OpenAI is not filing into a vacuum. SpaceX submitted its own confidential S-1 on April 1 and made its public filing on May 20, targeting a valuation between $1.75 trillion and $2 trillion with a roadshow beginning June 8. Together with Anthropic, which closed a funding round at a $900 billion valuation in May, the three companies represent a combined private-to-public value wave approaching $3.7 trillion.

The convergence is no coincidence. All three companies are capital-intensive enterprises burning through billions while racing to lock in infrastructure, compute capacity, and market position. Public markets offer the only pool of capital deep enough to sustain the spending levels these firms require. For OpenAI specifically, the IPO is inseparable from its compute commitments -- the $600 billion in cloud contracts and multi-gigawatt Trainium capacity agreements with AWS that form the operational backbone of its revenue model.

## Why This Matters

OpenAI going public would represent a watershed moment for the AI industry, and not merely because of the dollar figures involved.

An IPO above $1 trillion would instantly make OpenAI the most valuable company to ever list on a U.S. exchange at debut, surpassing even the largest prior tech offerings. It would provide the first truly transparent look at the financial realities of building frontier AI -- the actual costs of training and serving large language models, the real economics of consumer and enterprise AI subscriptions, and the true scale of infrastructure spending required to compete at the highest level.

For the broader AI ecosystem, the filing will serve as a benchmark. Every AI startup fundraising, every corporate AI budget, and every investor thesis about the sector will be measured against whatever OpenAI discloses in its public S-1. The company's revenue per user, gross margins on API services, and customer acquisition costs will become the reference points around which the entire industry is valued.

The filing also arrives at a moment of reckoning for enterprise AI spending. Reports of companies burning through AI budgets faster than expected, coupled with growing questions about return on investment, have introduced a note of caution into a market that spent the past two years in near-euphoria. OpenAI's financials will either validate the bulls or arm the skeptics.

## What to Watch Next

The confidential filing triggers a predictable sequence. The public S-1, which will disclose actual financial statements, typically arrives 15 to 21 days before the roadshow. If September remains the target for listing, expect the public filing and investor roadshow to materialize in August 2026.

Between now and then, the key variables are whether Altman and Friar resolve their timing disagreement, whether the market appetite for mega-cap tech IPOs holds through the summer, and whether OpenAI can demonstrate a credible path to profitability in its disclosures. The SpaceX IPO, expected to price by late June, will serve as a leading indicator of how public markets receive a money-losing, capital-hungry technology giant with enormous long-term ambitions.

One thing is certain: when OpenAI does go public, every line of its S-1 will be parsed not just for what it reveals about one company, but for what it says about the economics of artificial intelligence itself.
