Anthropic Signs $1.8 Billion Computing Deal With Akamai in Largest Contract in CDN Giant's History

4 min read 886 words 3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Anthropic committed $1.8 billion over seven years to Akamai's cloud platform, the largest deal in Akamai's history, as 80x annualized revenue growth forces the AI company to diversify compute beyond hyperscalers.

Anthropic has struck a $1.8 billion, seven-year cloud infrastructure deal with Akamai Technologies, marking the largest contract in the content delivery network pioneer's 28-year history and underscoring just how desperately the maker of Claude is scrambling for computing power to keep pace with explosive demand for its AI models.

The agreement, disclosed during Akamai's first-quarter earnings call on May 8 and first reported by Bloomberg, will see Anthropic tap Akamai's Connected Cloud platform for GPU and cloud computing services. Akamai referred to its new customer only as a "leading frontier model provider" during the call, but multiple outlets confirmed the counterparty is Anthropic, the San Francisco-based AI safety company whose Claude family of models has become one of the most widely adopted AI systems in enterprise software.

"These leaders in AI have chosen Akamai because their AI workloads need the scale, performance and reliability that our cloud platform provides," Akamai CEO Tom Leighton said on the earnings call, calling the contract "further validating our position as a key infrastructure provider in the AI economy."

The deal arrives at a moment of staggering growth for Anthropic. CEO Dario Amodei recently revealed that the company experienced 80-fold growth on an annualized basis in the first quarter of 2026, far outstripping an internal forecast of 10x. Anthropic's annualized revenue run rate has climbed from roughly $9 billion at the close of 2025 to $30 billion by April 2026, propelled largely by surging adoption of Claude Code, the company's AI-powered coding assistant. "I hope that 80-times growth doesn't continue because that's just crazy and it's too hard to handle," Amodei said. "I'm hoping for some more normal numbers."

That breakneck expansion has forced Anthropic into an aggressive, multi-front infrastructure buildout. The Akamai deal adds another layer to a computing stack that already includes a multibillion-dollar agreement with Amazon Web Services for up to five gigawatts of capacity, a separate partnership with Google and Broadcom for an additional 3.5 gigawatts beginning in 2027, and a recently inked arrangement with SpaceX to occupy all compute capacity at the Colossus 1 data center in Memphis, gaining access to more than 220,000 Nvidia GPUs and over 300 megawatts of power.

For Akamai, the contract represents a strategic inflection point. The Cambridge, Massachusetts-based company has spent recent years diversifying beyond its legacy content delivery business into cloud computing and cybersecurity. Its Cloud Infrastructure Services segment reported $95 million in Q1 2026 revenue, up 40 percent year-over-year, but that figure remains modest compared to the hyperscale incumbents. The Anthropic commitment effectively transforms Akamai's cloud ambitions from aspiration into validation. Total Q1 revenue came in at $1.074 billion, up 6 percent, while security revenue rose 11 percent to $590 million. Shares surged 27 percent to close at $148.38 on May 9, the stock's biggest single-day rally in over 22 years.

Why This Matters

The Akamai deal signals a structural shift in how frontier AI companies source compute. Until now, the AI infrastructure story has been dominated by three hyperscalers: AWS, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure. Anthropic's willingness to commit $1.8 billion to a mid-tier cloud provider suggests that demand for AI compute has grown so intense that even the largest cloud platforms cannot satisfy it alone. The result is a more distributed, multi-cloud infrastructure landscape in which companies like Akamai, CoreWeave, and Lambda Labs are carving out meaningful roles.

It also highlights the emerging tension between AI companies' insatiable compute appetites and the physical limits of data center capacity, power supply, and chip manufacturing. Anthropic is now juggling computing partnerships spanning Amazon, Google, SpaceX, and Akamai simultaneously, committing hundreds of billions of dollars in aggregate infrastructure spending. The sheer scale of these commitments raises questions about capital efficiency and whether the AI revenue boom can sustain such massive fixed-cost obligations over multiyear horizons.

For the broader AI industry, the deal reinforces a new competitive dynamic: access to compute is becoming as important as model quality. Companies that can secure diverse, reliable infrastructure pipelines will have a decisive advantage in training next-generation models and serving the growing wave of enterprise and consumer AI applications.

What to Watch

The key question now is execution. Akamai must rapidly scale its GPU infrastructure to meet the demands of a customer whose usage is growing at an almost unprecedented rate. Investors will be watching whether Akamai can deliver the capacity on schedule and whether the deal's economics hold as AI workload patterns evolve. Meanwhile, Anthropic's sprawling infrastructure portfolio, spanning at least four major providers, will test whether a distributed compute strategy can match the integration advantages of building on a single hyperscaler. The next major milestone will be Akamai's Q2 earnings, where the market will look for early signs of revenue recognition from the deal and any updates on capacity deployment timelines.
