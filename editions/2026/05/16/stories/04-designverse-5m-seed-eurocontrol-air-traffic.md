# DesignVerse Raises $5.5 Million to Modernize Europe's Air Traffic Management With AI

A Bucharest-based startup that quietly rewrote a decade-and-a-half-old EUROCONTROL system in a little over a month has just secured the capital to prove that was no one-off feat. DesignVerse announced this week it has raised $5.5 million (EUR 4.6 million) in seed funding to accelerate its AI-powered platform for modernizing mission-critical enterprise software -- the kind of sprawling, aging infrastructure that holds up airports, trading floors, and government operations across Europe.

The round was led by Begin Capital and Gapminder VC, with participation from Underline Ventures and a roster of angel investors drawn from inside Adobe, LSEG, and UiPath. It follows an earlier $850,000 pre-seed raise and brings the company's total disclosed funding to approximately $6.35 million.

## The EUROCONTROL Proof Point

Before this round was announced, DesignVerse had already earned a credential that money alone cannot buy. EUROCONTROL -- the intergovernmental organization that coordinates air traffic across 41 European countries, overseeing more than 10 million flights a year -- brought the startup in to overhaul a 15-year-old legacy application. The platform completed the job in just over a month. Internal estimates had pegged the same work at six months using conventional development methods.

The regenerated software now runs in airports and air traffic control centers across the continent. For a startup trying to sell AI-driven modernization into industries where the phrase "if it isn't broken, don't fix it" is practically a safety philosophy, that track record is a powerful sales tool.

## How the Platform Works

DesignVerse is not a generic AI coding assistant bolted onto enterprise needs. Its core insight is that legacy modernization fails not because engineering teams lack talent, but because design intent and production implementation are perpetually out of sync. Designers produce specifications; engineers manually reinterpret and rebuild them, introducing inconsistency and delay at every handoff.

The DesignVerse platform breaks that cycle by grounding its AI models in each customer's own design systems, component libraries, and technical documentation. Rather than generating generic code, the system produces functional enterprise applications that conform to a customer's existing architecture, compliance requirements, and operational constraints from the start.

"Large organisations still lose enormous amounts of time translating design work into production software," said Andrei Manolache, CEO and co-founder. "Designers create intent, but engineers must manually reinterpret and rebuild it, which leads to inconsistency and delays. DesignVerse removes that friction entirely, allowing teams to generate functional enterprise applications directly from their design systems."

Manolache, a former product design lead at Oracle, co-founded the company with Robert Dragutoiu, whose background is in systems engineering. The division of expertise is intentional: the platform sits precisely at the seam between design and engineering, and both sides need to be understood deeply.

"Many AI coding tools available today are designed primarily for experimentation or rapid prototyping," said Robert Dragutoiu, CTO and co-founder. "Enterprises operate complex infrastructure and legacy systems, often in mission-critical environments. Software generated by AI must integrate with existing architectures and run safely in production. Our models are designed specifically for those constraints."

## Traction That Speaks for Itself

The fundraise comes with an unusually concrete commercial milestone for a seed-stage company: DesignVerse crossed $1.1 million in annual recurring revenue in fewer than five months, with every dollar coming from enterprise clients. The sectors represented -- aviation, financial services, and cybersecurity -- are not known for moving fast or tolerating vendor risk. Winning paying customers in all three categories before raising a seed round suggests the product is solving a problem that organizations have been quietly desperate to address.

The broader market context reinforces the urgency. Across Europe, regulated industries are sitting on decades of accumulated technical debt. Legacy systems built in the 1990s and 2000s underpin operations that cannot simply be switched off for a rebuild. Traditional modernization projects are notoriously expensive, slow, and prone to failure. AI-assisted modernization tools have proliferated over the past two years, but most are optimized for greenfield development or rapid prototyping. DesignVerse's positioning -- purpose-built for enterprises that cannot afford downtime, cannot accept security lapses, and cannot tolerate AI-generated code that ignores their existing architecture -- addresses the segment of the market where the stakes are highest and the tolerance for generic solutions is lowest.

## What the Capital Will Fund

The seed proceeds will support expansion across Europe and into the United States, along with investments in the platform's core AI capabilities. The company has indicated a particular focus on deepening integrations with existing enterprise design systems, which functions both as a product improvement and as a competitive moat: the more tightly DesignVerse's models are trained on a customer's own standards and components, the harder it becomes for a competitor to offer a meaningful alternative.

The investor syndicate appears calibrated for this trajectory. Gapminder VC has a track record of backing Eastern European B2B software companies navigating regulated markets. Begin Capital brings sector connectivity. The angel network -- drawn from senior product and engineering roles at Adobe, LSEG, and UiPath -- provides both domain knowledge and potential commercial relationships in the industries DesignVerse most wants to penetrate.

## The Bigger Picture

DesignVerse's raise is a small but pointed signal in a year when AI investment has increasingly bifurcated between consumer-facing experiments and enterprise infrastructure bets. The latter category demands different things: not novelty, but reliability; not speed-to-demo, but safety-in-production. EUROCONTROL's willingness to let a startup touch the software coordinating European airspace -- and the fact that the result now operates at scale -- is the kind of validation that no press release can manufacture.

The company has a narrow window to extend that credibility before larger incumbents, from Microsoft to Palantir to a growing field of enterprise AI vendors, invest more seriously in the same problem. A $5.5 million seed round will not win a protracted platform war. But it may be exactly enough to add the enterprise logos and ARR growth that make the next raise -- and the customers it funds -- a different conversation entirely.
