# Multiverse Raises $70 Million to Future-Proof Workforces for the AI Era

**Category:** Business
**Date:** May 16, 2026

---

When AI threatens to render entire job categories obsolete, the companies that profit most may not be the ones building the models — but the ones teaching workers how to use them. Multiverse, a UK-based workforce development startup, is betting $70 million on exactly that thesis.

The London-headquartered company announced on May 15 that it had closed a $70 million funding round led by Schroders Capital's private equity division, pushing its valuation to $2.1 billion — a $400 million increase from its last fundraise. That prior round, a $220 million raise at a $1.7 billion valuation, closed all the way back in 2022, making this week's announcement a long-awaited signal that Multiverse has survived a turbulent few years and emerged with a sharpened focus.

Existing backers General Catalyst, Index Ventures, Lightspeed, StepStone, and D1 Capital all returned for the new round. The fresh capital will fund Multiverse's expansion across Europe and deepen its pivot toward artificial intelligence as the defining workplace skill of the decade.

"There are companies who desperately need the benefits AI can bring. There are AI companies. What has been missing is the layer that bridges the two," said Euan Blair, Multiverse's CEO and founder, in a statement announcing the raise. "This investment marks the moment Multiverse defines that category, and takes it across Europe. Getting outcomes from AI and unlocking productivity is not just a technology problem. It is a people problem. We exist to solve it."

## From Apprenticeships to AI Upskilling

Multiverse's journey to this point has been anything but linear. Founded in 2016 under the name WhiteHat — and rebranded as Multiverse in 2021 — the company initially focused on connecting individuals with apprenticeship opportunities in the UK, where government-backed schemes offer meaningful funding for both workers and employers. By 2022, it had sharpened its focus on technology apprenticeships and opened a New York co-headquarters as it set its sights on the American market.

That US expansion has since been quietly unwound. A chief product officer hired out of New York relocated to London; the American market no longer appears on the company's website. Alongside the retreat, the company reportedly shed dozens of employees amid widening losses, and faced scrutiny over completion rates on government-contracted training programmes.

But Multiverse appears to have used the adversity to streamline. Its catalogue of courses — once sprawling across dozens of non-technical disciplines — has been pared to 14 focused offerings in AI, data science, and engineering. The company now works with more than 1,000 enterprise customers and has approximately 22,000 active learners enrolled at any given time, with programmes typically running around 13 months. Revenue grew 50% year-on-year in 2025, and the company says it recorded its first cash-positive quarter in the January-to-March 2026 period.

## The Atlas Platform and a Strategic Acquisition

Central to Multiverse's product is Atlas, a proprietary AI coaching platform that adapts content to individual learners and tracks progress across structured training pathways. The company sells directly to businesses, which then offer Atlas-powered programmes to their workforces and apprenticeship participants — a B2B2C model that gives Multiverse leverage in enterprise sales while keeping learners at the centre.

In January 2026, Multiverse completed the acquisition of StackFuel, a Berlin-based AI training company, for an undisclosed sum. The deal accelerated Multiverse's European footprint and added depth to its AI curriculum at a moment when demand for AI skills across the continent is surging.

The customer base reflects the breadth of the opportunity. Multiverse's clients include John Lewis, one of the UK's best-known retailers, and Babcock International, a major defence contractor grappling with how to modernise a decades-old business for a world of autonomous systems and AI-driven logistics.

"Multiverse has helped us tackle a growing complexity and volume of data by equipping our people with practical, real-world data skills," said Lynsey Valentine, strategy director at Babcock, in a statement. "Our teams are already applying what they've learned to automate processes, unlock insight, and drive meaningful improvements well before graduation."

## Why Investors Are Paying $2.1 Billion for a Training Company

The valuation question is the obvious one: is $2.1 billion a reasonable price for a company that is, at its core, teaching people to use software?

Schroders Capital's Michael Mclean, head of private equity technology investments, made the case in the announcement. "The evolution of AI is creating transformative opportunities to drive productivity and growth across global economies," he said. "Multiverse is a leader in enabling this shift, helping organisations capitalise on these tailwinds. With growing momentum across Europe, Multiverse puts the focus on AI adoption, enabling employers to upskill their workforces and translate technology investment into tangible outcomes."

The argument reflects a broader market conviction that AI adoption — not AI development — is where the next wave of enterprise value will be created. Billions have been poured into building foundation models and infrastructure; comparatively little has gone into solving the human-side problem of getting workers to actually use the tools effectively. Multiverse is positioning itself as the picks-and-shovels play on corporate AI deployment, a category with significant recurring revenue potential given how rapidly AI capabilities are evolving and how frequently workforces will need to be re-trained.

The 50% revenue growth and first cash-positive quarter give investors something concrete to point to. Whether that momentum can sustain as Multiverse pushes into new European markets — against a backdrop of a US retreat and lingering questions about programme completion quality — remains the open question.

## The Bigger Picture

Multiverse's raise lands at a moment when the workforce disruption narrative around AI is shifting from hypothetical to operational. Major employers across finance, logistics, defence, and retail are no longer debating whether AI will change their workforce needs — they are scrambling to figure out how to manage the transition. Governments, particularly in Europe, are under pressure to demonstrate that the productivity gains of AI do not accrue only to technology companies and capital owners.

Multiverse's model — sell to the enterprise, train the worker — sits directly at the intersection of those pressures. Its focus on apprenticeship-style, outcome-oriented learning rather than short online courses also differentiates it from the MOOC-style platforms that dominated the last decade of edtech and largely failed to demonstrate sustained workforce impact.

The next 18 months will test whether Multiverse's narrower, harder focus can convert a strong quarter into durable growth — and whether teaching Europe's workforces to work alongside AI is a business as large as its backers are paying to believe.
