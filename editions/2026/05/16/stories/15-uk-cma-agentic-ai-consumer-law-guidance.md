# UK Regulators Issue Joint Guidance on Agentic AI Consumer Law and Competition Risks

Four of Britain's most powerful regulators have put the business world on notice: the age of autonomous AI agents acting on behalf of consumers has arrived, and the rules governing them are already in force.

In a coordinated regulatory blitz spanning March 2026, the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) and the Digital Regulation Cooperation Forum (DRCF) — comprising the CMA, the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA), the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO), and Ofcom — issued two landmark publications that together constitute the UK's most comprehensive regulatory statement yet on agentic AI. The message to businesses deploying AI agents in consumer-facing roles was unambiguous: existing law applies, enforcement is real, and ignorance is not a defence.

## Two Documents, One Clear Signal

On 9 March 2026, the CMA published dual guidance documents: practical compliance guidance for businesses using AI agents, and a wider policy paper exploring how agentic systems may reshape consumer markets. Three weeks later, on 31 March, the DRCF followed with a forward-looking foresight paper titled *The Future of Agentic AI*, cataloguing risks, mapping regulatory jurisdictions, and committing to ongoing horizon-scanning through 2027.

Together, the publications signal that the UK's regulatory apparatus — long criticised for moving too slowly on digital markets — is attempting to get ahead of agentic AI before it scales to a point where harm becomes systemic.

## What the CMA Guidance Actually Requires

The CMA's compliance guidance is built on four core principles that the authority frames not as new obligations but as existing consumer protection law applied to a novel operational context.

**Transparency** requires businesses to be open with consumers about when they are interacting with an AI agent rather than a human, and about what that agent can and cannot do. If the use of AI might affect a consumer's decision-making — say, by narrowing options or applying personalised pricing — disclosure is expected.

**Compliance by design** demands that AI agents be trained and configured to respect consumers' statutory and contractual rights from the outset, including rights around pricing, refunds, cancellations, and accurate product information. The CMA specifically highlights the Consumer Rights Act 2015 and the Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 as frameworks agents must be built around, not bolted onto.

**Human oversight** pushes back against the temptation to treat AI deployment as a "set it and forget it" exercise. Given the scale at which agents can operate — potentially interacting with hundreds of thousands of consumers in a short window — the guidance stresses that monitoring, escalation, and remediation processes must be in place before deployment, not retrofitted after complaints arrive.

**Swift remediation** closes the loop: when an agent is found to be performing incorrectly, businesses must act quickly. The CMA's concern here is velocity. Unlike a human employee who might mislead one customer, a misconfigured agent can replicate the same harmful behaviour across an entire customer base before anyone notices.

## The Accountability Question

Perhaps the most consequential clarification in the CMA's guidance concerns legal liability. The authority makes clear that it is the business deploying the AI agent — not the company that designed or trained the underlying model — that bears legal responsibility for consumer protection failures. This directly addresses the accountability gap that many technology companies have attempted to exploit by pointing downstream when things go wrong.

"Businesses remain responsible for what their AI agents do, in the same way as they are responsible for the actions of their employees," the CMA states in its guidance, a framing that should concentrate minds in boardrooms that have been treating AI deployment as an IT procurement decision rather than a consumer law obligation.

Enforcement teeth exist to back that principle up. Under the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCCA), the CMA now holds direct consumer enforcement powers that allow it to impose fines of up to **10% of global turnover** for breaches — a ceiling that brings the UK's consumer protection regime into rough equivalence with GDPR-era data protection penalties.

## Competition Risks: The Collusion Concern

Beyond individual consumer harms, the CMA's guidance raises a systemic competition concern that has received comparatively little attention: the potential for AI agents to facilitate coordinated pricing outcomes without any explicit instruction from the businesses deploying them.

The authority warns that agents operating in concentrated markets may learn to react to the pricing signals of competitor agents in ways that produce collusion-like outcomes — without any direct communication between businesses, and without any human having explicitly programmed anticompetitive behaviour. This "algorithmic collusion by accident" scenario, once largely theoretical, is now a named concern in formal regulatory guidance, a shift that antitrust practitioners say marks a significant evolution in how the CMA conceptualises market harm.

## The DRCF's Five-Level Autonomy Framework

The DRCF's foresight paper provides the conceptual scaffolding regulators will use to assess agentic AI going forward. The forum establishes a **five-level autonomy spectrum** — ranging from a merely reactive "tool" through to a truly autonomous actor that requires little human input — as a shared analytical framework across the four member regulators to determine where a given system sits and which regulatory obligations attach to it.

The paper catalogues seven categories of compliance risk the DRCF believes warrant priority attention: fragmented accountability across model providers and deployers; data protection and minimisation failures; prompt injection and agent manipulation; action bundling without informed consent; algorithmic collusion; dark patterns optimised against consumer outcomes; and online safety classification risk.

"Organisational responsibility for legal compliance is unchanged regardless of how autonomously an AI agent acts," the DRCF states in the foresight paper — a position consistent with the CMA's own consumer guidance and one that regulators clearly regard as a necessary corrective to what some businesses have treated as ambiguity.

## What Comes Next

The DRCF has committed to further horizon-scanning work across three areas through 2026 and 2027: the future of interfaces between users, firms, and digital services; consumer robotics and physical AI; and the near-term consumer experience of new technological developments. The inclusion of "consumer robotics and physical AI" on that list is a notable signal that regulators are already looking past software agents toward embodied AI systems.

For businesses, the practical implication of the March 2026 publications is that the window for treating agentic AI as a regulatory grey area has closed. The CMA has demonstrated — through both its guidance and its newly sharpened DMCCA enforcement powers — that it is prepared to hold deployers accountable under existing law without waiting for AI-specific legislation. Companies deploying or planning to deploy AI agents in consumer-facing contexts would be prudent to treat this not as a compliance exercise for the future, but as a legal obligation in the present.
