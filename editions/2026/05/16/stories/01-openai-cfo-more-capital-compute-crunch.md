# OpenAI CFO Says Company May Raise More Capital as Compute Crunch Deepens

OpenAI Chief Financial Officer Sarah Friar said the company may return to capital markets yet again -- even after closing what she called the largest private fundraising round in history -- as the artificial intelligence giant struggles to secure enough computing power to keep pace with explosive demand for its products.

Speaking at a financial conference on May 15, Friar made clear that the $122 billion OpenAI raised earlier this year may not be the end of its fundraising ambitions. The company, she said, is staring down a supply problem that money alone can barely solve: there simply is not enough compute available in 2026 to feed the beast OpenAI has built.

"We're going up a vertical wall of demand right now," Friar said, pushing back on any suggestion that OpenAI was missing its internal targets. "When we are not hitting targets, it's lack of compute that often is the thing that's slowing us down to some degree."

## The Numbers Behind the Crunch

The scale of OpenAI's compute ambitions is staggering. OpenAI President Greg Brockman testified under oath earlier this month that the company expects to spend $50 billion on computing power in 2026 alone -- up from roughly $30 million in compute costs back in 2017. That figure covers everything from GPU clusters used to train frontier models, to the inference infrastructure serving ChatGPT's hundreds of millions of users, to API compute for enterprise customers worldwide.

The financial math is punishing. OpenAI's revenue, estimated at roughly $3.7 billion in 2024 and projected at around $11.6 billion for 2025, does not come close to covering a $50 billion annual compute bill. Even if 2026 revenue reaches $25 to $30 billion -- an optimistic scenario -- compute spending alone would consume nearly double the company's top line. The company has also accumulated approximately $600 billion in future spending commitments tied to data center buildouts and long-term compute capacity agreements, a figure Friar has flagged internally as a potential strain on the balance sheet heading into any public market debut.

Friar said any future fundraising decision will hinge on several variables: demand trajectory, revenue growth, cash flow generation, and the size of the gap between the computing power OpenAI needs and what it can actually afford. She also flagged public markets as a potentially attractive long-term financing avenue, noting that they are "significantly bigger" than private markets -- a comment that arrives amid a reported internal debate over whether to pursue an IPO in Q4 2026 or push the timeline to 2027.

## A Record Round That May Not Be Enough

OpenAI's recent fundraising history reads like a highlight reel of Silicon Valley excess. In February 2026, the company raised $110 billion in one of the largest private funding rounds ever recorded, drawing commitments from Amazon ($50 billion), Nvidia ($30 billion), and SoftBank ($30 billion) at a pre-money valuation of $730 billion. By March, the round had grown further, with OpenAI announcing $122 billion in committed capital at a post-money valuation of $852 billion -- numbers that would have seemed fantastical just two years ago.

Yet Friar's comments suggest even this extraordinary haul may prove insufficient given the speed at which compute requirements are escalating. The company is simultaneously training the next generation of frontier models, scaling its inference infrastructure to serve a user base that spans consumers, enterprises, and government clients, and investing in research that pushes into entirely new territory. Each of those workstreams is capital-intensive on its own; running all three in parallel at OpenAI's scale requires a financial engine unlike anything the technology industry has previously attempted.

## Why This Matters

OpenAI's compute predicament is not merely a corporate finance story -- it is a leading indicator of where the entire AI industry is heading. The race to secure GPU capacity has already strained global supply chains, driven Nvidia's market capitalization to historic highs, and prompted hyperscalers like Microsoft, Google, and Amazon to accelerate their own data center construction programs. If OpenAI, the best-capitalized AI startup in history, is warning that compute scarcity is throttling its growth, the implications ripple outward to every company building on top of AI infrastructure.

There is also a competitive dimension. Anthropic, OpenAI's closest rival in foundation model development, is reportedly engaged in its own race to secure compute capacity -- with Anthropic's CFO making similar public comments about the constraints the company faces. The parallel messaging from two of the industry's most prominent AI labs signals that the compute crunch is not a company-specific problem but a structural feature of the current AI buildout cycle. Both companies are, in effect, competing for the same finite pool of GPU silicon while simultaneously racing to deploy models that require ever-larger quantities of it.

For investors, Friar's remarks add a layer of complexity to OpenAI's eventual IPO narrative. A company that requires perpetual capital infusions to keep pace with its own demand is not a typical growth story; it is a capital-intensive infrastructure bet that will require public market investors to adopt a long time horizon and a high tolerance for cash burn. The gap between revenue and compute expenditure is not a bug to be patched -- it is a fundamental characteristic of how frontier AI development works at this stage.

## What to Watch Next

The immediate question is whether OpenAI launches another fundraising process before any IPO -- and if so, at what valuation and from which class of investors. Friar's mention of public markets as a "significantly bigger" pool of capital suggests the company is actively thinking about how to sequence private rounds against an eventual listing. Watch for any announcements tied to specific compute partnerships -- particularly with hyperscalers or sovereign wealth funds -- that would give OpenAI the guaranteed capacity it needs without immediate dilution. And keep an eye on Q3 revenue disclosures: if top-line growth does not accelerate meaningfully, the gap between OpenAI's ambitions and its balance sheet will only widen heading into what promises to be the most consequential IPO decision in tech since Meta went public in 2012.
