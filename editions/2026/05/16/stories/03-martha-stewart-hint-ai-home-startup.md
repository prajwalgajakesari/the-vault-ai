# Martha Stewart's AI Startup Hint Raises Seed Funding to Manage Your Home Before Things Break

**Martha Stewart is entering the AI-agent wars through the mudroom — and she has $10 million to prove that a smart home doesn't need to be full of sensors, just a good address.**

Hint, an AI home management platform cofounded by Stewart, home-services veteran Yih-Han Ma, and AI engineer Kyle Rush, emerged from stealth this week with a $10 million seed round led by Slow Ventures. The funding — joined by Montauk Capital (which incubated the company), Tusk Venture Partners, Amplo, Energy Impact Partners, Hannah Grey VC, and The Points Guy founder Brian Kelly — sets up Hint to launch on desktop and iOS this summer.

The pitch is deceptively simple: give Hint your address, and it takes it from there.

## From Easter Brunch to VC-Backed Startup

The origin story lands almost too neatly for a Martha Stewart venture. At an Easter brunch on Stewart's farm, she met her neighbor Rush, a veteran AI engineer, and realized he was describing something she had been mentally blueprinting for years: software that could flag a leaky ceiling, an expiring insurance policy, or dangerously high utility bills before a homeowner ever noticed the damage.

"I've wanted to create something beyond education, something that could actually help proactively manage one's home the way that I do — but the technology wasn't ready for my vision," Stewart told Fortune. "Hint is."

The startup's co-founder and CEO, Yih-Han Ma, who brings a background in home services, is running the operational side. Together, the trio is positioning Hint not as another contractor marketplace or scheduling app, but as what they call an always-on, AI-native layer that monitors, advises, and anticipates — before things go wrong.

## How It Works

The onboarding flow takes about 30 seconds. "The first thing you do is give us your address," Ma told Fortune. "That's it." From that single data point, Hint pulls public records on the property, along with weather patterns, soil composition, air quality data, local real estate listings, and other environmental signals. Users can then upload inspection reports, warranties, utility bills, and insurance policies to deepen the platform's picture.

The result is a continuously updated home profile that surfaces guidance proactively — the kind of advice that usually lives in the back of a homeowner's mind, unfiled and forgotten. Hint can tell a Texas homeowner to pre-water their foundation before clay soil contracts in summer heat, prompt someone to shop insurance policies before auto-renewal locks in a higher rate, or flag when a problem is minor enough to skip a contractor call entirely.

Stewart's influence runs throughout the product. The name, the visual design language, and even the logo's particular shade of green — chosen to match the eggs from her farm's chickens — carry her fingerprint. She has personally tested the app's outputs on her own property, and the editorial guides Hint surfaces to users are drawn partly from content she has written for the platform directly.

## The Investor Thesis: Break the Coordinator Model

The startup is entering a market that has frustrated venture investors for years. Americans spend more than $500 billion annually on residential renovations and repairs, according to a 2025 Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies report. Yet 62% of homeowners told Angi's 2025 State of Home Spending survey they were more worried about affording maintenance than they were the year before — a sign that the money is flowing, but the anxiety is not abating.

Previous attempts to solve this with premium human services have run into a predictable wall. Honey Homes raised a $9.25 million Series A-1 in 2024 for a membership model built around dedicated handypeople. Birdwatch raised $3.2 million in seed for an "autopilot" home care product the same year. Both depend heavily on human labor, which is expensive, inconsistent at scale, and hard to margin-engineer your way out of.

Kevin Colleran, cofounder and managing director at Slow Ventures, says that's exactly the trap Hint is designed to avoid. "I've seen a lot of concierge and home management startups force their way through fragmentation with armies of coordinators and white-glove service," he told Fortune. "That's where the economics fail at scale. Hint uses AI to bend the curve the other way. The more Hint learns about your home, the more the system can do without human intervention."

For Colleran, the investment case started from something more visceral than spreadsheets: "I own this incredibly valuable asset and I have no idea if I'm taking care of it correctly."

## The Conflict-of-Interest Question

No AI home management business can duck the incentive problem for long. When Hint eventually connects users with products, contractors, or service providers, the platform is likely to earn affiliate or referral fees — the same revenue model that has quietly tilted recommendation engines across the consumer internet toward whoever is paying the highest margin, not whoever is offering the best deal.

Hint has preemptively staked out a firm position. The company says its recommendations are and will remain blind to commercial arrangements — no vendor pays for placement, no affiliate fee changes the ranking. "Once your bottom line depends on referral fees and take-rates, it becomes very hard to resist nudging people toward whoever pays you the most," Colleran acknowledged to Fortune, noting this was a key point he pressed before committing capital. Hint's stated answer: it is 100% on the side of the homeowner, full stop.

That pledge will be easy to keep at launch, when Hint is pre-revenue and the commercial architecture does not yet exist. The harder test comes once the business needs to generate returns on that $10 million — and the platform has hundreds of thousands of homeowners who trust it.

## A Crowded Race, With a Different Starting Line

The established players are not standing still. Thumbtack, which crossed $400 million in annual revenue growing 27% year-over-year, announced its own AI-powered guided experience in April 2026 — replacing the search bar with a conversational flow that helps homeowners diagnose problems and choose pros. Angi, the market leader at $1.19 billion in FY2024 revenue, is making similar moves across its platform.

But Hint's competitive argument is that it is not racing Angi or Thumbtack for the same real estate. Those platforms have conditioned consumers to come to them when something breaks. Hint wants to be the layer underneath, running continuously in the background, so the homeowner never has to search at all.

Whether Martha Stewart's brand — one of the most durable in American domesticity — can transfer credibility to a software startup remains an open question. But the model is conceptually clean, the team has pedigree in the space, and the timing is right: AI agents are proving capable of exactly this kind of continuous monitoring and anticipatory nudging across verticals from health to finance.

Home, with its $500 billion annual maintenance bill and its 62% anxious owners, was always an obvious next frontier. Hint just showed up first with a check.

*Hint is expected to launch on desktop and iOS in summer 2026.*
