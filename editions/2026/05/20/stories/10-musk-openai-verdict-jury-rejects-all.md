# Jury Rejects Every Claim in Musk v OpenAI After Less Than Two Hours of Deliberation

A nine-person jury in Oakland, California took less than two hours Monday morning to do what three weeks of blockbuster testimony, six billionaire witnesses, and a courthouse circus could not settle: they threw out every single one of Elon Musk's claims against OpenAI, CEO Sam Altman, co-founder Greg Brockman, and Microsoft, handing the world's most valuable private AI company a sweeping legal victory on the eve of what may be the most anticipated IPO in tech history.

The verdict was unanimous. U.S. District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers of the Northern District of California said from the bench that she accepted and adopted the jury's findings and dismissed the case in full. The entire proceeding — from the jury entering the deliberation room to the reading of the verdict — consumed less time than a typical board meeting.

## The Clock Ran Out on Musk

At its core, the verdict turned not on whether OpenAI betrayed its founding nonprofit mission, but on when Musk knew it might be doing so — and whether he waited too long to act.

The applicable statutes of limitations were strict: three years for a breach of charitable trust claim and two years for an unjust enrichment claim. Jurors determined that Musk knew, or reasonably should have known, about OpenAI's drift toward commercial structures well before 2024, the year he finally filed suit. OpenAI created its for-profit arm in 2019. Musk, Altman, and Brockman had discussed a potential for-profit conversion as early as 2017. On the stand for three days, Musk argued he held back because he believed Altman's reassurances, only losing patience in 2023 when Microsoft invested $10 billion in OpenAI's commercial entity in exchange for intellectual property rights and a share of future profits. "I would have filed a lawsuit sooner if I thought they had stolen the charity sooner," he told the jury.

OpenAI's legal team was unmoved. "He waited too long to sue," lead counsel Bill Savitt argued in his opening. After the verdict, Savitt told reporters outside the courthouse: "We were pleased that the jury saw the evidence as we did — that is to say, very conclusively tilting in one direction."

## The Verdict and Its Immediate Aftermath

None of the tech titans — not Musk, not Altman, not Brockman — were in the courtroom when the verdict was read. Outside, lawyers for OpenAI and Microsoft exchanged hugs. Musk's lead attorney, Steven Molo, immediately told the court his team intended to appeal. "We want to get going on the appeal, with all due respect to the court," Molo said. He characterized the outcome as a narrow decision on "technical legal issues" and told reporters he believed Musk's side had proved the substance of its case — it was the timing rules, he argued, that blocked the jury from reaching those merits.

Musk himself responded hours later on X, writing that "the judge and jury never actually ruled on the merits of the case, just on a calendar technicality." His other attorney, Marc Toberoff, was more concise on the courthouse steps: he said a single word to waiting reporters — "Appeal" — before departing.

Microsoft, cleared of aiding and abetting claims on the same statute-of-limitations grounds, issued a brief statement: "The facts and the timeline in this case have long been clear, and we welcome the jury's decision to dismiss these claims as untimely."

## The Road to Appeal

Musk's legal team has announced they will take the case to the Ninth Circuit U.S. Court of Appeals. Their central argument will invoke the continuing violation doctrine — a legal theory that can extend the statute of limitations when a pattern of wrongful conduct persists over time rather than occurring as a single discrete act. Musk's lawyers fought during trial to have that doctrine included in jury instructions; Judge Gonzalez Rogers declined to do so, a ruling that is expected to form the backbone of the appeal.

Legal observers caution that appellate success on those grounds is uncertain. The Ninth Circuit applies the continuing violation doctrine narrowly outside employment discrimination law, and Musk's team faces the challenge of convincing a panel that OpenAI's evolution from nonprofit to hybrid commercial entity constitutes an ongoing wrong rather than a series of identifiable decisions — each of which Musk could arguably have acted on years earlier. The appeal process is likely to consume a year or more.

## What Was at Stake — and What Now Clears for OpenAI

The financial stakes were extraordinary. Musk sought more than $150 billion in damages from OpenAI and Microsoft — a sum he said he would redirect entirely to OpenAI's nonprofit foundation rather than keep himself. He also demanded the removal of Altman and Brockman from their executive roles and the unwinding of the company's for-profit structure.

Had the jury sided with Musk and the judge agreed, OpenAI and Microsoft could have faced forced disgorgement into the nonprofit, a restructuring that would have upended the company's capital structure at a critical moment. OpenAI completed a major structural overhaul last year that left a nonprofit foundation in charge of its business operations while formalizing large ownership stakes for outside investors including Microsoft. That structure is now legally intact.

More immediately, the verdict removes the single largest legal cloud hanging over OpenAI's march toward a public offering. In March 2026, the company disclosed a fresh funding round of $122 billion that pegged its valuation at $852 billion. Analysts and investors tracking the IPO pipeline had cited the Musk litigation as a material risk factor — one that Monday's verdict has now neutralized at the trial court level. With the appeal timeline unlikely to produce injunctive relief, OpenAI's path to a public market debut estimated by some at a $1 trillion valuation is meaningfully clearer.

## A Trial That Exposed Silicon Valley's Founding Tensions

Beyond the legal outcome, the three-week trial served as a rare, compelled accounting of how one of the most consequential companies in the world was actually built — and how it came apart from one of its founders.

Altman told jurors that OpenAI's mission required that no single person control the development of artificial general intelligence, a principle he said influenced governance decisions from the outset. Musk framed the dispute in simpler moral terms from the witness stand: "It's not OK to steal a charity."

The proceedings also revisited the November 2023 boardroom crisis when Altman was abruptly fired by OpenAI's board and reinstated days later amid a staff revolt — an episode Musk pointed to as evidence of deeper dysfunction, while current and former board members described it as a governance rupture driven by concerns unrelated to his claims.

Six tech billionaires testified over the course of the trial. Outside the federal courthouse, demonstrators arrived daily with signs protesting AI development, wealth inequality, or both. The proceedings were, by any measure, remarkable for the concentration of wealth and institutional consequence on display.

## What Comes Next

OpenAI now emerges from its most consequential legal battle with its structure intact, its IPO timeline unimpeded at the trial level, and a unanimous jury finding in its favor. The company still faces an ongoing appeal, a continuing regulatory environment in which attorneys general and federal officials are scrutinizing its nonprofit-to-commercial conversion, and an AI competitive race that grows more expensive with each passing quarter.

For Musk, the loss extends a difficult stretch in his broader campaign against OpenAI — a company he co-founded with a reported $38 million investment, departed in 2018, and now competes against directly through his xAI venture. Whether the Ninth Circuit rewrites the story remains to be seen. What a California jury made clear in under two hours is that the clock, not the merits, decided this round.
