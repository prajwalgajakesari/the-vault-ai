# EU Reaches Omnibus Deal to Simplify AI Act Rules and Delay High-Risk Deadlines

At 4:30 in the morning on May 7, after an all-night negotiating session that at one point seemed likely to collapse entirely, European Union lawmakers emerged from Brussels with a provisional deal that rewrites the enforcement calendar for what remains the world's most comprehensive artificial intelligence law. The agreement on the so-called AI Omnibus buys European businesses more time to comply with the bulk of the EU AI Act's requirements, extends key protections to a broader set of companies, and adds a new prohibition on AI tools designed to generate non-consensual intimate imagery — all while leaving the Act's core architecture substantively intact.

The deal, struck between the European Parliament and the Council of the EU, was driven above all by a single urgent problem: major compliance deadlines for high-risk AI systems were barreling toward companies on August 2, 2026, even as the technical standards needed to guide that compliance remained unfinished. Leaving the calendar unchanged would have forced businesses to comply with rules that neither regulators nor standardization bodies had finished operationalizing — a situation industry groups and legal experts across the political spectrum had warned was unworkable.

What Changed — and What Did Not

The centerpiece of the agreement is a reset of the high-risk compliance timeline. Under the original AI Act, companies deploying AI systems in sensitive domains — biometrics, critical infrastructure, employment screening, law enforcement, education, and border management — were set to face the full weight of the Act's Annex III requirements this coming August. Under the new deal, that deadline shifts to December 2, 2027. AI systems embedded as safety components in regulated physical products — medical devices, machinery, toys, industrial equipment — get additional runway until August 2, 2028.

The agreement also pushes back transparency and watermarking obligations for AI-generated content, though only partially. Most transparency requirements under Article 50 will still apply from August 2, 2026 as originally scheduled. The watermarking obligation specifically — requiring AI-generated content to carry technical markers — is delayed until December 2, 2026, with systems already on the market given leeway through year-end.

Alongside the deadline extensions, the deal introduces a clarification that redraws the boundary of the Act's reach into industrial products. The machinery sector — one of twelve regulated product categories subject to the original Annex I framework — has been carved out of the AI Act's direct requirements. Machinery products will instead need to comply only with their existing sectoral safety rules, though the sector remains tethered to the AI Act through bridging standards, and the European Commission is bound to incorporate AI-specific requirements into sectoral legislation before the 2028 deadline. The deal also establishes a mechanism — through implementing acts — allowing other regulated sectors to seek similar carve-outs where their existing laws already contain AI-specific provisions comparable to the Act's requirements.

Broader Exemptions and New Prohibitions

The deal extends compliance benefits previously reserved for small and medium-sized enterprises to a wider category of companies. Small mid-cap firms with up to 750 employees and no more than €150 million in annual revenue will now qualify for simplified compliance guidance, reduced administrative penalties, access to regulatory sandboxes, and standardized documentation templates — tools that had previously been available only to smaller operators.

A new EU-level regulatory sandbox is established to allow innovative AI systems to be tested before receiving formal approval. Member states were already required to set up national sandboxes; under the Omnibus, they now have until August 2, 2027 to do so, with an EU-level sandbox operational by 2028.

The agreement also adds a prohibition that was not in the Commission's original proposal: a ban on AI systems that generate child sexual abuse material or that depict, without consent, the intimate parts or sexually explicit activities of an identifiable person — so-called nudifier tools. The ban was pushed to the front of the agenda after a wave of non-consensual sexual deepfakes generated by AI systems in recent months drew outrage across member states. It takes effect December 2, 2026.

The Industrial Standoff That Nearly Broke the Deal

The breakthrough arrived only after a near-collapse the previous week, when negotiations stalled over how to treat industrial AI. The European Parliament, acting as the loudest voice for industry concerns, had sought to exempt all regulated-product sectors — not just machinery — from the AI Act entirely, arguing that overlapping obligations with existing sectoral law created intolerable duplication and cost. That position found backing from an unexpected source: German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, who lobbied both fellow member states and the European Commission directly, even setting aside objections from his own coalition partner, the SPD.

The Commission had initially held firm against broad exemptions, but began to soften as Merz's CDU reportedly coordinated with the European People's Party in Parliament. A second negotiating session was convened. By the end of it, only the machinery sector had been carved out — one of twelve regulated categories — a result that satisfied neither Parliament's most industry-friendly members nor civil society groups that had spent months warning against any weakening of the Act's horizontal protections.

Laura Caroli, writing for Tech Policy Press, characterized the final result as a qualified success that nonetheless left both camps unhappy: "the outcome has left stakeholders from both civil society and industry frustrated rather than relieved." Industry groups including CCIA Europe and the Business Software Alliance argued the package did little to resolve practical compliance burdens or reduce legal uncertainty. Consumer advocates objected to expansions of sensitive data processing for bias mitigation — a provision that broadens the circumstances under which AI providers and deployers may process personal data for bias detection and correction, subject to safeguards.

What Businesses Should Do Now

The provisional agreement is not yet law. Both the European Parliament and the Council must formally adopt the text, and that process is expected to move on an accelerated timeline given the August 2 deadline approaching fast. Once published in the Official Journal of the European Union, the amendments enter into force three days later.

White & Case partner Tim Hickman noted that "the provisional agreement provides welcome clarity by providing for delayed enforcement and allowing businesses more time to analyse and implement applicable guidelines and standards" — but cautioned that companies should continue reviewing their AI deployments and building compliance roadmaps regardless of the new timelines.

The deal does not touch the AI Act's prohibitions that already came into force in February 2025, nor the general-purpose AI governance requirements that took effect in August of that year. The core obligations companies face — conformity assessments, technical documentation, human oversight requirements, post-market monitoring — remain unchanged. What has shifted is the timetable for when enforcement of those obligations begins for high-risk systems.

A Precursor to a Larger Fight

Analysts tracking EU digital policy are already looking past the AI Omnibus to its larger sibling: the Data Omnibus, which proposes amendments to the GDPR and would carry far-reaching implications for how AI systems may collect, retain, and process personal data. Unlike the AI Act, the GDPR faces no imminent enforcement deadline, meaning the Data Omnibus will move more slowly — and, some observers warn, may prove more consequential. The expanded ability to process sensitive personal data for bias mitigation in the AI Omnibus is only a preview of broader GDPR relaxations being contemplated, including narrowing the definition of personal data and recognizing AI training as a legitimate interest for data processing.

For now, European AI developers and deployers have more time than they did last week. The question the coming months will answer is whether the Commission, the standardization bodies still racing to finalize technical norms, and member state regulators standing up their national oversight frameworks can use that time to deliver the clarity that has so far been in short supply.
