# American Medical Association Issues First Patient-Facing Guidelines for AI Chatbot Use in Healthcare

The nation's largest physician organization has drawn a line in the digital sand: AI chatbots can help you prepare for your doctor's appointment, but they cannot be your doctor.

On May 20, the American Medical Association released what amounts to the first formal patient-facing guidance on using AI health chatbots safely — a five-prompt infographic distributed nationally that tells patients exactly how to query AI tools, and more critically, where those tools must stop. The release comes as a West Health-Gallup survey found that more than 66 million Americans — one in four adults — have already used an AI tool or chatbot for health information or advice, with roughly one in ten reporting they received advice they believed was unsafe.

"As technology evolves, it's vital that patients use AI to complement — not replace — the advice of their doctors," said AMA CEO John Whyte, MD, MPH. "These recommended prompts empower individuals to ask better questions, cut through jargon, and understand their options. Safe and informed use of AI helps patients make smarter health decisions, but medical expertise remains irreplaceable."

## What the AMA Is Telling Patients to Ask

The infographic is not a vague warning label. It is a prescriptive framework of five specific prompt templates, each designed to channel AI into a supporting role rather than a diagnostic one.

The first prompt instructs patients to frame symptom queries as open-ended explorations: *"I have these symptoms [description]. What are common causes, how do they differ, and what details would help narrow them down?"* The framing is deliberate — it invites possibility-mapping rather than self-diagnosis.

The second prompt addresses one of the most common patient pain points: medical jargon. Patients are encouraged to ask AI to *"Explain this to me in simple words: [medical term, doctor's diagnosis or instructions]."* The third prompt adds personal context — age, gender, lifestyle, goals — to generic health information. The fourth helps patients understand treatment tradeoffs: *"What are the different ways to treat [diagnosis name] and what are the benefits, risks, and side effects of each?"*

The fifth prompt may be the most strategically valuable of all: *"What questions should I ask my doctor to better understand this situation?"* In effect, the AMA is positioning AI as pre-visit preparation infrastructure — a tool for arriving at the appointment more informed, not for bypassing the appointment altogether. The AMA cautions that AI tools should never be used in emergencies, nor relied on for diagnosis and treatment decisions. Patients should protect their privacy and treat AI chatbots as supplements, not substitutes, for physician expertise.

## The Stakes Behind the Guidance

The timing is not coincidental. Since January 2026, OpenAI, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google have all launched or significantly expanded consumer-facing AI health products. OpenAI's ChatGPT Health, which allows users to connect patient portals and medical records directly, is now fielding more than 230 million health queries per week globally. None of these platforms are licensed medical providers, and all carry disclaimers stating they are not intended for diagnosis or treatment.

The gap between patient behavior and those disclaimers has become clinically visible. A February 2026 study published in *Nature Medicine* by Oxford researchers found that AI chatbots posed real risks due to inaccurate and inconsistent information, and that participants using them performed no better on health decisions than those relying on a standard Google search. That same month, ECRI — the nonprofit patient safety organization whose annual hazard rankings guide hospital policy nationwide — named AI chatbot misuse the top health technology hazard of 2026.

Physicians on the front lines have been watching the shift in real time. "My patients don't wait for me anymore," wrote Dr. Tod Stillson, a board-certified family physician and founder of ChatRx, in a May 2026 essay in KevinMD. "Patients started arriving at appointments having already run their symptoms through an AI chatbot. Sometimes they'd decided not to come in at all. Sometimes they were right. Sometimes they weren't." Dr. Stillson's observation captures the dual-edged reality: used well, AI can improve care navigation; used poorly, it can delay necessary treatment or generate false reassurance in patients who need urgent attention.

A Mass General Brigham study published in *JAMA Network Open* in April 2026 reinforced those concerns. Researchers testing 21 leading AI models found they reached a correct diagnosis more than 90 percent of the time with complete information — but failed to generate appropriate differential diagnoses more than 80 percent of the time when patient data was incomplete. That is precisely the real-world scenario: incomplete history, high anxiety, no physician in the loop. "Off-the-shelf large language models are not ready for unsupervised clinical-grade deployment," said corresponding author Dr. Marc Succi.

## The AMA's Harder Position on Congress

The infographic is the patient-facing piece of a broader AMA posture on AI in healthcare. In parallel, the organization has called on Congress to implement specific legislative safeguards for AI chatbots — particularly for mental health applications. The AMA's four-point congressional ask includes mandating safety monitoring and adverse event reporting, prohibiting advertising within mental health chatbot interfaces (especially for minors), requiring strict data protection standards with limits on collection and retention, and addressing what the AMA identifies as the most acute risks of AI-enabled mental health tools: emotional dependency, misinformation, and inadequate crisis response.

That last concern — inadequate crisis response — reflects a gap that no prompt template can fully close. An AI chatbot that fails to escalate a patient in suicidal crisis to emergency services is not a patient education problem. It is a systems design problem, and the AMA is signaling it expects regulatory intervention rather than voluntary compliance from technology companies. Healthcare Dive and FierceHealthcare have both reported that the congressional push is gaining traction as lawmakers consider whether existing consumer protection frameworks are sufficient for AI products that blur the line between information and medical advice.

## A Profession Racing to Keep Up

The AMA's own data illustrates how fast the landscape has shifted. Its 2026 Physician Survey on Augmented Intelligence, drawn from 1,692 physicians surveyed in early 2026, found that 81 percent of physicians now use AI in their practices — more than double the 38 percent reported just three years ago. More than three-quarters believe AI improves their ability to care for patients. Yet 88 percent express concern about potential skill erosion among clinicians who lean too heavily on AI-generated outputs, a fear most acute among early-career physicians.

The patient-facing guidance released today is the AMA's first attempt to shape how tens of millions of Americans interact with technology that already exists and is already in use. It is deliberately practical: five prompts, a clear set of prohibitions, and a consistent message that AI is a companion to physician care, not a substitute for it.

Whether that message travels as far as the ChatGPT health queries it is meant to inform is the harder question. The AMA has the credibility of 190-plus affiliated state and specialty medical societies behind it. The platforms it is trying to shape have 230 million weekly users and balance sheets measured in the hundreds of billions. The infographic is a start. The policy infrastructure to back it up is still being built.
