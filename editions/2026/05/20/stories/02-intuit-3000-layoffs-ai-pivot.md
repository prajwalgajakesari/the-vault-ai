# Intuit Cuts 3,000 Jobs and Closes Offices as TurboTax Owner Bets Everything on AI

**Category:** Business
**Date:** May 20, 2026
**Sources:** TechCrunch, Quartz, PYMNTS, Seeking Alpha, HCA Magazine, American Bazaar Online

---

Intuit, the financial software giant behind TurboTax, QuickBooks, Credit Karma, and Mailchimp, announced on May 20, 2026 that it is eliminating 3,000 jobs — roughly 17 percent of its 18,200-person global workforce — in what CEO Sasan Goodarzi is framing as the most consequential strategic repositioning in the company's history. Two U.S. offices, in Reno, Nevada, and Woodland Hills, California, will be wound down entirely. The layoffs push Intuit to the front of a line of tech giants that have collectively cut tens of thousands of jobs this year under the same stated rationale: artificial intelligence changes what human labor is actually needed for.

The scope of the cuts is striking for a company that generates consistent revenue from deeply embedded products. TurboTax alone processes taxes for tens of millions of Americans each year. QuickBooks serves small businesses across more than 100 countries. These are not products under competitive siege — they are near-monopolies in their categories. The decision to cut 17 percent of the workforce is not a survival move. It is a calculated bet that Intuit can deliver more value with fewer people if it aggressively couples its product suite to the current generation of large language models.

Goodarzi told employees the restructuring was designed to reduce organizational complexity and free up capital for AI investment. The centerpiece of that investment: multi-year partnerships with both Anthropic and OpenAI. Under the Anthropic deal, Intuit will embed Claude directly into its platforms while mid-market business customers will gain access to Anthropic's Claude Agent SDK to build customized financial compliance workflows. The OpenAI partnership mirrors that structure, integrating ChatGPT capabilities across Intuit's consumer products. Running in both directions, the agreements also make Intuit's tax, accounting, and marketing tools available natively inside Claude and ChatGPT — turning Intuit's data infrastructure into a distribution channel operating inside the two most widely-used AI assistants on the planet.

"We are reducing complexity across our organization and reallocating capital to accelerate our AI-driven strategy," Goodarzi said in a message to employees. "This is about building the skills and capabilities we need to win in an AI-first world." U.S. employees affected by the cuts will remain on payroll through July 31, 2026, and will receive severance of 16 weeks of base pay plus an additional two weeks for every year of service — a package that is above-market for the current layoff cycle. Intuit shares fell nearly 5 percent on the announcement before partially recovering as analysts characterized the restructuring charges as one-time costs against an otherwise durable subscription revenue base.

What distinguishes Intuit's move from its peers is the specificity of the AI commitment. Most companies announce layoffs with vague transformation language and leave the product roadmap implied. Intuit announced named, structured, multi-year partnerships with the two most prominent AI labs simultaneously with the workforce reduction. This is not a cost-cutting exercise dressed up as innovation. It is a deliberate architecture decision: remove the human layers that previously sat between Intuit's proprietary financial data and its customers, and replace them with model-driven interfaces that can answer a tax question, reconcile a QuickBooks account, or draft a Mailchimp campaign without a human intermediary. The cuts span all four of Intuit's major consumer-facing brands, suggesting no division was treated as exempt from the AI-first reorganization.

The broader pattern in 2026 is difficult to ignore. Amazon, Block, Cisco, Cloudflare, Meta, Microsoft, Pinterest, and Oracle have all announced significant headcount reductions this year, all citing the same AI transition logic — and 2026 is already on pace to exceed 2025's total tech layoff count. But Intuit's version is particularly high-stakes because it operates at the intersection of financial data and regulatory compliance. Deploying large language models into tax preparation and small business accounting carries real accuracy and liability risk. Getting a product recommendation wrong in a Mailchimp campaign costs a marketer some conversion rate. Getting a tax calculation wrong costs a TurboTax customer money and potentially triggers an IRS audit. The company is betting that its proprietary data, accumulated over decades of filed returns and reconciled accounts, is sufficient moat to keep the AI-powered versions of its products trustworthy — even as the human experts who maintained institutional knowledge about edge cases walk out the door.

The dual partnership structure — embedding Intuit data into Claude and ChatGPT, while simultaneously embedding Claude and ChatGPT into Intuit products — is the most interesting strategic signal in the announcement. It positions Intuit less as a software company and more as a financial data layer that flows through whatever AI assistant a user happens to be inside. If that architecture holds, competitors like H&R Block cannot simply build better tax UI; they would need to replicate decades of structured financial data and the distribution agreements to inject it into the platforms where consumers now ask financial questions. That is a genuinely defensible position.

Watch for Intuit's Q3 2026 earnings, expected in late June, which will be the first financial report to include restructuring charges and give investors a clear picture of how aggressively the company is redeploying savings into AI product development. Watch also for regulatory scrutiny: embedding AI models into tax-preparation software at scale is precisely the kind of use case that state attorneys general and the IRS have begun to examine. And watch whether Intuit's decision accelerates a race to the bottom on human expertise in financial software — or whether competitors find that leaning into human accountability becomes a trust signal in a market Intuit is rapidly vacating.
