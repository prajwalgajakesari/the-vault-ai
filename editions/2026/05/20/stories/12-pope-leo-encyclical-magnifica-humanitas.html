# Pope Leo XIV Will Present First AI Encyclical Alongside Anthropic Co-Founder Christopher Olah

On May 25, the Vatican will attempt something unprecedented: a sitting pope will share a stage at Synod Hall with a Silicon Valley AI researcher to unveil what may become the most consequential ethical document the Catholic Church has issued since it last grappled with an industrial revolution. The document is Magnifica Humanitas — "The Magnificent Humanity" — and its co-presenter is Christopher Olah, the Anthropic co-founder whose quiet crusade to understand how AI systems work internally has made him one of the most important, and least famous, figures in the technology he helped build.

The encyclical's release date is no accident. Pope Leo XIV, born Robert Francis Prevost, signed the letter on May 15 — precisely the 135th anniversary of Rerum Novarum, the landmark 1891 encyclical in which Pope Leo XIII confronted the industrial age's treatment of workers and laid the philosophical groundwork for modern Catholic social teaching. The echo is deliberate. Where Leo XIII faced factory floors and child labor, Leo XIV faces large language models, autonomous weapons, and the creeping displacement of human creativity and moral agency by systems no one fully understands.

What the Document Says

The full title — Magnifica Humanitas: On the Protection of Human Dignity in the Age of Artificial Intelligence — telegraphs the document's central argument: technology must remain subordinate to the human person, not the reverse. According to Vatican News and reporting from America Magazine, the encyclical addresses workers' rights in an era of AI-driven automation, the ethics of AI in warfare, and the broader question of whether systems built to optimize outcomes can be trusted to protect human creativity and moral agency.

The presentation at Synod Hall on May 25 at 11:30 a.m. will include Cardinal Secretary of State Pietro Parolin, Cardinal Victor Manuel Fernandez, and Olah — an unusual lineup that places a tech company co-founder at the same podium as two of the Vatican's most senior officials.

Why Olah, and Why Now

Christopher Olah is not a household name outside AI research circles, but within them he is revered. His work on neural network interpretability — the painstaking effort to map what is actually happening inside the layers of a large model — has shaped how the field thinks about AI transparency and safety. At Anthropic, he leads the interpretability team, whose mission is to make AI systems legible enough to be trusted.

His presence at the Vatican is both symbolically rich and practically pointed. As Olah noted on X ahead of the announcement: "The questions posed by AI are bigger than the AI community." The remark captures the spirit of the joint presentation: the Church is not asking Silicon Valley to validate its theology, but to acknowledge that questions of human dignity, accountability, and the limits of machine judgment belong to a conversation wider than any single industry.

The choice of Olah over a more commercially prominent figure — a CEO, a regulator, a politician — also carries a message. Interpretability is not about product launches or quarterly results. It is about whether humans can ever truly understand what they have built. That question sits at the heart of Magnifica Humanitas.

The Anthropic-Pentagon Shadow

Olah's appearance in Rome lands against a fraught backdrop for the company he co-founded. In late February 2026, Anthropic's negotiations with the Pentagon over a reported $200 million contract collapsed after the AI company refused to cede control of its technology for use in domestic mass surveillance. U.S. officials subsequently designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk to national security" — a designation that effectively shut the company out of certain defense-related work.

The contrast with OpenAI, which inked its own partnership with the Department of Defense around the same period, has sharpened the fault lines inside the AI industry over military applications. Anthropic's refusal drew criticism from some national security quarters and praise from civil liberties advocates. Now, days before the encyclical's release, one of Anthropic's founders will stand at the Vatican and argue — alongside the head of the Roman Catholic Church — that AI must not be weaponized against human dignity.

Whether that alignment is strategic, principled, or simply coincidental, it is striking. The company that said no to the Pentagon is saying yes to the Pope.

A Church Finding Its Voice on Technology

The Vatican has been building toward this moment for years. Pope Francis's 2023 "Rome Call for AI Ethics" attracted signatures from Microsoft and IBM, and the Holy See has sent delegates to AI governance forums at the G7 and the United Nations. But an encyclical is a different order of document — a formal teaching addressed to the entire Catholic Church, carrying doctrinal weight that no press release or forum communiqué can replicate.

With 1.4 billion Catholics worldwide, Magnifica Humanitas has the potential to shape how a significant fraction of the global population thinks about the technology reshaping their lives. JD Vance, the U.S. Vice President and a convert to Catholicism, said this week he was "looking forward to reading" the encyclical — a signal that its reach may extend well into secular policy debates.

The Stakes

The timing of Magnifica Humanitas could not be more charged. AI governance frameworks are still nascent. Regulatory regimes in the EU, the United States, and China are incomplete and contested. The industry is moving faster than the institutions trying to oversee it. Into that vacuum, the Catholic Church is inserting the oldest continuous institution in the Western world, armed with a 135-year-old template for challenging the assumptions of an industrial revolution.

The question is whether moral authority, however ancient and global, can translate into practical constraint on systems built in server farms in Northern Virginia and the San Francisco Bay Area. Olah's presence on May 25 suggests at least one person inside those companies believes it should.

Magnifica Humanitas will be released publicly on May 25, 2026.
