New York Lawmaker Proposes AI Dividend to Pay Americans Displaced by Automation

4 min read 982 words 3 sources
Key Takeaway

New York Assemblymember Alex Bores has proposed an AI Dividend program that would send direct payments to Americans when AI displaces workers, funded by AI token taxes.

As artificial intelligence reshapes the American economy at a pace that outstrips any prior technological shift, a New York lawmaker is advancing one of the most concrete policy responses yet: direct cash payments to workers whose jobs are eliminated by automation.

New York State Assemblymember Alex Bores, a Democrat now running for a seat in the U.S. House of Representatives, unveiled his AI Dividend proposal in April, outlining a contingency payment program that would activate when economic indicators confirm that AI is meaningfully displacing American workers. The plan represents a new breed of policy thinking — one that treats widespread automation not as a distant hypothetical but as an insurable risk demanding preparation today.

"The AI Dividend is about expanding freedom and choice," Bores wrote in the policy memo accompanying the announcement. "It gives Americans a direct stake in the wealth generated by AI."

How the AI Dividend Would Work

The program is designed around a set of economic tripwires rather than political discretion. Payments to eligible Americans would be triggered when federal data show sustained declines in labor force participation, wage compression in sectors most exposed to AI, or sharp increases in AI-driven productivity that are not matched by corresponding job growth.

Once activated, the AI Dividend would distribute direct payments to U.S. residents while simultaneously funding workforce transition programs, educational initiatives, and independent government oversight of AI systems. The proposal does not yet specify payment amounts or frequency — details that would presumably be hashed out through the legislative process.

Bores framed the mechanism as forward-looking insurance rather than punitive regulation. "It is not a punishment for innovation — it is an insurance policy," the memo stated, arguing that the window for creative policymaking closes once wealth has already concentrated in the hands of a few dominant firms.

Funding the Dividend: Equity Stakes and Token Taxes

The proposal's funding architecture is where it breaks the most new ground. Bores envisions a multi-pronged revenue strategy built on three pillars.

First, a tax on AI consumption measured in computational tokens — the units that underpin usage of large language models and other AI systems. As AI usage scales across industries, this mechanism would generate revenue in proportion to the technology's economic footprint.

Second, the federal government would acquire equity warrants in major AI companies, granting the right to purchase shares if those firms' valuations rise significantly. This structure would allow the public to share directly in the upside of AI-driven wealth creation rather than relying solely on after-the-fact taxation.

Third, the plan calls for broader tax code reforms that would address what Bores describes as a structural bias favoring capital investment over human wages. "Taxing the thing that is growing, AI, rather than the thing that is shrinking, wages, is simply sound fiscal management," the memo argues.

A Growing Wave of AI Layoffs

The proposal arrives against a backdrop of accelerating AI-linked workforce reductions. A Goldman Sachs report cited in the Bores memo found that AI adoption has contributed to the loss of approximately 16,000 jobs per month over the past year. The layoffs have cut across sectors and company sizes, but some of the most visible cuts have come from major technology and financial firms.

Meta laid off roughly 8,000 employees in restructuring moves tied to AI-driven efficiency gains. Standard Chartered announced plans to eliminate around 7,000 positions as it integrates AI across operations. Intuit cut approximately 3,000 workers while simultaneously hiring for AI-focused roles — a pattern that has become disturbingly common across the corporate landscape.

The trend extends well beyond tech. Companies from Oracle to HP have pointed to AI productivity gains when announcing headcount reductions, while simultaneously increasing capital expenditure on AI infrastructure. The dynamic has created what economists describe as a decoupling of productivity growth from employment growth — precisely the scenario the AI Dividend is designed to address.

The Policy Debate Takes Shape

Not everyone agrees that the situation demands intervention. Morgan Stanley released a report in mid-April arguing that AI's impact on the labor market has been "modest so far" and that historical technological shifts have typically expanded employment over time, even as they displaced specific roles in the short term. The firm acknowledged, however, that AI could defy this historical pattern.

The tension between these two views — imminent disruption versus gradual adaptation — sits at the heart of the emerging AI policy debate. Bores and his supporters argue that the unprecedented speed of AI development, combined with explicit statements from industry leaders about their intent to automate human labor, justifies proactive measures.

The warnings have come from the top of the industry itself. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has said he is "a little bit more worried about the labor impact" of AI compared to previous technological waves because of how fast it is moving. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman and Microsoft AI chief Mustafa Suleyman have made similar public statements about AI's potential to automate large categories of white-collar work within years rather than decades.

What Comes Next

The AI Dividend remains a campaign proposal — its path to legislation depends on Bores winning his congressional race in the 2026 midterms. But the idea has entered the broader policy conversation at a moment when AI regulation is emerging as a defining issue for voters.

Bores has already drawn opposition from powerful corners of the AI industry. His previous work co-sponsoring New York's RAISE Act made him a target of pro-AI super PACs, including Leading The Future, backed by Andreessen Horowitz, OpenAI president Greg Brockman, and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale.

Where most AI policy discussions have focused on safety guardrails and disclosure requirements, Bores is making an economic argument about who should benefit from AI-generated wealth. Whether or not his specific proposal advances, the question it raises is not going away: as AI transforms the economy, who gets the dividend?
