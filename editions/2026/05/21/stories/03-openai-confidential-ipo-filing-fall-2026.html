OpenAI Prepares Confidential IPO Filing Targeting Fall 2026 Public Debut

4 min read 990 words 3 sources
Key Takeaway

OpenAI is days away from a confidential IPO filing with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, targeting a September 2026 listing at a valuation that could exceed $1 trillion.

OpenAI is preparing to confidentially file its initial public offering paperwork with the Securities and Exchange Commission within days, according to people familiar with the matter, setting the stage for what could become one of the largest technology IPOs in history when the ChatGPT maker targets a public debut in September 2026. The filing would cap a remarkable eighteen-month transformation from nonprofit research lab to for-profit colossus valued at $852 billion -- and potentially north of $1 trillion by the time shares begin trading.

The Filing and the Banks

Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are leading the deal as joint lead underwriters, with JPMorgan also advising on the offering. The company plans to submit a confidential draft registration statement -- a mechanism that allows companies to share financial details with SEC reviewers without immediately disclosing them to the public. The confidential route, permitted under the JOBS Act for companies with less than $1.235 billion in annual revenue at the time of their last fiscal year, buys OpenAI time to negotiate with regulators and refine its prospectus before the inevitable public scrutiny begins.

Preliminary discussions have centered on raising at least $60 billion in the offering at the low end, with a target valuation that could push past $1 trillion. At those numbers, the IPO would dwarf every previous technology listing, surpassing the records set by Alibaba's $25 billion debut in 2014 and Arm Holdings' $5.2 billion offering in 2023. CEO Sam Altman has been pushing internally for a September listing date, though the timeline remains subject to market conditions and regulatory review.

A Revenue Machine With Questions Attached

OpenAI's financial trajectory provides the foundation for its sky-high ambitions. The company hit $25 billion in annualized revenue as of February 2026, up from $20 billion at the end of 2025, driven by explosive growth in ChatGPT subscriptions, API usage, and a nascent enterprise business. That translates to roughly $2 billion or more in monthly run-rate revenue -- a figure that would have been unimaginable for a company that generated virtually no commercial income just three years ago.

Yet the numbers come with asterisks. OpenAI has reportedly missed some internal revenue and user growth targets, and its cost structure remains punishing. The compute infrastructure required to train and serve frontier AI models consumes billions of dollars annually, and the company has yet to demonstrate sustained profitability. At its current private valuation of $852 billion, OpenAI trades at roughly 35 times annualized revenue -- a multiple that dwarfs comparable public companies, which typically trade between 9 and 14 times revenue. Public market investors will demand a clearer path to margins than private backers have required.

The $122 billion funding round that closed in March 2026 -- anchored by Amazon at $50 billion, with Nvidia and SoftBank each contributing $30 billion -- gave OpenAI a war chest and the $852 billion valuation that serves as the IPO's floor. That round also completed the company's long-contested conversion from its original nonprofit structure to a public benefit corporation, removing a legal obstacle that had clouded its path to public markets for years.

Two Giants, One Week

The timing of OpenAI's filing is striking. On May 20, SpaceX publicly released its own S-1 prospectus with the SEC, disclosing audited financials for its combined SpaceX-xAI entity for the first time. SpaceX is targeting a valuation of $1.75 trillion and a raise of up to $75 billion, which would make it the largest IPO in history.

The parallel filings create an unprecedented situation in equity capital markets. Both companies share core underwriters in Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, and both are expected to list on the same exchange. Investment bankers cannot recall a comparable scenario in which the same banks simultaneously shepherded two offerings of this magnitude. The dual listings will test whether public markets have the appetite to absorb potentially $135 billion or more in new equity from two private mega-companies in the same quarter.

A Cleared Legal Path

A critical factor in OpenAI's accelerated timeline was the resolution of Elon Musk's lawsuit against the company. A jury ruled that Musk's claims were time-barred, effectively removing the legal cloud that had threatened OpenAI's corporate structure, leadership, and finances. With Musk's challenge neutralized and the nonprofit-to-PBC conversion finalized, the last major structural barriers to a public listing have fallen away.

What This Means for the AI Industry

OpenAI's IPO, if it proceeds on schedule, will serve as the definitive market test for whether the artificial intelligence boom can sustain valuations that have been set exclusively by private capital. For three years, the AI sector's biggest companies have raised money at escalating valuations in closed rounds where a handful of sovereign wealth funds, Big Tech incumbents, and venture firms set the price. A public offering forces a fundamentally different reckoning -- one where quarterly earnings calls, short sellers, and retail investors all get a vote.

The listing will also establish a public benchmark against which every other AI company will be measured. Anthropic, xAI, and Databricks are all reportedly exploring their own paths to public markets. If OpenAI prices successfully above $1 trillion, it validates the thesis that generative AI companies deserve valuations on par with the largest technology platforms ever built. If the stock stumbles, it could trigger a reassessment across the entire sector.

For OpenAI specifically, the transition to public company life will impose new disciplines. Quarterly financial disclosure, executive compensation transparency, and the relentless pressure of public market expectations will constrain a company that has operated with the speed and secrecy of a startup. Sam Altman will face a scrutiny as a public company CEO that is categorically different from anything he has experienced in private markets. Whether OpenAI can maintain its breakneck pace of research and product development under that spotlight is the open question that $1 trillion will be riding on.
