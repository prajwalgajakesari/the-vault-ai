Mercury Raises $200 Million at $5.2 Billion Valuation Amid AI-Driven Startup Banking Boom

4 min read 935 words 3 sources
Key Takeaway

Mercury $200M Series D at $5.2B valuation reflects how AI-driven startup formation is creating second-order winners in fintech infrastructure.

Mercury, the fintech company that has quietly become the default banking platform for a generation of technology startups, has closed a $200 million Series D round at a $5.2 billion valuation -- a 49 percent jump from the $3.5 billion it commanded just 14 months ago. The raise arrives at a moment when artificial intelligence is accelerating new business formation at a pace not seen in decades, and Mercury is positioned squarely at the intake valve.

The Deal

The round was led by TCV, with returning investors Andreessen Horowitz, Coatue, CRV, Sapphire Ventures, Sequoia Capital, and Spark Capital all participating. The deal brings Mercury's total primary and secondary funding to approximately $765 million, according to PitchBook data. Mercury's previous raise was a $300 million Series C led by Sequoia Capital in March 2025.

Mercury co-founder and CEO Immad Akhund told Inc. that the company does not plan another raise. "We've been profitable for four years, so we don't have a plan to do another raise," he said. "We did this kind of opportunistically. We had a really strong 2025, where we grew. We were in a good position to do a raise."

The financials justify the confidence. Mercury has reached $650 million in annualized revenue as of Q3 2025 and has maintained four consecutive years of GAAP net income and EBITDA profitability -- a rarity among fintechs at this scale. The company now serves more than 300,000 customers, including one in three early-stage U.S. startups. Its client roster reads like a directory of the most consequential AI-era companies being built today: Supabase, ElevenLabs, Lovable, Linear, Phantom, and Tempo.

The AI Startup Surge

The timing of this raise is not incidental. Generative AI has collapsed the friction between an idea and a company, and Mercury -- which opens accounts for businesses at their earliest stage -- is a direct beneficiary of that shift. The company reported a 2.5x increase in applications in Q1 2026 compared to Q1 2025, a figure that outpaces the 18 percent year-over-year increase in new U.S. business applications reported by the U.S. Census Bureau over the same period.

"We are going to see more founders in the next five years than in the last twenty," Akhund wrote in a blog post announcing the round. "Mercury was created for this moment."

Neil Tolaney, general partner at TCV, framed the investment in similar terms. "We believe the next generation of entrepreneurs will be AI-native and will need a banking partner that helps them run their finances and build at the pace AI itself is setting," he said. "We see Mercury as that partner."

The data supports this thesis. More than 73 percent of Mercury's new customers now come from outside the AI and tech startup category, spanning ecommerce, professional services, and individual users -- evidence that the platform's appeal is broadening well beyond its Silicon Valley roots.

The Bank Charter Play

The fundraise comes weeks after Mercury received conditional approval from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency to establish Mercury Bank, N.A. -- a de novo national bank charter that represents perhaps the most strategically significant development in the company's history. Mercury applied for the charter in December 2025 and received conditional approval in April 2026.

The charter, once finalized, will allow Mercury to incorporate Zelle into its user accounts, expand lending products for both businesses and individuals, and build its own payments infrastructure -- reducing its dependence on partner banks Choice Financial Group and Column N.A. The bank's main office will be located in Salt Lake City, with former SoFi Bank executive Jon Auxier slated to serve as CEO and president of Mercury Bank.

PitchBook fintech analyst Rudy Yang told American Banker that the charter adds meaningful momentum. "Getting a bank charter only adds to their momentum, unlocking unit economics that weren't accessible before," Yang said.

Mercury must still obtain final authorization from the OCC, as well as pending approvals from the FDIC and the Federal Reserve, before Mercury Bank can open its doors. But conditional approval is widely viewed as the most significant regulatory hurdle in the de novo charter process.

Building the AI-Native Banking Stack

Mercury is not content to be a passive repository for startup deposits. Over the past year, the company has shipped a series of AI-native financial tools designed to make the banking relationship an active part of how businesses operate. Mercury Insights, the company's first in-product AI tool, provides real-time financial health analysis without spreadsheet exports. The company launched MCP and CLI developer tools for secure programmatic account access. And its acquisition of Central, an AI-native payroll startup, will bring payroll directly into the Mercury platform.

Later this year, Mercury plans to introduce Mercury Command, a natural-language interface that will allow customers to manage financial tasks end-to-end -- checking cash positions, categorizing transactions, sending invoices -- without leaving their account.

What It Means

Mercury's raise is a case study in how AI-driven macroeconomic shifts create second-order winners. The company is not building foundation models or selling GPU clusters. It is building the financial infrastructure layer that every new AI company needs on day one. In a world where company formation is accelerating, that position is enormously valuable.

The combination of sustained profitability, a broadening customer base, a federal bank charter in progress, and an AI product roadmap gives Mercury a multi-vector growth story that few fintechs can match. Whether the company can execute on all of these fronts simultaneously -- scaling banking infrastructure, navigating federal regulation, and shipping AI-native tools -- will determine whether this $5.2 billion valuation looks conservative or ambitious in hindsight.

For now, the market has rendered its verdict: in the age of AI-native startups, the company that banks the builders has become a builder worth betting on.
