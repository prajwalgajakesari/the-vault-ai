Trump Postpones AI Executive Order Hours Before Signing Over Fears It Could Block US Lead

4 min read 936 words 3 sources
Key Takeaway

Trump canceled a landmark AI executive order that would have created a voluntary 90-day federal review for frontier models, citing fears it could undermine US competitive lead.

President Donald Trump abruptly pulled the plug on a landmark artificial intelligence executive order on Wednesday, canceling a planned Oval Office signing ceremony just hours before it was set to take place. The move threw months of behind-the-scenes negotiations between the White House, federal agencies, and leading AI companies into sudden uncertainty — and laid bare the deepening tension at the heart of Washington's approach to the most consequential technology of the decade.

"I didn't like certain aspects of it," Trump told reporters from the Oval Office during an unrelated event. "I really thought it could have been a blocker."

The president made clear that his chief concern was competitive: the United States' lead over China in the global AI race. "We're leading China, we're leading everybody, and I don't want to do anything that's going to get in the way of that lead," he said.

What the Order Would Have Done

The draft executive order, which had been circulating among administration officials and industry stakeholders for weeks, was split into two major sections. The first would have established a voluntary 90-day pre-launch review process for frontier AI models — the most powerful systems produced by companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind. Under this framework, federal agencies would have been granted access to test unreleased models for cybersecurity vulnerabilities and national security risks before they reached the public.

The second section focused on cybersecurity, directing agencies to strengthen defenses across critical infrastructure — from power grids to rural hospitals — against AI-powered cyberattacks.

The National Security Agency was positioned to play a central role, empowered to conduct classified evaluations of frontier models. The Treasury Department would have established a new information-sharing agreement between AI developers and cybersecurity defenders in sectors like finance and healthcare. Meanwhile, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, the Office of the National Cyber Director, and the National Institute of Standards and Technology would have jointly defined which models fell under the new testing regime.

Critically, the entire framework was voluntary. American technology executives and administration officials have repeatedly pointed to more prescriptive approaches — particularly the European Union's AI Act — as cautionary examples of regulation that could throttle innovation.

A Sharp Reversal

The abrupt postponement marks a striking pivot for an administration that entered office openly hostile to AI safety regulation. On his first day, Trump repealed President Biden's sweeping October 2023 AI executive order, which had required leading companies to share internal safety test results and development details with the government. Vice President JD Vance, speaking at the Paris AI Summit earlier this year, cast the previous administration's approach as an innovation killer and championed a deregulatory posture.

Yet the threat landscape shifted dramatically beneath that rhetoric. In recent months, Anthropic's Mythos Preview model demonstrated an unprecedented ability to autonomously discover thousands of severe cybersecurity vulnerabilities across major operating systems and browsers. The revelation forced a recalibration. Vance himself acknowledged the shift in a press conference the day before the planned signing, saying the administration was working "in a collaborative way with the technology companies" to ensure "the American people are as safe as possible."

A former federal official who reviewed the draft order told CyberScoop that it reflected the government's maturing understanding of AI. Previous evaluations involved significant hand-holding from AI companies, the official said, but this order signaled a new phase: federal agencies believed they were now prepared to independently assess frontier models on their own terms.

Still, the official acknowledged that the administration's early anti-guardrails rhetoric had painted it into a corner. "Their tone was 'no institution of guardrails' and they don't have a better word for making sure that the capabilities of emergent frontier models don't disrupt security than to say 'let's test it and institute guardrails,'" the official said.

The Innovation-Safety Tightrope

Trump's last-minute reversal crystallizes the central dilemma confronting AI policymakers worldwide. The technology's dual-use nature — capable of both extraordinary economic value and profound security risks — makes any regulatory framework a high-wire act. Lean too far toward oversight and you risk ceding the competitive advantage to rivals unburdened by such constraints. Lean too far toward permissiveness and you leave critical systems exposed to threats that are already materializing.

The stakes are not theoretical. AI is already being deployed in targeted military operations by the United States, Israel, Russia, and Ukraine. It is supercharging global hacking campaigns and powering sophisticated surveillance systems. Ram Shankar Siva Kumar, who founded Microsoft's AI red team in 2019 with a small handful of specialists, now leads a much larger staff that includes experts in psychology, linguistics, and bioweapons. "Because of frontier harms, what we have done has really morphed," he told CyberScoop.

On Capitol Hill, lawmakers who once opposed autonomous kill decisions are reconsidering. Representative Don Beyer, a Virginia Democrat who co-chaired the Congressional AI Caucus, captured the logic driving the shift: if the United States insists on a human in the loop while adversaries do not, the advantage goes to the machine.

What Comes Next

The executive order is not dead — it is delayed. Administration officials have signaled that revisions are underway, though no new timeline has been set. The episode underscores a fundamental reality: even an administration ideologically committed to deregulation has found that the sheer velocity of AI advancement makes some form of government oversight unavoidable. The question is no longer whether Washington will act, but whether it can act fast enough — and whether the framework it eventually produces will be robust enough to matter without heavy enough to slow the very industry it aims to protect.
