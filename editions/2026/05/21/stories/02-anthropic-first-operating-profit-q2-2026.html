Anthropic Projects First Operating Profit as Q2 Revenue Hits $10.9 Billion

4 min read 835 words 3 sources
Key Takeaway

Anthropic expects its first-ever operating profit of $559 million in Q2 2026 on $10.9 billion in revenue, though the figure excludes stock-based compensation.

Anthropic, the safety-focused AI company behind the Claude model family, told investors this week that it expects to post its first-ever operating profit in the second quarter of 2026, projecting $559 million in operating income on revenue of approximately $10.9 billion. The milestone, first reported by The Wall Street Journal and confirmed by CNBC, represents a dramatic acceleration for a company that just last summer warned investors it would not turn a full-year profit until at least 2028.

The projected Q2 revenue figure marks a staggering 130 percent sequential increase from the $4.8 billion Anthropic recorded in Q1. To put the growth in context, the single quarter would exceed the company\u2019s total revenue for all of 2025. It is the kind of trajectory that outpaces the early growth curves of Google, Facebook, and Zoom at comparable stages of their corporate lives, according to PYMNTS analysis.

CEO Dario Amodei acknowledged the breakneck pace at a developer conference earlier this month. \u201cWe tried to plan very well for a world of 10x growth per year. And yet we saw 80x,\u201d Amodei said. \u201cAnd so that is the reason we have had difficulties with compute.\u201d At the same event, he described the revenue growth as having become \u201ctoo hard to handle.\u201d

The Coding Engine Behind the Surge

The primary driver of the revenue explosion is enterprise adoption of Claude Code, Anthropic\u2019s coding assistant. Companies across industries are integrating Claude into internal workflows at scale. Reuters reported that Bristol Myers Squibb is deploying Claude to more than 30,000 employees for drug discovery work. Meanwhile, spending data from corporate card platform Ramp shows Anthropic has overtaken OpenAI among verified business customers, a telling indicator of where enterprise dollars are flowing.

The enterprise tilt also helps explain Anthropic\u2019s cost structure. Unlike OpenAI, which subsidizes a massive base of free ChatGPT users, Anthropic carries fewer non-paying consumer accounts. That difference reduces the computational subsidy burden and allows a higher share of compute spending to be tied directly to revenue-generating workloads.

Compute Economics: From 71 Cents to 56 Cents

One of the most closely watched numbers in Anthropic\u2019s investor materials is the compute-to-revenue ratio. In Q1, the company spent 71 cents on compute for every dollar of revenue it earned. For Q2, that ratio is expected to fall to 56 cents, a meaningful improvement that reflects both growing revenue scale and efficiency gains in how Anthropic deploys its infrastructure.

Anthropic relies primarily on chips from Google and Amazon rather than Nvidia, and has made more conservative data center spending commitments than OpenAI. The Wall Street Journal reported that this approach has helped contain costs. The company recently struck a deal with Amazon for up to $25 billion in investment and 5 gigawatts of compute capacity, while Google plans to invest up to $40 billion with an additional 5 gigawatts of capacity from Google and Broadcom beginning next year.

The company also expanded its infrastructure through a partnership with SpaceX, securing a 300-megawatt compute footprint that enabled it to double Claude Code\u2019s rate limits for paid plans and raise API rate limits for Claude Opus models.

The Fine Print on Profitability

Investors and analysts should note a critical caveat: Anthropic\u2019s projected operating profit excludes stock-based compensation, a significant expense category for any pre-IPO technology company staffing up with top AI talent. The figure does include model training costs, but stripping out equity-based pay means the headline number overstates profitability by conventional accounting standards. On a fully loaded basis, the bottom line would look materially different.

Anthropic itself has cautioned that it may not sustain profitability for the full year. Planned increases in compute spending through the second half of 2026 could push the company back into the red, particularly as the massive infrastructure commitments from Amazon, Google, and SpaceX begin to translate into operational costs.

What to Watch Next

The financial projections were shared with investors as part of a funding round that could push Anthropic\u2019s valuation above $900 billion, surpassing OpenAI\u2019s $852 billion valuation from March. TechCrunch reported that Anthropic asked investors to submit allocations within 48 hours for what is expected to be roughly $50 billion in new capital, and the round may be the company\u2019s last private raise before a potential IPO as early as October.

The timing is charged. Reports emerged the same day that OpenAI could file its own IPO paperwork as soon as this week, setting up a potential public-market showdown between the two leading AI rivals. For Anthropic, the path forward depends on whether enterprise demand can continue to outrun infrastructure costs, whether the compute-to-revenue ratio keeps improving, and whether a single profitable quarter can become a durable trend rather than a one-time milestone. The next few months will determine if this is a turning point or a peak.
