# Recursive Superintelligence Exits Stealth With $650 Million to Build Self-Improving AI

A London-born startup led by one of AI's most cited researchers has emerged from stealth with $650 million in funding and a plan that reads like science fiction: build an AI system that can improve itself, indefinitely, without human intervention.

Recursive Superintelligence, co-founded by former Salesforce Chief Scientist Richard Socher and former Google DeepMind researcher Tim Rocktaeschel, announced the raise on May 13, valuing the company at $4.65 billion before it has shipped a single product. The round was led by GV (formerly Google Ventures) and Greycroft, with strategic participation from Nvidia and AMD Ventures -- a signal that the chip giants see autonomous AI research as a category worth backing early.

The company, which was founded in 2025 and currently employs roughly 25 people across San Francisco and London, is pursuing what researchers call recursive self-improvement: an AI system that can identify its own weaknesses, devise fixes, test the results, and repeat the cycle without a human in the loop. It is a concept long discussed in theoretical AI circles but never achieved in practice.

## The Technical Bet: Open-Endedness Over Scale

Where most frontier AI labs are pouring resources into building ever-larger language models, Recursive is taking a different path. The company's core technical approach centers on a concept called open-endedness -- systems that continuously generate new challenges and adaptations rather than optimizing toward a single fixed objective.

Rocktaeschel, a professor of artificial intelligence at University College London, led Google DeepMind's open-endedness and self-improvement teams before co-founding the company. His prior work includes rainbow teaming, a technique in which two AI systems co-evolve by attacking and defending against each other across millions of iterations, producing safety improvements that would take human testers far longer to discover. The method is now used across major AI labs.

"Our main focus is to build truly recursive, self-improving superintelligence at scale, which means that the entire process of ideation, implementation, and validation of research ideas would be automatic," Socher told TechCrunch. He drew a sharp distinction between what his company is building and existing forms of AI-assisted coding or research. "That's not recursive self-improvement. That's just improvement."

Socher also framed the company's long-term ambitions in sweeping terms. "We will start with AI research itself but eventually hope to expand its aperture to physics, chemistry and especially pre-clinical biology," he wrote on X. "AI will be to biology what calculus was to physics -- a new language and way of thinking that deals with complex systems."

## A Crowded Race With Existential Stakes

Recursive enters an increasingly competitive landscape of well-funded startups chasing variations on the same goal. Safe Superintelligence, co-founded by former OpenAI chief scientist Ilya Sutskever, is pursuing safety-first superintelligence research. Ineffable Intelligence, led by AlphaGo creator David Silver, raised $1.1 billion to build what it calls an AI superlearner using reinforcement learning. AMI Labs is exploring world models. Each company represents a different theoretical bet on how superhuman AI might be achieved.

What sets Recursive apart, according to its founders, is its commitment to automating the entire AI development pipeline itself -- not just the model, but the training, evaluation, and infrastructure surrounding it. The company's AI will search for improvements to its own code, its harness (the auxiliary programs used to enhance output), and its training and inference infrastructure.

The founding team's pedigree is a major draw for investors. Beyond Socher and Rocktaeschel, the roster includes Jeff Clune (formerly Uber AI and a pioneer in evolutionary computation), Josh Tobin (an early OpenAI engineer who led its Codex and deep research teams), and Tim Shi (co-founder of Cresta, which reached unicorn status). Peter Norvig, co-author of the definitive AI textbook and former Google research director, has also joined the venture.

## Analysis: The Compute Question

The size of the round is remarkable for a pre-product company with fewer than 30 employees, but it reflects a broader venture capital thesis: that elite AI research talent is itself a bankable asset class. Investors are less interested in near-term revenue than in positioning for a potential breakthrough that could reshape the entire technology industry.

If recursive self-improvement proves achievable, compute becomes the bottleneck -- and the accelerant. Socher acknowledged this directly. "In the future, a really important question will be: How much compute does humanity want to spend to solve which problems?" he told TechCrunch. "It becomes a matter of resource allocation eventually. It's going to be one of the biggest questions in the world."

That framing connects the startup's mission to some of the most consequential debates in technology today -- the geopolitics of semiconductor supply chains, the energy demands of AI infrastructure, and the question of who gets to decide what problems superintelligent AI works on first.

## What Comes Next

Recursive plans to use the funding to secure large-scale compute infrastructure and run its first autonomous training system, with a public launch targeted for mid-2026. Socher has pushed back against the neolab label, insisting the company will ship commercial products. "You'll have to wait quarters, not years," he said.

Whether the company can deliver on a promise that has eluded the field for decades remains an open question. But with $650 million, backing from two of the world's largest chip companies, and a team that reads like an AI research all-star roster, Recursive Superintelligence has bought itself a serious chance to find out.
