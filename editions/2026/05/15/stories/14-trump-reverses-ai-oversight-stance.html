# Trump Administration Quietly Embraces AI Oversight Ideas It Once Rejected

The same White House that dismantled Biden-era AI safeguards is now proposing an FDA-style approval process for frontier models — and a single AI system is the reason why.

---

Four months ago, the Trump administration was riding a deregulatory wave. The president had revoked his predecessor's executive order on AI safety, rechristened the AI Safety Institute as the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), and sent an unmistakable signal to Silicon Valley: build fast, we will stay out of your way.

That era is over.

In a policy reversal that has stunned technologists and lawmakers alike, the White House is now actively studying an executive order that would require pre-release government vetting of powerful AI models — a framework National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett has explicitly compared to the Food and Drug Administration's drug approval process. The catalyst for this about-face has a name: Mythos.

The Model That Changed Everything

Anthropic's Mythos, the frontier model the San Francisco startup has refused to release publicly, demonstrated something the national security establishment could not ignore. During internal testing, the system discovered and exploited zero-day vulnerabilities in every major operating system — weaknesses that had gone undetected for decades. The implications were immediate and chilling: if a model like Mythos could find these flaws, so could the next one, and the next one might not have a responsible developer at the helm.

"There should be regulations on AI," President Trump told reporters in early May, a sentence that would have been unthinkable from this White House just weeks earlier. The administration is now proposing that "future AIs that could potentially create vulnerabilities should go through a process so that they're released to the wild after they've been proven safe — just like an FDA drug."

The statement sent shockwaves through the tech industry, where executives had been counting on a permissive regulatory environment to maintain their competitive edge against Chinese rivals.

A Government Scrambling to Catch Up

The infrastructure for this new oversight regime is already being assembled. CAISI announced agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and Elon Musk's xAI to evaluate frontier AI models before public release, adding to existing arrangements with Anthropic and OpenAI. The center has conducted 40 evaluations to date, including assessments of models that have not yet reached the market.

But the ambition far outpaces the resources. The America First Policy Institute has described CAISI as "chronically underfunded," noting that its roughly 30 staff members have operated on just $30 million since the center was established in 2024 — less than comparable AI safety centers in Canada and Singapore. Congress approved up to $10 million in additional funding for CAISI in January 2026, along with $55 million for broader NIST AI research, but experts warn the sums remain inadequate for the scope of the task.

Chris Fall, who was tapped to lead CAISI at the end of April, is the agency's third leader in as many months. His predecessor, Collin Burns — a former member of Anthropic's technical staff — was dismissed after just days on the job, underscoring the political turbulence surrounding the role.

The Political Tightrope

The reversal has exposed fault lines within the administration's own coalition. David Sacks, who spearheaded the original deregulatory push as the White House's AI czar, departed the role in March. Since then, Chief of Staff Susie Wiles and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent have taken a more hands-on approach to AI policy, including a meeting with Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei that both sides described as "productive."

That meeting itself marked a dramatic thaw. Anthropic had been labeled a national security threat by the administration after refusing to grant the Pentagon unrestricted access to its technology — a designation the company is currently challenging in court. The fact that Wiles and Bessent sat down with Amodei suggests the White House recognizes it cannot govern AI without the cooperation of the companies building it.

Industry reaction has been sharply divided. Some executives argue that an FDA-style approval process would cripple American competitiveness and hand the advantage to China, where state-backed labs face no such constraints. Others quietly welcome the clarity that formal regulation would provide, having spent years navigating a patchwork of voluntary commitments and informal guidance.

What Comes Next

The contours of a potential executive order remain unclear, but the direction is unmistakable. The administration that arrived in office promising to unleash American AI innovation is now grappling with the reality that the technology it championed has outpaced every existing governance framework.

The question is no longer whether Washington will regulate frontier AI, but whether it can build the institutional capacity to do so competently. With 30 staff members, $30 million in cumulative funding, and a leadership carousel at CAISI, the gap between ambition and execution remains vast.

For an administration that built its tech policy on the promise of minimal interference, the pivot carries political risk. But as one senior official told reporters, the Mythos revelations left little choice: when an AI system can crack open every major operating system on the planet, "pro-innovation" and "pro-security" are no longer separable positions.
