# Gallup Finds 71 Percent of Americans Oppose AI Data Centers in Their Communities

Americans would rather live next to a nuclear reactor than an AI data center. That is the unmistakable conclusion of a Gallup poll released this week that found 71 percent of U.S. adults oppose the construction of data centers in their local communities, a level of resistance that exceeds public opposition to nuclear power plants and threatens to complicate the technology industry's aggressive infrastructure expansion plans.

The survey, conducted March 2 through 18 with a nationally representative sample of 1,000 adults, marks the first time Gallup has measured public sentiment on data center construction. The results paint a bleak picture for an industry that has positioned massive new facilities as the backbone of the artificial intelligence revolution. Just 27 percent of respondents said they favor a data center being built in their area, with a mere 7 percent expressing strong support. On the opposing side, 48 percent said they strongly oppose such construction, a degree of intensity that pollsters note is unusual for infrastructure questions.

The comparison to nuclear energy is especially striking. Only 53 percent of Americans oppose having a nuclear power plant built near them, an 18-percentage-point gap that underscores just how toxic the data center brand has become in the public imagination. Nuclear facilities carry decades of cultural baggage from Three Mile Island to Fukushima, yet they now poll as more acceptable neighbors than the server farms powering ChatGPT and Google Search.

## Resource Anxiety Drives the Backlash

The reasons behind the opposition reveal a public increasingly aware of the physical costs of the digital economy. Among those who oppose local data center construction, half cited concerns about excessive resource consumption. Water and energy topped the list at 18 percent each, reflecting growing media coverage of the staggering volumes of water required to cool servers and the strain these facilities place on electrical grids already struggling to meet demand.

"These results arrive at a moment when the federal government and technology companies are pushing hard to accelerate data center construction," noted Jeffrey M. Jones, the Gallup researcher who authored the analysis. The tension between Washington's AI ambitions and grassroots resistance could define infrastructure politics for years to come.

Beyond resource strain, 22 percent of opponents flagged quality-of-life concerns, including impacts on property values, health risks, increased traffic, and the sheer physical footprint of facilities that can span hundreds of acres. Another 20 percent worried about rising living costs, while 16 percent pointed to pollution, encompassing noise, air quality, water contamination, and light pollution from facilities that operate around the clock. Fourteen percent cited broader economic harms, and an equal share expressed general negative views about artificial intelligence itself.

## Environmental Worry Runs Deep

The environmental dimension of the opposition extends well beyond those who would directly live near a facility. Gallup found that 46 percent of all respondents said they worry "a great deal" about the environmental impact of AI data centers, with another 24 percent saying they worry "a fair amount." Taken together, 70 percent of Americans express at least moderate environmental concern about data centers, a figure that nearly mirrors the 71 percent local opposition rate and suggests the resistance is rooted in substantive policy anxiety rather than simple NIMBYism.

The poll lands amid a wave of local battles across the country. Communities in Virginia, Georgia, Texas, and the Midwest have pushed back against proposed data center campuses, citing many of the same concerns captured in the Gallup data. In some cases, county boards have imposed moratoriums on new construction, and state legislators have begun reconsidering the generous tax incentives that lured technology companies in the first place.

## The Industry's Narrow Path Forward

Supporters of data center construction, though outnumbered, do have an argument. Among the 27 percent who favor local development, 55 percent pointed to job creation, and two-thirds cited broader economic benefits. These are the same selling points that technology companies and their lobbyists emphasize in zoning hearings and legislative testimony, but the Gallup numbers suggest those appeals are persuading barely a quarter of the public.

"Data centers are becoming the defining NIMBY battle of the AI era," said Daniel Cooper, a land-use attorney who advises technology firms on siting disputes. "Companies used to worry about permits and power contracts. Now they have to worry about whether the community will let them build at all."

The findings present a genuine strategic dilemma for the technology industry and for policymakers who view domestic AI infrastructure as a national security priority. The Biden and now Trump administrations have both emphasized the importance of keeping AI compute capacity on American soil, and major companies including Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have announced tens of billions of dollars in planned data center spending. But those plans depend on local approval processes that, this poll suggests, face headwinds from a skeptical supermajority.

The Gallup survey carries a margin of error of plus or minus 4 percentage points at the 95 percent confidence level. Even at the most generous end of that margin, opposition would still stand at 67 percent, more than double the level of support. For an industry accustomed to building its way to dominance, the numbers represent an obstacle that cannot be engineered around. It will have to be negotiated, community by community, in the town halls and zoning boards where American infrastructure battles have always been fought.
