# Musk v OpenAI Trial Closes as Jury Prepares to Weigh Landmark AI Verdict

After three weeks of testimony that paraded Silicon Valley's most powerful figures through a federal courtroom in Oakland, California, the blockbuster trial pitting Elon Musk against OpenAI has reached its climax. Closing arguments concluded on May 14, and a nine-person jury will begin deliberating on Monday in a case that could reshape the structure of the world's most valuable artificial intelligence company -- and set precedent for how AI organizations govern themselves.

The stakes are staggering. Musk is seeking up to $134 billion in damages from OpenAI and Microsoft, removal of CEO Sam Altman and President Greg Brockman, and a reversal of the 2025 restructuring that converted OpenAI's for-profit subsidiary into a public benefit corporation. OpenAI, once a scrappy nonprofit Musk helped co-found in 2015 with $38 million in donations, is now valued at approximately $850 billion and racing toward an IPO that could push it near the $1 trillion mark.

## Two narratives, one courtroom

The closing arguments crystallized two irreconcilable versions of events. Musk's lead attorney, Steven Molo, painted Altman and Brockman as promise-breakers who betrayed a charitable mission to enrich themselves. He pointed to testimony from former OpenAI executives Ilya Sutskever and Mira Murati, as well as ex-board members Helen Toner and Tasha McCauley, all of whom said Altman had lied to them.

Molo drove the point home with a vivid analogy: "Imagine that you're on a hike, and you come upon one of those wooden bridges that you see on a trail, and it's over a gorge. A woman standing by the entry to the bridge says, 'Don't worry -- the bridge is built on Sam Altman's version of the truth.' Would you walk across that bridge?"

OpenAI's attorney Sarah Eddy countered that no promises were ever made to Musk about maintaining a nonprofit structure and that his donations carried no strings. She argued that Musk "never cared about the nonprofit structure" and that "what he cared about was winning." Eddy noted that Musk himself explored creating a for-profit subsidiary in 2017, undermining his claim as defender of the nonprofit mission.

Eddy also attacked Musk's motives, framing the lawsuit as a strategic play to hobble a rival. Musk launched his own AI company, xAI, in 2023, which is reportedly planning to go public as part of SpaceX at a target valuation of $1.75 trillion. Meanwhile, Musk was notably absent from the final day of his own trial -- he had flown to China with President Trump despite the judge's order that he remain available.

## The nonprofit question

At the heart of the case lies a deceptively simple question: did OpenAI abandon its founding mission? The organization was established as a nonprofit research lab dedicated to developing artificial general intelligence safely and for the benefit of humanity. In 2019, it created a capped-profit subsidiary. In 2025, it restructured further into a public benefit corporation, granting Microsoft a 27 percent stake.

OpenAI argues the nonprofit still controls the for-profit entity and remains committed to its mission. But legal scholars are skeptical. "We're left with this nonprofit that doesn't have any voice," Jill Horwitz, a law professor at Northwestern University who studies nonprofits, told MIT Technology Review. "It barely has a staff. It's unclear how on earth the nonprofit is supposed to exercise its duties and control the entire company."

Seven of the nonprofit's eight board members also sit on the for-profit board, and the nonprofit hired employees only a month before the trial began. Altman himself acknowledged in a video shown to the jury that the nonprofit board's failure to permanently remove him in 2023 was "its own kind of governance failure."

## An advisory verdict with binding consequences

The legal mechanics of this trial add another layer of complexity. The jury's verdict will be advisory only -- the final decision on liability rests with US District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers. A separate remedies phase, to be argued before the judge alone starting Monday, will determine potential damages and structural changes if OpenAI is found liable.

The trial featured testimony from an extraordinary roster of tech leaders, including Musk, Altman, OpenAI board chair Bret Taylor, and Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella. It also produced its share of courtroom drama: OpenAI's lawyers presented a golden trophy of a donkey's rear end, originally given to an employee whom Musk had called a "jackass" for prioritizing safety over speed in AGI development.

## What comes next

A verdict could arrive as soon as next week, though complex deliberations may take longer. The implications extend far beyond the two parties. If Judge Gonzalez Rogers sides with Musk, it could unwind OpenAI's corporate restructuring, threaten its IPO timeline, and establish new legal boundaries around nonprofit-to-profit conversions in the tech sector. If OpenAI prevails, it would validate the increasingly common practice of AI labs evolving from mission-driven nonprofits into commercial juggernauts.

The US House oversight committee has also launched a separate investigation into Altman's potential conflicts of interest, including personal investments in startups that do business with OpenAI, adding political pressure regardless of the trial's outcome.

Either way, the trial has already laid bare the tensions at the heart of the AI industry -- between safety and speed, mission and money, and the question of who ultimately controls the technology that may define the century. As Horwitz put it: "The public interest in the nonprofit loses, no matter who wins or loses this trial."

*The jury begins deliberations Monday, May 19. The Vault will provide continuing coverage as the verdict approaches.*
