# NVIDIA Loses $170 Billion in Value as China Rejects H200 AI Chips

Less than 24 hours after the U.S. Commerce Department cleared roughly 10 Chinese firms to purchase NVIDIA's H200 AI processors, Beijing delivered a stunning rebuke: it told those companies not to buy them. The reversal erased approximately $170 billion from NVIDIA's market capitalization on May 15, as shares plunged as much as 4.6% intraday, turning what had been a record-setting rally into one of the chipmaker's sharpest single-day retreats of 2026.

A Deal Undone Before It Began

The whiplash played out over just two trading sessions. On May 14, during President Trump's summit with Chinese President Xi Jinping in Beijing, word emerged that the Commerce Department had licensed a group of China's biggest technology companies — including Alibaba, Tencent, and ByteDance — to purchase up to 75,000 H200 units each. NVIDIA stock surged more than 4% on the news, touching a fresh all-time high as investors anticipated a reopening of the company's most contested market.

By the morning of May 15, that optimism had evaporated. Speaking to reporters aboard Air Force One as he departed China, Trump confirmed that no sales had materialized. "They've chosen not to do that because they want to try and develop their own," Trump told the Wall Street Journal, adding that Chinese authorities had effectively blocked the purchases.

U.S. Trade Representative Jamieson Greer reinforced the message, stating that chip export controls had not been a primary topic during the bilateral talks. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick went further at a Senate hearing, conceding that Beijing is actively directing investment toward its own domestic semiconductor industry — a tacit acknowledgment that the H200 approval may have been more of a diplomatic gesture than a commercial breakthrough.

The Scale of the Collapse in China

The numbers tell a stark story of how far NVIDIA's position in China has deteriorated. At its peak, NVIDIA commanded roughly 95% of China's advanced AI chip market — a near-monopoly built on the dominance of its A100 and H100 processors. When tightened U.S. export controls cut off access to those higher-end chips, NVIDIA designed the H200 specifically as a compliant alternative that could still serve Chinese customers.

But the strategy has collided with Beijing's accelerating push for technological self-sufficiency. According to analysts cited by TradingKey, NVIDIA's share of the Chinese AI chip market has cratered from that 95% peak to approximately 8% in early 2026, while domestic Chinese chips crossed 55% market share for the first time in the first quarter of this year. NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang himself acknowledged in April that the company's China market share had effectively fallen to zero for its highest-performance products.

Chinese chipmakers, led by Huawei and aided by firms like DeepSeek, have moved aggressively to fill the gap. While Huawei's current roadmap does not project a chip matching the H200's performance until late 2027, Beijing appears willing to accept the performance trade-off in exchange for supply-chain independence. Government directives have quietly instructed domestic technology firms to pause H200 orders and prioritize locally manufactured alternatives.

Broader Market Contagion

NVIDIA's selloff did not stay contained. The broader semiconductor sector buckled, with Nasdaq 100 futures falling 1.78% and S&P 500 futures dropping 1.24% during the pre-market session. Intel shed more than 5%, AMD fell over 4%, and Micron Technology declined 4.5%. TSMC, Qualcomm, and Tesla all dropped between 2% and 3%.

The rout was compounded by a re-escalation of U.S.-Iran tensions on the same day, triggering a synchronized global selloff that hit cryptocurrencies, precious metals, and Asia-Pacific index futures. Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Solana each fell more than 3%, spot gold dropped over 3%, and silver plunged more than 9% intraday.

What Comes Next

Some analysts argue that the door is not fully closed. Rich Smith, writing for The Motley Fool, noted that when the history of Chinese companies smuggling servers to access NVIDIA chips is considered, the underlying demand is clearly real. "The real question is whether China will allow its companies to slake their thirst for Nvidia chips, or insist they buy domestic to boost Chinese industry," Smith wrote, adding that either way, NVIDIA would likely do fine over the long term.

The timing is particularly fraught for NVIDIA. The company is scheduled to report fiscal first-quarter 2026 earnings on May 20, with Citi projecting revenue could reach $80 billion for the quarter. A strong report could steady investor nerves — but the China question now looms as a structural headwind that no single earnings beat can resolve.

For years, NVIDIA's dominance in AI chips seemed unassailable. The H200 episode reveals something more uncomfortable: even when Washington says yes, Beijing can simply say no — and the $170 billion cost of that veto landed squarely on NVIDIA's shareholders.
