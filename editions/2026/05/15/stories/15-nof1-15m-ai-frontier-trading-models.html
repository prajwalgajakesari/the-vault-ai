# SUI Group and Karatage Back $15 Million Round for AI Trading Lab Nof1

The funding arrives after Nof1's Alpha Arena experiment demonstrated that frontier AI models still lose money trading on their own — and that the real opportunity lies in the infrastructure around them.

When eight of the world's most powerful AI models were each handed $10,000 in real capital and set loose on live cryptocurrency markets, the results were humbling. Across 32 trading sessions, the models finished in profit just six times. The overall portfolio shed roughly a third of its value.

For most observers, that would look like a failure. For Nof1, the AI research lab that ran the experiment, it was a thesis confirmed — and now a $15 million reason to keep building.

SUI Group Holdings Limited (NASDAQ: SUIG) and London-based hedge fund Karatage Opportunities co-led the round, announced May 15. SUI Group committed $3 million to Nof1 as part of a broader $6 million deployment that also included a $3 million strategic investment in Recursive Superintelligence, a separate AI research outfit that emerged from stealth with a $650 million funding round.

The Alpha Arena Proving Ground

Nof1's flagship experiment, Alpha Arena, has become something of a spectacle in AI circles. The competition pitted frontier large language models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, xAI, and Alibaba against each other in live crypto perpetual contract trading on decentralized exchange Hyperliquid, with zero human intervention.

In Season 1, Alibaba's Qwen3 Max took the crown with a 22.3 percent return on its $10,000 stake across 43 trades. DeepSeek Chat V3.1 placed second with a more modest 4.89 percent gain. Season 1.5 saw a mystery entrant — later revealed to be xAI's Grok 4.20 — claim victory with a 12.11 percent aggregate return over two weeks.

But the headline numbers mask a deeper pattern. Most models lost money, and they lost it in characteristic ways. According to Nof1 founder Jay Azhang, the models displayed distinct trading personalities: one showed a persistent bullish bias, another had no aversion to short-selling, and a third reached aggressively for high leverage.

"Handing money directly to an LLM and letting it trade on its own — that path doesn't work yet," Azhang said. He argued that the models possess genuine strengths in research and tool selection but exhibit systematic weaknesses at the execution level. "You need basically a very sophisticated harness and scaffolding and data platform in order to even give them a chance."

That scaffolding is precisely what Nof1 intends to build with the new capital.

From Benchmark to Platform

Season 2, now in development, will mark a significant pivot. Rather than benchmarking third-party models, Nof1 plans to train its own proprietary frontier models purpose-built for financial markets. The new models will be equipped with web search capabilities, extended reasoning time, and multi-step execution — features designed to close the gap between raw intelligence and profitable trading.

Beyond Season 2, Nof1 has outlined plans for a consumer-facing platform featuring what it calls the first coding agents for markets. The platform would deploy Nof1's proprietary models using an open-ended AI architecture designed to improve continuously, with the stated ambition of outperforming both human and algorithmic traders over time.

The leadership team backing that ambition draws from heavyweight institutions. Nof1's roster includes researchers and entrepreneurs with senior experience at DeepMind and Renaissance Technologies, the legendary quantitative hedge fund — a lineage that straddles the two worlds the company is trying to merge.

Strategic Calculus for SUI Group

For SUI Group, the investment fits into a broader push to build public-market exposure around agentic finance. The Nasdaq-listed company said it intends to use Nof1's models to drive yield and returns for its own treasury, tying the deal directly to its corporate strategy rather than treating it as a passive venture bet.

Marius Barnett, Chairman of SUI Group, framed the twin investments as complementary bets on different time horizons. "Nof1 aims to build the scaffolding and intelligence to power AI-driven trading today, while Recursive Superintelligence is working toward foundational capabilities that will define what AI can do tomorrow," he said.

Karatage Opportunities, which owns 5.63 percent of SUI Group's common stock, co-led the round alongside its strategic advisory role. Karatage co-founders Barnett and Stephen Mackintosh serve as SUI Group's Chairman and Chief Investment Officer, respectively — an intertwined relationship that underscores the tight alignment between the two firms on their AI finance thesis.

What It Means

The Nof1 round lands at an inflection point for AI in financial markets. The hype cycle around autonomous AI traders has been running hot, but real-world data from experiments like Alpha Arena tells a more nuanced story: frontier models are powerful reasoning engines that still need purpose-built infrastructure to translate intelligence into returns.

With $25 million raised to date and a team blending elite AI research credentials with quantitative finance pedigree, Nof1 is betting that the winners in AI trading will not be the model makers themselves, but the companies that build the specialized platforms to channel that intelligence into markets. The $15 million round suggests at least some institutional investors agree — even if the AI models themselves have not yet learned to stop losing money.
