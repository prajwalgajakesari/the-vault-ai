# America Has 1,200 AI Bills and No Good Framework to Evaluate Any of Them

The United States is drowning in AI legislation and starving for AI policy. State legislatures introduced more than 1,200 AI-related bills in 2025 alone, enacting just under 150 of them. By March 2026, lawmakers in 45 states had already filed 1,561 new bills, an acceleration that shows no sign of slowing. Yet no level of government, state or federal, has produced a coherent test for deciding which of those proposals actually deserve to become law.

The result is a compliance patchwork that is beginning to fracture the domestic AI market. Companies building or deploying artificial intelligence now face a state-by-state obstacle course of overlapping, sometimes contradictory obligations with no unified federal standard to anchor them. IBM Chairman and CEO Arvind Krishna captured the tension in a Fox Business interview this week, warning that Washington must strike a balance on regulation or risk losing ground in the global AI race.

## Fifty States, Fifty Theories

The scale of state-level activity is staggering, but the real problem is that the bills are built on incompatible regulatory philosophies. California's SB 53, signed last September, focuses on transparency requirements for frontier AI developers. New York's Responsible AI Safety and Education Act, known as the RAISE Act, mandates stricter incident reporting, including a 72-hour disclosure window, and created a new oversight office inside the Department of Financial Services. Texas went a different direction entirely with its Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act, prohibiting specific intentional misuses while establishing a 36-month regulatory sandbox for higher-risk applications.

Connecticut became the latest major player two weeks ago, when both chambers passed Senate Bill 5 by wide margins after years of failed attempts at broader AI regulation. The bill requires large generative AI providers with more than one million monthly users to embed provenance data into AI-generated audio, image, and video content. It also imposes new employer disclosure requirements when layoffs are connected to AI adoption.

Meanwhile, states that moved early are already retreating. Colorado, which passed one of the nation's first comprehensive AI laws in 2024, is pursuing what amounts to a legislative do-over, scrapping onerous audit mandates in favor of narrower transparency requirements and pushing the effective date to January 2027. Utah has similarly narrowed its disclosure rules and swapped omnibus approaches for nine targeted bills.

As the law firm Cooley noted in an April analysis, "even the most comprehensive AI regulatory regimes are being recalibrated as implementation approaches."

## Washington's Whiplash

Federal policy has offered no anchor. If anything, it has added to the confusion. President Trump signed an executive order on December 11, 2025, directing the Department of Justice to challenge state AI laws deemed inconsistent with a "minimally burdensome" national standard. The order established a DOJ AI Litigation Task Force and conditioned broadband funding on states aligning their rules with federal preferences.

Yet just one day earlier, Trump signed the 2026 National Defense Authorization Act, which conspicuously excluded any language preempting state AI laws. And when the Senate voted on stripping an AI regulation moratorium from the budget reconciliation bill, the tally was 99 to 1 against it, a near-unanimous rejection of broad federal preemption.

The White House then released its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence on March 20, 2026, urging Congress to enact sweeping legislation that would preempt state laws imposing "undue burdens." But Congress has so far declined to move, leaving companies caught between a presidential administration pushing for federal supremacy and a legislature unwilling to deliver it.

The incoherence matters beyond domestic compliance. The EU is implementing its tiered AI Act, China is deploying frontier capability under state direction, and Singapore and the UK have positioned themselves as governance hubs through voluntary frameworks. The United States, as Yale's Jeffrey Sonnenfeld, NYU's Gary Marcus, and Yale's Stephen Henriques argued in Fortune, "is currently betting without clearly identifying which bet it has placed."

## The Missing Framework

The Fortune commentary, published May 15, proposed a three-stage test that cuts through the noise. The first stage asks whether existing consumer protection, civil rights, and privacy law already covers the harm a bill targets. Connecticut Attorney General William Tong made this point concretely in a February 2026 advisory memorandum, outlining how current state law already applies to a substantial share of AI-related conduct without any new legislation. Massachusetts Attorney General Andrea Joy Campbell issued a similar advisory.

The second stage, for bills that survive the first filter, weighs costs against benefits across four dimensions: harm reduction, national security resilience, innovation environment, and competitive concentration. The third stage tests whether the surviving proposal is properly targeted, durable against workarounds, adaptable through sunset clauses or sandboxes, and actually enforceable by the agencies charged with administering it.

Applied rigorously, this framework would thin the legislative field dramatically. Many state bills duplicate protections already available under Title VII, the Americans with Disabilities Act, and existing consumer protection statutes. Others impose compliance costs that fall hardest on smaller firms least equipped to absorb them, effectively widening the moat for the handful of companies that dominate frontier AI development.

## What Comes Next

The next twelve months will likely set the pattern for the decade. Colorado's revised AI law faces a June 30 enforcement deadline. California and New York laws are in force. Texas is operating under its regulatory sandbox. Connecticut's SB 5 awaits the governor's signature. And mandatory pre-release approval for frontier AI models is being seriously discussed in Congress for the first time, spurred in part by Anthropic's disclosure of a model it withheld from public release due to autonomous cyber capabilities.

The sheer volume of legislation is not evidence of progress. Without a shared method for separating necessary regulation from legislative noise, America risks hardening a patchwork that nobody designed and few can defend, all while its global competitors move forward with clearer, if imperfect, strategies of their own.
