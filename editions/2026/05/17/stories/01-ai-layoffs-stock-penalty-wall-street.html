# Wall Street Punishes AI-Linked Layoffs as 56 Percent of Companies Trade Lower After Cuts

The corner offices sold the story perfectly: slash headcount, cite artificial intelligence, signal to the market that the future belongs to leaner, smarter machines. Wall Street, it turns out, is not buying it.

A CNBC analysis published May 17, 2026, examining 23 S&P 500 companies that explicitly tied workforce reductions to AI found that 13 of them — 56 percent — were trading below the level at the time of their layoff announcements as of May 15. For the companies whose shares fell, the average decline was approximately 25 percent. The numbers puncture one of the most durable myths of the AI boom: that framing a layoff as a technology upgrade is a reliable stock-price catalyst.

The Casualties Are Household Names

The damage is not confined to small-cap experiments. Nike, which cut roughly 800 roles to accelerate supply-chain automation, has seen its stock slide nearly 35 percent since announcing those cuts. Salesforce, which shed 4,000 employees while simultaneously promising shareholders an AI-powered efficiency renaissance, is down approximately 32 percent. Fiverr took perhaps the most jarring blow of all: after CEO Micha Kaufman eliminated 250 positions — 30 percent of the entire workforce — and declared the company would rebuild as an "AI-first" organization, shares collapsed 54 percent.

The breadth of the selloff suggests investors are applying a discount not just to individual companies but to the entire narrative that AI-driven restructuring equals shareholder value creation.

Investors Smell AI-Washing

The market's skepticism has a name in analyst circles: "AI-washing." The term describes companies that invoke artificial intelligence as the rationale for layoffs that might otherwise be attributed to weakening demand, balance-sheet stress, or plain-old strategic miscalculation. Investors, seasoned by years of tech hype cycles, appear increasingly adept at spotting the difference.

Gartner gave the skeptics an empirical foundation in early May with a survey of 350 global enterprises each generating at least $1 billion in annual revenue. The research firm found that roughly 80 percent of those organizations had reduced their workforces under the banner of intelligent automation — yet workforce reduction rates were nearly identical among companies reporting high AI return on investment and those reporting marginal gains or negative outcomes. Cutting people, the data suggested, simply does not create AI returns.

"Many CEOs turn to layoffs to demonstrate quick AI returns; however, this disposition is misplaced," said Helen Poitevin, Distinguished VP Analyst at Gartner. "Workforce reductions may create budget room, but they do not create return. Organizations that improve ROI are not those that eliminate the need for people, but those that amplify them."

Why the Playbook Is Backfiring

The traditional logic behind layoff-driven stock pops rests on a simple equation: fewer employees means lower costs, lower costs mean higher margins, higher margins mean a higher multiple. That chain of reasoning has historically rewarded companies that execute painful but credible restructurings.

The AI variant breaks the logic at two points. First, investors cannot easily verify that AI is genuinely replacing the functions of dismissed workers rather than simply providing cover for cuts the company needed to make anyway. Second, and more fundamentally, the market appears to be pricing in the risk that companies shedding technical and operational talent may be undermining the very capacity they need to execute an AI strategy. A workforce reduction that empties an engineering floor does not automatically produce a smarter company — it may just produce a smaller one.

Fiverr's predicament illustrates the contradiction most sharply. Kaufman told employees for months to embrace AI tools and upskill aggressively. When the layoffs arrived anyway, the episode generated headlines about betrayal rather than transformation — and the stock responded accordingly.

Broader Implications for the AI Investment Thesis

The pattern has consequences that extend well beyond the companies named in the CNBC analysis. Corporate boards across the S&P 500 have watched peers race to attach the AI label to every strategic initiative, and a cohort of them has discovered the strategy carries reputational and financial risk. If Wall Street has begun penalizing AI-washing rather than rewarding it, the incentive structure that has driven a wave of headline-grabbing workforce reductions shifts materially.

The Gartner research adds a structural dimension: the firms achieving the best AI returns are investing in human-AI collaboration — training workers to supervise, guide, and scale autonomous systems — rather than eliminating those workers outright. Gartner projects that autonomous business will become a net-positive job creator by 2028 to 2029 as new AI-adjacent roles emerge faster than legacy positions disappear. Companies that cannibalize their talent base now may find themselves without the human capital needed to capture that upside.

What to Watch

The real test arrives in the next two to three earnings cycles. Analysts will be scrutinizing whether the companies that announced AI-linked layoffs can translate cost savings into measurable productivity gains — revenue per employee, operating margins, new product velocity — that justify the restructuring. If those metrics fail to materialize, the 25 percent average stock decline on record may deepen. Conversely, companies that can show hard evidence of AI-augmented output growth — rather than simply a leaner headcount — stand to differentiate themselves sharply in a market that has grown allergic to the buzzword without the backing.

For now, the message from investors is clear: in 2026, simply saying the word "AI" no longer gets you a free pass on the pink slips.
