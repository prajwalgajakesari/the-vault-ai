# Profound Launches 'Aim,' an Always-On Agent That Optimizes Brands for AI Search

When Profound closed a $96 million round in February, chief executive James Cadwallader told Fortune that the collapse of the search box was "arguably the biggest disruption in the history of capitalism." On July 2, the New York startup made its bet on that thesis concrete: it launched Profound Aim, an always-on background agent that watches how brands surface inside ChatGPT, Gemini and Perplexity and hands marketing teams a running list of what to do about it.

Aim is not another dashboard. That is precisely the point Profound is making. The company has spent two years building tooling to measure brand visibility inside AI answers, and the result, executives argue, was a flood of data that marketers had no time to act on. Aim is the pivot from measurement to execution, an agent designed to close the loop between signal and work.

From dashboards to decisions

"Marketing teams don't need another dashboard. They need to know what to do next," said James Cadwallader, CEO and Co-Founder of Profound. "Aim works in the background, constantly looking for the moments that matter. It tells your team what changed, why it matters, and what to do about it. Instead of spending hours figuring out where to focus, marketers can spend their time actually moving the needle."

The agent continuously monitors the signals that now define discovery in an AI-mediated internet: prompt trends and volumes, brand visibility, citations, sentiment, competitive benchmarks, factual accuracy across AI platforms, and so-called agentic traffic. It marries those signals with a brand's own context and priorities pulled from a connected knowledge base, then surfaces the opportunities most likely to move a team's goal metrics.

Profound describes five core capabilities. Always-on opportunity detection scans visibility, sentiment and accuracy across AI responses. Prioritized recommendations explain what changed, why it matters and the business impact. Project creation converts each opportunity into a structured brief with specific tasks and recommended agent workflows. AI-powered execution then routes the work to specialized Profound Agents for research, content creation and optimization, with marketers approving every step. Finally, a closed-loop optimization layer measures how completed work actually shifted the targeted metric and tunes future recommendations accordingly.

That last piece is what Profound is calling its differentiator. Rather than the generic suggestions of a general-purpose assistant, the company says every Aim recommendation is grounded in a proprietary AI harness and highly contextualized data. Early users echo the framing. "The projects Aim surfaces are literally gold. They are perfectly aligned to what we're trying to accomplish," said Sarah Shaffer, Organic Growth Manager at Plaid.

The rise of GEO

Aim lands in the middle of a fast-forming discipline that goes by several names: generative engine optimization, answer engine optimization, or simply GEO. As consumers increasingly ask ChatGPT, Gemini and Perplexity for recommendations rather than typing keywords into Google, the old machinery of SEO, built around blue links, keyword rankings and click-through rates, is losing its grip. In the "zero-click" internet, the prize is no longer a top result but a favorable mention inside a synthesized answer that the user may never click away from.

That shift has minted a new category, and Profound is its best-capitalized player. The company raised $96 million in February at a $1 billion valuation in a round led by Lightspeed Venture Partners, with Sequoia Capital and Kleiner Perkins participating, reaching unicorn status roughly 18 months after its seed. In total, Profound has raised more than $155 million across four rounds. It says it now works with thousands of teams and counts Figma, Walmart, Ramp, MongoDB, Chime and U.S. Bank among its customers, with reporting elsewhere putting its enterprise base at more than 700 companies, including roughly 10% of the Fortune 500.

The strategic logic behind Aim is to move Profound up the value chain. Measurement tools are useful but commoditizable; an agent that does the work is stickier and harder to replace. By connecting AI-search intelligence directly to marketing execution, Profound is positioning itself less as an analytics vendor and more as, in its own words, the marketing platform for the era of AI, an emerging "command center" for how brands appear across AI systems.

Why marketing is being rebuilt

The deeper story is that AI search is quietly rewriting the economics of digital marketing. For two decades, SEO rewarded volume: publish enough optimized pages and rank for enough queries, and traffic followed. Answer engines break that model. They cite a handful of sources, summarize them, and often satisfy the user without a click, which means brand outcomes hinge on being one of the few sources an LLM trusts and surfaces, and on how it characterizes you when it does.

That makes sentiment and factual accuracy marketing problems, not just PR ones. If Perplexity describes a product inaccurately or a competitor is cited more favorably in ChatGPT, there is now revenue at stake and, increasingly, tooling to detect and correct it. Cadwallader has argued that "in the future, every company on the planet will care deeply about how AI talks about and surfaces, and at some point interacts with, their brand." Aim is a wager that once brands can see those signals, the next thing they will demand is an agent that acts on them automatically.

It also fits the broader agentic turn sweeping enterprise software in 2026, where vendors are racing to ship background agents that do work rather than merely report it. Marketing, with its endless queue of content, optimization and monitoring tasks, is fertile ground.

What to watch next

The open question is whether always-on agents like Aim can reliably tie their recommendations to hard business outcomes, or whether "AI visibility" remains a proxy metric in search of proof. Profound's closed-loop design is an answer to that skepticism, but customers will want to see targeted visibility scores translate into pipeline and revenue.

Watch, too, for the competitive response. GEO has drawn a crowd of startups and legacy SEO platforms adding AI-visibility features, and the incumbents, Google, OpenAI and others, control both the answer engines and, potentially, their own optimization tooling. Google's move toward information agents could reshape the scoreboard Profound is built on. And as the AI platforms themselves grow more agentic, the ultimate test may be whether brands are optimizing for human readers at all, or increasingly for the machines that shop on their behalf.
