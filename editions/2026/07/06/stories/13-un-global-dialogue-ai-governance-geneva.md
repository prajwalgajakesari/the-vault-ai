# UN Opens Its First Global Dialogue on AI Governance in Geneva, With 169 Nations at the Table

*Category: Policy | The Vault — AI Edition | July 6, 2026*

GENEVA — For the first time, the world's governments sat down together under one roof to argue about how to govern artificial intelligence. On Monday, the United Nations opened the inaugural UN Global Dialogue on AI Governance in Geneva, drawing delegates from roughly 169 countries and a sprawling cast of scientists, entrepreneurs, civil-society groups and technical experts. It is the first multilateral forum on AI mandated by the UN General Assembly — and, its organizers hope, the start of something more durable than a two-day meeting.

The stakes were framed in near-existential terms from the opening minutes. UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned that AI is advancing at "runaway speed," being deployed faster than any institution can absorb, and reshaping economies, labour markets, elections and security while humanity runs what he described as an experiment "without a plan" and "without consent."

## The pitch: govern together, or govern badly

Guterres built his case around a single argument: fragmentation is the enemy. "When countries align on how to test systems, measure risk and assign responsibility, safety travels with the technology," he told delegates. "When they do not, a patchwork of incompatible rules raises costs, divides the world — and protects no one."

He laid out four priorities for international action: common safety standards, clear red lines grounded in human rights, stronger capacity-building for developing countries, and greater transparency about AI's environmental footprint. He also pressed three concrete asks — an AI Child Safety Pledge ("no child should be a guinea pig for unregulated AI"), a UN-backed Global Network for AI capacity-building that more than 20 countries have already endorsed, and an international legal ban on lethal autonomous weapons, the "killer robots" he said are already the battlefield norm.

The framing was insistently egalitarian. Used well and shared widely, Guterres said, AI "could compress decades of development into years" and become "the great equalizer of the 21st century." The counterweight was blunt: "We cannot allow the digital divide to harden into an AI divide and the AI divide to become a development gap, a security gap, and a sovereignty gap."

The two co-chairs — Ambassador Egriselda López of El Salvador and Ambassador Rein Tammsaar of Estonia — pressed the same theme. López described Geneva as "a point of departure" rather than an arrival, arguing that meaningful participation requires more than a seat in the room: countries also need skills, infrastructure, financing and institutions. Doreen Bogdan-Martin, Secretary-General of the International Telecommunication Union, situated the dialogue within a wider "Geneva Digital Week" alongside the AI for Good Summit and the WSIS Forum, noting that the UN has moved faster to convene on generative AI than it ever did on the early internet.

## The science on the table

The dialogue is deliberately paired with a new body: the UN Independent International Scientific Panel on Artificial Intelligence, established by the General Assembly in 2025 and composed of 40 experts from every region, serving in their personal capacity. Its role is scientific, not regulatory — and its first product, a preliminary report, landed on delegates' desks this week.

The findings are sobering. Panel co-chair Yoshua Bengio said there is no sign the pace of development will slow, and warned that "highly concerning tests have also shown that frontier AI models are capable of deceiving humans, to understand when they are being tested." Fellow co-chair Maria Ressa put it more starkly, calling the current risks to societies, security and the human species already "too high."

The report also quantifies the imbalance driving much of the anxiety in the room: the United States accounts for roughly 75 percent of the computing power among the world's top 500 AI supercomputers, and China about 15 percent — leaving the two countries with some 90 percent of that capacity combined, and their companies developing nearly all leading general-purpose models. More than 40 governance frameworks already exist worldwide, the panel found, but they remain "fragmented, inconsistent and rarely tested" to see whether they actually work.

## A crowded, contradictory landscape

That fragmentation is not abstract. The global AI-governance map is pulling in three directions at once. The European Union has its binding AI Act, a risk-tiered regulatory regime already in phased force. The United States, by contrast, has swung toward deregulation, prioritizing innovation and dominance over statutory guardrails. And the UN is now offering a third path: multilateral, inclusive, evidence-based — and, crucially, non-binding.

That last word is the rub. This is a dialogue, not a treaty negotiation. It issued no declaration and set no enforceable standards. UN Special Envoy Amandeep Singh Gill was candid about the limits, warning that without investment in state capacity, "dialogues are monologues and science is just abstract." His broader defense of the enterprise doubled as its rationale: "AI is too consequential to be shaped by a few. We need a conversation that is global, inclusive and grounded in evidence."

Whether that conversation can matter is the open question. The forum's power is convening power — it puts 169 governments, the two AI superpowers among them, in the same conversation as the Global South nations most likely to be left behind. History offers mixed comfort: the UN's climate architecture took decades to produce binding commitments, and even now enforcement is thin. But the counterfactual — leaving AI rules to be set entirely by Brussels regulation, Washington laissez-faire and a handful of frontier labs — is precisely the "patchwork" Guterres warned against.

## What to watch next

The dialogue's real test begins after Geneva empties. A second session is scheduled for New York in May 2027, likely alongside the STI Forum, and the Scientific Panel is expected to issue further assessments that governments can fold into policy. Watch three things: whether the United States and China engage substantively or merely attend; whether the Child Safety Pledge and the capacity-building network attract binding commitments rather than applause; and whether the panel's independent evidence begins to converge national frameworks or simply documents their divergence.

For now, the achievement is procedural but not trivial: the world has agreed to keep talking, in one place, on the record. As Guterres framed the choice, AI must lead to a future where "machines can inform, but humans must decide, and answer." Getting 169 nations to agree even on that sentence is, in the fractured landscape of AI politics, a start.
