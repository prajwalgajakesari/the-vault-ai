## A data giant reaches for the workflow layer

The company that has long sat on the pharmaceutical industry's data now wants to sit on its workflows too. IQVIA, the healthcare data and analytics giant that supplies clinical research services and commercial intelligence to nearly every major drugmaker, has rolled out IQVIA.ai, a unified agentic AI platform that already runs more than 150 specialized AI agents aimed at the slowest, most manual corners of drug development and commercialization.

The pitch is blunt: clinical trials take years, and much of that time is spent on repetitive, human-intensive tasks that software can now shoulder. IQVIA.ai bundles those agents behind a single conversational front end, letting customers deploy ready-made or configurable agents against real-world life sciences workflows rather than stitching together point tools. The platform is built on IQVIA's proprietary data and "Healthcare-grade AI," with an AI foundation developed in a multi-year collaboration with NVIDIA drawing on components including NVIDIA Nemotron and the NeMo Agent Toolkit.

## What the 150+ agents actually do

The agents are organized as orchestrators supervising sub-agents, an arrangement IQVIA likens to a conductor routing work to different sections of an orchestra. An orchestrator hands off narrow tasks such as clinical coding, structured data extraction, transcription and summarization to specialist agents, keeping human experts in the loop at each step.

Some of the most concrete gains are in trial operations. The start-up phase of a clinical trial alone often runs about 200 days and is heavily manual; IQVIA's trial start-up agent is designed to compress that. Another agent analyzes trial protocols to extract participant inclusion and exclusion criteria, reasoning through the problem in phased steps to speed site start-up and, by extension, site selection and feasibility. A clinical data review agent uses automated checks to catch data issues early, cutting a review cycle from roughly seven weeks to as little as two.

The agents extend past the trial itself. On the commercialization side, orchestrator agents analyze patient records, prescriptions and lab results to map patient journeys and treatment pathways in days rather than weeks, while a field companion agent briefs sales teams before each healthcare-provider meeting using prescribing patterns and digital behavior.

"From molecule to market, AI promises to be transformative for life sciences and healthcare," said Avinob Roy, a vice president and general manager for commercial analytics solutions at IQVIA.

The scale of the effort is notable. IQVIA says it has filed more than 100 AI-related patents, and that 19 of the top 20 pharmaceutical companies have already begun folding its agents into their workflows.

## Why agentic AI is reshaping pharma operations

"IQVIA.ai reflects our longstanding commitment to translating innovation into practical, high impact solutions for life sciences," said Bernd Haas, senior vice president of AI and technology solutions at IQVIA. "By bringing together our data, expertise and Healthcare-grade AI within a unified, agentic platform, IQVIA.ai enables organizations to move faster and smarter while meeting the rigorous standards of trust and reliability that are required in the industry."

That framing captures why agentic AI is landing so hard in this sector. Drug development is a high-cost, low-yield endeavor — tens of billions spent annually to bring a few dozen drugs to market — and its timelines are dominated by process, not just science. Agents that autonomously grind through protocol analysis, data cleaning and market mapping attack cost and speed at once, while the regulatory, privacy and quality guardrails that make pharma slow also make IQVIA's regulated, curated data a moat rivals cannot easily copy.

## A crowded and hardening field

IQVIA is not moving into open territory. The life sciences software market has turned into an agentic arms race. Veeva, the dominant clinical and commercial cloud vendor, unveiled Falcon in May — an agentic platform with standard agents spanning its clinical, regulatory and safety applications, with early-adopter availability slated for November 2026 — and paid roughly $100 million in March to acquire AI engagement startup Ostro. Salesforce, meanwhile, has rebranded its life sciences push as Agentforce and says more than 140 organizations, including Novartis, AstraZeneca and Moderna, are using its platform. On the trial-data side, Dassault-owned Medidata remains a formidable presence in electronic data capture and eClinical tools.

IQVIA's differentiator is that it owns both the data and, through its contract-research arm, the operational execution — a combination Veeva and Salesforce, which sit closer to the CRM layer, largely lack.

## What to watch

The initial IQVIA.ai release targets high-value clinical, commercial and real-world use cases, with more agents and capabilities promised for the fourth quarter of 2026 — the moment to judge whether the library deepens or stays shallow. Watch, too, for validated outcome data: adoption by 19 of the top 20 drugmakers signals interest, but the harder proof is whether agent-run trials measurably shorten timelines and survive regulatory scrutiny. And watch the coopetition. IQVIA has joined Veeva partner programs even as it competes with Veeva's platform, a sign that in agentic pharma, today's rival may also be tomorrow's distribution channel.
