## US Clears License-Free AI Chip Exports to the UAE

The United States has handed the United Arab Emirates one of the most consequential prizes in the global race for artificial intelligence infrastructure: near-frictionless access to America's most advanced computer chips. On July 10, 2026, the Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) announced it would remove the UAE from its restrictive export-control country groups and elevate it to Country Group A:5, the tier reserved for Washington's closest technology allies, allowing the Emirati government and approved companies to import advanced AI chips and servers without applying for individual export licenses.

The move ends years of tight restrictions that had forced case-by-case licensing of high-end semiconductors bound for the Gulf, and it operationalizes a US-UAE Artificial Intelligence Cooperation framework signed in May 2025. In its announcement, BIS said it would strip the UAE out of EAR Country Groups D:3 and D:4 and reclassify it as A:5, a status it said was "warranted in light of the ongoing U.S.-UAE military partnership and the UAE's commitment to preventing the diversion and misuse of sensitive U.S. technology."

## What Changed

Under the new rule, the UAE government and certain approved firms can receive Commerce-controlled military items, some commercial satellites and spacecraft, and, critically, advanced computing items including AI chips license-free. BIS tied the upgrade to the UAE's designation as a "Major Defense Partner" and its support for US operations in the region. Commerce also noted the UAE had "reaffirmed its commitment" to matching investments in US AI infrastructure under the 2025 framework.

The practical winners are the companies at the center of the UAE's AI ambitions. Abu Dhabi-based G42 and its Core42 unit, along with US firms operating locally, no longer need licenses for the chips and servers that power large data centers. The regulatory changes were implemented through a Federal Register notice available for public inspection.

Emirati officials welcomed the decision. UAE Minister of State Saeed Al Hajeri said the reclassification reflected "international confidence" in the country's export-control and compliance framework, adding that "the UAE is now the first Arab nation to achieve this designation, joining the ranks of America's most trusted technology and strategic partners."

## The Stargate Payoff

The clearance directly feeds the marquee Stargate UAE project. G42 chief executive Peng Xiao said the company expects its first shipments of advanced AI semiconductors "within months," enough to bring online the initial 200 megawatts of a planned 1-gigawatt cluster. Xiao said deliveries would "mostly" consist of Nvidia chips, with additional hardware from AMD and Cerebras Systems. For a country betting its post-oil future on becoming a global compute hub, the license-free channel converts diplomatic goodwill into physical silicon on data-center floors.

## Analysis: A Deliberate Bet Against Beijing

The reclassification is the clearest expression yet of the Trump administration's core wager on AI-chip geopolitics: that selling advanced hardware to trusted partners locks them into the American technology stack and keeps them out of China's. Administration officials have repeatedly argued that if the US refuses to supply the Gulf, states like the UAE and Saudi Arabia will simply buy Chinese alternatives, ceding both market share and strategic influence. The A:5 upgrade turns that argument into policy, trading tighter control for deeper alignment.

But the same features that make the deal attractive make it contentious. Advanced chips are fungible and hard to track, and the UAE's historically close commercial ties to China have long worried export hawks. Senate Democrats alleged in June that intelligence officials had found G42 routing US chip technology toward programs that strengthen China's missile systems, despite the firm's earlier pledge to cut its China links, an allegation that hangs over the license-free grant.

The loudest critic has been Senator Elizabeth Warren, ranking Democrat on the Banking Committee, who called the arrangement "corrupt." Warren argued the benefit flows to entities entangled with the president's business interests: "We already know that the UAE royal behind G42 and MGX secretly bought a 49% stake in the Trump crypto company, World Liberty Financial. Now, Trump's Commerce Department is giving G42 license-free access to advanced AI chips and promising favorable treatment for MGX, despite reported concerns about the diversion of sensitive technology to China and other national security risks."

BIS says the A:5 status still rests on the UAE's compliance track record and continued safeguards against diversion, and that shipments remain conditioned on those commitments.

## What to Watch

Watch whether the first G42 chip deliveries arrive on Xiao's "within months" timeline, and how much of the 1GW Stargate build actually comes online in 2026. Watch Congress: Warren and other Democrats may push for hearings, oversight letters, or legislation to attach diversion-tracking conditions to the exception. And watch Beijing's response, and whether any evidence emerges of restricted hardware leaking from the UAE toward Chinese end users, which would test the entire premise of trading control for alignment.
