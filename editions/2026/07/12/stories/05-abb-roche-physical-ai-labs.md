On July 9, 2026, two of Switzerland's largest multinationals placed a shared bet on the same idea: that the next frontier for artificial intelligence is not a chatbot but a robot arm on wheels, gliding between analyzers in a hospital laboratory. ABB Robotics announced a global collaboration with Roche Diagnostics to develop and bring to market robotic solutions for clinical labs — a deal that pushes the buzzy concept of "physical AI" out of the trade-show demo and toward the diagnostic bench.

## What the two companies are building

The collaboration, formalized in a joint statement, splits the early work into two complementary tracks. In Roche's pathology business, ABB will develop robots to handle and organize slides, supporting the shift toward digital, connected pathology workflows. In central diagnostics labs — Roche's largest testing segment — ABB will deploy autonomous mobile manipulators: robots that combine a mobile base with a manipulator arm to move samples and materials between instruments without fixed tracks or conveyors.

The pitch is flexibility. Traditional lab automation locks instruments into rigid, hard-wired lines. Mobile manipulators can reroute on the fly, connecting islands of equipment and taking on the repetitive fetch-and-carry work that consumes technician time. "The collaboration is a major milestone in accelerating lab automation, introducing more autonomous and versatile robotics into clinical laboratories at scale," said Marc Segura, President of ABB Robotics. "With Roche, we will be able to bring the latest physical AI to labs all around the world, addressing major challenges that are hindering labs from providing the best outcomes for patients."

Under the division of labor, ABB supplies the industrial-grade hardware, its AI-powered robotics portfolio and the orchestrating software; Roche contributes deep diagnostic and application knowledge to tailor the systems to real clinical settings. The companies framed sample preparation and intra-logistics — the internal movement of specimens — as the first bottlenecks they intend to attack.

## Why now

The timing is driven by strain. Roughly 70 percent of treatment decisions rely on diagnostic testing, yet labs worldwide are short-staffed as fewer professionals enter demanding, intensive roles. Aging populations and the move toward personalized medicine are simultaneously pushing test volumes and complexity higher. The result is a widening gap between what labs are asked to produce and the people available to produce it.

"Partnering with ABB Robotics allows us to bring a new level of robotic automation to laboratories of all sizes — it's about vital flexibility and consistency in clinical diagnostics," said Marc Boehm, Lifecycle Leader for Core Lab at Roche Diagnostics. "By automating repetitive tasks, we empower laboratory staff to focus on complex analysis, which directly accelerates the journey from diagnosis to critical treatment decisions for patients."

That framing — robots as backfill for scarce labor rather than replacement for it — is deliberate. Both companies are careful to position the machines as handling the dull, repetitive intra-logistics so that technicians and biologists can concentrate on higher-value interpretation.

## The physical-AI angle

The deal is a marquee example of a broader trend sweeping healthcare. "Physical AI" refers to robots that learn from real-world data and adapt to dynamic, unstructured environments rather than following pre-programmed paths — the same paradigm chipmakers and robotics firms have been pushing for factories and warehouses, now aimed at the lab. A clinical lab is an appealing proving ground: high-value, repetitive, tightly regulated, and desperate for throughput.

For ABB, the move deepens a lab-automation strategy it has been telegraphing all year, including AI-enabled lab robotics demos at the SLAS 2026 conference in February. It also lands during a period of upheaval for the unit: earlier in 2026, ABB agreed to sell its robotics division to Japan's SoftBank, a reminder that the business making these healthcare bets is itself changing hands. For Roche, tapping an industrial-robotics partner rather than building in-house signals that diagnostics giants increasingly see automation expertise as something to source from outside the life-sciences world.

Skeptics will note what the announcement does not contain: no financial terms, no unit targets, no firm dates for first commercial deployment. This is a development-and-commercialization framework, not a shipped product, and clinical labs are conservative buyers where validation, regulatory scrutiny and error tolerance move slowly. Turning a compelling demo into a validated instrument that a hospital lab will trust with patient samples is the hard part.

## What to watch

Three things will tell whether this becomes real. First, timelines and pilots — watch for named reference labs running the mobile manipulators in production, and any regulatory clearances. Second, whether ABB's pending handover to SoftBank accelerates or disrupts the Roche roadmap. And third, whether rivals in lab automation respond with their own physical-AI partnerships. If mobile manipulators prove they can shave hours off turnaround times without compromising accuracy, the "lab of the future" stops being a slogan — and becomes procurement.
