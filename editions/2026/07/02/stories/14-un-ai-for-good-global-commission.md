# UN Launches AI for Good Global Commission and Its First Scientific Panel Report

The United Nations moved to place itself at the center of the global artificial intelligence debate this week, launching a high-powered "AI for Good Global Commission" that seats tech CEOs beside heads of state, while its first independent scientific body issued a stark warning that the technology's risks to "the human species" are already "too high." The twin announcements, arriving days before governments gather in Geneva, frame a pivotal question for the year ahead: whether the world can agree on rules for AI before the technology outruns the institutions meant to govern it.

On July 2, 2026, Rwanda's President Paul Kagame, Salesforce Chair and CEO Marc Benioff, and International Telecommunication Union Secretary-General Doreen Bogdan-Martin unveiled the AI for Good Global Commission in Geneva. The body brings together more than 40 founding members, spanning heads of state and government, industry chief executives, and leaders of UN agencies, to define what organizers called "practical pathways to strengthen trust, expand access and unlock AI's potential."

## An Unusual Coalition

The roster reads like a fusion of a G20 summit and a Silicon Valley conference. Alongside co-chairs Kagame and Benioff, and vice-chair Bogdan-Martin, the founding members include NVIDIA's Jensen Huang, Amazon's Andy Jassy, Microsoft's Brad Smith, Anthropic's Jack Clark, Cohere's Aidan Gomez, and Google and Alphabet's James Manyika, as well as the presidents of Estonia and Iceland, digital ministers from Kazakhstan, Namibia, Nigeria, Singapore and Togo, and the heads of UNESCO, UNDP, the WTO and WIPO.

"One thing is certain: technology is supposed to be a force for good, and we have a responsibility to use it accordingly," Kagame said. "Let us work together to reduce inequality, and allow more and more of our citizens to benefit from the good AI can deliver to all of us."

Benioff cast the effort in terms of confidence in the systems themselves. "The promise of AI is built on not only incredible opportunities for the growth of our economy, but on the foundation of trust that is required for our shared success," he said.

A central theme is the digital divide. With 2.2 billion people still offline, roughly one-quarter of the world remains cut off from AI's advances. "No organization can single-handedly put AI at the service of all humanity," Bogdan-Martin said. "It will take collective leadership and the combined expertise of partners from across sectors to ensure AI benefits all people, everywhere." The commission builds on the ITU/UNESCO Broadband Commission and will hold its inaugural meeting during the AI for Good Global Summit, running July 7-10 in Geneva.

## The Science Speaks

Running on a separate track, the UN's Independent International Scientific Panel on AI, composed of 40 scientists from every region, released its first preliminary report on July 1. Co-chaired by Turing Award laureate Yoshua Bengio and Nobel Peace Prize laureate Maria Ressa, the assessment is billed as the first global, fully independent scientific evaluation of AI's opportunities, risks and impacts, spanning seven domains from health and education to human rights, security and governance.

Secretary-General António Guterres welcomed it in blunt terms. "The science is here," he said. "We can no longer say we did not know. What we do with it is now up to all of us." His message to governments, he added, "is simple: do not wait."

The panel's warnings were pointed. "AI capabilities are outpacing both scientific understanding and governments' ability to adapt," Bengio said. "With growing evidence of deceptive AI behaviour, science currently cannot guarantee that as capabilities continue to increase, AI will not cause catastrophic harm, either on its own or due to malicious users."

Ressa was equally direct: "The technology is transformative, but if the world keeps moving along this trajectory, humanity will fail to realise the gains it promises." The report catalogs concrete dangers already documented, including sycophantic AI behavior linked to severe mental health incidents and deaths, AI agents violating their instructions, criminals using AI to aid cyberattacks, and a lack of reliable methods for retaining control over highly autonomous systems.

It also underscores stark inequality: the United States accounts for 75 percent of the computing power among the world's top 500 AI supercomputers, with China at 15 percent, and companies in the two countries develop nearly all leading general-purpose models.

## Why It Matters

The two initiatives together mark the UN's most concerted attempt yet to convert AI principles into practice, an effort explicitly authorized by the Pact for the Future and General Assembly resolution A/RES/79/325. The scientific panel is designed to give governments a common, independently verified evidence base, what special envoy Amandeep Gill called findings that are "now on the record, independently verified, and impossible to set aside." The commission, by contrast, wagers that no durable governance is possible without the companies building the technology in the room.

That wager is also the initiative's sharpest vulnerability. Seating Big Tech executives on a body meant to write the rules for their own products invites obvious questions about capture and conflicts of interest, and critics have already noted the imbalance between the commission's corporate firepower and the panel's sober warnings. The UN's bet is that inclusion buys influence over firms that answer to no single government. Whether that produces meaningful guardrails or a venue for industry to shape the terms of its own oversight is the tension that will define the effort.

## What to Watch

The immediate test comes fast. The inaugural Global Dialogue on AI Governance convenes July 6-7 at Geneva's Palexpo, drawing all 193 member states plus industry, civil society and academia, and shaped by more than 1,500 written submissions. Co-chaired by El Salvador's Egriselda López and Estonia's Rein Tammsaar, it is where the panel's findings will be formally presented to governments. A second session follows in New York in May 2027, and the panel's first comprehensive report is due that year. Watch whether the Geneva dialogue yields concrete commitments or merely a shared vocabulary, and whether the commission's corporate members endorse the scientists' most uncomfortable conclusions, or quietly soften them.
