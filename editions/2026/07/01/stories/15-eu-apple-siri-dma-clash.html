EU and Apple Clash Over Siri AI Delay as the Digital Markets Act Meets Generative AI

4 min read 980 words 3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Apple CEO Tim Cook and EU tech chief Henna Virkkunen held 'constructive' talks after Apple delayed its Gemini-powered Siri AI in Europe, making the fight the first real test of how the Digital Markets Act's interoperability rules apply to flagship generative-AI features.

The most consequential meeting in the generative-AI industry this week was not a product launch. It was a video call between Apple chief executive Tim Cook and the European Union's top technology official, convened to answer a question that will shape how AI ships on regulated platforms for years: can Apple put its most ambitious Siri upgrade on European iPhones without falling foul of the bloc's competition law?

On June 30, Cook held what both sides described as a "constructive" virtual meeting with Henna Virkkunen, the European Commission's Executive Vice-President for Technological Sovereignty, Security and Democracy. An EU spokesperson said the exchange covered "topics of common interest, on which the work continues," according to the Financial Times, which first reported the talks. Two people familiar with the discussion said it included how Apple might launch its reinvented Siri in Europe while avoiding the multimillion-dollar fines that come with breaching the Digital Markets Act.

The meeting followed one of the sharpest public standoffs yet between a Big Tech gatekeeper and Brussels over an AI product, turning a narrow interoperability fight into a test case for the collision between Europe's platform-openness agenda and the AI features that have become the industry's main competitive battleground.

How the standoff started

At its WWDC26 developer conference in June, Apple unveiled Siri AI, a rebuilt assistant powered by Apple Intelligence, and then confirmed it would not ship in the EU alongside iOS 27 and iPadOS 27. In an unusually blunt newsroom statement, Apple placed the blame on regulators: "Unfortunately, due to the Digital Markets Act (DMA), Apple will not be able to ship Siri AI in the European Union with the release of iOS 27 and iPadOS 27. Over the past several months, EU regulators did not accept any of Apple's proposed solutions to bring Siri AI to the EU while safely supporting other virtual assistants."

The dispute turns on interoperability. Under the Commission's reading of the DMA, Apple's designation as a gatekeeper means rival voice assistants must be able to reach the same on-device features and data that Siri uses. Apple argues that granting that access without safeguards would hand third parties direct access to users' private data and the ability to control other installed apps. To thread the needle, the company designed what it calls a Trusted System Agent, an intermediary meant to let other assistants tap Siri-equivalent capabilities safely, and proposed launching Siri AI while phasing that system in over 18 months.

Brussels was unmoved. The day after Apple's statement, Commission spokesman Thomas Regnier told reporters he needed to "set the record straight," saying, as reported by Reuters: "The decision not to roll out Siri AI in the EU is Apple's and Apple's only because absolutely nothing in the DMA prohibits Apple from introducing new products in the EU." He added that "Apple was simply unable to develop interoperability solutions that meet essential EU privacy and security standards," and characterized the 18-month proposal as a request for an exemption from the law's obligations rather than a genuine compliance plan.

Why this is bigger than Siri

Two features make this fight a precedent rather than a one-off.

First, the assistant at the center of it is not purely Apple's own. Earlier reporting on iOS 27 indicated Apple's revamped Siri leans on Google's Gemini models for its most demanding reasoning. That means the regulatory question is layered: a gatekeeper's flagship AI feature, built partly on a rival gatekeeper's model, subject to a rule that would force it open to still more rivals. The DMA was written before generative assistants became the marquee feature of a smartphone, and Siri AI is the first hard test of how its interoperability mandates map onto them.

Second, the mechanism at issue, deep access to on-device data and app control, is exactly what makes a modern AI assistant useful and exactly what makes it dangerous if opened carelessly. That tension is not unique to Apple. Any gatekeeper that wants to ship an agentic assistant in Europe, from Google to Microsoft to Amazon, will eventually face the same demand to let competitors reach the same hooks. How Brussels and Apple resolve the privacy-versus-openness trade-off here will signal whether the DMA becomes a reason to delay AI rollouts in Europe or a workable framework companies learn to build around.

There is also a tell in who showed up. Cook's personal involvement, months after signaling he would stay engaged in Apple's government relations, suggests the company is more eager to resolve the impasse than its combative newsroom posts implied. Siri AI is central to Apple's software roadmap; leaving Europe, one of its largest markets, without the headline feature of the year is not a stance Apple can hold indefinitely.

The competition-versus-innovation bind

Brussels faces its own bind. The DMA's purpose is to pry open closed ecosystems so smaller players can compete, and backing down under pressure from the largest gatekeeper would undercut the law's credibility. But if strict enforcement means European users are the last to get flagship AI features, the Commission risks the politically awkward outcome of a rule meant to help consumers being blamed for depriving them. Apple has been happy to frame the delay in exactly those terms, and every month Siri AI is absent from EU devices strengthens that narrative. Both sides, then, have an incentive to find a face-saving path: Apple wants its feature shipped, and the Commission wants compliance rather than a headline about European users going without.

What to watch

Three things over the coming months. First, whether Apple and the Commission agree on any version of the Trusted System Agent, or a shorter transition than 18 months, that lets Siri AI launch without a formal exemption. Second, whether Brussels opens a formal non-compliance proceeding, which could put Apple on the hook for fines of up to 10 percent of global turnover and turn negotiation into litigation. Third, whether other gatekeepers preemptively adjust their own European AI plans based on how this resolves. The Gemini connection is the wildcard: a rule forcing one gatekeeper's AI open, when that AI runs partly on another gatekeeper's model, is uncharted territory, and the answer Europe lands on will echo well beyond Apple.
