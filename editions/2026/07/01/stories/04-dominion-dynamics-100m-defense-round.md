Ottawa-based Dominion Dynamics has closed a $139-million CAD ($100 million USD) Series A, a round the company and Axios both describe as the largest defense-technology financing in Canadian history. The deal, announced June 30 and detailed by Axios on July 1, lands barely a year after the startup was founded and pushes the total it has raised since launch to $169 million CAD. It is also a marker of something larger: the defense-tech boom that produced U.S. giants like Anduril is spilling into allied markets, and Canada is racing to build its own.

The raise was led by existing backer Georgian, with a sprawling cap table that reads like a who's who of the NATO investment world. New participants include Valor Equity Partners, the Valor Atreides AI Fund, Expeditions, Lakestar, OMERS Ventures, BDC Capital's Strong North Fund, Deloitte Ventures, Royal Bank of Canada and JDY Capital. Follow-on support came from British Columbia Investment Management Corporation, Bessemer Venture Partners, Garage Capital, Golden Ventures and Silent Ventures. The round was all-equity and all-primary capital, meaning every dollar flows into the company rather than cashing out early investors.

## A founder from the Anduril playbook

Dominion is led by founder and CEO Eliot Pence, who spent four years leading global growth at Anduril Industries before starting the company in June 2025. He is explicitly modeling Dominion on the American "neoprime" template: software-first, product-led, self-financing the development of hardware before winning the government contracts that traditional primes like Lockheed Martin depend on.

Pence frames the round less as a company milestone than as a national inflection point. "Canada once built technology the rest of the world wanted, then convinced itself that was someone else's role," he said in a statement carried by Axios. "We started Dominion to show the capability never left, and this round lets us build at the scale and speed the moment demands."

In an interview with BetaKit, he was blunt about the stakes beyond his own balance sheet. "It would be misplaced hubris for me to assume that this is really just about Dominion," Pence said. "It's about a moment in time."

## What the money builds

Dominion is spending the capital on two flagship systems. AuraNet is a command-and-control platform that fuses scattered sensors and communications into a single operating picture. Scout is an autonomous "robo-wingman," a collaborative aircraft the company has committed $50 million to building that is meant to extend the reach of crewed aircraft into remote, punishing environments, starting with the Arctic.

The Arctic is the whole thesis. Dominion's pitch is that Canada struggles to monitor, let alone defend, its vast northern territory given harsh terrain and thin troop numbers. Its answer is a mesh of software, sensors, drones and vehicles that autonomously collect, communicate and analyze data in real time to spot threats and eventually anticipate them using AI. That is not just a slide deck: during Operation Nanook-Nunalivut this past February through April, Canadian Rangers used Dominion's software and sensors for mission tracking, planning and real-time communications in the High Arctic.

"Velocity is the capability that we've proven out the most," Pence told BetaKit, saying investors were drawn to the startup's "capacity to build things that matter" and the speed at which it has put them into users' hands. The company took AuraNet "from an idea to deployment" in roughly six months.

## Why now: Canada turns inward

The timing is inseparable from a broader Canadian pivot. After decades of underspending relative to allies, Ottawa published its first-ever defense-industrial strategy in February, a document that does not mince words: "Long-held assumptions have been upended — about the end of imperial conquest, the durability of peace in Europe, and the resilience of old alliances." Canada has met its 2% NATO spending pledge and, with other members, has committed to reach 5% by 2035.

Prime Minister Mark Carney has made building domestic defense capacity and reducing reliance on the United States a central theme, and investors are reading the round through that lens. Georgian lead investor Margaret Wu told BetaKit the financing "signals that the global investment community sees the opportunity in Canada" and a chance to fuel "more sovereign players for NATO." Pence has positioned himself close to those rooms, co-founding the Alliance of Canadian Defence Companies and taking a seat on Carney's advisory committee on Canada-U.S. economic relations.

## The analysis: allied autonomy as infrastructure

Dominion's raise is a data point in a bigger structural shift. The defense-tech wave that Anduril, Palantir and their peers rode in the U.S. is now being deliberately replicated in allied capitals that no longer want to depend on American systems, timelines or export approvals. What is being funded is not weapons in the old sense but AI, robotics and autonomy treated as strategic infrastructure. When a NATO-wide cap table backs an Ottawa startup building autonomous Arctic surveillance, the message is that software-defined defense is becoming a sovereignty question, and capital is flowing to whoever can move fastest.

There are real risks. Neoprimes spend heavily before landing contracts, and a recent BDC and Icebreaker report found smaller firms still struggle to break into Canada's defense sector. Dominion's success ultimately hinges on Ottawa overhauling its procurement, which has historically favored slow, hardware-heavy incumbents.

## What to watch

Three things. First, procurement: whether Dominion converts Arctic demonstrations into durable Canadian Armed Forces contracts will validate or undercut the neoprime model. Second, expansion: the company plans to grow from 50 staff to more than 100 and open U.S. and European offices by the end of 2026, testing whether a "pan-NATO defence neoprime" is a real category or a pitch. Third, the Arctic itself, an increasingly contested theater, and whether Dominion's autonomy stack meaningfully advances the security conversation there.
