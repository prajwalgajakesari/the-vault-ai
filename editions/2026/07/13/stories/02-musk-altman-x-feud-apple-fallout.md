# Musk and Altman Turn Apple's OpenAI Lawsuit Into a Public Feud on X

Apple's lawyers filed the complaint on Friday. By Saturday morning, the two most combustible personalities in artificial intelligence had turned it into a spectacle. Within hours of Apple accusing OpenAI of an orchestrated campaign to steal its hardware secrets, Elon Musk and Sam Altman were once again clawing at each other on X — dragging a trade-secret dispute between two former partners into the open, and reminding investors just how personal the AI arms race has become.

## What Apple actually alleged

On July 11, 2026, Apple sued OpenAI, its hardware venture io, and two former Apple employees in federal court in Northern California, alleging a systematic theft of confidential hardware designs, supplier data, and technical specifications. The company that OpenAI once partnered with to put ChatGPT inside the iPhone now describes a scheme it says reached the top of OpenAI's hardware organization.

"At every level, from members of its Technical Staff to its Chief Hardware Officer, and in coordination with business partners, OpenAI has been stealing Apple's trade secrets and confidential information," Apple wrote in its filing, as reported by CNBC.

The complaint names Tang Yew Tan, OpenAI's chief hardware officer and a former Apple vice president, and Chang Liu, a former senior electrical engineer. Apple claims Tan directed candidates interviewing at OpenAI to bring "actual parts" from Apple for "show and tell" sessions, and that Liu kept a work-issued laptop and downloaded dozens of confidential files after his departure. More broadly, Apple says OpenAI has hired more than 400 of its former employees for its hardware push and coached departing staff on how to avoid immediate removal from Apple's systems so they could keep accessing internal data.

OpenAI has denied wrongdoing, telling multiple outlets it has no interest in other companies' trade secrets. Apple has separately said it is not interested in anyone else's secrets — only in protecting its own.

The relationship soured after OpenAI moved into hardware, most visibly with its roughly $6.4 billion acquisition last year of io, the startup founded by former Apple design chief Jony Ive.

## The feud spills onto X

The litigation handed Musk — who runs SpaceX and its xAI unit, and who lost his own long-running suit against Altman earlier this year — a fresh opening. He amplified the news and revived his "Scam Altman" nickname for the OpenAI chief.

"He takes scamming to a whole new level," Musk wrote of Altman, per CNBC.

Altman did not let it sit. "Homeboy you're the one selling public market investors on short-term space datacenters," he shot back, a jab at Musk's pitch to place AI data centers in orbit. Altman argued that Musk's fixation was really about OpenAI's latest model. The timing was pointed: this week OpenAI shipped GPT-5.6 Sol while Musk's team released Grok 4.5, and Fortune reported the two men escalated to openly accusing each other of scamming investors as both SpaceX and OpenAI march toward the public markets.

The bad blood is old. Musk left OpenAI's board in 2018 after donating tens of millions, then sued Altman over what he called an "opaque web of for-profit OpenAI affiliates." A California jury sided with Altman this year; Musk has said he will appeal.

## Why it matters

Strip away the insults and the lawsuit is really about talent and silicon. Apple's core claim — 400-plus former employees now at OpenAI — is a legal reframing of the industry's brutal talent war, in which poaching and the knowledge that walks out the door with an engineer are increasingly hard to separate. By suing, Apple is testing whether trade-secret law can slow the churn that money alone cannot.

It also lands as Apple retools around on-device AI. Bloomberg's Mark Gurman reported July 12 that Apple is skipping the M6 Pro, Max, and Ultra to fast-track an AI-focused M7 generation built for higher memory bandwidth and heavier on-device processing, with an M8 on a 1.4nm process already in development for 2028. Apple's silicon roadmap is the moat OpenAI's hardware ambitions threaten — and the asset the lawsuit is meant to protect.

For OpenAI, the timing is delicate. The company filed a confidential S-1 in May and, per CNBC and The New York Times, is weighing a Q4 2026 listing versus a slip to 2027, with Altman reportedly treating anything below a $1 trillion valuation as a "non-starter." A splashy trade-secret suit and a public brawl with Musk are not the pre-IPO narrative underwriters prefer.

## What to watch next

Watch for OpenAI's formal response and any motion to dismiss or compel arbitration, and whether Apple seeks an injunction that touches io's roadmap. Watch the discovery fight over what those 400-plus hires actually took. And watch the IPO calendar: if Apple's suit gains traction in the coming weeks, it could become a line item in OpenAI's risk factors — and give Musk fresh ammunition for the next round on X.
