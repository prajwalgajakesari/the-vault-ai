# Canada Launches AI for All Strategy With Over $2 Billion and Goal of 250,000 Jobs

Prime Minister Mark Carney bets big on sovereign AI infrastructure, job creation, and national literacy training in the most ambitious technology policy Canada has ever attempted.

TORONTO -- Standing in the city where much of Canada's artificial intelligence talent was forged, Prime Minister Mark Carney on Thursday unveiled "AI for All," a sweeping five-year national strategy that commits more than $2.3 billion in new federal spending to transform Canada from an AI research pioneer into a full-fledged AI economy. The plan targets the creation of 250,000 new AI-related jobs by 2031 and aims to raise business adoption of AI from a paltry 12 percent to 60 percent by 2034 -- numbers that, if achieved, would represent one of the most dramatic technology transitions any G7 nation has attempted.

"AI is here. The question is whether it will improve the lives of all Canadians or benefit only a few," Carney said at the announcement. "AI can shorten our emergency room wait times and make a small business more competitive, if it is governed by Canadian values with a clear goal of improving the lives of all Canadians."

The Money Trail

The strategy's $2.3 billion price tag is distributed across several major spending pillars. The Business Development Bank of Canada will deploy $500 million to help small and medium-sized enterprises integrate AI tools through its LIFT program. Another $500 million will establish a new Canadian Tech Growth Fund to provide flexible growth capital -- and in some cases direct equity stakes -- in promising domestic AI firms. The federal government is also injecting an additional $700 million into the Compute Access Fund to subsidize high-performance computing costs for companies building and running AI models. A further $500 million flows through regional development agencies to support business AI adoption on the ground.

On the infrastructure side, the strategy builds on Budget 2024's $2 billion allocation for the Canadian Sovereign AI Compute Strategy, which includes up to $1 billion for public supercomputing infrastructure and up to $700 million for a competitive AI Compute Challenge that has already earmarked up to $240 million for Canadian AI champion Cohere. The government says partnerships currently being finalized will deliver 850 megawatts of compute capacity by 2030, with the potential to scale to 2.3 gigawatts -- a direct challenge to the dominance of Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, which currently control 85 percent of Canada's public cloud market.

Sovereignty at the Core

The emphasis on "sovereign AI" runs through the entire document. Carney and AI Minister Evan Solomon have framed the strategy around the argument that Canada is dangerously dependent on foreign technology infrastructure. "Canada's new AI for All Strategy is about putting artificial intelligence to work for Canadians," Solomon said. "It will give people the confidence to use AI safely, help businesses adopt it, and ensure more of the value is created here at home."

The strategy proposes building a "world-leading public supercomputer," expanding the CIFAR AI Chairs program from 130 to nearly 200 researchers, and launching a Sovereign Technology Alliance -- first established with Germany earlier this year -- to deepen AI collaboration with trusted allies. Canada has already signed AI cooperation agreements with 12 nations, including the United Kingdom, Australia, India, and the European Union.

On the human capital front, the plan pledges 90,000 AI-related jobs and work placement opportunities for young Canadians, a National AI Literacy Initiative offering free training to all citizens, and AI learning kits for more than 3,000 educators. Every post-secondary student, the strategy promises, will have access to trusted AI agents regardless of their field of study.

The Skeptics Weigh In

Not everyone is convinced. Conservative deputy leader Melissa Lantsman called the strategy "a very ambitious plan which lacks a lot of details," noting that Canada has already lost more than 112,000 jobs since January. NDP Leader Avi Lewis pushed for stronger guardrails, warning that the plan proposes "a massive boost to business adoption of AI, with no concern for the consequences this will have for workers."

Industry voices offered a more nuanced take. Daniel Wigdor, co-founder and CEO of Canadian AI venture studio AXL, told BetaKit that Canada's core problem remains translating research into commercial success. "If we get that balance right -- commercialization, application, and trust -- Canada can create the next generation of global AI companies instead of exporting another generation of breakthroughs," Wigdor said. Council for Canadian Innovators CEO Patrick Searle echoed the concern, arguing the real test is whether the strategy stays focused or "tries to do too many things at once."

Why This Matters

Canada's AI for All strategy arrives at a pivotal moment in the global AI race. With the worldwide AI market projected to reach US$4.8 trillion by 2033, nations are scrambling to stake their claims. Canada holds a unique position: the country's academic institutions, led by pioneers like Yoshua Bengio and Geoffrey Hinton, essentially invented modern deep learning. Yet that intellectual capital has largely been commercialized abroad. Only 12 percent of Canadian businesses currently use AI, placing the country among the slowest adopters in the G7.

The strategy's focus on sovereign compute infrastructure is particularly significant given the ongoing trade tensions with the United States. By building domestic data center capacity and reducing reliance on American cloud providers, Canada is hedging against the geopolitical risks that have dominated North American economic relations. The $500 million Tech Growth Fund, with its provision for government equity stakes, signals a willingness to play a more interventionist role in tech markets -- a departure from Canada's traditionally hands-off approach.

The conspicuous gap, however, is on the regulatory side. Despite promising new consumer privacy legislation, online safety laws, and a "Canada Trusted AI Certification" program, the strategy is thin on timelines and enforcement mechanisms. In a world where the EU's AI Act is already being implemented and the UK has established its own AI Safety Institute, Canada risks falling behind on governance even as it races ahead on investment.

What to Watch Next

The coming months will reveal whether AI for All is a genuine inflection point or a well-funded wish list. Key milestones include the fall 2026 federal budget, where details on the Tech Growth Fund and tax incentives for reinvesting tech gains are expected; the finalization of sovereign data center partnerships targeting 850 megawatts by 2030; and the introduction of the promised privacy and online safety legislation. For Canada's AI ecosystem -- and for the 250,000 workers whose future jobs depend on it -- the clock is now running.
