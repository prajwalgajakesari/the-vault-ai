# Coralogix Raises $200 Million to Build the Observability Layer for AI Agents

**The Boston-headquartered, Israeli-founded startup closes a Series F at a $1.6 billion valuation, betting that autonomous AI systems will need their own monitoring infrastructure -- and that dashboards are already on their way out.**

BOSTON -- As enterprises rush to deploy AI agents that can autonomously write code, investigate outages, and manage production systems, a critical question is emerging: who watches the watchers? Coralogix, a software observability startup founded in Israel and headquartered in Boston, is staking $200 million in fresh capital on its answer.

The Series F round, announced Tuesday, was co-led by Advent and the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board (CPPIB), with participation from Greenfield Partners and Brighton Park Capital. The financing values Coralogix at $1.6 billion post-money and brings its total funding to $550 million. It arrives just 11 months after a $115 million Series E -- a cadence that underscores the velocity of investor appetite for AI infrastructure plays.

## The Thesis: Observability Becomes the Operating System for AI

Founded in 2014, Coralogix helps companies monitor the health and performance of software systems by collecting and analyzing logs, metrics, and traces -- essentially a continuous record of what a software system is doing and how it is behaving. The platform serves more than 5,000 customers worldwide, including IBM, Tradeweb, and JFrog, across financial services, media, retail, and cloud-native industries.

But the company is making a pointed bet that the observability market -- long dominated by dashboard-centric incumbents like Datadog, New Relic, and Splunk -- is undergoing a structural shift. AI-powered applications are generating telemetry data at volumes and speeds that legacy monitoring platforms were never designed to handle. At the same time, AI agents are becoming the primary consumers of that data, investigating incidents and operating production systems with decreasing human involvement.

"The interface layer is slowly getting eroded," CEO and co-founder Ariel Assaraf told TechCrunch. "Most of the usage is going to be around, 'How do I connect my LLM to this? How do I operate this through my CLI?'" In plain terms, his customers are less interested in logging into a dashboard and more interested in asking an AI assistant what went wrong.

The shift is already visible in Coralogix's own usage data. More than half of the startup's enterprise customers now interact with the platform through either Olly -- its built-in AI agent -- or their own AI models via command-line and agentic interfaces, rather than traditional dashboards. Coralogix supports three modes of operation on a single data foundation: human-led investigation, conversational AI collaboration, and fully automated agent workflows.

## The Numbers Behind the Momentum

The funding round is backed by strong commercial traction. Coralogix grew revenue by more than 60 percent year-over-year and now counts roughly 30 customers spending more than $1 million annually. The company surpassed $100 million in annualized revenue more than a year ago, though Assaraf declined to disclose current figures.

The startup employs more than 600 people globally, with its largest offices in the United States and Israel, and a growing India hub of about 100 employees. The India operation, which Business Standard reports is growing at 50 percent year-over-year, serves customers including Kotak Mahindra Bank, JioStar, SonyLIV, and Delhivery. Coralogix processes petabytes of production data daily across eight regions, including GovCloud for public sector and regulated industries.

"AI is fundamentally changing the way enterprises operate, and observability is quickly evolving into a core layer of business intelligence," said Alek Ferro of Advent. "Coralogix has consistently stayed ahead of that transformation, building a platform designed for the scale, speed and complexity of the agentic era."

## Architecture as Moat

What distinguishes Coralogix from its larger competitors, the company argues, is an architecture built from the ground up for full-fidelity data ingestion. Where legacy platforms rely on sampling -- analyzing only a fraction of system data to manage costs -- Coralogix uses a streaming engine and schema-free data lake with customer-owned storage in open formats. The result is that enterprises retain complete operational data rather than a subset, which becomes critical when AI systems need comprehensive context to diagnose problems or automate remediation.

"Long before the industry started talking about AI agents, we built around the idea that observability needed complete data, open access and infrastructure customers could truly control," Assaraf said in the company's official announcement. "In the AI era, dashboards are no longer the starting point for observability. Intelligence is. We didn't reposition for this shift. We were built for it."

## Where the Money Goes

Coralogix plans to deploy the capital across three areas: accelerating its AI-native observability capabilities, including deeper development of Olly, MCP, and CLI-based workflows; expanding its telemetry data infrastructure to support real-time processing and long-term retention at enterprise scale; and scaling global adoption, particularly among organizations in fintech, cybersecurity, and AI infrastructure that are moving away from legacy monitoring tools. The company is also investing in security research teams across India and Israel.

## The Bigger Picture

The raise lands in an observability market being rapidly reshaped by the agentic era. Every major vendor -- from Datadog to Splunk -- is embedding AI into monitoring and incident-response workflows. But Coralogix is betting that the transition runs deeper than bolting AI onto existing platforms. If AI agents become the primary operators of production software, then the entire observability stack needs to be rebuilt around machine-speed ingestion, open data access, and autonomous investigation -- not human-friendly dashboards.

Assaraf said Coralogix does not expect to raise additional capital and is working toward profitability over the next few years. The company is also preparing to operate with the financial discipline of a public company, though he stopped short of committing to an IPO timeline. "In the AI era, execution and speed matter more than any point-in-time valuation," he said. "We wanted to accelerate, expand, and take a further step into this AI game that we believe we are leading in our space."

For the observability market, the message is clear: in a world where machines increasingly monitor and manage other machines, the companies that own the data layer underneath may end up owning the most critical infrastructure of all.
