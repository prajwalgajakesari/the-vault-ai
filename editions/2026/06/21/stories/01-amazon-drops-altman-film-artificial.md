# Amazon Drops Luca Guadagnino's Nearly Finished Sam Altman Film 'Artificial' Amid $50 Billion OpenAI Deal

*The Vault — AI Edition · Business · June 21, 2026*

Hollywood does not often hand back a finished prestige film with an Oscar-nominated director attached and a cast worth a dozen red carpets. Amazon MGM Studios just did exactly that — and the reason has less to do with the movie than with the $50 billion the company has riding on its subject.

Amazon has dropped *Artificial*, Luca Guadagnino's nearly completed drama about OpenAI chief executive Sam Altman, walking away from a project it commissioned, financed and tested for audiences. The film, which stars Andrew Garfield as Altman and dramatizes the chaotic five days in November 2023 when Altman was fired and rehired by OpenAI's board, will now be shopped to rival studios by Guadagnino's agency, CAA. The decision, first reported by Variety and confirmed across the trades this week, arrives barely four months after Amazon announced a $50 billion investment in OpenAI — the very company at the center of the film.

In a statement, an Amazon spokesperson framed the exit as a creative courtesy rather than a retreat. "We have the utmost respect and admiration for Luca Guadagnino as an award-winning filmmaker — not to mention a longstanding relationship that we hope to continue," the spokesperson said. "We believe that 'Artificial' will be better served if it were released by a different studio and are working closely with the filmmaking team to find the film a new home."

## A Finished Film, Suddenly Homeless

By any normal measure, *Artificial* was on the runway. Written by Simon Rich — the former *Saturday Night Live* writer and novelist who has built a reputation for sharp, satirical takes on Silicon Valley — the film was shot in San Francisco and Italy and styled, by those who have seen it, as a *Social Network*-style chronicle of ambition, betrayal and boardroom knife-fights. Garfield leads as Altman, with Monica Barbaro as former OpenAI chief technology officer Mira Murati, Russian actor Yura Borisov as co-founder and former chief scientist Ilya Sutskever, and Ike Barinholtz as Elon Musk. The supporting bench is deep: Cooper Hoffman, Jason Schwartzman, Cooper Koch, Billie Lourd, Zosia Mamet, Chris O'Dowd and Mark Rylance all appear.

The movie centers on what insiders at OpenAI later called "the blip" — the November 2023 episode in which the company's nonprofit board abruptly ousted Altman, only to reinstate him days later after employees threatened a mass walkout and Microsoft offered to absorb the entire staff. It is, in other words, a film about corporate power, governance failures and the people who build artificial intelligence — material that has only grown more charged as those same companies have become trillion-dollar forces.

Crucially, *Artificial* was not an abandoned experiment. The film had completed multiple test screenings, and according to multiple reports those screenings drew warm, even enthusiastic reactions. Before pulling the plug entirely, Amazon had quietly pushed the release from a presumed 2026 slot into an undisclosed early-2027 window — a delay that, in hindsight, looks like a studio stalling on a problem it could not creatively edit away.

## Why a $50 Billion Deal Changed the Math

The problem was never the film. It was the cap table.

In February 2026, Amazon announced a multi-year strategic partnership with OpenAI anchored by a $50 billion commitment — a deal that expands OpenAI's use of Amazon Web Services and pairs the two companies on custom AI infrastructure. That single transaction transformed Altman from an arm's-length biographical subject into a business partner whose company Amazon is now financially yoked to for years.

Releasing a satirical drama about your largest new AI partner's most embarrassing week is the kind of conflict that no marketing department wants to litigate. Even if *Artificial* treats Altman fairly — and early viewers suggest it is more nuanced than a hit piece — the optics are unavoidable. A studio owned by Amazon, distributing a film that re-stages the firing of the CEO whose company Amazon just backed with $50 billion, invites exactly the kind of "is this propaganda or sabotage?" coverage that a $50 billion partnership is built to avoid.

This is the part that should interest anyone tracking the AI landscape, not just the film industry. The episode is a vivid illustration of how thoroughly the major AI labs have become entangled with the platforms that distribute culture. When the same conglomerate owns a cloud business, a film studio, a streaming service and a multibillion-dollar stake in a frontier lab, the editorial independence of any one division becomes negotiable. *Artificial* is a clean test case: a finished, well-received film killed not on merit but because its subject became a strategic asset. The chilling implication — that stories about AI's most powerful figures may struggle to find a home inside the very companies now bankrolling AI — is the real story here.

## What to Watch Next

For now, the film's fate rests with CAA Media Finance, which began running screenings for rival buyers within days of Amazon's exit. The early shopping rounds have been bruising: Netflix, Focus Features, Warner Bros.' Clockwork label and A24 have all reportedly passed after seeing the film. The art-house distributor Mubi is said to be actively pursuing it, with Neon also possibly circling — a signal that *Artificial* may ultimately land with an independent willing to lean into, rather than away from, its controversy.

The questions now are commercial and symbolic. Will a buyer pay a premium for a polished Guadagnino film with awards-season pedigree, or will the cost of distributing a movie that needles the most powerful man in AI scare off the deep-pocketed studios? And will Altman, OpenAI or Musk respond once the film actually reaches audiences? However it resolves, *Artificial* has already told a story its makers never scripted — about who gets to tell the story of AI, and who now owns the cameras.
