# DeepSeek Nears $7.4 Billion Round From Tencent in One of China's Largest AI Deals

Chinese AI startup DeepSeek is on the verge of closing a 50 billion yuan ($7.4 billion) funding round -- its first ever from outside investors -- in a deal that would rank among the largest startup financings in China's history and signal a dramatic escalation in the global AI arms race. The round, which Bloomberg first reported on Tuesday, would value the Hangzhou-based company at between $52 billion and $59 billion, catapulting it into the uppermost tier of private AI companies worldwide.

Tencent Holdings, the social media and gaming conglomerate behind WeChat, is set to be the largest external backer with approximately 10 billion yuan (roughly $1.5 billion) committed. Battery manufacturing giant Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. (CATL) follows with an expected 5 billion yuan investment. Beijing's state-backed National Artificial Intelligence Industry Investment Fund is also participating, lending the round an explicit endorsement from the Chinese government. Additional investors in final-stage talks include gaming developer NetEase and e-commerce giant JD.com, though the total number of participants is expected to remain below ten.

What makes this round especially unusual is the sheer commitment from DeepSeek's own founder. Liang Wenfeng, who built DeepSeek out of his quantitative trading firm High-Flyer Quant, is personally investing 20 billion yuan -- approximately $2.85 billion -- into the deal, accounting for roughly 40 percent of the total raise. Until now, the company had been funded entirely from High-Flyer's coffers, making this maiden round a watershed moment for a lab that has operated with unusual financial independence.

Liang has been unequivocal with investors about the company's direction. In at least one meeting with prospective backers, the founder pledged to prioritize groundbreaking AI research over short-term commercialization and committed to keeping DeepSeek's models open-source while pursuing artificial general intelligence. "We won't go closed-source," Liang has stated in previous public remarks. "In disruptive tech, closed-source moats are fleeting. Even OpenAI's closed-source model can't prevent others from catching up. Therefore, our real moat lies in our team's growth -- accumulating know-how, fostering an innovative culture."

The deal is expected to close within the next couple of weeks, according to people familiar with the matter.

Why This Matters

The sheer size of this round reshapes the competitive calculus of global AI. At a $52 billion to $59 billion valuation, DeepSeek now sits in roughly the same tier as Anthropic, which has been setting the stage for a potential trillion-dollar public listing. It also dwarfs the valuations of most other Chinese AI labs, including Moonshot AI and Zhipu AI, which raised at far more modest figures.

More critically, the deal validates an approach to AI development that runs counter to the dominant Western playbook. While OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind have built their businesses around proprietary models and aggressive commercialization, DeepSeek is betting that open-source research and relentless cost efficiency will prove more durable. The company sent shockwaves through global markets in January 2025 when it released an AI model that matched American rivals' performance while using substantially fewer Nvidia chips -- a revelation that triggered one of the largest single-day market value drops in Nvidia's history.

DeepSeek's researchers have since demonstrated new training methods for large language models that allow performance to scale without a proportional increase in cost. As PYMNTS noted, "If approaches like DeepSeek's prove reliable beyond research settings, they could alter the economics of AI deployment in sectors that depend on cost discipline, including commerce, payments, and enterprise software."

The participation of China's national AI fund adds a geopolitical dimension. At a time when U.S. export controls continue to restrict Chinese companies' access to advanced Nvidia chips, Beijing's willingness to funnel state capital into DeepSeek suggests the government views the lab as a strategic national asset -- one capable of advancing China's AI capabilities even under hardware constraints.

What to Watch

The immediate question is whether DeepSeek can maintain its research-first philosophy once it has outside shareholders expecting returns. The company's valuation already assumes future revenue at scale, yet Liang has explicitly deprioritized commercialization. That tension will intensify as the AI industry enters a phase where investors are demanding proof that hundreds of billions in infrastructure spending will generate real business value. Watch for whether DeepSeek introduces enterprise products or API pricing in the months following this close -- and whether its open-source commitment survives the pressure of a $59 billion price tag.
