# Martin Scorsese Joins Black Forest Labs as Adviser, Sparking Hollywood AI Backlash

Martin Scorsese, the 83-year-old director whose name is synonymous with cinematic craft, has signed on as an adviser to Black Forest Labs, the German generative AI startup behind the FLUX image model. The announcement, disclosed on June 2, immediately detonated one of the sharpest debates Hollywood has seen over artificial intelligence -- pitting the director's stated enthusiasm for a new creative tool against a film community already raw from years of fighting to keep AI out of the production pipeline.

Scorsese framed the partnership narrowly around storyboarding. In a company video, he demonstrated how FLUX generates images from text prompts, producing wintry Eastern European town scenes for his next film, "What Happens at Night," a drama starring Leonardo DiCaprio and Jennifer Lawrence.

"Cinema is a young medium, only around 125 years old, so we have to be open to how it can evolve," Scorsese said in a statement. "Now with this tool, I can share what I'm visualizing more clearly and efficiently to my creative team -- the production designer, art designer and cinematographer."

He compared the move to his previous technology experiments: 3D cinematography on "Hugo" and the digital de-aging techniques used in "The Irishman." He added that during preproduction, "time costs money, and this allowed us to move faster without sacrificing quality or craft."

What Is Black Forest Labs?

Black Forest Labs, headquartered in Freiburg, Germany, with offices in San Francisco, was born out of Stability AI, the company behind Stable Diffusion. Its flagship product, the FLUX generative image model, has attracted major enterprise partners including Adobe, Canva, Snap, and Meta, which signed a multi-year contract reportedly worth $140 million. In December 2025, the company raised $300 million in a Series B round co-led by Salesforce Ventures, with participation from Andreessen Horowitz, Nvidia, and General Catalyst, valuing Black Forest Labs at $3.25 billion. Scorsese signed on through startup investor BroadLight Capital.

The company represents one of the most well-funded players in the generative image space, and Scorsese's endorsement gives it something no funding round can buy: cultural legitimacy from one of cinema's most revered living auteurs.

The Backlash

The reaction from parts of Hollywood was swift and furious. Boots Riley, the director of "Sorry to Bother You" and "I Love Boosters," took to X to deliver a blunt assessment.

"My guess: at 83, they gave his family a gang of money," Riley wrote. "He wanted the income stream for them and feels like 'AI' will fall on its face anyway, so he doesn't give a f--k." He added that if that interpretation was wrong, "extra f--k him."

Riley's critique crystallized a sentiment rippling through the creative community: that Scorsese's involvement, however limited to storyboarding, legitimizes a technology many artists view as an existential threat to their livelihoods. Storyboard artists, concept designers, and visual development professionals -- the very people who translate a director's vision into images before a single frame is shot -- see generative AI as a direct replacement for their work.

Guillermo del Toro, another titan of auteur filmmaking, has been among the most vocal opponents. He has said he would "rather die" than use generative AI in his films and slammed those who believe "art can be done with a f--king app." Steven Spielberg recently stated on the "IMO" podcast that AI should not be "the final word" in the creative process, adding: "I don't believe there is any substitute for the soul. I don't think that is an algorithm that's inventible."

Christopher Nolan, as chair of the Directors Guild of America, has warned that directors must navigate "myriad issues" around "control of our work and how it might be manipulated through AI" as the guild negotiates a new contract with studios. "We don't want innovation to just be an excuse to pay our members less," Nolan said.

A Divided Creative Class

Yet Scorsese is not alone among A-list directors embracing AI. James Cameron joined the board of Stability AI in 2024. Steven Soderbergh used Meta's AI tools to create sequences in his Cannes Film Festival documentary "John Lennon: The Last Interview" and plans to use the technology extensively on an upcoming film. Director Jon Erwin used AI-generated backgrounds to shoot the Amazon series "The Old Stories: Moses" on a Los Angeles soundstage in a single week with a 100-person crew, arguing that AI could actually create more production jobs by making projects cheap enough for studios to greenlight.

The divide reveals something deeper than a technology debate. It is a generational and economic fault line. Directors at the top of the industry -- those whose budgets, reputations, and leverage insulate them from displacement -- are the ones most willing to experiment. The artists most threatened are those further down the credits: storyboard artists, concept illustrators, background painters, and visual effects workers whose roles AI tools are already capable of approximating.

What This Means

Scorsese's partnership with Black Forest Labs marks what may be the most consequential endorsement of generative AI by a working filmmaker to date. It does not resolve Hollywood's AI debate, but it shifts the Overton window. When the director of "Goodfellas" and "Taxi Driver" says generative AI helped him move faster without sacrificing craft, the argument that the technology has no place in filmmaking becomes harder to sustain.

The question is no longer whether AI will enter the filmmaking workflow. It is who gets to control it, who profits from it, and who gets displaced by it. For storyboard artists watching an 83-year-old icon sign on with a $3.25-billion AI company, the answer feels increasingly clear -- and increasingly alarming.
