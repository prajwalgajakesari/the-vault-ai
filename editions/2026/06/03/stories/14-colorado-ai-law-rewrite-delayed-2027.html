# Colorado Rewrites Landmark AI Law, Delaying Enforcement to 2027 and Narrowing Scope

Two years of fierce debate over the nation's first comprehensive AI regulation end with a compromise that trades algorithmic accountability for procedural transparency -- and buys the industry another six months.

DENVER -- Colorado's ambitious experiment in artificial intelligence regulation has been gutted, rebuilt, and delayed -- all in under two weeks.

On May 14, Governor Jared Polis signed Senate Bill 26-189, a sweeping rewrite that repeals and replaces the state's landmark 2024 Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) just weeks before it was set to take effect on June 30. The new law pushes enforcement to January 1, 2027, and jettisons three of the original statute's most consequential requirements: mandatory risk management programs, annual impact assessments, and the duty of reasonable care to prevent algorithmic discrimination.

What remains is a disclosure-and-rights framework that requires companies to tell consumers when automated decision-making technology (ADMT) influences consequential decisions about hiring, lending, housing, healthcare, education, and government services -- and to explain themselves within 30 days when those decisions go against the consumer.

"Everybody lost and everybody won," said Senate Majority Leader Robert Rodriguez, a Denver Democrat who sponsored both the original 2024 law and its replacement. "We still have consumer protections. It's not as much as I would have liked. We're still the only state in the country to pass this legislation."

From Risk Regulation to Notice and Disclosure

The conceptual shift is dramatic. The 2024 law borrowed its architecture from the EU AI Act: a risk-based regime that classified certain AI systems as "high-risk" and imposed affirmative obligations on how they were built, tested, and governed. SB 189 abandons that model entirely.

Under the new framework, developers must provide deployers with technical documentation covering intended uses, training data categories, known limitations, and instructions for human review. Deployers must give consumers clear notice before using ADMT in consequential decisions and, after an adverse outcome, deliver a plain-language explanation of the technology's role within 30 days. Consumers gain rights to correct inaccurate personal data and request meaningful human review -- conducted by a person with authority to override the automated decision who does not simply defer to the system's output.

The law also introduces sector-specific carve-outs absent from the original statute. HIPAA-covered entities are broadly exempt for non-employment uses. FDA-regulated medical devices are excluded entirely. Insurers already subject to Colorado's algorithmic discrimination rules are deemed compliant. And activities like cybersecurity, fraud prevention, and anti-money-laundering are carved out from the definition of "consequential decisions."

Federal Pressure and Industry Pushback

The rewrite did not happen in a vacuum. President Donald Trump's December 2025 executive order on AI policy singled out Colorado's law as "excessive State regulation" and directed federal agencies to challenge state AI statutes deemed inconsistent with a deregulatory national framework. An AI Litigation Task Force established by the order intervened in a federal lawsuit brought by Elon Musk's xAI against the original Colorado AI Act. On April 27, 2026, a federal magistrate judge stayed enforcement after the state attorney general stipulated to the delay.

Polis spokesman Eric Maruyama said the bill "protects consumers and is a nation-leading model created through thoughtful collaboration. The Governor looks forward to signing SB-189 and making Colorado a top state for innovation and entrepreneurship."

The Colorado Technology Association, a trade group central to the negotiations, called the result "meaningful progress." Its president and CEO, Brittany Morris Saunders, said the bill "is a compromise, shaped by intentional stakeholder engagement, and moves Colorado toward a more workable approach for startups, small businesses, employers, and technology leaders across our state."

Not Everyone Is Celebrating

Consumer advocates and labor groups accepted the compromise but made clear they view it as a floor, not a ceiling.

Kjersten Forseth, legislative director for the AFL-CIO and a spokesperson for the People's Alliance for Responsible Technology, called SB 189 "a good first step to protect the interests of everyday Coloradans from some of the negative consequences of AI" but warned that "there's still work to be done to ensure Big Tech companies are transparent and accountable."

Robert Lindgren, also with the Colorado AFL-CIO, was more pointed in testimony before the legislature: "Gone are the risk management requirements, impact assessments, annual reviews and discrimination reporting. It introduces a cure period that lets developers delay accountability and allows discriminatory practices to continue under current law."

State Rep. Javier Mabrey, a Denver Democrat who voted for the bill, said the measure does "nowhere near enough" and called AI-driven decision-making in hiring, housing, and education "incredibly alarming." He vowed to push for letting consumers -- not just the attorney general -- file lawsuits when they believe they have been wronged.

What It Means for the National Landscape

Colorado's retreat from the EU-style model sends a signal to every statehouse considering AI regulation. The state was the proof of concept for comprehensive, risk-based AI governance in America. Its pivot to a lighter, transparency-focused regime -- executed under federal pressure and industry litigation -- suggests the EU AI Act template will not be the dominant framework for U.S. states.

The law vests enforcement exclusively in the Colorado Attorney General under the Consumer Protection Act, with a 60-day right to cure violations and no private right of action. The cure period sunsets on January 1, 2030. Attorney General Phil Weiser must finalize implementing rules by January 1, 2027 -- the same day the law takes effect.

One provision that survived and may prove consequential: SB 189 voids any contract clause attempting to indemnify a party for its own discriminatory use of automated decision-making technology, a direct shot across the bow of AI procurement agreements nationwide.

For companies operating in Colorado, the message is clear: the compliance burden has lightened considerably, but the clock is ticking. The rest of 2026 should be spent inventorying AI systems, updating consumer-facing processes, and watching the attorney general's rulemaking closely. Colorado may have hit reset on its AI law, but the debate over how to regulate the technology is far from over.
