# OutSystems Unveils Open Agentic Systems Platform With A2A and MCP Integration

*Tuesday, June 3, 2026 | 4 min read | 870 words*

**Key Takeaway:** OutSystems launches an open Agentic Systems Platform with native A2A and MCP protocol support, aiming to prevent LLM lock-in for enterprises deploying AI agents at scale.

---

AMSTERDAM -- At its annual ONE Conference on Tuesday, OutSystems pulled back the curtain on an ambitious reimagining of its low-code platform, unveiling what it calls the Agentic Systems Platform -- an open, protocol-native foundation designed to let enterprises build, orchestrate, and govern fleets of AI agents without tying their futures to any single model provider.

The announcement arrives at a moment when enterprise AI agent adoption has reached near-universal levels but governance has not kept pace. OutSystems's own research, drawn from a survey of nearly 1,900 global IT leaders, found that 96 percent of enterprises are already running AI agents in some capacity. Yet 94 percent of those same organizations report that agent sprawl is compounding technical debt and security risk -- a tension the company is now positioning its platform to resolve.

## The Architecture: A2A, MCP, and the Enterprise Context Graph

At the heart of the new platform is the OutSystems Agent Experience, a layer that exposes a suite of tools and services built on two increasingly important open protocols: Google's Agent-to-Agent (A2A) protocol and Anthropic's Model Context Protocol (MCP). Together, these standards allow AI agents built on different models and frameworks to communicate, share context, and coordinate tasks without proprietary middleware.

The platform is powered by what OutSystems calls the Enterprise Context Graph -- a dynamic operational context layer that maps an organization's business logic, data relationships, and compliance requirements. When paired with Amazon Bedrock's model management capabilities, the system lets enterprises swap between foundation models based on cost, performance, or sovereignty requirements without rewriting their agent architectures.

"Enterprise leaders are realizing they need leverage and control in this highly fragmented and dynamic AI market. To maintain that leverage and protect margins, organizations must separate their proprietary business logic and data from specific AI providers," said Woodson Martin, CEO at OutSystems. "Our Agentic Systems Platform provides the open and neutral platform to ensure optionality and control."

## Three Pillars of Product Innovation

The Amsterdam conference featured product launches across three domains.

**Agentic Systems Engineering** received the deepest technical expansion. OutSystems announced a native integration with Kiro, the agentic development environment from AWS, giving developers a governed pathway to build and manage agents directly on the OutSystems platform. The company also launched Legacy Modernization Services powered by AWS Transform -- an automated pipeline that migrates systems running on COBOL and Lotus Notes into modern agentic architectures. Both services are available in preview for select customers, with expanded AWS Transform capabilities planned for Q3.

**Agentic Enterprise Orchestration** represents the next generation of OutSystems' existing Agent Workbench. Now powered by the Enterprise Context Graph and Amazon Bedrock, it offers advanced agent evaluations, precise guardrails, integrated semantic search, and what the company describes as the ability to architect an "agile workforce of AI agents." The orchestration layer is generally available today for existing customers.

**Agentic Industry Solutions** debuted with an end-to-end Banking Solution for Loan Origination -- the first in a planned family of vertical-specific agent packages. Leveraging domain-specific models through Amazon Bedrock for finance, legal, and document processing use cases, the banking solution targets one of the most compliance-heavy sectors in enterprise software. The full solution is expected in early Q3, though developers can access the Banking and Financial Services Agent Kit on OutSystems' Forge marketplace today.

## The Governance Gap

The product launches land against a backdrop of mounting concern about ungoverned AI agent deployments. OutSystems' 2026 State of AI Development report paints a stark picture: while 49 percent of respondents describe their agentic AI capabilities as "advanced" or "expert," only 12 percent have implemented a centralized platform to manage agent sprawl. Thirty-eight percent are running a mix of custom-built and pre-built agents across fragmented environments with inconsistent oversight.

"The ability to orchestrate an agentic enterprise will transform our operations," said Mike Schmitt, CEO at Butler Plus, one of several customers who demonstrated agentic deployments at the conference alongside the Netherlands Red Cross and AllianceCorp Manufacturing. "We are no longer just using AI for isolated tasks; we are deploying governed agents that handle mission critical processes, while maintaining strict compliance."

## Sovereignty and the AWS Bet

A deepened AWS collaboration underpins much of the platform's architecture. The distributed design features full runtime isolation and new self-hosting capabilities, allowing customers to run AI workloads in whatever jurisdiction their data sovereignty posture demands. OutSystems indicated that partnerships with EU sovereign cloud providers are forthcoming, signaling that the company views regulatory compliance as a competitive differentiator rather than an obstacle.

## The Broader Landscape

OutSystems is not operating in a vacuum. Microsoft recently open-sourced an Agent Governance Toolkit covering all 10 OWASP agentic AI risks. AWS launched its own Agent Registry for enterprise governance. Gartner has predicted that 40 percent of enterprise applications will feature task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026, up from less than 5 percent in 2025.

The question facing OutSystems -- and the industry -- is whether governance tooling can scale as fast as agent deployment. With 96 percent adoption and only 12 percent centralized governance, the gap is already wide. Every additional agent deployed without oversight compounds the very risks that drove OutSystems to build this platform in the first place. Whether an open, protocol-native approach can close that gap faster than proprietary alternatives remains the bet Amsterdam is making.
