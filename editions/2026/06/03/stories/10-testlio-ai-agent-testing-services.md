# Testlio Launches AI Agent Testing Services With Human-in-the-Loop Quality Assurance

*As AI agents gain digital wallets and autonomous decision-making power, the Austin-based QA firm bets that humans remain the indispensable safety net.*

---

When an AI agent independently authorizes a payment on behalf of a customer and something goes wrong, who catches it? Not another AI, argues Testlio. The Austin-based quality assurance company this week launched its AI Agent Testing services, a dedicated offering that inserts trained human evaluators into the testing loop for agentic AI systems -- the autonomous software agents increasingly trusted with high-stakes tasks like executing payment workflows, managing customer interactions, and making real-time decisions.

The launch marks the third pillar in Testlio's expanding AI testing portfolio, following its end-to-end AI testing solution introduced in November 2025 and an AI chatbot testing service rolled out in April 2026. But agent testing, the company argues, represents a fundamentally different challenge -- one where the stakes are higher, the failure modes subtler, and the consequences more costly.

"Testlio is uniquely positioned for this moment, combining years of proven global payments testing with this new AI agent validation service," said Summer Weisberg, CEO of Testlio. "As enterprise AI agents evolve from handling simple customer queries to executing complex agentic payment workflows, where AI is granted digital wallets to independently authorize transactions, organizations need a partner that goes beyond automated simulation."

## Why Agent Testing Is Different

Traditional software testing follows deterministic logic: given input X, expect output Y. AI agents shatter that paradigm. They plan, reason, and act across multi-step workflows in ways that shift with data, context, and model updates. An agent processing a refund might work flawlessly in English on a U.S. payment method but fail catastrophically when handling a localized alternative payment method in Southeast Asia -- a failure that automated test suites would likely never simulate.

Testlio's approach draws on its 13-plus years of QA expertise and a global community of more than 80,000 vetted professional testers. The company's AI-certified testers run what it describes as thousands of "what-if" scenarios, probing agents in the unpredictable, real-world conditions where failures actually occur. The service evaluates planning, execution, and coordination in single- or multi-agent systems, including those built on the Model Context Protocol (MCP) standard that has gained significant traction as an interoperability layer for AI agents.

The proprietary evaluation framework is designed to catch the issues that automated tools miss. Testlio highlights three critical gaps: agents fail across unpredictable real-world edge cases that scripted tests cannot anticipate; automation-only evaluations miss nuanced behavioral issues around tone, intent, and cultural context; and agentic failures often manifest as quiet liabilities and policy violations rather than obvious crashes.

## The Numbers Behind the Need

The urgency is backed by data from Testlio's earlier AI testing deployments. Among early adopters of the company's broader AI testing solution, 82 percent of AI issues involved hallucinations or misinformation, particularly in chatbot and retrieval-augmented generation systems. Seventy-nine percent of bugs were classified as medium or high severity, directly affecting user trust, product credibility, and brand reputation.

The broader market context reinforces the demand. Industry data shows that only 11 percent of companies have AI agents fully operational in production, despite 25 percent running pilot programs. Nearly 60 percent of AI initiatives are expected to struggle reaching production scale due to gaps in validation, monitoring, and AI-ready data foundations. And at least 60 percent of AI-generated code contains issues requiring human intervention.

"Every interaction is a brand trust moment," Weisberg said in an earlier statement about the company's chatbot testing launch. "When those moments go wrong -- a hallucination, an off-brand response, a safety failure -- they erode trust and loyalty that took years to build."

## A Platform Built on 2.6 Million Test Cases

Testlio's agent testing service is powered by its LeoAI Engine, a proprietary intelligence layer built from more than 2.6 million test cases and insights from over 600,000 devices. The platform includes LeoMatch, a system that pairs testers to engagements based on domain expertise, language skills, and market familiarity. The company claims this matching process assembles testing teams three times faster than manual selection and uncovers twice as many critical issues.

For the agent-specific offering, testers undergo continuous AI upskilling through Testlio Academy, completing courses on assessing fairness, reasoning quality, safety risks, and ethical integrity. The company also introduced LeoPulse, a scoring system that aggregates results across safety, reliability, and capability into an AI confidence score weighted by risk severity, ensuring that serious failures are not masked by strong performance in less critical areas.

## The Bigger Picture

Testlio's bet on human-in-the-loop agent testing arrives as the QA industry undergoes a tectonic shift. The global software testing market is projected to grow from $55.8 billion in 2024 to $112.5 billion by 2034, driven largely by AI adoption. But the testing paradigm itself is changing: when your product embeds an AI agent, you are no longer testing fixed logic -- you are evaluating behavior that shifts with every model update and data change.

The company, founded in 2012 by Kristel Kruustuk, operates as a fully remote organization with team members in more than 150 countries. Its client roster includes Netflix, Strava, Whatnot, and Merck. Testlio is ISO 27001:2022 certified and holds Microsoft Supplier status.

As AI agents move from pilot projects to production systems -- handling payments, making medical recommendations, managing customer relationships -- the question of who tests the testers becomes existential. Testlio's answer: trained humans, armed with the right frameworks and operating at global scale, remain the last line of defense between an autonomous agent and a very real mistake.
