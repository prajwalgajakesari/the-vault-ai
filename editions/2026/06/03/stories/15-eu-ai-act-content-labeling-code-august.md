# EU Finalizes AI Content Labeling Code of Practice Ahead of August 2026 Deadline

*With two months until Article 50 transparency obligations take effect, the European Commission delivers a voluntary framework that tells AI providers how to watermark their outputs and deployers how to label their deepfakes.*

BRUSSELS -- The European Commission has finalized its Code of Practice on marking and labeling AI-generated content, delivering a voluntary but politically significant framework just weeks before the August 2, 2026 enforcement deadline for Article 50 of the EU AI Act. The code, drafted by 17 independent experts and refined through two public drafts and hundreds of stakeholder submissions, now gives companies across the 27-member bloc a concrete roadmap for transparency compliance.

The stakes are considerable. Article 50 violations carry administrative fines of up to 15 million euros or 3 percent of worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher. And because the code is expected to become the de facto reference point for regulators and courts assessing compliance, its voluntary label belies its practical weight.

## Two Sections, Two Audiences

The code is structured around a clean division of labor between the companies that build AI systems and those that use them.

Section 1 targets providers -- the OpenAIs, Mistrals, and Googles of the world -- and addresses the technical machinery of marking and detection. At its core is a revised two-layered approach: providers must embed secured metadata, following open standards such as the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) framework, and apply imperceptible watermarking interwoven with the content itself. Fingerprinting and logging are available as optional fallback mechanisms for cases where active marking fails. Providers must also offer a free detection interface -- an API or public tool -- that lets users verify, with confidence scores, whether content was generated or manipulated by their system.

The approach is deliberately multi-layered. The Commission acknowledges that C2PA metadata alone is insufficient because it can be stripped through screenshots, social media uploads, or file format conversion. Watermarks are more resilient but not immune to sophisticated attacks. Together, the two layers create defense in depth.

Section 2 speaks to deployers -- the businesses, consultants, and organizations that use generative AI systems professionally. Their obligation is disclosure: when AI produces a deepfake or generates text published on matters of public interest, users must be told. The section lays out modality-specific rules. Real-time video requires a persistent icon alongside an opening disclaimer. Recorded video may rely on a disclaimer, icon, and end credits. Images must carry a clearly visible, fixed icon. Audio-only deepfakes need audible disclaimers, repeated for longer formats.

The second draft, published March 5, removed an earlier taxonomy that attempted to distinguish between fully AI-generated and AI-assisted content -- a distinction stakeholders found unworkable in practice. In its place, the code now focuses on design and placement requirements for icons, labels, and disclaimers, ensuring a minimum level of uniformity while giving signatories room to adapt.

## The EU Icon and Interim Measures

One of the code's more visible outcomes will be a standardized EU icon for AI-generated content. The annex to the second draft includes illustrative examples, and a task force has been proposed to develop a final, uniform, interactive version. Until that icon is ready, deployers may use an interim text label reading simply "AI."

The ambition is to create a universal visual shorthand that Europeans will learn to recognize across platforms and media types.

## Industry Pushback and Compromise

The code did not emerge without friction. The Information Technology Industry Council (ITI), representing major global technology companies, pushed throughout the drafting process for maximum flexibility. In a December 2025 position paper, ITI argued that the code should avoid prescribing specific marking techniques, warning that a rigid approach would become quickly outdated. The group also cautioned against over-labeling, arguing that flagging every minor use of AI would cause label fatigue and paradoxically make unlabeled content appear more trustworthy.

The final code reflects many of those concerns. Compared to the first draft, the second version streamlined commitments, introduced optional elements, and provided greater flexibility. The Commission framed the changes as reducing compliance burden while promoting open standards.

Creative industries secured their own accommodations. Artistic, satirical, and fictional works receive proportionate treatment: disclosures must not interfere with the work's integrity, though AI involvement must still be acknowledged. Content under human editorial review can rely on existing procedures, provided the deployer documents who carries editorial responsibility.

## The Deepfake Problem

The code addresses only lawful deepfakes -- content that is not itself illegal. Non-consensual intimate imagery, defamatory fabrications, and terrorist content fall under separate enforcement regimes, primarily the Digital Services Act and national criminal law. That distinction was underscored in early 2026 when French authorities investigated non-consensual deepfakes generated using Grok, referring the images to prosecutors under the DSA -- a reminder that some synthetic media problems require content removal, not labeling.

## What Companies Must Do Now

Enterprises that produce synthetic content face a narrow window. With the code finalized in early June and enforcement beginning August 2, companies have roughly two months to bring their operations into compliance. The operational program includes inventorying all synthetic content production, mapping workflows to Article 50 obligations, implementing the required marking stack, deploying user-facing labels, and establishing audit trails.

A limited six-month grace period may apply to AI systems already on the market by the August deadline, but new systems released after that date face the full requirements immediately.

The participation of major AI providers as signatories will be closely watched. Once companies like OpenAI, Google, and Meta commit to specific technical standards -- particularly C2PA for metadata -- those choices will set the market baseline, making it difficult for smaller players to deviate. The code may be voluntary in name, but the market dynamics it sets in motion are anything but.

National competent authorities across EU member states will conduct market surveillance, investigate complaints, and perform audits, with the European AI Board coordinating enforcement across borders.
