# NVIDIA Survey Shows AI Delivering Clear Return on Investment Across Healthcare and Life Sciences

Artificial intelligence in healthcare has crossed the threshold from promising experiment to proven business driver, according to NVIDIA"s second annual State of AI in Healthcare and Life Sciences survey. The report, published in February 2026 and covering more than 600 industry professionals, found that 70 percent of organizations are now actively deploying AI in their operations, up from 63 percent in the previous year. That seven-percentage-point jump marks a decisive shift: AI is no longer a pilot project confined to innovation labs. It is reshaping revenue, cutting costs, and redefining how care is delivered.

## The Money Trail

The financial picture is striking. Among management respondents, 85 percent said AI has increased their annual revenue and 80 percent reported reduced annual costs. These are not marginal gains. Forty-four percent of respondents said AI boosted revenue by more than 10 percent, with smaller companies benefiting disproportionately -- 56 percent of small firms reported revenue growth exceeding that threshold. On the cost side, 35 percent of all respondents and 44 percent of small companies reported cost reductions greater than 10 percent.

That bottom-line confidence is translating directly into budget commitments. Eighty-five percent of respondents said their AI spending would increase in 2026, with 46 percent planning jumps exceeding 10 percent. Only 3 percent anticipated cuts. The spending priorities themselves tell a story about maturity: last year, 47 percent of respondents said their top priority was identifying new AI use cases, but that figure dropped to 37 percent this year. In its place, 47 percent now cite optimizing existing AI workflows and production cycles as their primary focus -- a clear signal that the industry is shifting from exploration to execution.

## Where AI Is Delivering Returns

The survey breaks down ROI by sector, revealing that different corners of healthcare are finding value in different applications.

In medical technology, the clearest wins come from imaging. Fifty-seven percent of respondents in this segment reported measurable returns from AI-assisted radiology and diagnostics -- think radiologists using algorithms to flag areas of concern on scans, working faster and more accurately than they could alone.

For pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies, drug discovery is the headline use case. Forty-six percent of respondents in this segment said AI for drug discovery and development ranked among their top ROI drivers. The ability to screen molecular compounds, predict protein interactions, and accelerate clinical trial design is compressing timelines that once stretched across a decade.

Digital healthcare providers pointed to virtual health assistants and chatbots as their top ROI source, cited by 37 percent of respondents. Meanwhile, 39 percent of payers and providers -- hospitals, primary care practices, and insurance companies -- named administrative tasks and workflow optimization as their leading area of return. It is less glamorous than genomics or drug design, but automating scheduling, documentation, coding, and care coordination adds up fast.

"Over the next 12-18 months, the most visible and scalable impact of AI will come from logistics and administrative streamlining," said John Nosta, president of NostaLab, a healthcare think tank. "That"s where adoption curves are already steep -- scheduling, documentation, coding, utilization management and care coordination."

## Generative AI and the Rise of Agents

The technology mix powering these gains has shifted notably. Generative AI and large language models now rank as the top workload across the industry, cited by 69 percent of respondents -- up sharply from 54 percent the prior year. Data analytics held second place, with predictive analytics third.

The most intriguing newcomer is agentic AI. Added to the survey for the first time this year, AI agents -- autonomous systems capable of reasoning through multi-step tasks -- are already in use or under assessment by 47 percent of respondents. Twenty-two percent have deployed them, and another 19 percent plan to within the next year. Their top applications include knowledge management and retrieval (46 percent), literature review and analysis (38 percent), and internal process optimization (37 percent). In pharma and biotech specifically, 55 percent are using agentic AI for literature review, and nearly half deploy it for drug discovery and biomarker identification.

## Open Source as Strategic Pillar

Perhaps surprisingly for an industry defined by regulatory caution, 82 percent of respondents said open source software and models are moderately to extremely important to their AI strategy. The appeal lies in customization: healthcare organizations can fine-tune foundation models for domain-specific applications rather than relying on general-purpose tools.

"Open models will shape the intellectual field," said Nosta. "They are essential for exploration and for keeping the field honest. But in clinical environments where safety, liability and accountability are nonnegotiable, proprietary systems will remain necessary for validation, integration and trust. The key insight here is that discovery will be open, and deployment will demand stewardship."

## What Comes Next

Challenges remain. Smaller organizations report budget constraints (40 percent) and insufficient training data (33 percent) as top barriers, while larger enterprises cite data privacy and security (39 percent) and regulatory and ethical issues (37 percent). For agentic AI specifically, 40 percent say compliance with healthcare regulations -- HIPAA, FDA approval processes, GDPR -- strongly influences implementation strategies.

But the trajectory is unmistakable. With adoption at 70 percent and climbing, budgets expanding across the board, and measurable returns flowing from imaging suites, drug labs, and back offices alike, healthcare AI has moved beyond proof of concept. The question is no longer whether AI works in healthcare. It is how fast the rest of the industry can catch up.

"Healthcare organizations that successfully integrate AI are those that explicitly fund and prioritize evaluation as a core operational function, ensuring AI delivers measurable improvements in safety, quality and patient care over time," said Dr. Annabelle Painter, clinical AI strategy lead at Visiba U.K.
