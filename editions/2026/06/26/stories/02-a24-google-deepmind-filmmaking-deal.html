Google Invests $75 Million in A24 for a DeepMind AI Filmmaking Partnership

4 min read 812 words 4 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Google has taken its first-ever equity stake in a Hollywood studio, investing roughly $75 million in A24 for a non-exclusive, multi-year DeepMind research partnership focused on AI filmmaking tools, even as A24's own filmmakers and fans revolt.

The studio that built its brand on protecting the artist has just invited Silicon Valley's most powerful AI lab inside the tent. On Monday, A24 and Google unveiled a research partnership that hands Google's DeepMind unit access to the independent studio's production process in exchange for roughly $75 million and a promise to build a new generation of AI filmmaking tools. According to the Wall Street Journal, the cash injection marks the first time Google has ever taken a financial stake in a Hollywood film studio, an investment roughly in line with what Thrive Capital put in during A24's last funding round.

The deal is structured as a multi-year, non-exclusive research collaboration rather than a content licensing arrangement. DeepMind researchers will embed alongside A24 and its tech arm, A24 Labs, using filmmaker feedback to prototype and refine tools, while the studio gains access to DeepMind's research and infrastructure. Crucially, the agreement explicitly walls off A24's content library and data: Google does not get access to the studio's films, and there is no mandate for A24 directors to use anything the partnership produces. The first application in development is an AI storyboard generator, a visual blueprint directors and crews consult before cameras roll, a tool A24 was reportedly already prototyping internally.

The program is being run by Scott Belsky, the Behance co-founder and longtime Adobe digital executive who joined A24 in early 2025 to oversee its digital initiatives. Belsky has tried to draw a sharp line between this effort and the consumer-facing image generators that have spooked the creative community. The new tools, he told the Wall Street Journal, "won't look anything like the prompted generation type of AI that people feel uncomfortable with." On the Google side, the framing is one of artist-first development. "We believe the best way to develop tools that empower artists is to work directly with them," DeepMind CEO and co-founder Demis Hassabis wrote in a blog post announcing the deal. "By collaborating with filmmakers and industry leaders like A24 from the beginning, we can build new AI features to support artists in authentic, meaningful storytelling that helps enable their creative vision."

The Hollywood-AI tension A24 just stepped into

The backlash was immediate and personal. A24 has spent more than a decade cultivating a fiercely loyal audience that views the studio as a bulwark against algorithmic, focus-grouped filmmaking, and many of those fans treated the Google deal as a betrayal. They flooded A24's Instagram and X accounts with comments calling the move "rancid" and threatening to cancel their support. Within days, A24 was forced to publicly defend itself. "This is a research partnership," communications representative Sophia Shin said in a statement first reported by Wired. "We're working side-by-side with DeepMind's researchers to learn, iterate, and build, having an active hand in shaping new tools and workflows." The studio's core argument: better to influence the technology than to have it imposed later. "We'd rather have a seat at the table than on the sidelines."

The friction is not just external. Some of A24's own filmmakers have been openly hostile to generative AI. "Backrooms" director Kane Parsons recently called the technology "a symptom of broader cultural and economic rot," telling The Australian that "if I could snap my fingers and make generative AI disappear forever, I probably would." That tension is the real story here. A24's brand equity is built on trust with artists, and a $75 million check from Google tests how far that trust stretches. By accepting it, A24 is betting that proximity buys influence, that being a co-author of the tools is safer than being their eventual subject.

The deal also lands amid a broader stampede of studios pairing off with AI firms. Lionsgate expanded its partnership with Runway this month to build AI-generated franchises from its IP, Netflix acquired Ben Affleck's AI filmmaking startup InterPositive earlier this year, and Disney flirted with OpenAI even as it sued Midjourney and MiniMax for copyright infringement. A24's version is notably more guarded, no IP handed over, no obligation to use the output, but it signals that even the industry's most artist-protective player now sees AI participation as unavoidable.

What to watch next

The first real test will be the storyboard tool itself: whether working directors actually adopt it, or whether it sits unused as the opt-in nature of the deal allows. Watch, too, for how A24's filmmaker roster responds, given that directors like Parsons and the "Heretic" duo have publicly drawn anti-AI lines in their own credits. And the structural question looms largest: if a $75 million, data-protected, non-exclusive arrangement still triggers this much fan revolt, the deal becomes a referendum on whether any Hollywood-AI partnership can be built without eroding the very creative credibility that made the studio worth investing in.

"This partnership exists because we want to dictate what tools get built for artists, and so they have a voice in shaping them rather than having tools handed to them. We'd rather have a seat at the table than on the sidelines."
- Sophia Shin, Communications representative, A24
