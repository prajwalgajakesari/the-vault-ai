Neura Robotics Lands Up to $1.4 Billion From Amazon and Nvidia to Mass-Produce Humanoids

4 min read 954 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

NEURA Robotics' $1.4 billion Series C — backed by Amazon, NVIDIA, Tether and the European Investment Bank — is among the largest ever for a full-stack robotics company and positions the German startup as Europe's leading humanoid contender.

Europe's Humanoid Champion Just Raised the Biggest Robotics Round in History

A German startup founded in a small town in Baden-Württemberg has just secured what it calls — and industry observers are hard-pressed to dispute — the largest capital raise ever for a full-stack robotics company. On June 10, 2026, NEURA Robotics announced a Series C of up to $1.4 billion backed by Amazon, NVIDIA, Qualcomm, Tether, Bosch, Schaeffler, and the European Investment Bank. The round values NEURA at approximately $7 billion and sets the stage for a bold ambition: five million robots deployed worldwide by 2030.

The announcement signals that the race to dominate physical AI is no longer just a Silicon Valley or Shenzhen story.

The Round: Structure, Scale, and Who's Writing the Checks

The $1.4 billion figure is a ceiling, not a lump sum. A portion of the capital is milestone-contingent, meaning NEURA will unlock the full amount only as it hits specific operational and deployment targets. The structure blends equity from strategic and financial investors with participation from the European Investment Bank — the EIB's involvement representing an unusual injection of quasi-public European capital into a private humanoid robotics play.

The investor list reads like a cross-section of the global AI supply chain. Amazon's Nafea Bshara, Vice President and Distinguished Engineer, noted the investment reflects "our shared conviction in the transformative potential of Physical AI," with Amazon supplying cloud infrastructure via Bedrock, SageMaker, and its Trainium AI chips. NVIDIA contributes its accelerator ecosystem. Qualcomm's EVP Nakul Duggal emphasized on-device intelligence: "Robotics is one of the most demanding edge AI use cases, where systems must perceive, reason and act instantly, reliably and on device for safety-critical applications."

Tether, the stablecoin issuer turned tech investor, led the round — a notable choice that signals how capital from the crypto economy is increasingly flowing into hard-tech ventures. Industrial partners Bosch and Schaeffler bring manufacturing DNA; Bosch CEO Stefan Hartung cited "significant growth opportunities in the field of humanoid robotics," while Schaeffler CEO Klaus Rosenfeld pointed to his company's product families and manufacturing expertise as positioning it "at the heart of this crucial technology."

What NEURA Actually Builds

Founded in 2019 by David Reger in Metzingen, Germany, NEURA has assembled a portfolio of cognitive robots spanning industrial, commercial, and consumer use cases.

Its flagship humanoid, the 4NE-1, is designed to work physically alongside humans, equipped with advanced proprioception and multimodal sensing. The MiPA is a household-oriented robot aimed at consumer and care applications. The industrial lineup includes MAiRA, LARA, and MAV — arm and mobile platform variants targeting factory floors, logistics centers, and healthcare facilities.

Binding the hardware together is the Neuraverse, an open software ecosystem where robots continuously exchange learned skills and real-world data across deployments. NEURA is also rolling out NEURA Gyms — large-scale physical training environments that combine real-world sensor interaction, simulation, and multimodal learning pipelines. Think of them as data centers for embodied intelligence.

The company already has more than $1 billion in orders on its books.

Reger's Vision: Physical AI as the Next Computing Platform

NEURA's CEO has been consistent in his framing of the opportunity. "The future of AI will not only live on screens," Reger said in the announcement. "It will move, interact, learn and work beside us in the real world." He has also pushed back against the notion that transformative AI companies can only emerge from the United States: "Many believed globally relevant AI infrastructure companies could only emerge from Silicon Valley. We believe the next generation of AI leaders can emerge anywhere in the world where there is enough vision, engineering talent and execution speed."

NEURA is pitching itself not just as a robotics company but as AI infrastructure — the physical-world equivalent of a cloud platform.

The Humanoid Funding Race Heats Up

NEURA's round is the latest and largest salvo in a broader surge of capital into humanoid and physical AI. Billion-dollar commitments have stacked up across 2025 and 2026: Figure AI, Physical Intelligence (Pi), Apptronik, and Agility Robotics have all raised major rounds, largely from US-based investors.

What makes NEURA distinctive is its European base and explicit "full-stack" architecture — controlling sensors, actuators, compute, and the learning platform. Most competitors rely on third-party components for at least part of the stack.

The EIB's participation is noteworthy in geopolitical terms. Nicola Beer, the bank's Vice President, framed the investment explicitly as a strategic move: "By backing NEURA Robotics, the European Investment Bank is putting serious European firepower behind the next wave of physical AI and cognitive robotics." As the EU grapples with technological dependency on US and Chinese AI infrastructure, NEURA represents a rare European contender at the frontier.

NEURA's five-million-robot target by 2030 implies a manufacturing scale-up that will require the industrial partnerships — with Bosch, Schaeffler, and presumably others — that the company has been systematically assembling.

What to Watch Next

Several near-term indicators will reveal whether this round translates into execution. Watch for large-scale deployment contracts with named enterprise customers — the $1 billion orderbook needs to convert into live revenue. The rollout of NEURA Gyms will be a meaningful signal of the company's ability to build data infrastructure that underpins long-term platform defensibility.

The milestone-contingent structure means capital disbursement tracks against performance — slippage in deployment timelines could complicate the fundraise's final close. Tether's lead position will also draw scrutiny; its strategic value in a robotics context beyond capital remains to be demonstrated.

Most broadly, watch whether NEURA's round catalyzes additional European deep-tech investment, or whether it remains an outlier in a landscape still dominated by US players. The answer will say a great deal about Europe's ambitions in the physical AI era.
