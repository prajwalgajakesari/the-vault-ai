## CNN Sues Perplexity as the Publisher-vs-AI Copyright War Widens to Nine Plaintiffs

The lawsuit the AI industry had been bracing for arrived on May 28, when CNN filed a 54-page federal complaint against the "answer engine" startup Perplexity AI in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York. The case, Cable News Network Inc. v. Perplexity AI, Inc. (No. 1:26-cv-04427), alleges that Perplexity unlawfully scraped more than 17,000 CNN stories, photos, videos, and other works — all without payment or authorization.

CNN's filing is believed to be the first copyright suit by any television network in the AI era. It arrives as the conflict between publishers and AI platforms reaches a new threshold: at least nine organizations now have active suits against Perplexity, and more than 50 copyright cases against AI companies are pending in U.S. federal courts.

## What CNN Says Perplexity Did

The complaint lays out a two-track theory of harm. On copyright, CNN argues that Perplexity's crawler scraped its content — ignoring robots.txt after licensing negotiations collapsed — and fed it into an AI search index that returns "identical or substantially similar" material, replacing the original rather than linking to it.

On trademark, the complaint targets Perplexity's former ads promoting its ability to help users skip the "extra steps and clicks" required to access news, and specifically its "Comet Plus" subscription tier, which Perplexity advertised as providing access to CNN's premium content despite no such licensing relationship existing.

"Perplexity's use infringes CNN's exclusive rights in its federally registered trademarks, and has caused and is likely to cause confusion, mistake, or deception as to whether the articles Perplexity provides are associated or affiliated with, or are sponsored, endorsed, or approved by CNN," the complaint reads. "Perplexity's use is intended to reap the benefit of consumers' trust in CNN."

A CNN spokesperson was direct about the stakes: "The public rely on high-quality news journalism reported by human beings to understand their world, which is frequently dangerous and expensive to produce. Commercial operators can and must pay to make use of it. We prefer that they do so through sensible licensing arrangements, but if they refuse to do that as Perplexity has so far refused to do, they will have to pay through legal damages. There is no free option."

CNN is seeking statutory damages and an injunction barring Perplexity from using its content.

## The Growing Plaintiff List

CNN's filing extends a pileup that has been building for over a year. The full roster of organizations now suing Perplexity reads like a media industry directory: The New York Times, News Corp and Dow Jones (publisher of the Wall Street Journal), the New York Post, the Chicago Tribune, Encyclopedia Britannica, Merriam-Webster, Reddit, and Japan's Yomiuri Shimbun. Each suit carries its own fact pattern, but the common thread is the same: Perplexity is accused of harvesting copyrighted material, synthesizing it into AI-generated answers, and monetizing the result without compensating the creators.

The breadth of plaintiffs — spanning daily newspapers, reference publishers, a social platform, a foreign press giant, and now a television network — signals that these are not industry-specific grievances but a coordinated challenge to Perplexity's core business model.

The company is not without allies. Time, USA Today, Le Monde, and Der Spiegel have all signed licensing deals with Perplexity, demonstrating that a negotiated path exists. CNN's complaint makes clear that it tried to walk that path and was rebuffed.

## Perplexity's Defense

Perplexity did not respond to requests for comment on the CNN suit. The company's most substantive public statement came in response to the earlier Dow Jones lawsuit: a spokesperson called that filing "an adversarial posture between media and tech that is — while depressingly familiar — fundamentally shortsighted, unnecessary and self-defeating." The company has also argued, more simply, that "you can't copyright facts."

Perplexity frames itself as an index and synthesis tool, analogous to a search engine rather than a publisher. But its legal exposure runs deeper than a traditional search engine's, because users who get synthesized answers rarely need to click through to the original source — the core economic harm publishers allege.

## Why This Case Is Different From Training-Data Suits

The Perplexity litigation sits in a distinct legal category from suits targeting companies like OpenAI and Google. Those cases ask whether ingesting text to train a model constitutes fair use — a question about events deep in the past, in compute clusters far from consumers.

Perplexity's suits are about what happens in real time, at the moment of query. The allegation is that its product actively fetches, reproduces, and displays copyrighted material as the user watches. That is a harder case for Perplexity to defend under fair use doctrine, because the output substitutes for the original rather than transforming or commenting on it. If courts side with publishers, every query touching a news story becomes a potential liability — and Perplexity's cost structure could become untenable without mandatory licensing agreements.

## What to Watch

Three signals will define the next chapter. First, whether CNN and Perplexity enter mediation before discovery — negotiations broke down once, but a federal complaint changes the pressure. Second, whether the nine plaintiff cases get consolidated in New York, which could accelerate a ruling on the "answer engine" liability theory. Third, whether Perplexity's fundraising holds under sustained legal costs.

CNN's dual-track approach — copyright plus trademark — could prove strategically important if fair-use defenses blunt the copyright claims. It may become a template for the next wave of plaintiffs.
