Accenture Bets on Agentic Customer Service With Strategic Investment in Netomi

The world's largest IT consultancy has decided that the future of customer service will be run by software agents, and it is putting money behind a startup it believes can build that future. On April 30, 2026, Accenture announced it had invested, through Accenture Ventures, in Netomi, an enterprise customer-experience AI platform, and signed a strategic partnership aimed at helping large enterprises rebuild their service operations around agentic AI.

The investment landed alongside a much larger headline number: Netomi disclosed that it had raised $110 million in a Series C round led by Accenture Ventures, with participation from Adobe Ventures, WndrCo, SLW, NAVER Ventures, Metis Strategy and Fin Capital. The raise brings Netomi's total funding to more than $160 million and positions the company as one of the better-capitalized players in a crowded race to automate enterprise customer interactions. Accenture declined to disclose the size or terms of its own stake.

The deal

What Accenture is buying is less a financial position than a distribution channel — in both directions. The partnership embeds Netomi's agentic platform directly into Accenture's customer-experience and service practice, while giving Accenture's consultants the playbooks, training and direct platform access to deploy it inside the world's largest companies. In Accenture's framing, Netomi's technology slots into a client's existing tools and customer touchpoints "without operational disruption," a phrase that speaks directly to the chief anxiety of any enterprise buyer weighing an AI overhaul.

At the center of Netomi's product is a conversational AI platform with a no-code orchestration layer that lets companies run what the firm describes as a coordinated system of AI agents. Those agents are designed to anticipate customer needs, take action across channels and operate within governance and brand-compliance guardrails — the unglamorous but decisive requirement for any system touching regulated, high-volume customer traffic. Netomi says its agents work across chat, email and voice, handling everything from routine requests to complex, multi-step workflows.

The customer roster is the company's strongest selling point. Global brands including Delta Air Lines, United Airlines, MetLife, Paramount, DraftKings, the NBA and Ingram Micro already use Netomi to interact with customers, and the platform powers the United Airlines chat assistant inside the airline's mobile app. Netomi describes itself as the agentic CX platform built "for the highest-stakes environments — operating at massive scale, under regulatory oversight, where failure is not an option." That positioning — reliability over novelty — is precisely what Accenture's enterprise clients tend to pay for.

Why a consulting giant is moving now

Accenture's executives framed the bet around a stark statistic from the firm's own research: 87% of consumers say they are likely to avoid a brand after just a single negative experience. In an era of rising support volumes and thinning patience, that number reframes customer service from a cost center into a brand-survival problem — exactly the kind of expensive, enterprise-wide transformation that consultancies are built to sell.

"Agentic AI is opening an entirely new chapter for customer experience," said Ndidi Oteh, CEO of Accenture Song, the company's marketing and experience arm. "One where brands can respond with greater empathy, consistency and intelligence at every touchpoint. Netomi's platform doesn't just make service faster; it strengthens the connection between people and the brands they trust."

Netomi's leadership cast the tie-up as a way to put its software in front of buyers it could never reach alone. "By partnering with Accenture, we're equipping the firm that designs, implements, and operates customer experience inside the world's largest enterprises with direct access to the Netomi platform — along with the playbooks and training that enable deployment at scale," said Puneet Mehta, founder and CEO of Netomi. "Bringing the two together creates an end-to-end system for transforming customer experience with AI — and will define the standard for how that transformation happens going forward."

John Bolze, Accenture Song's global AI solutions lead, was blunter about the appeal: Netomi's platform "acts as an intelligent extension of the human agents, improving response times and enhancing overall performance." The framing matters. Accenture is selling augmentation, not replacement — a message calibrated for executives wary of headlines about AI gutting their workforces.

Analysis: the scramble to own the service layer

The deal is best understood as a land grab. Customer service is one of the few enterprise functions where agentic AI has a clear, measurable business case — deflected tickets, faster resolutions, lower headcount cost per interaction — and where the technology is mature enough to deploy at scale today. That combination has set off a race among the global services giants to own the layer of software that sits between a brand and its customers.

Whoever controls that layer controls an enormous, recurring relationship. Accenture, with roughly 786,000 employees and a consulting franchise built on enterprise complexity, has structural advantages that pure-play AI vendors lack: the relationships, the implementation muscle and the credibility to walk a Fortune 500 board through a multi-year transformation. What it has historically lacked is differentiated, owned technology to anchor those engagements. Strategic investments like the one in Netomi — and the steady drumbeat of similar Accenture Ventures deals across the AI stack — are how the firm is buying its way into that position rather than building from scratch.

For Netomi, the validation is real but so is the dependency. Jeffrey Katzenberg, the DreamWorks co-founder and WndrCo managing partner who joined Netomi's board, called Accenture "the firm that understands enterprise complexity better than anyone on earth." Early backers reportedly include AI pioneers Greg Brockman, Demis Hassabis and Mustafa Suleyman — a pedigree that signals the company is taken seriously well beyond the consulting world. Yet tethering a startup's growth to a single distribution partner concentrates risk: Accenture's roadmap, sales priorities and competing partnerships will now shape Netomi's trajectory.

What to watch next

The first signal will be proof of deployment. Partnerships of this kind are announced with fanfare and judged by reference customers, so watch for named, measurable rollouts — ideally inside the high-stakes accounts Netomi already counts as clients — within the next two to three quarters. A second signal is exclusivity: Accenture has cultivated relationships across the agentic-AI ecosystem, and how much it leans on Netomi versus rival platforms will reveal how strategic this bet really is. Finally, watch the competitive response. With Adobe also participating in the round and every major consultancy chasing the same service-automation budgets, the question is no longer whether enterprises will hand customer service to AI agents, but which vendor-and-integrator pairing they will trust to run it.
