Salesforce is buying its way deeper into the agentic-AI race. The customer-relationship-management giant announced Monday that it has signed a definitive agreement to acquire Fin, the AI customer-service platform formerly known as Intercom, for roughly **$3.6 billion** — one of the largest enterprise-software deals of the year and the biggest ever for an Irish-founded technology company.

The acquisition, unveiled June 15, hands Salesforce a battle-tested AI agent capable of resolving complex customer queries across live chat, email, WhatsApp, SMS, phone, and Slack. More importantly, it gives the company a ready-made, fast-to-deploy product to bolt onto **Agentforce**, the AI-agent platform that has become the centerpiece of CEO Marc Benioff's pitch to investors and customers alike.

"Fin brings proven agent technology, a deep commitment to customer success, and an incredible AI team that will complement Agentforce with powerful service agent capabilities," Benioff said in a statement announcing the deal. "Together, we'll help companies of every size seize this opportunity — accelerating time to value with trusted agents that deliver measurable outcomes at scale."

## The deal terms

Under the agreement, Salesforce will acquire Fin for approximately $3.6 billion in cash, subject to customary purchase-price adjustments. The transaction is expected to close in the fourth quarter of Salesforce's fiscal year 2027 — which, owing to the company's offset fiscal calendar, falls in the first few months of calendar 2027.

Notably, Salesforce is keeping Fin's leadership in place. Co-founder and CEO **Eoghan McCabe** will continue running the business, and co-founder Des Traynor will remain in charge of research and development. That continuity signals Salesforce wants Fin's product velocity and engineering culture intact rather than absorbed and diluted.

Fin began life 15 years ago as Intercom, the customer-messaging startup that once raised $125 million to challenge incumbents like Salesforce. The company rebranded to Fin to mark its pivot from a messaging tool into an AI-first agent company. At the heart of that transformation is **Apex**, Fin's proprietary AI model purpose-built for customer support, which the companies say has posted "industry-leading resolution rates" that outperform top commercially available frontier models.

McCabe framed the sale not as a surrender but as an accelerant. "With the resources of Salesforce this will only accelerate," he wrote in a post on X. "And yet little will practically change. I'll still be CEO, Des will still be running R&D, we'll both still be committed to continuing to lead this category." In Salesforce's release, he called the deal a "major win" for consumers, adding that Fin's "technology has defined this category and set the new standards for what great customer service looks like today."

## Why this matters

The Fin deal is the clearest signal yet that the enterprise-AI war has shifted from chatbots to autonomous agents — software that doesn't just suggest answers but resolves problems end-to-end. For Salesforce, customer service is the beachhead. It is the use case where AI agents can demonstrate hard, measurable ROI: deflected tickets, faster resolution times, and lower headcount costs.

Agentforce has been a rare bright spot in Salesforce's growth story. The platform reached **$1.2 billion in annual recurring revenue in the first quarter of fiscal 2027, up 205% year over year**, according to figures Salesforce cited in announcing the Fin acquisition. The company's framing is telling: Fin's packaged, off-the-shelf offerings and proprietary models will complement Agentforce's "deeply customizable platform" with "fast-to-value deployment options" aimed squarely at smaller businesses and commercial organizations that need to launch quickly and integrate with existing systems.

In other words, Salesforce is buying speed-to-market. Agentforce is powerful but bespoke; many customers — especially mid-market and SMB buyers — want something they can switch on in days, not quarters. Fin fills that gap, and it does so with a product already deployed at scale across thousands of support teams.

The acquisition also caps a relentless run of AI dealmaking by Salesforce. Last year the company agreed to buy Convergence.ai to accelerate Agentforce's development, and in February it moved to acquire Cimulate, a startup focused on AI-powered product discovery and agentic commerce. The Fin purchase dwarfs both — a statement that Benioff intends to win the agent race through acquisition as aggressively as through in-house engineering. It arrives amid a broader market frenzy: rival enterprise vendors, hyperscalers, and a wave of well-funded startups are all racing to own the "AI agent" layer of the corporate stack just as that category begins to generate real revenue.

There is a competitive subtext, too. Fin spent years positioning itself as a direct rival to Salesforce in customer service. By absorbing the challenger, Salesforce neutralizes a fast-rising competitor while acquiring the very model and team that made it a threat — a classic consolidation play in a category that is consolidating fast.

## What to watch next

The first question is regulatory. A $3.6 billion combination of two customer-service platforms may draw antitrust scrutiny, though Fin's relatively modest market share against incumbents could keep the review routine. Investors will also watch whether Salesforce can retain Fin's engineering talent — the Apex and Operator teams are the real asset here, and AI researchers are notoriously mobile.

Beyond the close, the integration math is what counts. Can Salesforce graft Fin's fast-deploy packaging onto Agentforce without slowing either down? Will the combined offering accelerate that 205% ARR growth, or will overlap and customer confusion blunt it? And with rivals circling the same agentic-AI prize, the Fin deal may be less an endpoint than the opening move in a much larger consolidation wave. For now, Salesforce has made its bet clear: in the age of AI agents, owning the agent is worth $3.6 billion.
