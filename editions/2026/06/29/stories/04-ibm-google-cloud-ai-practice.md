# IBM and Google Cloud Forge a Multi-Billion-Dollar Enterprise AI Alliance Built on Gemini

Big Blue just placed a very large bet on a model it does not own. On June 4, IBM and Google Cloud announced a new global Google Cloud Practice that will field thousands of Google Cloud-certified IBM consultants and forward-deployed engineers to push Google's Gemini models into the world's most regulated industries. IBM is calling it a "multi-billion-dollar opportunity," and the framing tells you everything about where the enterprise AI war is actually being fought: not over whose model is smartest, but over who has the bodies to wire those models into a bank's core systems without breaking them.

## The Deal

The partnership fuses two assets. On one side is IBM Consulting Advantage, IBM's AI-powered delivery platform for designing and shipping agents and industry workflows. On the other is Google Cloud's Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, along with its security and data stack. The pitch to clients is a single throat to choke for moving AI "beyond pilots" and into production while modernizing the aging core systems underneath.

IBM is building a portfolio of industry-specific AI agents on Consulting Advantage and optimizing them for Gemini Enterprise. The named targets are expansive: banking, government, retail, telecommunications, energy, security, insurance, and life sciences. IBM consultants will now design, build, and govern enterprise-grade agents directly on Google Cloud, combining IBM's pre-built assets and reusable agents with Google's agent runtime, governance controls, and enterprise safety features.

The companies are not starting from zero. They cited their work with Airbus, where IBM and Google Cloud helped spin two aerospace businesses into fully independent operations in under 18 months, updating more than 100 critical systems across engineering, manufacturing, and customer service. That is the template: deep, messy, regulated transformation that no foundation model can do on its own.

## What the Executives Are Saying

"Enterprises are facing one of the most complex modernization cycles in decades," said Mohamad Ali, Senior Vice President and Head of IBM Consulting. "By expanding our work with Google Cloud, we're giving clients a clearer and more reliable path to scale AI across their business, combining deep industry expertise, hybrid-cloud modernization, and an AI-first delivery platform."

Google framed the move as a supply problem as much as a demand one. "This partnership significantly expands the pool of expert Google Cloud consultants in the market to meet surging demand for AI," said Kevin Ichhpurani, President, Global Partner Ecosystem at Google Cloud. "By combining Google's agentic infrastructure with IBM's deep industry expertise and proven delivery frameworks, we are ensuring joint customers can move beyond pilots to deploy and govern production-grade AI agents across their entire cloud environment."

That "move beyond pilots" line is the tell. The dirty secret of 2026's enterprise AI boom is that most corporate deployments are still demos. The companies that can convert proofs-of-concept into governed, compliant, revenue-touching production systems are the ones who get paid.

## The Land Grab Around Gemini

IBM is not Google's only new soldier, and the timing is no coincidence. In April, Google Cloud committed $750 million to accelerate partners' agentic AI development, seeding the ecosystem with incentives. Four days after the IBM news, on June 8, NTT DATA expanded its own Google Cloud collaboration, launching a Gemini Enterprise practice with a goal of 5,000 certified experts and a joint roadmap to co-build up to 500 AI agents across banking, insurance, manufacturing, and retail.

Cognizant has stood up a dedicated Gemini Enterprise practice and shipped an agentic retail customer-experience product on the platform. Accenture expanded its Google partnership through a Gemini Enterprise Acceleration Program, bringing thousands of AI-skilled engineers and forward-deployed engineers to the table. Google is embedding its own forward-deployed engineers alongside Accenture, Capgemini, Cognizant, Deloitte, HCLTech, PwC, and TCS.

In other words, Google is not picking a single integrator. It is arming every major systems integrator at once and letting them compete for the same accounts, multiplying its reach into enterprises where its sales force has historically trailed AWS and Microsoft.

## The Real Battle: Who Controls Deployment

The strategic logic snaps into focus when you look across the field. Microsoft is running the opposite playbook: vertical integration. Its governed agent stack is native to Azure AI Foundry and fused with Microsoft 365 Copilot, complete with its own MAI models, a policy engine, and an identity fabric meant to make the model, the platform, and the workflow data feel like a single, sticky procurement decision. OpenAI, for its part, has assembled a Frontier Alliances roster, including McKinsey, BCG, Accenture, and Capgemini.

Google's bet is that enterprises do not want to be locked into one vendor's identity layer, and that the integrators, not the model labs, own the client relationships that matter. By turning IBM, NTT DATA, Cognizant, and Accenture into a distributed Gemini sales-and-delivery army, Google trades some control for enormous reach.

For IBM, the calculus is sharper still. After spinning off Kyndryl and reshaping itself around hybrid cloud and consulting, IBM lives or dies on services revenue. Hitching its consulting engine to Gemini lets it sell transformation work without betting the franchise on its own watsonx models, which it is still integrating here via watsonx Orchestrate and watsonx.data. It is a hedge dressed as an alliance, and a direct shot at Accenture, the consulting giant that has set the pace on generative-AI bookings.

## What to Watch

The numbers to track are not model benchmarks but billable headcount and conversion rates: how fast IBM actually certifies "thousands" of consultants, and how many of these engagements graduate from pilot to production. Watch, too, for channel conflict. With Google arming a half-dozen rivals on the same platform, margins could compress and accounts could get crowded. And watch the regulated verticals, banking, government, insurance, where IBM's relationships are deepest and where a single governance failure could sour the whole thesis. The model is increasingly a commodity. The consultants who can deploy it, safely and at scale, are not, and that is the prize everyone is now racing for.
