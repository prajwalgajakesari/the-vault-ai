# OpenAI Launches a Self-Serve Ads Manager Inside ChatGPT

OpenAI has crossed a line it spent years insisting it would never approach. The company that built ChatGPT into a household name has opened a self-serve Ads Manager, letting any U.S. business sign up, fund an account, and buy advertising directly inside the chatbot that roughly 1.1 billion people now use every month. With that move, a research lab famous for refusing to clutter its product with commercial messages has quietly become an advertising platform.

The rollout, which expanded into beta this spring, hands advertisers the full self-service toolkit that marketers expect from Google or Meta: register as an advertiser, add payment details, set budgets, bids, and pacing, upload creative, launch campaigns, and watch performance roll in through a portal. OpenAI also stripped away the old $50,000 minimum that had walled off its earlier pilot, throwing the door open to small businesses and solo founders alike.

## From Pilot To Platform

OpenAI framed the launch as an extension of an existing program rather than a sudden pivot. "We're expanding ChatGPT ads with new ways for advertisers to buy and manage campaigns - built around our ads principles," the company wrote in a blog post announcing the changes.

The mechanics reveal how serious the effort is. In the first phase, advertisers bought ChatGPT ads on a CPM basis - paying per thousand impressions - as a way to gauge demand and early performance. The company has since added cost-per-click (CPC) bidding, letting advertisers tie spend to the actions people take after seeing an ad. That shift from brand-style impression buying to performance-style click buying is the clearest signal that OpenAI wants to compete for the same direct-response budgets that fuel search advertising.

Measurement came next. OpenAI introduced a Conversions API and pixel-based tracking so advertisers can see what happens after an ad engagement - a purchase, a lead, a sign-up. The company says advertisers receive aggregated performance insights without access to individual conversations, an attempt to square ad targeting with the privacy expectations of users who treat ChatGPT as a confidant.

The platform did not launch in a vacuum. Dentsu, Omnicom, Publicis, and WPP - the four holding companies that dominate global ad buying - are collaborating with OpenAI to support businesses purchasing ChatGPT ads. Adobe, Criteo, Kargo, Pacvue, and StackAdapt have joined as technology partners. That roster matters: it means the existing advertising-industrial complex is already plugging into ChatGPT's pipes.

## The Money Behind The Mission

The numbers explain the urgency. OpenAI is reportedly targeting roughly $2.5 billion in ad revenue this year and as much as $100 billion by 2030. For a company burning enormous sums on compute and model training, advertising is no longer a philosophical question - it is a structural necessity. ChatGPT's audience of about 1.1 billion monthly users is one of the largest captive attention pools ever assembled, and until now it has been entirely unmonetized at the ad level.

The landing page makes the pitch plainly, telling advertisers they can "reach people as they explore options, compare choices, and make decisions in ChatGPT, with relevant ads that fit naturally into the experience." That phrase - "explore options, compare choices, make decisions" - is the language of high-intent commercial search, the most valuable real estate in digital advertising. OpenAI is positioning conversational AI not as a sidebar to the buying journey but as the place where the journey happens.

It is worth noting the contrast across the industry. Anthropic, maker of Claude, has stated that its own products remain ad-free, a deliberate divergence from the path OpenAI is now taking. The two leading labs are betting on opposite business models for the same underlying technology.

## A New Optimization War

While OpenAI builds the supply side of conversational advertising, others are racing to help brands win the organic side. On June 17, Adobe announced Brand Visibility, an enterprise solution that fuses Semrush's AI visibility data with Adobe's content optimization tools to track how brands surface across ChatGPT, Google AI Mode, Microsoft Copilot, and Perplexity. The product draws on nearly 300 million real-world AI search prompts, which Adobe calls the largest global database of its kind.

"In a world where customers often interact with an AI tool before ever reaching a business's website, visibility is everything now," said Anil Chakravarthy, President of Adobe's Customer Experience Orchestration Business. He added that "early customer demand has exceeded expectations" following Adobe's Semrush acquisition.

The data underscores why brands are anxious. Adobe found that AI traffic to U.S. retail sites surged 1,324% between October 2024 and May 2026, and travel-sector AI traffic jumped 2,215% over the same window. As discovery migrates from blue links to chatbot answers, a new discipline - generative engine optimization, or GEO - is emerging to do for AI answers what SEO did for search rankings.

Together, the two launches sketch the shape of a maturing market. OpenAI is monetizing attention with paid placements; Adobe and Semrush are selling the tools to measure and influence the organic mentions that surround them. It is, in miniature, the entire search-advertising economy being rebuilt inside the chat window.

## What Comes Next

The open questions are familiar but unresolved. Will users tolerate ads inside a product they have come to trust for unvarnished answers? How will OpenAI keep sponsored content from corrupting the perceived neutrality of a chatbot's recommendations? And will advertisers find that a conversation converts better, or worse, than a search result?

What is no longer in question is the direction. With a self-serve Ads Manager, CPC bidding, conversion tracking, and the world's biggest agencies on board, OpenAI has formally entered the advertising business. The era of the ad-free chatbot, at least at OpenAI, is over.
