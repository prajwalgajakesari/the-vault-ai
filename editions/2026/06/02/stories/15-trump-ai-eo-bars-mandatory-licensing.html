# AI Executive Order Explicitly Bars Mandatory Licensing While Creating Federal Cybersecurity Clearinghouse

President Trump on Tuesday signed an executive order that simultaneously expands federal oversight of frontier AI systems and explicitly prohibits the government from ever requiring companies to obtain a license before releasing them -- a philosophical balancing act that reveals the deep tensions at the heart of Washington's AI debate.

The order, titled "Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security," asks AI companies to voluntarily submit their most powerful models for government testing up to 30 days before public release. But buried in the same section is a line that no prior executive order on technology has contained: "Nothing in this section shall be construed to authorize the creation of a mandatory governmental licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement for the development, publication, release, or distribution of new AI models."

It is a remarkable piece of political engineering -- an order that creates a new government review apparatus while building a firewall against its own expansion.

From 90 Days to 30: How Industry Lobbying Reshaped the Order

The executive order that Trump signed Tuesday is not the one his administration drafted. An earlier version, scheduled for a ceremony in late May with Silicon Valley CEOs in attendance, called for a 90-day voluntary review window. Trump pulled it at the last minute.

"We're leading China, we're leading everybody, and I don't want to do anything that's going to get in the way of that lead," Trump told reporters at the time.

Behind the scenes, former White House AI czar David Sacks and National Economic Council deputy director Ryan Baasch pushed for the anti-licensing language and a shorter review window. Industry insiders had lobbied for a two-week window; the final 30-day compromise landed between that and the original 90. The signing itself was notably subdued -- done privately, without the planned CEO photo opportunity.

The Cybersecurity Clearinghouse: A Concrete Step

While much of the order relies on voluntary cooperation, one provision carries more structural weight. The order directs the Treasury Department, the National Security Agency, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, and the Office of the National Cyber Director to stand up an "AI cybersecurity clearinghouse" within 30 days.

The clearinghouse will coordinate vulnerability scanning across the AI ecosystem, discover and validate security flaws, and prioritize remediation and patch distribution -- all in collaboration with private-sector AI developers and critical infrastructure operators such as power companies and hospital administrators.

This provision was driven in part by a specific catalyst. In April, Anthropic's Mythos Preview model demonstrated superhuman ability to identify critical vulnerabilities in widely used operating systems, sending shockwaves through Washington. Senior administration officials, including Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Vice President JD Vance, became increasingly invested in governing AI's spread after that demonstration.

The clearinghouse represents the order's most tangible outcome: a centralized hub for sharing AI vulnerability intelligence across government and industry, modeled loosely on existing frameworks for critical infrastructure protection but adapted for the speed and scale of AI-discovered exploits.

The Voluntary Paradox

The central tension in the order is philosophical. It asks companies to cooperate but prohibits the government from compelling them to do so. OpenAI's chief global affairs officer framed the dynamic carefully: "As AI capabilities continue to advance, we believe effective safety frameworks should continue to be developed through democratic institutions, informed by technical expertise and broad stakeholder input, to promote accountability and public trust."

Scientific American called the order a "drastic shift" from the administration's earlier laissez-faire approach. And it is -- but only to a point. The order directs the Department of Justice to treat AI-assisted hacking and unauthorized access as a high-priority enforcement area, and it directs federal agencies to develop benchmarks for assessing AI models' cyber capabilities. These are real policy moves, but they operate within a framework that is fundamentally voluntary at its foundation.

The enterprise implications are significant. Companies operating in regulated sectors such as finance, energy, and healthcare will likely face pressure from their own compliance teams to participate in the voluntary review, even without a legal mandate. The clearinghouse, meanwhile, could become a de facto standard-setter if enough companies participate, creating a soft regulatory floor through market dynamics rather than statute.

What Comes Next

This is not Trump's first AI executive order. In December 2025, he signed an order directing the development of "one rulebook" -- a national AI policy framework intended to preempt the patchwork of state-level AI laws emerging across the country. Tuesday's order builds on that foundation but adds a cybersecurity dimension that was absent from the earlier directive.

The 30-day clock for the cybersecurity clearinghouse starts now. Whether the voluntary review framework attracts meaningful participation from frontier AI labs -- or becomes a hollow gesture -- will depend on whether companies see strategic advantage in cooperating with a government that has promised, in writing, never to make them.

The explicit anti-licensing language may be the order's most durable legacy. Future administrations seeking mandatory AI regulation would need to overcome not just political resistance but a documented presidential commitment against it -- a commitment that the current industry lobbied hard to secure and will work even harder to preserve.
