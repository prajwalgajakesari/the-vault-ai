KEY TAKEAWAY

Roughly 200 Stanford graduates walked out of Sundar Pichai's commencement address to protest Google's defense AI ties, making military and surveillance uses of AI the focus of campus dissent.

Sundar Pichai Faces Boos and a Walkout at Stanford Commencement Over Google's Defense AI Ties

Sundar Pichai returned on Sunday to the campus where he earned a master's degree, expecting a homecoming. Instead, the Alphabet and Google chief executive delivered Stanford's 135th commencement address to a stadium punctuated by boos, Palestinian flags, and the sight of roughly 200 graduating students rising from their seats and filing out as he spoke. The walkout — chants of "free Palestine" trailing behind the departing caps and gowns — turned a celebratory ritual into the latest flashpoint in a widening confrontation between the AI industry and the students and workers who object to where its technology ends up.

The grievance was specific. Protesters were not booing AI hype in the abstract; they were targeting the business decisions of the company Pichai runs.

What Happened on the Field

The demonstration unfolded as Pichai took the stage at Stanford Stadium. Organizers said hundreds of students participated in some form, and SFGate reported that around 200 graduates physically walked out during the address. Signs in the crowd read "ICE SPIES WITH GOOGLE AI," "GENOCIDE RUNS ON GOOGLE," and "FREE FREE PALESTINE," according to a press release tied to the protest. Online video showed students waving Palestinian flags and chanting as they exited.

"We are walking out because we refuse to glorify the corporations that fuel this violence and exercise our power to choose differently," read a statement associated with the protest.

The walkout was coordinated by several campus activist groups, including the Stanford chapter of Students for Justice in Palestine, the No Tech for Apartheid campaign, and a group called Tech for Liberation. In its own statement, the Stanford SJP framed the moment as a generational rejection of the industry's pitch: "Today, Sundar Pichai was met with the sight of hundreds of students who showed they could not be allured anymore with the talk of a dollar or rapidly expanding AI."

It was the third consecutive year that Stanford's commencement has drawn a pro-Palestinian walkout, following similar-sized demonstrations in 2024 and 2025.

The Contracts at Issue

At the center of the protest is Project Nimbus, a $1.2 billion contract that Israel signed with Google and Amazon in 2021 to supply cloud computing and AI services to the Israeli government and military. The deal establishes a secure instance of Google Cloud on Israeli soil, enabling large-scale data analysis and AI workloads. Critics, citing reporting on internal Google documents, say the services on offer include capabilities such as AI-assisted facial detection, automated image categorization, and object tracking. The contract reportedly limits Google's ability to deny service to specific Israeli government entities.

Activists also pointed to Google's ties to U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement, and to the broader ecosystem of defense and surveillance AI — including Palantir, the analytics firm with contracts supporting both the Israeli military and the Trump administration's immigration-enforcement push.

Google declined to comment beyond pointing reporters to Pichai's speech.

A Speech That Sidestepped AI

Notably, Pichai never mentioned AI by name. Tech executives have absorbed a season of jeers on the commencement circuit — Eric Schmidt, his own predecessor, paused through hisses at the University of Arizona, and a speaker at the University of Central Florida was booed for calling AI "the next Industrial Revolution." Pichai appeared to have read the room in advance.

"I know today is about giving you all advice. But people have also been giving me a lot of advice on what to say. Actually, it's been the same advice, and it's about what not to say," he told graduates, before declaring AI "truly immaterial" to his remarks and urging the class to stay optimistic and not take life too seriously.

The irony was not lost on the protesters: the technology Pichai declined to name was precisely the technology at the heart of their objection.

The Backlash to the Backlash

The protest drew sharp criticism from parts of Silicon Valley. Vinod Khosla, the venture capitalist and co-founder of Sun Microsystems, called the demonstration "biased, idiotic, short-sighted and very selfish" in a post on X, arguing the students "ignored the bottom 3 billion people on this planet that could benefit from AI."

Pichai himself had previewed his approach weeks earlier on the Hard Fork podcast, striking a more conciliatory tone. "Anytime we have driven technology progress I think it helps drive progress in the world," he said, adding that this generation would "be a big part of driving that progress and also dealing with the impact of that technology. I think we have to be very mindful of that."

A Pattern, Not a One-Off

The Stanford walkout is the visible edge of a conflict that has been building inside the AI industry for years. Project Nimbus has provoked dissent within Google itself: in 2024, the company fired 28 employees who staged sit-ins at offices in New York, Sunnyvale, and Seattle, an episode that galvanized the No Tech for Apartheid movement. Internal unrest has continued since. In April 2026, the Electronic Frontier Foundation accused Google and Amazon of "choosing to look the other way" on how their services are used.

The pressure has produced uneven corporate responses. Microsoft, also criticized for its work with the Israeli military, moved in late 2025 to restrict an Israeli military unit's use of its technology after an investigation found its cloud services were being used to surveil Palestinians, and pledged in June 2026 to tighten its human-rights measures. Google and Amazon have so far stood by Nimbus.

What makes the campus protests distinct is the convergence of two anxieties. One is geopolitical — opposition to the war in Gaza and to surveillance of immigrants. The other is economic and existential: surveys of recent graduates show many believe AI threatens their job prospects and is corroding parts of society. For a cohort entering a labor market reshaped by the very tools these companies sell, the line between "AI will help you" and "AI is being used against people" has collapsed.

What to Watch

The immediate question is whether the dissent stays symbolic or begins to cost. Google has weathered worker firings, EFF rebukes, and now a high-profile commencement embarrassment without altering its defense posture. Whether sustained pressure from talent pipelines — the Stanford engineers and computer scientists these firms recruit from — eventually shifts contracting decisions remains the open variable.

For now, the protests signal that for a meaningful slice of the next technical generation, the prestige of building AI no longer outruns the question of what it builds. As the industry races toward ever-larger defense and government deals, that question is unlikely to leave the stage.
