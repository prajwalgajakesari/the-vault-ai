Amazon Raises Record C$14 Billion in Canadian-Dollar Bonds to Fund Its AI Buildout

4 min read 893 words3 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

Amazon's record C$14B Canadian-dollar bond sale, which drew double the demand and broke Alphabet's month-old record, shows Big Tech is increasingly tapping global debt markets to bankroll a ~$200B AI capex boom.

Amazon has turned to one of the world's quieter corners of the corporate debt market to help bankroll its artificial-intelligence ambitions — and in doing so, it just broke a record that had stood for barely a month.

On June 8, the e-commerce and cloud giant sold C$14 billion (roughly US$10 billion) of high-grade bonds, the largest corporate debt offering ever denominated in Canadian dollars. The deal surpassed the previous record — a C$8.5 billion sale from Alphabet — that the Google parent had set only weeks earlier, underscoring how voraciously Big Tech is now scouring global capital markets to feed its AI buildout.

Demand was overwhelming. Investors placed more than C$28 billion of orders for the offering, double the amount Amazon ultimately raised, according to reporting from Bloomberg and Reuters. The sale was split into five tranches of senior unsecured notes, with maturities ranging from three to 30 years.

A record sale, and then another loan

The Canadian-dollar bond was only part of a remarkable 48-hour borrowing spree. Two days after pricing the bonds, Amazon arranged a separate $17.5 billion bank loan, also earmarked for AI infrastructure, according to TechCrunch. Together, the two moves added roughly $31.5 billion in fresh financing in a span of days.

The proceeds are destined for the same place: data centers, servers, custom silicon and the sprawling physical plant that powers Amazon Web Services' AI offerings. Amazon has guided that its 2026 capital expenditures could reach approximately $200 billion, the bulk of it tied to AI.

The Canadian deal is also just one thread in a much larger fundraising campaign. Since the start of 2025, Amazon has tapped debt markets for more than $80 billion, including a roughly $15 billion U.S.-dollar offering in March that drew about $126 billion in peak demand, plus issuance in euros and Swiss francs. Diversifying across currencies lets the company raise enormous sums without saturating any single market.

"Not on a hunch"

Behind the borrowing binge is a CEO who has staked his tenure on the AI wager. "We're not investing approximately $200 billion in capex in 2026 on a hunch," Andy Jassy told investors this spring, defending the spending as a deliberate bet rather than fear of missing out.

Jassy has been blunt about the company's posture. "We're not going to be conservative in how we play this," he wrote to shareholders, projecting that AI-driven demand could eventually push AWS from a roughly $142 billion annual run rate toward as much as $600 billion over the next decade. He disclosed earlier this year that AI revenue within the cloud unit had already reached an annualized run rate of more than $15 billion.

That ambition comes with near-term pain. Amazon's free cash flow fell from about $38 billion in 2024 to roughly $11 billion in 2025 as capital spending surged — a squeeze Jassy has likened to the early, cash-hungry years of building AWS itself. The company is effectively betting that today's borrowed billions will compound into the dominant AI cloud franchise of the 2030s.

Why the bond market keeps saying yes

The most telling signal in the Canadian deal is not the headline number but the order book. When a single corporate borrower can attract C$28 billion of demand in a market as comparatively shallow as Canadian-dollar credit, it tells you institutional investors are hungry for high-grade, long-dated paper from the handful of companies seen as AI's surest winners.

That dynamic is reshaping how the AI boom gets paid for. For most of the past two years, hyperscalers funded data centers largely from their own cash flows. But the scale of 2026's ambitions — Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta and Amazon are collectively expected to spend several hundred billion dollars this year — has outrun even their formidable operating cash. The result is a pivot to debt, and a competition to lock in financing before rates move or appetite cools.

There is a strategic logic to going abroad. By issuing in Canadian dollars, euros and Swiss francs, Amazon reaches pools of capital that rarely get to buy a name of its caliber, often at attractive relative pricing, while leaving room to return to the deep U.S. market later. Alphabet's earlier Canadian record and Amazon's swift one-upmanship suggest a new playbook in which the world's biggest borrowers systematically work every major currency bloc.

The risk is concentration. A growing share of investment-grade fixed income is now exposed to a small cluster of companies all making the same enormous, unproven bet that AI demand will materialize on schedule. For now, bondholders are treating that bet as close to risk-free. Whether that confidence holds depends on a question no order book can answer: whether the revenue from all this infrastructure arrives fast enough to justify the debt funding it.

What to watch next

Watch Amazon's free cash flow and capex guidance at its next earnings report for signs of whether the spending pace is moderating or accelerating. Keep an eye on whether Microsoft, Meta and Alphabet follow Amazon into foreign-currency debt markets, which would confirm a broader shift toward globalized AI financing. And watch credit spreads on hyperscaler bonds — any widening would be the first market signal that investors are starting to price in doubt about the AI capex boom.
