OpenAI Buys TBPN, the Silicon Valley Talk Show, in Its First Media Acquisition

5 min read 1021 words4 sources
KEY TAKEAWAY

OpenAI's first-ever media acquisition, buying the $30M-revenue talk show TBPN, signals that frontier AI labs are now racing to own distribution and narrative, not just models.

The frontier AI lab that built ChatGPT has bought a talk show. OpenAI has acquired TBPN — short for the Technology Business Programming Network — the daily, founder-led Silicon Valley livestream that has become a kind of SportsCenter for the tech industry. It is OpenAI's first acquisition of a media property, and it signals that the most valuable startup in the world now wants to own not just the technology, but the conversation around it.

The deal, announced by OpenAI and confirmed across TechCrunch, CNBC, Variety and The Wall Street Journal, places one of the buzziest shows in tech directly inside the company it spends three hours a day talking about. Financial terms were not disclosed, but multiple outlets reported the price landed in the "low hundreds of millions" of dollars — a striking sum for a program that did not exist two years ago.

A cult show with real numbers

TBPN is hosted by former tech founders John Coogan and Jordi Hays. It airs live on YouTube and X for three hours on weekdays, then drops as a podcast on Spotify and Apple Podcasts, covering technology, business, AI and defense. Launched in late 2024, it has built a devoted following in Silicon Valley as a venue where industry power players can speak candidly and get questioned by fellow insiders. Top executives — Mark Zuckerberg, Satya Nadella, Marc Benioff and OpenAI's own Sam Altman among them — have appeared to react to the news of the day and occasionally make some of their own.

The cult following translates into a real business. After roughly $5 million in advertising revenue in 2025, TBPN is on track to pull in more than $30 million this year, according to The Wall Street Journal. That trajectory — a 6x revenue jump in a single year off a show with around 70,000 viewers per episode — helps explain why a company on the brink of an IPO would pay up for it. OpenAI says TBPN will continue as its own brand, with the lab helping it scale further.

Inside OpenAI, the show will sit within the company's strategy organization and report to Chris Lehane, OpenAI's chief global affairs officer and the company's most prominent political operative. The acquisition was championed by Fidji Simo, OpenAI's head of applications, who framed it as part of a communications strategy that does not fit conventional molds.

"The standard communications playbook just doesn't apply"

In a memo accompanying the deal, Simo said OpenAI would tap the founders' "amazing comms and marketing instincts" beyond the show itself, arguing that TBPN will "bring AI to the world in a way that helps people understand the full impact of this technology on their daily lives." She went further on why an AI lab needs a talk show at all, noting that for an atypical company like OpenAI, "the standard communications playbook just doesn't apply." Simo also pledged that TBPN would keep editorial independence, continuing to "run their programming, choose their guests, and make their own editorial decisions."

The hosts cast the move as a step up from commentary into influence. "While we've been critical of the industry at times, after getting to know Sam and the OpenAI team, what stood out most was their openness to feedback and commitment to getting this right," Hays said in a statement. "Moving from commentary to real impact in how this technology is distributed and understood globally is incredibly important to us."

Altman, for his part, has called TBPN his favorite tech show and insisted the deal won't soften its coverage. "I don't expect them to go any easier on us, am sure I'll do my part to help enable that with occasional stupid decisions," he wrote in a social media post.

Why an AI lab is buying attention

Strip away the personalities and the deal fits a clear pattern: the AI labs are racing to control distribution, not just models. As the underlying technology commoditizes and rivals like Anthropic and Google close performance gaps, the scarce resource increasingly becomes attention and narrative — who explains AI to the public, and on whose terms. OpenAI already runs its own podcast for long-form conversations; buying TBPN gives it a daily, high-credibility megaphone aimed squarely at founders, investors and policymakers.

That is also what makes the deal uncomfortable for some observers. A company valued in the hundreds of billions, frequently the subject of TBPN's own segments, now owns the show — and routes it through a strategy chief in Lehane, a Washington veteran known for hardball political tactics and for lobbying the Trump administration on AI policy. Editorial-independence promises are common in media acquisitions; they are also notoriously difficult to enforce when the owner is the most newsworthy entity in the room. The structural tension is plain: TBPN's value to OpenAI rests on the credibility it built as an outsider, and that credibility is precisely what the acquisition puts at risk.

It is worth noting what OpenAI is not buying: a traditional newsroom. TBPN is closer to live entertainment and industry insider-baseball than investigative journalism, which lowers the stakes somewhat. But the broader move — an AI lab purchasing a media outlet that shapes how the industry talks about itself — is a first, and unlikely to be the last.

What to watch next

Three things will reveal whether this is a savvy distribution play or a credibility own-goal. First, the coverage: does TBPN keep pressing OpenAI and its rivals as hard after the deal closes, or do the rough edges quietly sand down? Second, the format expansion: watch for OpenAI to fold its existing podcast, marketing and developer-relations efforts into the TBPN brand, and possibly to launch new shows or formats built around it. Third, the copycats: if owning a daily show pays off in narrative control ahead of OpenAI's expected IPO, expect Anthropic, Google, Meta and others to start shopping for media properties — or building their own. The line between AI lab and media company just got a lot blurrier, and the rest of the industry is watching to see if it holds.
