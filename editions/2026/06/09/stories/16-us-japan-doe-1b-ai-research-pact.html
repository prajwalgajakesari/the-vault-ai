# US and Japan Commit Billion to a Joint AI Research Effort Through the DOE

On June 4, 2026, the United States Department of Energy and Japan's two lead science ministries announced what they are calling a historic billion, five-year partnership — the largest government-to-government AI research commitment either nation has ever made to the other. Japan becomes the first international partner in the DOE's Genesis Mission, a sweeping initiative to harness artificial intelligence and advanced computing across American national laboratories.

The deal is equal parts symbolic and structural. Each country will contribute 00 million over five years, with the money flowing through eleven joint scientific teams that will link twelve DOE National Laboratories, one DOE Office of Science User Facility, and twelve leading Japanese research institutions. The focus areas span some of the hardest problems in modern science: fusion energy, quantum information science, advanced materials, biotechnology, particle physics, and what the agencies describe as “autonomous laboratory systems” — AI-driven research environments where algorithms design experiments, operate robotic equipment, and iterate on results without waiting for human intervention.

“This partnership brings together two of the world's great scientific powers to accelerate discovery and unlock breakthroughs that will shape the future,” said Dr. Darío Gil, DOE Under Secretary for Science and the Genesis Mission's lead architect. Gil has pushed the mission as a framework not just for domestic AI research but for building a coalition of allied nations willing to pool computational resources and scientific talent.

The partnership was negotiated with Japan's Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology (MEXT) and Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI). Dr. Yasuyoshi Kakita, Vice-Minister for Policy Coordination at MEXT, offered a window into Japan's strategic calculus: “Under Japan's Seventh Basic Plan for Science, Technology and Innovation, we are expanding investments in science and technology, recognizing AI and computing resources as essential to both research excellence and industrial competitiveness.”

Participating researchers will have access to some of the most powerful supercomputing infrastructure on the planet — DOE's own high-performance computing systems and Japan's Fugaku supercomputer, which remains among the fastest machines in the world. Early projects already taking shape include partnerships among RIKEN, the University of Tokyo, and the National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS) working alongside DOE national labs to develop next-generation autonomous laboratories powered by AI and robotics. The collaboration builds formally on the U.S.-Japan Technology Prosperity Deal signed in 2025, which established a broader framework for bilateral cooperation across government, academia, industry, and research institutions.

Why it matters

The Genesis Mission partnership with Japan is the clearest signal yet that the United States is moving to build an allied computing and energy research bloc — one explicitly designed around AI infrastructure rather than traditional defense or trade frameworks.

Allied compute strategy. The deal is as much about compute as it is about science. By linking DOE national labs to Fugaku and Japanese institutional supercomputers, Washington and Tokyo are quietly assembling a shared AI research stack that no single nation could build alone. The ability to run frontier scientific models across both countries' infrastructure without export control friction is a significant practical advantage — and a template for future partnerships.

Science in the age of AI. The partnership's emphasis on autonomous laboratories reflects a deeper bet: that AI will not just accelerate existing research workflows but fundamentally change how science is done. When algorithms can propose hypotheses, run experiments, and analyze results at machine speed, the bottleneck shifts from laboratory throughput to the availability of curated data, domain-specific AI models, and validated simulation environments. That is precisely the kind of capability DOE national labs and Japan's research institutions have in abundance.

Competition with China. Neither government mentioned China explicitly in their announcements — they didn't need to. China has been running its own massive AI-for-science programs and has made fusion, quantum, and materials research national priorities. The Genesis Mission's move to internationalize through Japan, a close U.S. treaty ally with world-class science infrastructure, looks less like a coincidence and more like a deliberate effort to build a counterweight. The structure of the deal — bilateral, institution-to-institution, with joint teams rather than just shared publications — is designed for depth rather than optics.

What to watch. The DOE has signaled that Japan will not be the only international partner in the Genesis Mission. Watch for announcements involving South Korea, the United Kingdom, and the European Union in the coming months. The shape of those deals — which research areas, which labs, how compute access is governed — will reveal whether the Genesis Mission is becoming a genuine science alliance or a looser collection of bilateral MOUs. Also worth monitoring: whether Congress funds the U.S. 00 million commitment at the pace the DOE has outlined, given ongoing debates over the federal discretionary budget.
