# NeuralTrust Raises $20 Million to Secure the Enterprise's Growing Swarm of AI Agents

Most large companies cannot tell you how many AI agents are running inside their walls right now. They cannot say with confidence what each one is permitted to touch, or whether one has been quietly talked into emailing a customer list to an outside address. That blind spot is precisely the business NeuralTrust is building, and on June 17 the Barcelona-based startup said investors had handed it $20 million to scale into it — a seed round the company describes as the largest cybersecurity seed financing ever raised by a European company.

The round was led by Munich's Alstin Capital, with participation from VentureFriends, Seaya, Kibo Ventures, Banc Sabadell, EA Ventures Plug and Play Fund, and Finaves, the venture arm of IESE Business School. The company also cites public backing from the European Innovation Council and Spain's State Research Agency. The money will go toward engineering hires, tighter integration across NeuralTrust's product stack, and an aggressive push deeper into the European enterprise market — with a US entry flagged as a near-term move.

## The governance gap

NeuralTrust, founded in 2024 by Joan Vendrell, Victor Garcia, and Alejandro Domingo, sells software that does for AI agents what identity and access management once did for human employees and service accounts: discover them, track them, watch what they do, and stop them when they go wrong. The founding team carries enterprise pedigree — Vendrell was previously chief data officer at fashion retailer Mango and has worked at Amazon and McKinsey; Domingo led AI transformation programs as an associate partner at McKinsey; Garcia built security systems for telecoms and banks. The company now operates out of Barcelona and London, with a Munich office planned.

The problem it targets is one of sprawl. Inside a single large organization, agents multiply without coordination: one team stands up a customer-service bot on one platform, another automates back-office work on a different stack, and vendor software ships with its own embedded agents. Each connects to internal systems, databases, and outside tools, and each acts on its own. Security teams, NeuralTrust argues, mostly cannot see the full picture, let alone halt a misbehaving agent in time.

"AI agents are now part of enterprise operations, but the controls protecting them are still catching up," said co-founder and CEO Joan Vendrell, framing the round as a bet on building the missing infrastructure layer. In a separate interview with Tech Funding News, he put the stakes more bluntly: "It's different if your ChatGPT gives you a wrong answer — you can live with that. But if you connect AI to your email system and it sends emails to outside addresses, leaking internal information, that's a disaster."

## How the platform works

NeuralTrust's product is organized into three layers that share a common spine. TrustGate is a gateway that brokers every model call, tool call, and Model Context Protocol request, acting as a single chokepoint for agent traffic. TrustGuard is a real-time engine meant to detect and block threats regardless of how or where an agent was built. TrustLens maps every agent across the organization and traces what each does, inside and outside the security perimeter.

The scale claims are unusual for a seed-stage firm. NeuralTrust says it inspects millions of agent interactions a day and currently observes a 1.2% attack rate — roughly one in 80 interactions involving an attempt to exfiltrate data, hijack a tool, or break a policy. Its customer roster skews toward large enterprises, including Iberia, Air Europa, Abanca, and investor Banc Sabadell; the company says 92% of its customers generate more than $1 billion in annual revenue, and that in the first quarter of 2026 it doubled its entire 2025 annual recurring revenue, though it declined to disclose hard figures. NeuralTrust also cites external validation: Gartner has listed it as a Representative Vendor in two Market Guides, KuppingerCole named it a Leader in its 2025 Leadership Compass for Generative AI Defense, and MarketsandMarkets placed it as a Leader in its 2026 Agentic AI Security Quadrant.

## Why "agentic identity" is becoming its own category

The round lands as AI-agent security hardens into a distinct market rather than a feature bolted onto existing tools. The analyst case for urgency is stark: Gartner forecasts that by 2027, 40% of enterprises will shut down or scale back autonomous agents because governance problems only surface after something breaks in production, and projects that Fortune 500 companies could each run more than 150,000 agents by 2028 — while only 13% feel ready to manage them. Against that backdrop, the discipline NeuralTrust sells looks less like cybersecurity hygiene and more like the agentic equivalent of identity and access management: every autonomous actor enumerated, scoped, monitored, and revocable.

Incumbents have noticed. Palo Alto Networks acquired Protect AI for a reported $500 million-plus in 2025, a signal that platform vendors now treat agent governance as core rather than optional. That cuts two ways for NeuralTrust — it validates the category but compresses the window for independents to define it. The company's pitch is that it offers a genuine control layer rather than a point solution, and Alstin Capital partner Alexander Meyer-Scharenberg leaned on exactly that framing. "AI agents are entering enterprise infrastructure faster than security teams can adapt, so the window to establish control is now," he said. "They've built a genuine control layer, not another point solution, and enterprises can operationalise it today."

NeuralTrust is also wagering that geography is a feature. With EU AI Act obligations tightening, Vendrell says government and banking buyers increasingly want vendors headquartered outside the United States. "There are real issues now regarding technological and defence sovereignty in the EU," he noted. "Some customers — especially governments — are very sensitive about where the technology they buy comes from."

## What to watch

The open question is timing, not need. Enterprises broadly accept that ungoverned agent fleets are a liability; what is unsettled is whether they fund the control layer before an incident forces their hand, or after. Watch three things: whether NeuralTrust's Q1 ARR momentum holds as it scales headcount; how fast it executes the promised US expansion into a market where its largest competitors already sit; and whether consolidation accelerates. If a platform vendor was willing to pay north of $500 million for a rival, the runway for independents to shape this category — rather than be absorbed into it — may be shorter than $20 million can stretch.
