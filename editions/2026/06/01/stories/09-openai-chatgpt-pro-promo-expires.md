The honeymoon pricing is officially over. As of midnight on June 1, OpenAI ended the promotional rate doubling on its $100-per-month ChatGPT Pro subscription and cut Codex agentic coding usage from a promotional 10x allocation back to the standard 5x level. For power users who built workflows around the generous launch terms, the adjustment is more than a billing change — it is a signal that the era of subsidized AI access is drawing to a close.

The ChatGPT Pro tier, launched in December 2025 at $100 per month, initially offered twice the standard usage limits on GPT-5.5 and o3-level reasoning models as an incentive to attract early subscribers. That promotional period, originally set for 90 days, was quietly extended twice before OpenAI confirmed in a May 28 blog post that it would expire on May 31 without further renewal.

"We introduced the launch promotion to give Pro subscribers time to explore the full capabilities of our most advanced models," an OpenAI spokesperson said in a statement to The Information. "As usage patterns have stabilized, we are moving to standard allocations that reflect the sustainable economics of operating these models at scale."

## Codex Takes the Bigger Hit

While the Pro tier adjustment is significant, the sharper reduction hits Codex, OpenAI's agentic coding platform launched in May 2026. During its first two weeks, Codex offered a 10x usage multiplier to Pro subscribers — effectively giving paying users ten times the normal allocation for autonomous coding tasks that can run for minutes at a time, consuming substantial compute resources.

That multiplier dropped to 5x on June 1, halving the effective Codex allocation overnight. For developers who had begun relying on Codex to handle multi-file refactoring, test generation, and pull request workflows, the change forces an immediate recalculation of how aggressively they can use the tool.

The timing is notable. GitHub Copilot's simultaneous shift to usage-based billing on the same date means developers face a one-two punch: reduced Codex allocations from OpenAI and unpredictable costs from GitHub. Several prominent developers noted on social media that June 1, 2026, may be remembered as the day AI coding tools stopped being cheap.

"The promotional period was never going to last forever, but cutting it the same week GitHub changes its billing feels like the industry collectively decided developers had too much fun," wrote software engineer and newsletter author Gergely Orosz on X.

## The Economics Behind the Change

The promotional reductions reflect the brutal unit economics of frontier AI models. OpenAI's GPT-5.5 and o3 reasoning models are among the most computationally expensive systems ever deployed, with estimated inference costs of $15 to $60 per million output tokens depending on the model and task complexity.

At promotional rates, heavy Pro users were consuming an estimated $300 to $500 worth of compute monthly for a $100 subscription. OpenAI, which reported annualized revenue of roughly $16 billion in early 2026 but continues to operate at a loss, cannot sustain that ratio as its user base grows toward 400 million monthly active users.

The move also aligns with a broader industry trend. Anthropic raised Claude Pro pricing from $20 to $30 in late 2025. Google increased Gemini Advanced rates for heavy API users in March 2026. And the venture capital market, while still pouring record sums into AI companies, is increasingly demanding a credible path to profitability.

## What to Watch Next

OpenAI has hinted that a new "Pro Plus" tier at a higher price point may arrive later in 2026, offering the heavier allocations that power users have come to expect. The company is also reportedly developing a metered billing option that would let users pay per query above their monthly cap rather than hitting a hard limit. For now, the message is clear: the price of frontier AI is going up, and the promotional era that helped build the industry's massive user base is giving way to the harder work of making these products pay for themselves.
