# GitHub Copilot Switches to Usage-Based Billing, Sparking Developer Backlash Over Costs

The golden age of predictable AI coding costs may be over. As of June 1, GitHub Copilot charges developers by the token instead of a flat rate -- and some are reporting projected cost increases of 25x or more.

When GitHub announced in late April that it would move all Copilot plans from flat-rate subscriptions to token-based billing, the response from the developer community was swift and overwhelmingly negative. The official GitHub community discussion thread amassed more than 400 comments and nearly 900 downvotes within days, signaling a rare level of organized discontent among the platform's user base.

The new system, which took effect June 1, replaces the old model of counting "premium requests" with "GitHub AI Credits" that are consumed based on actual token usage -- including input, output, and cached tokens. Plan prices remain nominally unchanged: Copilot Pro stays at $10 per month, Pro+ at $39, Business at $19 per user, and Enterprise at $39 per user. But instead of a fixed number of requests, each plan now includes AI Credits equal to its dollar price -- $10 in credits for the $10 Pro plan, for example.

The math is where things get painful for heavy users. Under the old system, a multi-hour autonomous coding session and a quick chat question cost the same: one premium request. Under the new model, that same agentic session can burn through $30 to $40 in credits, meaning a Pro subscriber could exhaust their entire monthly allotment in a single working session.

The Numbers That Sparked Outrage

The backlash crystallized around jaw-dropping cost projections shared across Reddit and social media. One developer claimed their monthly bill would balloon from $29 to nearly $750. "This new usage model is just stupidly expensive. I'm adjusting mine by cancelling," the Redditor wrote. "At that cost, it is no longer cost-effective or useful in any practical way."

Another user reported projected costs shooting from roughly $50 to approximately $3,000 per month -- a 60x increase that drew widespread disbelief.

Making matters worse, the safety net that previously existed has been eliminated. Under the old system, developers who exhausted their premium requests could fall back to a lower-cost model and continue working. That fallback is gone, replaced by hard credit limits and admin budget controls.

GitHub's Defense: The Economics Were Unsustainable

GitHub's Chief Product Officer Mario Rodriguez framed the change as an economic necessity in the company's official blog post. "Copilot is not the same product it was a year ago," Rodriguez wrote. "It has evolved from an in-editor assistant into an agentic platform capable of running long, multi-step coding sessions, using the latest models, and iterating across entire repositories."

Rodriguez argued that the old premium request model was "no longer sustainable," noting that GitHub had been absorbing escalating inference costs as the platform grew more powerful. The implication was clear: developers had been getting a subsidized deal, and the subsidy was ending.

To ease the transition for enterprise customers, GitHub is offering promotional credit boosts for the first three months: $30 in monthly credits for Business users (normally $19) and $70 for Enterprise users (normally $39). The company also introduced pooled credits across organizations, so unused individual allotments do not go to waste.

A Divided Community

Not everyone sympathizes with the vocal critics. Some developers have pushed back on the astronomical cost projections, arguing they come primarily from "vibe coders" -- those who offload entire development workflows to AI rather than using it as a targeted tool. "The only way it gets crazy like that is if you are purely 'vibe coding' with a ton of bloated iterations," one Reddit user wrote. "It's pretty affordable for even small outfits if used as a tool, on pretty much any provider."

But others see a deeper problem of misaligned incentives. As one commenter noted: "Microsoft provided this billing method and they kept making it easier and easier to burn through massive numbers of tokens on single premium requests that could churn for hours or even days while spawning dozens or even hundreds of sub-agents." GitHub spent the past two years actively promoting agentic development, multi-step coding sessions, and repository-wide assistance as the future of programming -- then effectively penalized the developers who adopted that vision most enthusiastically.

The Bigger Picture

GitHub's pricing shift reflects a broader industry reckoning. Anthropic and OpenAI have similarly moved enterprise customers to token-based billing, and the economics of AI inference remain brutal. AI News reported that Uber's CTO has said the company burned through its entire 2026 AI budget ahead of schedule, with 11 percent of code updates now written by AI agents.

For individual developers and small teams, the practical calculus is already shifting. Some are migrating to competitors like Anthropic's Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, or local open-source models that offer more predictable pricing. Others are simply learning to be more deliberate about when and how they invoke AI assistance.

The irony is hard to miss. GitHub built a product so good that developers became dependent on it, subsidized the cost to drive adoption, then changed the billing model once the habit was formed. Whether that constitutes smart business strategy or a bait-and-switch depends on where you sit -- and how large your next Copilot bill turns out to be.
