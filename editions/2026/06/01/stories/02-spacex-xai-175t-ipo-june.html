# SpaceX-xAI Launches Road Show for Largest IPO in History at $1.75 Trillion Valuation

Elon Musk's combined rocket-and-AI empire kicked off its investor road show this week, pitching what will be the largest initial public offering in the history of capital markets: a $1.75 trillion debut on the Nasdaq that aims to raise up to $75 billion and shatter the record set by Saudi Aramco's $29 billion listing in 2019. The company will trade under the ticker SPCX, with pricing expected on June 11 and shares opening for trading as early as June 12.

The road show, code-named Project Apex inside Goldman Sachs, marks the culmination of a breathtaking 18 months of corporate engineering. SpaceX confidentially filed its S-1 with the SEC in April, then made the prospectus public on May 20, revealing for the first time the financial reality behind the most hyped private company on Earth. The filing confirmed that SpaceX generated $18.67 billion in revenue in 2025 and $4.69 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone, a 15.4% year-over-year increase. But the numbers also exposed a painful truth: the company posted a net loss of $4.94 billion in 2025 and another $4.28 billion in just the first three months of 2026, bringing cumulative losses to $41.3 billion.

Goldman Sachs holds the coveted lead-left underwriter position, followed by Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citigroup, and JPMorgan Chase as joint book-running managers. In total, 21 investment banks form the syndicate, an unusually large group that signals both the sheer scale of the offering and SpaceX's desire to distribute demand across every major institutional network on Wall Street.

The xAI Factor

The financial complexity traces back to February 2026, when SpaceX absorbed Musk's artificial intelligence company xAI in an all-stock transaction that valued the combined entity at $1.25 trillion, making it the largest private-sector merger in history. The AI division, now branded SpaceXAI, posted revenue of $3.2 billion in 2025 while burning approximately $14 billion in cash. In the first quarter of 2026 alone, the AI segment recorded $818 million in revenue against an operating loss of $2.469 billion. SpaceX's S-1 stated bluntly that its Space and Connectivity segments contributed the "substantial majority of consolidated revenue."

The prospectus leaned heavily into a grand narrative. "We believe the next paradigm shift for humanity is the creation of a resilient, perpetually expanding spacefaring civilization that drives continuous innovation across new frontiers," SpaceX wrote in the filing. The company argued that confining humanity to one planet "constitutes a single point of failure and carries existential risk," adding with characteristic Musk bravado: "We do not want humans to have the same fate as dinosaurs."

Robert Willens, an accounting expert at Columbia Business School, offered a more measured assessment of the financial structures in the filing. Speaking to Fortune about the related-party transactions between SpaceX and entities controlled by Musk allies, Willens noted that while the prospectus used standard protective language for Tesla transactions, it conspicuously omitted that language for leases with Valor Equity Partners, the fund run by Musk confidant Antonio Gracias. "They know when to say it and when not to," Willens told Fortune.

What Investors Are Actually Buying

Underneath the $1.75 trillion headline, SpaceX is effectively three businesses bundled into one. Starlink, the satellite internet division, is the crown jewel: 10.3 million subscribers across 164 countries, $11.4 billion in 2025 revenue, and operating profit of $1.188 billion in the first quarter of 2026. The space launch business completed 165 Falcon 9 missions in 2025, though more than 70% were internal Starlink deployments. And the AI division, the youngest and hungriest segment, is burning capital at a rate that dwarfs the rest of the company combined, with $12.7 billion in capital expenditures in 2025 alone.

The valuation implies a price-to-sales ratio of roughly 94 times, higher than any company currently in the S&P 500. Palantir, the current leader by that metric, trades at approximately 67 times. Much of the premium rests on the promise of orbital AI data centers, a vision that requires the fully reusable Starship rocket to work reliably -- something it has not yet achieved after 12 test flights.

Why This Matters for AI

The SpaceX IPO is not just a Wall Street event; it is a stress test for the entire AI investment thesis. At a moment when OpenAI, Anthropic, and other AI giants are also exploring public listings, SpaceX is asking the market to assign a $250 billion valuation to an AI subsidiary that generates less than $4 billion in annual revenue. For context, Anthropic's most recent private valuation of roughly $900 billion corresponds to a price-to-sales ratio of about 20 times -- one quarter of what SpaceX is effectively demanding for xAI.

The offering will also reshape the index landscape. Under a Nasdaq 100 rule revision that took effect May 1, SpaceX will automatically enter the index on its 15th trading day, roughly July 6, triggering an estimated $22 billion to $27 billion in forced buying from ETFs and passive funds. The California Public Employees' Retirement System (CalPERS) and the comptrollers of New York State and New York City, collectively representing over $1 trillion in assets, have already sent a formal protest letter calling SpaceX's governance structure "the most management-friendly in the history of the U.S. public markets."

What to Watch Next

The next 10 days will determine whether institutional appetite matches the ambition. Investors should watch for three signals during the road show: the final pricing range, which could push the valuation toward $1.8 trillion or above; the allocation split between long-only institutions and hedge funds, which will indicate conviction levels; and whether Musk himself participates in investor meetings, a wildcard in any deal bearing his name. If demand holds, June 12 will mark the day a rocket company, an AI lab, a satellite internet provider, and a social media platform all become one publicly traded entity -- and tens of millions of passive investors will own a piece whether they chose to or not.
