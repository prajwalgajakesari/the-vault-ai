# Alphabet Plans to Raise $80 Billion to Fund AI Infrastructure Buildout

*The Vault -- AI Edition | June 1, 2026 | 4 min read | 887 words*

**Category:** business

**Key Takeaway:** Alphabet is tapping equity markets for $80 billion -- including a $10 billion private placement from Berkshire Hathaway -- to bankroll an AI infrastructure buildout that could push its 2026 capital expenditures above $180 billion.

---

Google parent company Alphabet announced on Sunday one of the largest equity capital raises in corporate history, unveiling plans to sell $80 billion in stock to finance its rapidly escalating artificial intelligence infrastructure buildout. The move, which includes a $10 billion private placement from Warren Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway, underscores just how aggressively Big Tech is betting on AI -- and how expensive that bet has become.

The announcement comes as Alphabet races to keep pace with rivals Amazon Web Services, Microsoft, and Meta Platforms in a compute arms race that analysts expect will cost the four hyperscalers a combined $700 billion or more in AI-related capital expenditures this year alone.

## The Deal Structure

The $80 billion capital raise is split across three components. The first is $30 billion in concurrent underwritten public offerings, comprising $15 billion in depositary shares representing mandatory convertible preferred stock and $15 billion in Class A Common Stock and Class C Capital Stock. The second is a $40 billion at-the-market offering program for Class A and Class C shares, expected to begin in the third quarter of 2026. The third is the $10 billion Berkshire Hathaway private placement, split evenly between $5 billion in Class A shares at $351.81 per share and $5 billion in Class C shares at $348.20 per share.

Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan Chase, and Morgan Stanley are serving as joint book-running managers for the underwritten offerings, with Goldman Sachs also acting as placement agent for the Berkshire deal.

“The company is experiencing strong demand for its AI solutions and services from enterprises and consumers, at levels that are exceeding the company’s available supply,” Alphabet said in its official statement. “By scaling its investments, the company seeks to expand its foundational infrastructure to support the significant growth opportunity ahead.”

## Why Now

Alphabet’s fundraising push arrives at a moment of extraordinary ambition. At Google I/O last month, CEO Sundar Pichai disclosed that the company expects to spend between $180 billion and $190 billion on capital expenditures before year-end -- up from an earlier estimate of $175 billion to $185 billion, and nearly double its 2025 spending. In 2025, roughly 60 percent of capex went to servers and 40 percent to data centers and networking equipment.

When an analyst asked Pichai what keeps him awake at night, his answer was blunt: “Compute capacity. Be it power, land, supply chain constraints: how do you ramp up to meet this extraordinary demand for this moment?”

The equity raise also supplements an aggressive debt strategy. Over the past year, Alphabet has raised more than $85 billion in debt across six major currencies and markets, including a global bond issuance exceeding $30 billion in February, an $11 billion raise in sterling and Swiss francs from European investors, and a $25 billion bond sale in November. Its total debt balance now exceeds $100 billion.

## The Berkshire Signal

Berkshire Hathaway’s $10 billion commitment adds a layer of strategic credibility to the offering. The conglomerate first invested $4.3 billion in Alphabet stock in November and has since built a position valued at approximately $20 billion prior to Sunday’s announcement, making Alphabet one of its top holdings alongside Apple. The new investment suggests Buffett’s team sees Alphabet’s AI spending as a long-term value play rather than speculative excess.

## Analysis: The AI Arms Race Enters a New Phase

Alphabet’s decision to tap equity markets at this scale signals a new phase in the AI infrastructure race. Until now, hyperscalers have primarily funded their buildouts through operating cash flow and debt. Turning to equity dilution -- even for a company with Alphabet’s balance sheet -- suggests the sheer magnitude of required investment is outpacing traditional financing methods.

Constellation Research analyst Holger Mueller put it plainly: “The AI wars are heating up and it costs a lot of money to participate, with data centers being the main expense. Alphabet’s AI buildout has already made a substantial dent in its cash flow, and it has dipped into the debt markets already, so a stock sale is its best option.”

The numbers tell the story. With 2026 capex guidance at $180 billion to $190 billion and 2027 expected to rise significantly further, Alphabet is effectively signaling that the current AI investment cycle has no ceiling in sight. Wall Street analysts project combined Big Tech AI capital expenditures will surpass $1 trillion in 2027.

Alphabet’s stock, which has more than doubled in the past year, slipped in extended trading following the announcement -- a reminder that even bullish investors weigh dilution risk against growth potential. Still, the company framed the stock plan as a way to “fund its investments in a balanced way while retaining a healthy balance sheet.”

For the broader tech industry, the message is clear: the AI infrastructure buildout is no longer a discretionary investment. It is existential.
