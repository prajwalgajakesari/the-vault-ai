# SoftBank Commits Up to €75 Billion to Build 5 Gigawatts of AI Data Center Capacity in France

SoftBank Group has announced what it calls its largest AI infrastructure investment in Europe: a commitment of up to €75 billion ($87 billion) to develop and operate 5 gigawatts of artificial intelligence data center capacity across France. The deal, unveiled at the 2026 Choose France summit hosted by President Emmanuel Macron, cements the country's bid to become the continent's leading hub for next-generation compute power and marks a dramatic escalation in the global race to build the physical backbone of the AI era.

## The Deal

The investment will roll out in phases. The first, valued at €45 billion, targets 3.1 gigawatts of AI data center capacity in the Hauts-de-France region by 2031. Initial facilities are planned for three sites: Dunkirk (Loon-Plage), Bosquel, and Bouchain. SoftBank will work with SB Energy and other strategic partners to execute the buildout, with additional sites across France to follow in subsequent phases.

"AI is entering a new era, and the countries that build the infrastructure for this transformation will shape the future of technology, industry and society," said Masayoshi Son, Chairman and CEO of SoftBank Group. "SoftBank is proud to make this major commitment to France. With its industrial capabilities, talent base and national ambition, France is uniquely positioned to become a leading AI infrastructure hub in Europe."

The commitment reflects personal diplomacy between Macron and Son that began during the French president's visit to Japan earlier this year. Bloomberg previously reported that Son had floated an investment of as much as $100 billion in France after being intrigued by a direct approach from a head of state -- a rare move that prompted serious internal review. In an interview with French outlet La Tribune, Son said he was "very impressed by the fact that Emmanuel Macron is so personally committed to ensuring France's economic success."

## Strategic Partnerships and Industrial Ambition

The project is not simply a real estate play for server racks. SoftBank is partnering with Schneider Electric to build a large-scale industrial production cluster at the Port of Dunkirk, comprising two facilities: one operated by SoftBank to manufacture enclosures, and one by Schneider Electric to integrate data center power modules. The cluster will combine SoftBank's robotics and automation capabilities with Schneider's industrial expertise and local supply chain network.

"The challenge of AI is to deliver both speed and energy efficiency at scale -- and Schneider Electric's role is to enable and accelerate this transformation as the energy technology partner," said Olivier Blum, CEO of Schneider Electric. "By connecting energy and AI, we provide the electrical and digital backbone that makes high-performance, efficient and sustainable infrastructure possible."

State-owned nuclear energy giant EDF is also involved, offering the site of a former power plant in Bouchain for data center development. Bernard Fontana, EDF's Chairman and CEO, emphasized that the project demonstrates "France's ability to host large-scale digital infrastructure, supported by competitive, sovereign and low-carbon electricity."

French economic minister Roland Lescure called the announcement "testament to President Emmanuel Macron's ambition to position France as a leading destination all along the AI value chain," citing the country's fast access to Europe's most reliable electrical grid, a strong digital and industrial ecosystem, and government willingness to fast-track procedures for strategic projects.

## Why This Matters

The scale of SoftBank's French commitment -- €75 billion for 5 gigawatts -- represents a significant new front in the global AI infrastructure arms race. To put it in perspective, the entire European data center market was estimated at roughly 10 gigawatts of total capacity at the end of 2025. SoftBank's French buildout alone could add half that figure across a single country.

The investment also arrives amid mounting scrutiny of AI's energy appetite. In the United States, opposition to data center construction is intensifying over environmental concerns and questions about grid strain and utility pricing. France's advantage here is notable: its nuclear-dominated electricity grid is among the lowest-carbon and most reliable in Europe, offering a cleaner energy profile than the natural gas-heavy approach SoftBank is pursuing for its Ohio data center project in the U.S.

For SoftBank, the French gambit diversifies its AI infrastructure portfolio beyond the United States, where it is already committed to the $500 billion Stargate initiative with OpenAI, Oracle, and Abu Dhabi's MGX, plus a separate large-scale Ohio data center powered by a $33 billion natural gas plant. Questions persist about whether Son can secure enough financing to realize all of these ambitions simultaneously -- Bloomberg has reported that SoftBank recently scaled back plans for a $10 billion margin loan backed by its OpenAI stake to as low as $6 billion after facing creditor hesitation.

For France and Europe, the deal advances the concept of "digital sovereignty" -- the idea that nations should control their own AI compute infrastructure rather than depending entirely on U.S. hyperscalers. Macron has been an outspoken advocate for European AI independence, championing homegrown players like Mistral AI alongside foreign investment.

## What to Watch

The true test will be execution. Phase one's 2031 deadline gives SoftBank roughly five years to deliver 3.1 gigawatts of capacity in northern France -- an enormous construction and engineering challenge. Financing remains the critical variable: with SoftBank stretching across multiple continent-spanning mega-projects, investors and analysts will be watching closely for signs of overextension. The project's success could establish a template for how sovereign AI infrastructure deals work in Europe, while failure -- or significant delays -- could cool the continent's ambitions to compete with the U.S. and China in the AI compute race.
