# OpenAI Kills Sora After Burning $2.7 Billion in Six Months, Collapses Disney Deal

**Category:** Business
**Date:** March 29, 2026
**Sources:** TechCrunch, Variety, Hollywood Reporter, CNN

OpenAI shut down Sora on March 24, 2026, less than six months after the app launched in September 2025. The company cited shifting strategic priorities, but the numbers tell a starker story: at peak operation, Sora was costing OpenAI roughly $15 million per day in inference expenses.

Every 10-second video clip required approximately $130 in compute. Do the math on global usage and you arrive at a lifetime compute bill exceeding $2.7 billion. The total revenue from in-app purchases reached just $2.1 million. The company was losing roughly $1,300 on every transaction.

Downloads peaked in November 2025. By shutdown, monthly usage had collapsed 66 percent from that peak. The product never found product-market fit. Users either ran out of novelty or ran out of credits. The early adopter wave moved through the product in roughly eight weeks, then engagement flatlined. TechCrunch reported that the median user generated fewer than three videos before abandoning the platform.

The Disney relationship made the shutdown timing particularly brutal. The shutdown arrived 30 minutes after a scheduled meeting with Disney executives where everyone discussed an ongoing partnership as if it remained viable. Disney had planned a $1 billion strategic investment in OpenAI, which would have licensed 200+ characters from Disney, Marvel, Pixar, and Star Wars for Sora integration. No money had actually been transferred. Disney execs learned about the termination during a follow-up call. One former Disney executive told Variety the company now views OpenAI as strategically unreliable.

The optics were almost theatrical in their brutality. A company planning to bet a billion dollars on a partnership gets informed the partnership is dead 30 minutes after being told it is moving forward. That is not just a failed product. That is a fundamental breakdown in internal communication or external signaling. Either OpenAI did not actually believe Sora had a future, or it was too disorganized to tell its potential partners.

Where did Sora market research team get redirected? Robotics. Specifically, OpenAI transferred Sora physical-world understanding engine to projects around world simulation for robotic systems. The idea is sound: a video model trained to predict realistic physics in human environments can teach machines how to interact with those same environments. Training robots in simulation, then deploying in physical space, requires simulation accuracy that matches reality. Sora understanding of how cloth moves, how objects fall, how humans interact with their environment—all of that translates directly.

It is a sensible salvage operation for intellectual property that cost billions to develop. But it also signals that video generation itself, as a product, is not where OpenAI sees leverage. The company is making a bet that physical-world simulation matters more than consumer video generation.

Competitors like Runway and Pika operate video generation models at dramatically lower cost per output. Neither company burned through $2.7 billion. OpenAI advantage in raw compute and training data did not translate into an advantage in efficiency or user economics. That is a humbling lesson for a company that built its recent reputation on scaling advantages.

The shutdown also coincided with OpenAI decision to indefinitely pause a planned erotica mode for ChatGPT. The company offered no explanation for the pause, but the timing suggests internal governance questions about content policy, brand risk, and the gap between what is technically possible and what is strategically wise to release.

TechCrunch posed the broader question after the shutdown: Is this just normal corporate strategy, or are we about to see a wider pullback on AI-generated video? It is a fair question. Generative video has consumed enormous capital across the entire industry. Every frontier lab funded research into video generation. The hype cycle reached fever pitch in 2025, with dozens of startups raising Series A on the promise of being the next Sora.

Sora collapse might trigger industry recalibration or it might be dismissed as a problem specific to OpenAI cost structure. What seems clear: consumer video generation, at least through the inference cost model OpenAI was using, has extremely poor unit economics.

One other detail worth noting: OpenAI bet on video generation represented a strategic fork in the road. The company could have pursued foundation model licensing (selling weights and compute to downstream users) or consumer products (building direct-to-user applications like ChatGPT). Sora was the latter bet. Its failure does not invalidate consumer AI products as a category, but it does suggest that OpenAI may not have the operational DNA to execute them profitably outside of text-based interfaces.

The Disney partnership collapse stings in a particular way. Disney does not kill multibillion-dollar partnerships over bad quarterly numbers. It kills them when it detects fundamental instability in the partner. OpenAI sudden strategy pivots and cost blow-ups now come with a reputational cost that extends beyond Sora.

For robotics researchers watching the news, the silver lining is clear: Sora team is probably the most experienced video generation engineers on earth. They will bring that expertise to physical world modeling. The robot that learns to pick objects in a bin might get smarter because of the infrastructure built by people who trained a model to generate Disney characters.

It is a typical Silicon Valley outcome. The consumer product fails catastrophically. The underlying research gets repurposed. The company pivots. And the users who spent credits and time with Sora are left with a service that simply stopped existing.
