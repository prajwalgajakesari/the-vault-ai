# Dutch Court Bans xAI's Grok From Generating Nonconsensual Nude Images

**March 29, 2026**

A Dutch judge just did something that hasn't happened often enough: ordered an AI company to actually stop something.

The Amsterdam District Court ruled that xAI's Grok cannot generate or distribute sexual imagery of people without consent. The ruling covers people "partially or wholly stripped naked." Daily fines of 100,000 euros ($115,350) for each day of non-compliance. The decision is immediate and enforceable.

The case was brought by Offlimits, a Dutch nonprofit monitoring online violence, in cooperation with the Victims Support Fund. Their argument was straightforward: Grok's image generation tools could create hyper-realistic deepfake nude montages of women and children. The tool was being used exactly as it shouldn't be, and xAI was making money off the behavior.

xAI's legal defense was equally straightforward: impossible to prevent. Any system sophisticated enough to generate images can be misused. You can't guarantee abuse prevention. Therefore, asking us to guarantee it violates our business model.

The court disagreed.

Here's what happened that tipped the scales. Offlimits produced a video moments before the hearing showing them using Grok to generate a nude image of a real, identifiable person. The safeguards the company claimed to have implemented in January failed completely. The restraint was theoretical. The evidence was video.

The company had claimed it was responding to abuse by restricting image editing to paid subscribers only. But a paid subscriber is still a paying customer. The business model was still monetizing the behavior, just in a slightly more restricted form.

xAI's history with this specific tool had been contentious for months. Complaints about Grok spanning the Americas, Europe, Asia, and Australia. The company had been on notice. They had time to build systems that actually worked. And yet here was a demonstration that the systems didn't.

What makes this ruling unusual is how clearly it establishes liability. The judge didn't say "we're concerned about this." The judge said "you must stop." And they attached a price tag to non-compliance that's significant enough to hurt: 100,000 euros per day means roughly 3 million a month if Grok stays online and continues breaking the order.

At that price point, xAI has three choices. Genuinely fix the tool. Geo-block the Netherlands. Shut down the feature entirely. All three are expensive. All three represent business consequences for the behavior.

But here's where it gets complicated for the broader AI industry. The Dutch court is 1-0 against a specific violation. What happens when similar rulings start accumulating? France bans a feature. California bans another. The UK establishes a precedent. Suddenly you've got a fragmented landscape where the same tool behaves differently in different jurisdictions.

That's terrible for users. But it's also terrible for companies optimizing for a unified global product. The incentive structure flips. Instead of "how do we get away with this," the question becomes "which version do we ship everywhere?" and the answer is usually the most restrictive one.

xAI will probably appeal. They'll argue about jurisdiction, about the feasibility of filtering, about freedom of expression. The Dutch court has given them a roadmap of why those arguments failed. It'll be interesting to see if they find new ones.

The deeper question is whether this ruling will accelerate a trend or just remain an isolated incident. Every major AI company has features that enable abuse. Every company claims abuse prevention is hard, which is true. But the claim "it's hard so we shouldn't try" has never held up in court once the stakes are clear enough.

Grok's nonconsensual nude generation was never going to be defendable. The tool had one purpose, and that purpose was harm. When a judge sees evidence that the harm is real, happening, and monetized, the legal position collapses. xAI had months to get ahead of this. They didn't. Now they're dealing with a court order instead.

The fine structure is worth noting. 100,000 euros per day is expensive enough to matter but cheap enough that xAI could theoretically absorb it for a few months while appealing. That's the judge telegraphing: "I'm serious, and I know you might test me, and I'm ready." Fines in the millions-per-day range would be more coercive but less likely to survive appeal. 100k per day is the sweet spot of painful without being absurd.

What probably matters more than this specific ruling is the precedent. A court has now established that you can't hide behind "abuse prevention is hard" if you're making money off the abuse. That standard ripples outward. Every tool that enables harm while claiming safeguards will now face legal scrutiny that they didn't before.

For xAI, this is specifically bad. But for every company building image generation, video synthesis, or any tool with clear abuse potential, this should trigger planning conversations. Not "how do we defend this," but "are we really okay with this being our product?"

That conversation probably should have happened before the lawsuits started. Now it's happening in real time, with real money on the table. The Dutch court just moved the needle.
