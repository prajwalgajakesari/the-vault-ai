OpenAI Kills Sora After Burning $2.7 Billion in Six Months, Collapses Disney Deal

The Vault — AI Edition · March 29, 2026
Estimated reading time: 4 minutes

OpenAI shut down Sora on March 24. The video generation product—launched with fanfare just six months earlier—couldn't survive its own infrastructure costs.

The math was brutal. At peak, Sora burned $15 million per day in inference compute. Every 10-second generated clip cost approximately $130 to produce. Over six months, total compute spending likely exceeded $2.7 billion. Lifetime in-app purchase revenue? $2.1 million.

The company didn't even bother with a graceful sunset. Disney found out about the shutdown 30 minutes after finishing a working meeting where executives were still discussing their partnership. Disney had planned a $1 billion stake in OpenAI, which would have given Sora access to 200+ characters from Disney, Marvel, Pixar, and Star Wars. No money had changed hands yet. The deal evaporated along with the product.

Sora's death wasn't due to poor quality or lack of interest. Downloads peaked in November 2025, then collapsed—a 66% decline by shutdown. The product worked. Users didn't want it badly enough to justify the cost structure.

OpenAI's response reveals where the company thinks the real opportunity lies. The research team responsible for Sora isn't being disbanded. It's being redirected toward world simulation for robotics. The physical-world understanding Sora developed—how objects interact, how physics behaves—turns out to be directly applicable to training robotic systems. The same infrastructure that could generate a 10-second video for $130 can now train a robot arm to understand spatial relationships.

This is a quiet admission that video generation might not be the lever that breaks AI forward. Something else is.

The shutdown also claimed a quieter casualty: OpenAI indefinitely paused its planned erotica mode for ChatGPT, which had been in testing with select users. That feature required its own content moderation infrastructure. With Sora already hemorrhaging money, there was no appetite for additional operational complexity.

Wall Street is watching. Sora's failure suggests that compute economics, not model capability, will determine which AI products survive. Text-based interfaces work because you can serve inference at scale. Real-time generation at high fidelity? That's a different business problem entirely. OpenAI has apparently decided that problem belongs in robotics, not entertainment.
