# Apple Will Let Gemini and Claude Replace ChatGPT Inside Siri With iOS 27

**Category:** Business
**Date:** March 29, 2026
**Sources:** Bloomberg, MacRumors, Tom Guide, TechRepublic

Apple plans to fundamentally restructure how Siri accesses AI capabilities, according to Bloomberg Mark Gurman reporting on March 26. Starting with iOS 27, iPadOS 27, and macOS 27, Apple will introduce a new system called Extensions that lets users download AI chatbot applications and directly integrate them into Siri backend. It is a significant architectural shift that makes Siri a platform rather than a product.

Google Gemini, Claude, and other third-party AI assistants will be available as options. Users can select which model powers their Siri queries rather than being locked into ChatGPT or Apple internal small language models. The system works by letting Siri route queries to the user preferred AI backend, similar to how Safari can use Google, DuckDuckGo, or Bing as search engines. This represents the most significant architectural shift in Siri history since Apple introduced voice control to iOS.

The announcement is expected at WWDC on June 8, 2026. The feature is currently in testing with iOS 27, which developers expect to arrive in public beta by late summer. Early reports suggest Apple will maintain ChatGPT as the default, but will make switching backends trivial for users who prefer alternatives.

The strategic implication cuts deep: Apple is essentially positioning itself as a platform layer for AI rather than a model layer. Instead of building a world-class foundational large language model in-house (which would require hiring away talent from OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic while competing on compute budgets it cannot win), Apple is accepting that role and building infrastructure to let users choose.

This is noteworthy because it represents a rare moment of competitive candor from Apple. The company typically insists on vertical integration and complete product coherence. Siri, in the old architecture, was supposed to be Apple answer to ChatGPT. It clearly is not, and after two years of extensions development and failed internal training runs, Apple apparently decided to stop pretending. The company has accepted that it will not build a best-in-class foundational model, at least not on the timeline required to stay competitive.

The technical details matter. Apple will presumably validate extensions through review similar to the App Store process. Developers can submit AI assistant integrations that users can then install. This creates an app ecosystem around Siri, which has historically been a weakness of the platform. Siri can finally do things it was not built for, because it is now just a routing layer to better-trained models.

OpenAI had an exclusive arrangement to provide ChatGPT through Siri since 2024. That deal becomes non-exclusive under the Extensions architecture. OpenAI should still benefit from default Siri integration—the company has invested heavily in the relationship—but it loses any competitive moat. Google, Anthropic, and other providers now have a direct path to Apple users without negotiating bilateral licensing deals. The marketplace becomes transparent.

The timing works in Google favor particularly. Google just released Gemini 3.1 across consumer channels, and the search giant has been struggling to establish consumer touchpoints that compete with ChatGPT direct adoption. Siri integration suddenly becomes a significant distribution channel. For the first time, Google has a plausible path to reach hundreds of millions of iPhone users without waiting for OpenAI cooperation.

Anthropic enters this marketplace with Claude having strong developer mindshare and recent momentum. The company has benefited from positioning Claude as an alternative to OpenAI, and Siri Extensions give that positioning direct consumer reach. Users who have heard about Claude can now try it easily from their phone voice assistant.

For developers, the Extensions system likely means new opportunities to build Siri-integrated applications without waiting for Apple to negotiate API access or build features themselves. The developer community has long complained about Siri limitations and Apple unwillingness to open the platform. Extensions solves that by making third parties responsible for their own integrations.

For Apple users, it means Siri finally becomes a useful voice interface to the AI you actually want to use. The history of Siri is a history of profound disappointment compared to what alternative assistants can do. Users have had to choose between a terrible Apple voice assistant or manually typing into ChatGPT. Now they can have decent AI that responds to voice.

For Apple itself, the move is a calculated retreat from a product category where it could not compete. But it solves a major UX problem for users who found Siri AI capabilities insufficient compared to available alternatives. Sometimes admitting you cannot win a market is more pragmatic than spending billions trying. Apple brand value comes from integration and experience, not from being best-in-class at every technology. Letting users choose their AI backend preserves the integration part while removing the pretense that Apple internal models are competitive.
