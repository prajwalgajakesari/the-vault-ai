Waymo Hits Half a Million Rides Per Week as Robotaxi Race Heats Up

The Vault — AI Edition · March 29, 2026
Estimated reading time: 6 minutes

Waymo passed 500,000 paid rides per week this month. That's not a milestone. That's a business.

The climb has been steep. In May 2024, Waymo was running 50,000 rides per week. In 28 months, it achieved tenfold growth. The fleet stands at approximately 3,000 vehicles operating across 10 US cities, accumulating over 4 million fully autonomous miles weekly. Scale like that attracts competitors who smell blood in the water.

Waymo now operates in Phoenix, San Francisco, and Los Angeles as established markets. This past year added Austin, Atlanta, Miami, Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, and Orlando—seven Sun Belt cities in the span of 12 months. Every one of them targeting retirees and temperature-sensitive riders who prioritize ride comfort over price.

The company has publicly committed to hitting 1 million rides per week by year-end. Do that math: it needs to 2x in nine months. Waymo is saying that's possible.

Competitors aren't sitting still. Zoox expanded to Austin and Miami this same week, quadrupling its service area. Uber announced Europe's first robotaxi partnership, teaming up with Pony AI and Verne in Zagreb. The robotaxi race shifted from "if this works" to "how fast can we grow."

Safety questions shadow the whole sector. The NHTSA and NTSB are investigating Waymo's handling of school buses—apparently the system has been making aggressive lane changes near bus stops. Regulators seem concerned that a robot's decision-making process, no matter how statistically safe, might not align with how humans expect vehicles to behave in sensitive situations.

Meanwhile, Serve Robotics deployed its sidewalk delivery robot in Chicago and apparently couldn't help itself: the robot crashed through a bus shelter, shattering glass. Days later, a Coco Robotics machine did nearly the same thing at another Chicago bus shelter. Two separate companies, two nearly identical failure modes, one week apart.

These aren't Waymo-scale incidents. Delivery robots are far less mission-critical than rideshare. But they reveal something uncomfortable: when you deploy autonomous systems at any scale, they find failure modes humans haven't imagined. The question isn't whether failures happen. It's how the companies respond when they do.

Waymo's safety record is strong, statistically speaking. The company publishes detailed performance metrics. But statistical safety and user comfort operate on different wavelengths. A human driver might hesitate near a school bus. A robot makes the decision in microseconds based on sensor data and learned patterns. Both approaches are "correct"—until someone feels unsafe.

The industry's bet is that scale overwhelms these concerns. Get enough rides under belt, earn enough consumer trust, and regulatory scrutiny shifts from "Is this safe?" to "How do we make this safer?" That's the difference between a product and an incumbent.

Waymo is behaving like an incumbent. Rapid expansion into Sun Belt markets, public commitments to growth targets, infrastructure investment in maintenance facilities and charging networks. The company has shifted from proving robotaxis work to proving they can be profitable at scale.

Zoox and Pony AI are still in the startup phase: enter new markets, build operational experience, avoid catastrophic failures, attract Series E funding. Uber's playing a different game: it doesn't build robotaxis. It licenses the technology from others and collects the margin. That's how Uber wins when multiple approaches work—by partnering rather than betting on one.

By Q3 2026, expect Waymo to announce its millionth-ride week. Zoox will be in maybe three more cities. Pony AI will be profitable in Asia and breaking into Europe. Uber will have announced partnerships in five countries.

The robotaxi market isn't consolidating around one winner. It's fragmenting into regional players, each with different economics and customer bases. Waymo dominates US rideshare. Pony AI dominates Chinese cities. Zoox is the US wildcard. Uber is the global aggregator.

That's actually healthier for the technology's long-term development than a monopoly would be. But it makes the near-term landscape messier and the paths to profitability less obvious.

One thing's certain: the era when "autonomous vehicles are 10 years away" was a credible punchline is over. They're here. The question now is whether they'll be allowed to mature, or whether the first serious accident will trigger regulatory backlash that kills the whole sector. That answer won't come this year.
