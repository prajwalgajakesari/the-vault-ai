# Anthropic's Mythos Model Leaks Through Unsecured Database, Cybersecurity Stocks Crater

**Category:** LLMs & GenAI
**Date:** March 29, 2026
**Sources:** Fortune, Investing.com, CoinDesk

Security researchers discovered roughly 3,000 unpublished documents sitting in an unsecured Anthropic database on March 25. Fortune broke the story by responsibly disclosing the leak directly to Anthropic, which then revoked public access. The root cause: simple human error in Content Management System configuration that left a draft blog post accessible to search engines and anyone with the correct URL. No advanced hacking required. Just misconfigured cloud permissions and insufficient internal security review.

The leaks revealed an AI model called Claude Mythos, described internally as by far the most powerful AI model we have ever developed. The model sits atop a new pricing tier called Capybara, positioning it above the current flagship Opus in both capability and cost. Internal benchmarks showed Mythos outperforming all public Claude models across standard evaluations.

Mythos showed significantly higher performance on software coding, academic reasoning, and cybersecurity tasks compared to existing Claude models. But the technical capability improvements paled against the safety concerns embedded in the leaked documents. The concerning part: internal Anthropic documents contained stark warnings. One phrase appeared repeatedly in safety reviews: cyber capabilities that far outpace defender efforts. The model apparently can identify and exploit software vulnerabilities in ways that current defensive tools struggle to address.

The market reacted immediately and visibly. CrowdStrike dropped 7 percent the day the story broke. Palo Alto Networks fell 6 percent. The iShares Expanded Tech-Software Sector ETF (IGV) declined nearly 3 percent. Cybersecurity stocks got hit hardest because Mythos documented capabilities directly threatened the value proposition of defensive security tools. If AI can find zero-days faster than humans, the entire cybersecurity industry faces disruption.

Bitcoin, which often serves as a risk-sentiment indicator for emerging tech volatility, tumbled back to $66,000 after trading higher earlier that week. The move suggested broader risk-off sentiment beyond cybersecurity sectors specifically.

The irony was not lost on observers: Anthropic, a company founded on the principle of safety-focused AI development and rigorous safety research, could not keep its own database secure. The company built its entire public positioning around moving carefully, testing thoroughly, and shipping responsibly. The gap between aspirational claims and operational reality was suddenly visible to everyone.

Two additional details added to the pressure. Red-team operator METR conducted independent analysis of Anthropic agent monitoring systems and discovered novel vulnerabilities the company had not documented. The security gaps suggested that Anthropic internal safety testing may have missed attack vectors that adversarial researchers found quickly. Around the same time, Nicolas Carlini from Anthropic gave a public demonstration of autonomous zero-day discovery—a technique where AI systems independently find and exploit previously unknown software bugs. The timing was poor. The demonstration showed the raw capability exists in current models, which suddenly felt less like academic research and more like a threat disclosure.

Anthropic acknowledged the leak within hours, confirmed the existence of Mythos and Capybara, and stated both were still in testing phases. The company released no timeline for public availability. The statement attempted to frame the leak as part of normal product development, but it came too late for that framing to stick. The market had already priced in the risk.

Security analysts immediately began speculating whether releasing a model with documented cyber capabilities that outpace defenders represented a risk the industry should accept, regardless of the company intentions. Would Anthropic release Mythos commercially? Under what conditions? With what safeguards? The company provided no clarity, which bred more uncertainty.

The incident raised unavoidable questions about the entire industry. If a safety-focused AI lab with substantial resources and explicit security commitments experiences this kind of operational failure, what should we expect from competitors with different incentive structures? And if frontier AI models can already identify zero-days better than human researchers, how close are we to a world where patch cycles fall behind exploit development cycles?

One implication haunted security teams watching the news: the defensive advantage of security-through-obscurity just evaporated. If Mythos can find bugs automatically, then keeping vulnerabilities quiet becomes harder. Exploit development accelerates. And the defenders have no good options.
