# Stanford's 2026 AI Index: Costs Crash, China Closes In, and Benchmarks Keep Breaking

## Stanford's 2026 AI Index lands with a familiar message, and a sharper edge

Stanford's Institute for Human-Centered AI published the ninth edition of its AI Index on Monday, and the 400-plus-page document reads less like a progress report than a scoreboard at halftime. Capability keeps rising. Costs keep falling. And the distance between the United States and China, for years the Index's most quoted chart, has narrowed to the margin of measurement error on most public benchmarks.

The through-line of the 2026 edition, led again by Nestor Maslej and Stanford's Ray Perrault, is compression. Training runs that cost nine figures two years ago are being matched by models trained for a fraction of the price. Inference, the metric enterprises actually pay for, has gotten radically cheaper. And the policy, safety and labor questions that used to trail the technology are now arriving on the same news cycle.

## Money is back, and it is concentrated

Private AI investment rebounded to roughly $152 billion globally in 2025, a 38% jump over 2024 and the highest total the Index has ever recorded. Generative AI alone pulled in about $78 billion, more than half of it flowing to a handful of U.S. labs and their infrastructure partners. Corporate AI investment, which folds in M&A and internal capex, cleared $310 billion.

The concentration is stark. The top five AI recipients captured 41% of all venture dollars in the category, a level of tilt the Index authors flag as historically unusual even for frontier tech. Enterprise adoption, long the soft spot of the AI story, finally moved: 71% of surveyed organizations reported using generative AI in at least one business function, up from 55% a year earlier, though only 28% reported material bottom-line impact.

## Benchmarks are saturating faster than they can be built

The Index's capability section is, by the authors' own admission, running out of runway. On MMLU, GPQA, HumanEval, MATH and MMMU, leading 2025 models cleared 90% accuracy, and in several cases effectively tied human expert baselines. The report notes that six major benchmarks introduced since 2023, including GPQA-Diamond and SWE-bench Verified, were more than 80% saturated within 18 months of release.

The response has been a pivot toward harder, more adversarial evaluations: ARC-AGI-2, FrontierMath, Humanity's Last Exam, and agentic task suites like OSWorld and RE-Bench. On these, the best systems still score between 15% and 55%, giving the field a new set of mountains to climb.

## The U.S.-China gap keeps closing

For the third consecutive year, the Index's geopolitical chapter is the one policymakers will flip to first. U.S. institutions produced 42 notable models in 2025 to China's 29, but the performance gap has nearly vanished. On MMLU, the delta between the top U.S. and top Chinese model shrank from 17.5 points in 2023 to 1.6 in 2025. On HumanEval, Chinese models led outright on two quarters' releases.

Open-weight models are doing much of that work. Alibaba's Qwen line, DeepSeek's R-series and Moonshot's Kimi releases now trade places at the top of the open leaderboards, and the Index estimates Chinese labs account for 58% of notable open-weight releases in 2025, up from 34% a year earlier. The gap between the best open model and the best closed model on standard benchmarks has narrowed to about 4 percentage points.

## Compute up, cost down, incidents up

Frontier training compute continues to double roughly every five months, with the largest 2025 run estimated at 8.2e26 FLOP, a figure that would have seemed fantastical in the 2023 Index. Yet inference costs for GPT-3.5-level quality have fallen more than 280-fold since November 2023, from about $20 per million tokens to roughly $0.07, a curve the report compares to early solar.

Less celebratory: the AI Incident Database logged 412 reported incidents in 2025, up 56% year-over-year, spanning deepfake fraud, model jailbreaks deployed in the wild, and safety failures in deployed agentic systems. The Index also tracks a 19% jump in AI-related legislation worldwide, with 112 countries now having some form of AI-specific law or binding guidance on the books.

## What it means

The 2026 Index's subtext is that AI is exiting its laboratory phase. The interesting numbers are no longer parameter counts but unit economics, adoption rates, incident curves and export-control reach. The United States still leads at the frontier, but its lead is measured in months, not generations. And the policy apparatus, for the first time in the Index's history, is moving at something close to the speed of the models.
