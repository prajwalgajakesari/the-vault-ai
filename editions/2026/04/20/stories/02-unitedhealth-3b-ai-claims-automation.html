UnitedHealth Bets $3 Billion on AI to Automate Claims, Pharmacy, and Member Support

UnitedHealth is converting the nation's largest health insurer into an AI-first operator, deploying 22,000 engineers, a member-facing chatbot named Avery, and automated prior-authorization systems even as courts and regulators scrutinize how its algorithms decide patient care.

## The biggest insurer in America is rebuilding itself around AI

UnitedHealth Group, the largest health insurer in the United States, is pouring $3 billion into artificial intelligence in a sweeping effort to rewire the way it processes claims, fills prescriptions, and talks to its members. Executives confirmed this month that roughly $1.6 billion of that commitment will land in 2026 alone, making it the single largest corporate AI push in the history of American health care.

The strategy, detailed in an April 6 STAT News investigation and echoed by Becker's Payer Issues, is less a pilot program than a wholesale reconstruction of UnitedHealth's operating system. More than 22,000 software engineers across the company and its Optum subsidiary are now building and deploying agents that read medical records, flag suspected fraud, select billing codes, and make triage decisions that historically ran through human reviewers. More than 80% of those engineers already use AI tools in their daily work, the company says.

"Since the advent of generative AI, we've really doubled down on training, on investments, on driving meaningful use cases," said Sandeep Dadlani, chief executive of Optum Insight, in remarks to STAT.

## Meet Avery, the chatbot going to 20.5 million members

The most visible face of the push is Avery, a generative-AI "companion" UnitedHealthcare launched on March 26. Avery is live today for 6.5 million commercial members and 160,000 Medicare Advantage members, and the company says it will reach 20.5 million members — spanning commercial, Medicare, and Medicaid books — by year-end.

Avery answers questions about coverage, cost estimates, rewards, ID cards, provider searches, and claim status. It can call providers on a member's behalf to schedule appointments and, according to the company, resolves about 90% of inquiries without handing off to a human advocate.

"People want health care to be easier to use and tailored to their personal needs," Dan Kueter, chief executive of UnitedHealthcare's commercial business, said in announcing the launch. "Avery is one way we are responding to consumer demand for a more coordinated and simpler experience."

## Pharmacy and fraud: where the money actually gets made

The back-office bets are arguably bigger than the chatbot. Optum Rx's PreCheck Prior Authorization platform, which uses AI to draft and approve prior authorizations automatically, has compressed average approval time from 8.5 hours to under 30 seconds, the company reports. On January 1, 2026, Optum Rx expanded PreCheck to more than 45 medications across 20 health systems, reaching roughly 75,000 physicians. The company says AI has let it eliminate reauthorizations for 180 chronic-condition drugs — about a 25% cut in prescription reauthorizations overall.

AI is also being trained on UnitedHealth's enormous claims data set to detect fraud, waste, and coding anomalies — a pitch the company believes will defend margins as medical costs keep rising. "At our core, we're a data company," executives told Becker's Payer Issues, framing the AI buildout as a natural extension of Optum's analytics heritage.

## The context nobody at headquarters wants to talk about

The $3 billion bet lands at an uncomfortable moment. UnitedHealth is still climbing out of the 2024 Change Healthcare ransomware breach, one of the largest in U.S. health-care history, and continues to face federal civil and criminal investigations into its Medicare billing practices. On March 9, a Minnesota federal judge ordered UnitedHealth to hand over a broad set of internal documents about nH Predict, an AI tool used by its naviHealth subsidiary to manage post-acute care under Medicare Advantage. Plaintiffs in that class action allege the algorithm's recommendations were wrong in about 90% of appealed cases — a figure UnitedHealth disputes — and that the company kept using it because only 0.2% of denied patients ever appeal.

The company maintains that nH Predict does not make coverage decisions, and that Avery and its claims agents keep humans in the loop for anything material. Patient advocates are unconvinced. They note that automating approvals is the easy half of the problem; automating denials at scale is what regulators, plaintiffs' attorneys, and members are watching.

## What to watch

Three things will tell the market whether UnitedHealth's bet pays off. First, regulatory posture: the outcome of the nH Predict discovery battle, and any enforcement action from the Department of Justice or CMS, will set the rules of the road for AI-driven utilization management across the industry. Second, denial rates: if UnitedHealth's approval and denial metrics move sharply after Avery and PreCheck scale, expect congressional hearings and state insurance-department subpoenas to follow. Third, competitors: Elevance, CVS/Aetna, Cigna, and Humana are all scrambling to match Optum's agent stack, and the capital arms race that follows will decide who controls the next decade of health-care automation.

For now, the message from Minnetonka is unambiguous. Health care is the largest vertical in the U.S. economy, and UnitedHealth intends to be the AI company that runs it.
