# Anthropic Ships Claude Opus 4.7, Closing the Gap to Mythos on Coding and Vision

Anthropic on Thursday released Claude Opus 4.7, a mid-cycle upgrade to its flagship production model that lifts coding and vision performance close to its still-gated Mythos frontier system while leaving pricing untouched at $5 per million input tokens and $25 per million output tokens. The release, dated April 16, 2026, lands just four days after Anthropic first detailed Mythos Preview to a short list of Glasswing partners, and it is aimed squarely at the enterprise buyers who have so far been unable to get their hands on the bigger model.

The headline numbers make the strategy clear. On SWE-bench Verified, Opus 4.7 scores 87.6 percent, up from 80.8 percent on Opus 4.6. On the harder SWE-bench Pro, which tests resolution of real issues against open-source repositories, it posts 64.3 percent, an eleven-point jump from 4.6's 53.4 percent and well ahead of OpenAI's GPT-5.4 at 57.7 percent and Google's Gemini 3.1 Pro at 54.2 percent. On CursorBench, the autonomous-coding eval that has become a de facto test of IDE-grade agents, 4.7 lands at 70 percent versus 4.6's 58 percent.

"Opus 4.7 is where we put the model that most enterprises should actually run in production," Anthropic Chief Product Officer Mike Krieger said in a briefing with reporters. "Mythos is the frontier, but 4.7 is the workhorse, and on the benchmarks that matter for real engineering work it is now within arm's reach of the frontier."

The vision improvements are the other half of the story. Anthropic says 4.7 can ingest images at substantially higher resolution than 4.6, a change that matters less for consumer photo tasks than for the dense screenshots, diagrams, PDFs and CAD renders that enterprise workflows actually feed into the model. Early Glasswing tester Figma said in a statement that 4.7 "reads our design files the way a senior product engineer would," and Anthropic is pitching the upgrade as the missing piece for customers trying to build agents that operate computers and review UI flows end to end.

Alongside the model, Anthropic shipped a redesigned Claude Code desktop app and pushed Claude Cowork, its shared-context workspace product, to general availability on macOS and Windows. It also opened a preview of Routines, a scheduler that lets Claude agents run recurring jobs against a user's connected tools without re-prompting. VentureBeat, which tested the redesigned Claude Code ahead of launch, described Routines as "the first credible attempt by a frontier lab to sell an always-on agent to IT departments without asking them to stand up their own orchestration stack."

Chief Executive Dario Amodei framed the incremental release as deliberate. "We think the responsible path is to ship the capabilities we can broadly deploy today, with the safeguards we can broadly deploy today," Amodei said on a call with analysts. "Opus 4.7 is that model. Mythos is a different conversation, and we are taking our time on it on purpose." Anthropic has acknowledged that 4.7's cybersecurity capabilities were differentially suppressed during training and that the released model carries automatic blocks on prohibited security-tooling requests, safeguards Mythos Preview does not yet ship with.

Chief Science Officer Jared Kaplan, in a separate post, described the gains as "a lot of quiet infrastructure work finally paying off in the eval numbers," crediting improvements to the post-training stack and to how the model verifies its own outputs on long-running tasks. Enterprise customers appear to agree. Stripe engineering lead Ritu Menon, one of the on-record references Anthropic lined up for launch, said her team saw "a meaningful step up on multi-file refactors and on understanding our internal design system from screenshots alone" during private testing.

The strategic read is straightforward. Anthropic is executing a two-track release cadence: a cautious, broadly available Opus line that gets pushed forward every few months, and a gated frontier Mythos line available only to vetted Glasswing partners under stricter governance. That lets Anthropic bank revenue on the safer model, whose annualized run rate the company has said is approaching $10 billion, while it continues red-teaming and deployment planning on Mythos. It also gives it a credible answer when competitors launch their own frontier systems: 4.7 now leads every major coding leaderboard, even if it is, by Anthropic's own admission, not the most capable model the company has trained.

What to watch next. Anthropic has hinted that Mythos general availability is unlikely before late summer, and Amodei declined on Thursday to commit to a date. Investors will be watching whether the 4.7 release accelerates enterprise ARR growth through the second quarter, whether Routines moves out of preview in time for customers to build around it before re:Invent, and whether rivals respond with price cuts rather than new models. For now, Anthropic has bought itself the one thing the frontier race rarely offers: a few months of daylight at the top of the benchmarks without having to open the most dangerous door in the lab.
