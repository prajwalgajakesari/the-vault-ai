# White House Moves to Preempt State AI Laws, Setting Federal Floor for Frontier Models

## A federal floor, by executive fiat

President Trump on April 17 signed an executive order instructing federal agencies to treat a growing thicket of state artificial intelligence laws as preempted wherever they conflict with a new national framework to be administered by the National Institute of Standards and Technology. The order, titled Establishing a Unified National Framework for Artificial Intelligence, is the administration's most aggressive attempt yet to clear the field for frontier AI developers who have spent the past eighteen months warning that compliance costs from overlapping state regimes are becoming unworkable.

Senior White House officials, briefing reporters ahead of the signing, framed the move as a competitiveness measure rather than a deregulatory one. The order directs NIST to publish binding evaluation, red-teaming, and disclosure standards for frontier models trained above a 10^26 FLOP threshold within 120 days, and designates the Commerce Department as the lead enforcement agency. A 50-state patchwork would cripple American AI leadership before it ever crosses the finish line, one official said.

## The preemption mechanism

The legal architecture leans on two levers the administration believes can survive court review without fresh legislation from Congress. The first is a spending-conditions regime modeled loosely on the Section 253 telecommunications precedent: states that wish to receive federal AI-related grants, Department of Energy compute credits, or to sell covered AI services into federal procurement must certify that their own statutes do not impose requirements materially inconsistent with the NIST framework. Roughly $12 billion in fiscal 2026 and 2027 funding is conditioned in this way, according to an accompanying OMB memorandum.

The second lever is a conflict-preemption declaration. The order instructs the Department of Justice to file statements of interest in any private litigation arising under state AI laws and to argue that those statutes are preempted where they duplicate, exceed, or contradict the federal floor. California's SB 53 and its 2025 amendment SB 53A, New York's RAISE Act, and Colorado's AI Act are named explicitly in an appendix as candidates for preemption review. Provisions targeting frontier-model incident reporting, pre-deployment evaluations, and algorithmic discrimination in high-risk deployments would be most exposed.

Notably, the order carves out consumer protection, deceptive trade practices, and tort remedies, apparently acknowledging that a broader preemption would collide with longstanding state police powers and invite a steeper Tenth Amendment challenge.

## Reactions split along familiar lines

Industry response was swift and largely favorable among the largest labs. Trade groups representing OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, and Meta praised the move as bringing regulatory coherence, though several companies declined to comment on the record. Smaller open-source developers were more cautious, warning that a NIST-centered compliance regime could still entrench incumbents if evaluation requirements are expensive to meet.

State attorneys general reacted with open hostility. California's Rob Bonta, New York's Letitia James, and Colorado's Phil Weiser issued a joint statement within hours calling the order a lawless attempt to commandeer state legislatures and pledging to file suit. At least eleven Democratic AGs are expected to join, with multi-district litigation likely to land in the Ninth and Second Circuits. Legal scholars interviewed by Reuters noted that conflict-preemption arguments unsupported by an underlying federal statute tend to fare poorly, particularly after the Supreme Court's recent skepticism of broad executive preemption claims.

Civil society groups were similarly critical. The Center for Democracy and Technology, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, and the AI Now Institute each argued the NIST floor is substantively thinner than California's disclosure regime, particularly on post-deployment monitoring and whistleblower protections. Labor groups highlighted that the order does nothing to address workplace AI surveillance, an area where state legislatures have been most active.

On Capitol Hill, reaction cut across party lines in unexpected ways. Senator Ted Cruz, who has championed federal preemption legislation, welcomed the order but warned it is no substitute for a statute. Senator Josh Hawley called the spending conditions a giveaway to Big Tech. Senate Commerce Chair Maria Cantwell said her committee would hold hearings in May.

## What to watch

The first legal flashpoint will likely come when California moves to enforce SB 53A's September reporting deadline against a frontier lab that invokes the federal framework as a shield. Gibson Dunn, in a client alert circulated Friday, advised developers to maintain dual compliance postures until at least one circuit court rules. A preliminary injunction against the spending conditions could arrive before summer; a definitive ruling on conflict preemption is unlikely before 2027.

For now, the order reshapes the battlefield without ending the war. The federal floor exists, whether it holds is a question for the courts.
