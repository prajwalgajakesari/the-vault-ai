New York Locks In RAISE Act With Final Chapter Amendment, Setting 2027 Deadline for Frontier AI Developers

Governor Hochul's March 27 chapter amendment finalizes the RAISE Act, New York's frontier AI safety law, cutting peak penalties but preserving 72-hour incident reporting and whistleblower protections ahead of a January 1, 2027 effective date.

Governor Kathy Hochul on March 27 signed the chapter amendment that finalizes New York's Responsible AI Safety and Education Act, closing a nine-month negotiation that began when she conditionally approved the original bill in December and ending any remaining doubt about the shape of the nation's most aggressive state-level frontier AI law. The amended statute takes effect January 1, 2027, giving covered developers roughly nine months to stand up safety programs, incident-reporting pipelines, and whistleblower notices before New York's Department of Financial Services can begin assessing penalties.

The final package, carried by State Senator Andrew Gounardes and Assemblymember Alex Bores, lowers the headline fines that had drawn the loudest industry objections. The maximum civil penalty drops from $10 million to $1 million for a first violation, and from $30 million to $3 million for repeat violations, while preserving the core obligations that make the RAISE Act distinct from any other U.S. AI statute now on the books. A new oversight office inside DFS, funded through fees on the developers it regulates, will issue rules, review safety plans, and publish an annual public report on frontier AI risk in New York.

## Who is covered, and what they must do

The RAISE Act applies to large frontier developers: companies with at least $500 million in annual revenue that train models using more than 10^26 floating-point operations or spend more than $100 million in compute on a single training run. That two-pronged threshold is deliberately narrow. It captures OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI, and a short list of others, while leaving academic labs and most startups outside the perimeter.

Covered developers must write, implement, and publish a safety and security protocol describing how they assess catastrophic risks, apply mitigations, commission third-party evaluations, defend model weights against theft, and govern internal compliance. The law retains its 72-hour clock for reporting critical safety incidents to the state and adds a tighter 24-hour window for incidents posing an imminent risk of death or serious physical injury. Developers are also barred from deploying a frontier model that poses an unreasonable risk of critical harm, a liability hook that survived the amendment intact.

Whistleblower provisions are among the most expansive in any U.S. technology statute. Employees and contractors who report conduct posing a substantial risk of critical harm to the Attorney General, DFS, or internal channels are protected from retaliation, and developers must post notices and brief new hires on those rights.

## How it stacks up against California, Brussels, and Washington

The chapter amendment visibly pulls New York closer to California's Transparency in Frontier Artificial Intelligence Act, signed into law last year after Governor Gavin Newsom vetoed the more expansive SB 1047. New York and California now share the same revenue and compute thresholds for large frontier developer, and both require published frameworks, third-party evaluations, and whistleblower protections. New York is stricter in two respects that matter: its 72-hour incident clock is dramatically faster than California's 15 days, and its deployment prohibition on unreasonably risky models has no direct analog in SB 53.

Against the EU AI Act, the contrast is one of architecture rather than stringency. Brussels regulates AI horizontally by use case and risk tier, with separate obligations for general-purpose models above 10^25 FLOPs. New York ignores downstream use cases entirely and regulates a handful of upstream labs, closer in spirit to the EU's GPAI Code of Practice than to the Act's risk classification scheme.

The White House has pushed in the opposite direction. The Trump administration's AI Action Plan and accompanying legislative framework call for federal preemption of state AI laws, arguing a patchwork will hobble U.S. competitiveness. That position has split the industry. OpenAI and Anthropic have signaled they can operate under aligned New York and California regimes; the Chamber of Progress, the Business Roundtable, and the Leading the Future super PAC have lobbied hard against the RAISE Act, calling it duplicative and chilling. More than 50 congressional Republicans have publicly broken with the administration's preemption push, complicating any near-term federal override.

For frontier labs, the practical effect is that compliance planning can no longer wait. Safety frameworks drafted for California now need a New York overlay, incident-response playbooks need a 72-hour trigger, and general counsels have roughly three quarters to get it done.
