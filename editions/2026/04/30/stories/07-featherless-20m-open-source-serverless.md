# Featherless AI Raises $20 Million to Make 30,000 Open-Source Models Available on Demand

AMD Ventures and Airbus Ventures co-lead a Series A bet that the future of AI inference belongs to open models running on diverse hardware -- not proprietary stacks controlled by a handful of giants.

---

A two-year-old startup born out of an open-source research community has raised $20 million to prove that enterprises should not have to choose between the convenience of cloud AI and the freedom of open-source models. Featherless.ai, a Singapore-headquartered company founded in 2024, announced its Series A round on Wednesday, with AMD Ventures and Airbus Ventures co-leading the investment. BMW i Ventures, Kickstart Ventures, Panache Ventures, and Wavemaker Ventures also participated.

The capital will fund three priorities: scaling global infrastructure, launching a dedicated marketplace for specialized open models, and deepening technical integration across diverse hardware architectures to continue driving down the cost of AI inference.

## The Problem With Open-Source AI Today

Open-source AI models have proliferated at a staggering pace. Hugging Face alone hosts hundreds of thousands of models, and new fine-tuned variants appear daily. But there is a brutal gap between downloading a model and running it reliably in production. Developers face a gauntlet of GPU provisioning, cold-start latency, compatibility headaches, and unpredictable per-token pricing that makes budgeting nearly impossible.

Featherless.ai attacks this problem with a serverless inference platform that makes more than 30,000 open models -- spanning language, vision, and audio -- available through a single API. The company says it has solved the cold-start challenge, enabling models to load within seconds while maintaining high GPU utilization. Instead of traditional per-token pricing, Featherless offers a flat subscription model designed to give customers cost certainty at scale.

"We're building the infrastructure that makes open-source AI practical and reliable at scale, ensuring that enterprises can build on a foundation they actually own rather than one they merely rent," said Eugene Cheah, co-founder and CEO of Featherless.ai.

## An Open-Source Pedigree

The founding team is not a group of enterprise software veterans chasing the AI boom. Cheah, along with CTO Harrison Vanderbyl and COO Wesley George, emerged from the RWKV open-source community. RWKV is a novel model architecture developed under the Linux Foundation that was designed to challenge the dominance of transformer-based models by offering more efficient inference characteristics. That technical background gives Featherless a level of credibility in the open-source AI world that money alone cannot buy.

The team's deep roots in open-source development also shape the company's philosophy on hardware. Through a strategic collaboration with AMD, Featherless.ai ensures that its most popular open-source models run natively on AMD's ROCm software stack. In an industry where Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem has long held a near-monopoly on AI compute, this is a deliberate bet on hardware diversity.

"When a few dominant players control the entire stack, it stifles competition and limits what developers can imagine," Cheah said. "This investment signals a turning point in the AI market."

## Why AMD, Airbus, and BMW Are Paying Attention

The investor lineup tells a story about where enterprise AI demand is heading. AMD Ventures' involvement is straightforward: every open-source model that runs well on ROCm instead of CUDA expands AMD's addressable market in the data center GPU wars. For AMD, Featherless is both a technical partner and a distribution channel.

Airbus Ventures and BMW i Ventures represent something different -- industrial giants that need AI inference at scale but have strong reasons to avoid locking themselves into a single proprietary stack. Aerospace and automotive companies operate in regulated environments where auditability, data sovereignty, and supply-chain diversification are not abstract principles but operational requirements. Open-source models running on heterogeneous hardware give these enterprises a structural cost advantage and a hedge against vendor concentration risk.

The participation of Kickstart Ventures, Panache Ventures, and Wavemaker rounds out the syndicate with investors who bring geographic reach across Southeast Asia, Canada, and the broader Asia-Pacific region -- markets where Featherless.ai's Singapore headquarters provides a natural beachhead.

## The Marketplace Bet

Perhaps the most strategically interesting element of the announcement is the planned marketplace for specialized models. While Featherless currently serves as infrastructure -- plumbing that connects models to production -- a marketplace would position the company as a discovery and distribution layer for the long tail of fine-tuned and domain-specific models that enterprises increasingly need.

This is a crowded ambition. Hugging Face, Together AI, Replicate, and others all occupy adjacent territory. But Featherless' combination of serverless delivery, flat pricing, and hardware-agnostic deployment could carve out a distinct niche, particularly for enterprises that want to run specialized models without managing their own GPU clusters or negotiating complex cloud contracts.

## What to Watch

Featherless.ai is still early. Founded just two years ago and now sitting on roughly $25 million in total funding including its earlier seed round, the company is dwarfed by competitors with hundreds of millions in backing. The test will be whether its serverless approach and subscription pricing can win enough enterprise workloads to sustain the infrastructure costs that come with serving 30,000-plus models on demand.

The AMD partnership is the variable to watch most closely. If Featherless can demonstrate that production AI workloads run reliably and affordably on ROCm, it will not just grow its own business -- it will help reshape the competitive dynamics of the entire AI compute market. For an industry that talks constantly about openness but remains deeply dependent on a single chipmaker's software stack, that would be a meaningful shift.
