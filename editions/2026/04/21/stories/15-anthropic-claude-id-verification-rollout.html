# Anthropic Rolls Out Government ID Verification for Some Claude Users

The AI safety company now requires a passport or drivers license -- plus a live selfie -- for users flagged for suspicious activity, making it the first major chatbot maker to demand biometric identity checks.

If you have been using Claude and suddenly get asked to hold up your passport to a webcam, you are not alone. Anthropic quietly updated its support documentation last week to confirm that some Claude users will now be required to submit a government-issued photo ID and a live selfie to continue accessing certain features -- a first among leading AI chatbot providers, and a move that has ignited fierce debate over where the line falls between platform safety and user privacy.

The verification process, handled by third-party identity firm Persona Identities, takes under five minutes and accepts passports, drivers licenses, state IDs, and national identity cards. Photocopies, screenshots, digital or mobile IDs, student cards, and temporary paper documents are all rejected. Users must use a camera-equipped device to capture a live selfie that is matched against their ID photo.

Who Gets Flagged -- and Why

Anthropic says the prompts are not universal. An Anthropic spokesperson told Engadget that "this applies to a small number of cases where we see activity that indicates potentially fraudulent or abusive behavior, which violates our usage policy." Users may also encounter verification when accessing certain advanced capabilities or during routine platform integrity checks.

The policy sits within a broader effort to prevent access to powerful AI models by actors in adversary nations -- including China, Russia, and North Korea -- and to comply with evolving legal obligations around AI safety. According to Anthropic's official support page, "Identity verification helps us prevent abuse, enforce our usage policies, and comply with legal obligations."

Some users have reported encountering the verification prompt during signup for the Max subscription tier, suggesting that premium access may be one of the triggers. Anthropic has not published a comprehensive list of scenarios that activate the requirement.

Privacy Assurances -- and Skepticism

Anthropic is at pains to reassure users about data handling. The company says it acts as data controller, while Persona operates as the processor under contractual restrictions. ID images and selfies are stored by Persona, not on Anthropic's own systems, and all data is encrypted in transit and at rest.

"We set the rules for how it's used and how long it's kept," Anthropic stated in its support documentation. "Persona is contractually limited in how they can use your data: only to provide and support verification and to improve their ability to prevent fraud." The company also pledged that identity data will not be used to train its AI models, will not be shared with third parties for marketing, and will be deleted according to retention limits set by Anthropic and applicable law.

But critics are not convinced. Persona's own history has drawn scrutiny: the San Francisco-based firm counts Founders Fund -- co-founded by Palantir co-founder Peter Thiel -- among its major investors. Earlier in 2026, a security researcher reported finding Persona's uncompressed front-end code exposed on a U.S. government server, fueling speculation about surveillance ties. Persona denied any connection to DHS or ICE while acknowledging government contracts related to workforce security. The company also uses multiple subprocessors including AWS, Google, OpenAI, and Stripe, creating additional potential data exposure points.

Anthropic has declined to specify exact data retention timeframes, a gap that privacy advocates have highlighted as a concern.

Industry Outlier -- For Now

The policy makes Anthropic an outlier among top-tier AI providers. Neither OpenAI nor Google requires government ID verification for regular consumer access to ChatGPT or Gemini, though OpenAI does require a "Verified Organization" process with government IDs for API developers seeking access to its most advanced models. Discord attempted a similar Persona-based age verification rollout in February 2026 but delayed it following significant user backlash.

The user reaction to Anthropic's move has been similarly heated. Discussions on Reddit and Hacker News have featured subscribers threatening to cancel their plans, with many questioning why an AI chatbot needs passport-level identity verification. The criticism is particularly pointed given that Anthropic has built its brand around safety and responsible AI -- values that some users feel are now in tension with collecting biometric data.

The Bigger Picture

Anthropic's decision reflects a growing tension across the AI industry: as models become more capable, the pressure to restrict access to verified, trustworthy users intensifies. Export controls, executive orders on AI safety, and international pressure to prevent adversaries from leveraging frontier models are all pushing companies toward stricter gatekeeping.

Whether ID verification becomes an industry norm or remains a controversial Anthropic experiment will depend largely on how users respond with their wallets -- and whether regulators start mandating similar measures. For now, Anthropic is betting that the security benefits outweigh the privacy tradeoffs. Not everyone is buying it.
