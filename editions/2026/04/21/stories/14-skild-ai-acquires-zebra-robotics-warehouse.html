# Skild AI Acquires Zebra Technologies Robotics Unit to Build AI Warehouse Fleet Brain

Any robot. Any warehouse. One brain. Now at scale.

A Pittsburgh-based startup that barely existed three years ago just made the boldest move yet in the race to automate the world's warehouses. Skild AI announced on April 15 that it has acquired Zebra Technologies' Robotics Automation business -- including the battle-tested Symmetry Fulfillment orchestration platform -- in a deal whose financial terms were not disclosed. The transaction fuses Skild's "omnibodied" AI foundation model with Zebra's enterprise-grade fleet management software, creating what the company calls the first end-to-end intelligent automation stack for logistics.

The combined entity can now theoretically deploy humanoids for pick-and-place, robotic dogs for inspection, industrial arms for packing, and autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) for material movement -- all coordinated by a single AI layer. It is a vision that takes direct aim at the fragmented status quo, where warehouses cobble together proprietary systems from multiple vendors that cannot talk to each other.

The Skild Brain: One Model, Every Body

At the heart of the deal is the Skild Brain, a large-scale multimodal AI model that processes sensor data -- vision, lidar, force feedback -- to generate real-time motion commands for robots it has never been specifically trained on. Unlike traditional controllers requiring bespoke software for each platform, the Skild Brain abstracts away robot kinematics, allowing a single policy network to operate across disparate morphologies: quadrupeds, humanoids, tabletop arms, and mobile manipulators alike.

"Warehouse automation today is extremely fragmented," said Deepak Pathak, Skild co-founder and CEO. "Through this deal, Skild has an opportunity to redefine how humans and various kinds of robots can work together."

That flexibility is what Skild means by "omnibodied intelligence." The same underlying model generalizes across all robot forms without retraining -- a capability that, if it delivers on its promise, could collapse integration timelines by up to 40 percent compared with traditional vendor-specific deployments, according to a Forrester benchmark cited by industry analysts.

Symmetry: The Missing Orchestration Layer

What Skild lacked before this acquisition was a proven way to coordinate dozens or hundreds of robots simultaneously in live warehouse environments. Zebra's Symmetry platform fills that gap. Originally developed through Zebra's 2021 acquisition of Fetch Robotics for 290 million dollars, Symmetry handles scheduling, fleet management, task allocation, collision avoidance, and dynamic re-routing across multiple robots and human operators in real time, using data from wearable devices worn by frontline workers.

Every new deployment now feeds the Skild Brain's data flywheel, making the system smarter across every environment it operates in. The result is one intelligent layer that can run an entire warehouse -- not just isolated pockets of automation.

Funding Firepower

Skild AI is not short on capital. In January 2026, the company closed a massive 1.4 billion dollar Series C round led by SoftBank Group, valuing the startup at over 14 billion dollars. The round drew participation from NVIDIA Ventures, Macquarie Capital, Jeff Bezos through Bezos Expeditions, and returning backers Lightspeed, Sequoia Capital, Coatue, and Felicis. Strategic investors included Samsung, LG, Schneider, and Salesforce Ventures. The company had previously raised more than 500 million dollars in earlier rounds led by GV with NVIDIA participation.

The funding trajectory tells its own story: Skild grew from zero to approximately 30 million dollars in revenue in just a few months during 2025, a pace virtually unheard of in hardware-adjacent AI.

"We believe this omni-bodied learning is essential for building AGI that works reliably in the physical world," said Abhinav Gupta, Skild co-founder and president.

Competitive Landscape

The acquisition positions Skild against formidable incumbents. Amazon Robotics dominates within its own fulfillment network. Boston Dynamics and GreyOrange each offer proprietary ecosystems. But Skild's bet is that the market will shift toward open, modular architectures -- a single brain that works with any hardware -- rather than locked-in vendor stacks.

The timing aligns with broader industry momentum. IDC projects AI-driven warehouse automation will reach a 12 billion dollar market by 2028, while Gartner predicts 30 percent of global warehouse operations will be fully automated by 2027. Yet only 12 percent of current deployments achieve cross-compatible integration across robot types -- precisely the problem Skild claims to solve.

What Comes Next

The deal also carries a certain poetic symmetry. Alan Zhuolun Zhao, now at Skild AI, previously worked on the Fetch Robotics technology that Zebra acquired. "It's where I first learned the unforgiving world of deployment," Zhao said. "Now everything has come full circle."

For warehouse operators weighing their automation strategies, the message from Skild is clear: stop buying robots and start buying intelligence. Whether the Skild Brain can truly deliver on the promise of one model controlling every machine in a facility -- reliably, safely, at enterprise scale -- will be the defining test of this acquisition. But with nearly two billion dollars in total funding, partnerships with NVIDIA and ABB Robotics, and now a proven orchestration platform under its roof, Skild AI has assembled the most complete toolkit any robotics AI company has ever held.

The warehouse of the future may not need a dozen software vendors. It may just need one brain.
