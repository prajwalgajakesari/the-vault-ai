# ICLR 2026 Opens in Rio de Janeiro With 3,462 Papers and 10 Outstanding Awards

The machine learning world descends on Rio de Janeiro this week for the International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR) 2026, where 3,462 papers will be presented alongside a slate of 10 Outstanding Paper Awards that spotlight some of the most consequential research directions in AI today -- from ethically sourced training data to safer alignment techniques.

Running April 23-27 at the Riocentro Convention and Event Center, ICLR 2026 drew approximately 19,525 valid submissions, making it the largest edition of the conference yet. After desk rejections and withdrawals, program chairs rendered final decisions on 13,763 papers, accepting 5,355 for an overall acceptance rate of roughly 27.4% -- the lowest in three years. Of those, around 100 earned coveted oral presentation slots, approximately 900 were selected as spotlights, and the remaining 4,350 will be presented as posters.

## Ten Papers Rise to the Top

The Outstanding Paper Awards span a remarkably wide range of topics. Common Corpus, the largest openly licensed dataset for LLM pre-training at roughly two trillion tokens, was recognized for tackling one of the most pressing problems in AI: copyright-compliant training data. Q-RAG, which trains retrieval embedders through reinforcement learning for multi-step long-context question answering, reflects a broader trend the program committee highlighted -- the convergence of retrieval and reasoning into dynamic, iterative systems.

Other winners include FIRE, which addresses the stability-plasticity tradeoff through Frobenius-isometry reinitialization; WebDevJudge, a framework for evaluating LLMs as judges of web development quality; SafeDPO, which augments direct preference optimization with safety constraints; and MedAgentGym, a scalable training environment featuring 72,413 biomedical tasks for code-centric agents.

Rounding out the list are papers on exchangeability in graph neural network representations, generative auto-bidding for advertising, cognitive map formation through movement modeling, and a pointed critique exposing statistical misspecification in the widely used DPO alignment method -- a paper that signals growing maturity in the alignment subfield.

## CMU Leads the Institutional Pack

Carnegie Mellon University will present 194 papers at the conference, spanning oral and poster sessions across 14 research categories. Notable CMU contributions include UALM, a Unified Audio Language Model that handles audio understanding, text-to-audio generation, and multimodal reasoning in a single architecture, as well as Mamba-3, an efficient sequence modeling approach, and EditBench, which evaluates code-editing capabilities in LLMs using real-world contexts.

Imperial College London also highlighted its presence, with researchers contributing work on crystal structure prediction, diffusion language models, and privacy-preserving AI. The department emphasized that its acceptances demonstrate strength in translating foundational advances in argumentation, formal verification, and reinforcement learning into practical, deployable systems.

## A Review Process Under Siege

This year conference arrives under a cloud of controversy. In November 2025, a malicious actor exploited the OpenReview API, exposing the identities of authors, reviewers, and area chairs for a large subset of submissions. The breach triggered reports of collusion attempts, threats, and harassment directed at reviewers. Program chairs responded by resetting all review scores to their pre-rebuttal state, freezing discussions, and reassigning area chairs to infer outcomes from completed deliberations.

The integrity crisis did not stop there. Analysis by Carnegie Mellon professor Graham Neubig found that 21% of 75,800 public ICLR review comments were highly suspected of being entirely AI-generated. Some submitters reported that every single review they received appeared to be machine-written, complete with hallucinated citations and fabricated criticisms. Researcher Liu Yong called for more professional reviewers to support theoretical research, noting that the automated review epidemic has created particular challenges for papers in certain domains.

The ICLR 2026 program chairs deployed two independent LLM detection systems across all 76,139 reviews from 18,054 reviewers and flagged suspicious content to area chairs for human verification.

## Looking Ahead

Despite the turbulence in peer review, the research on display in Rio reflects a field that is rapidly maturing. The Outstanding Paper selections reveal a clear pivot from raw scale toward efficiency, safety, and domain-specific rigor. Model compression, quantization, and reasoning chain condensation dominate the accepted papers, while custom evaluation frameworks for real-world deployment are steadily replacing generic leaderboard benchmarks.

As thousands of researchers gather along Guanabara Bay this week, the conversations in the hallways may prove as consequential as the papers on the walls -- particularly around how the community can safeguard the integrity of peer review in an era when the very models being evaluated are also writing the evaluations.
