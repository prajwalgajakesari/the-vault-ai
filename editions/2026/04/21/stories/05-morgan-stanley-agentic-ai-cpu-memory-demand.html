# Morgan Stanley Says Agentic AI Will Add $32.5 to $60 Billion to Data-Center CPU Market by 2030

The bank argues the AI investment cycle is broadening beyond GPUs, with CPUs becoming the "control layer" for autonomous systems and memory emerging as the durable choke point.

The artificial intelligence boom has been a GPU story. Morgan Stanley thinks the next chapter will be written in CPUs and memory chips.

In a sweeping research note published Sunday, Morgan Stanley estimated that the rise of so-called agentic AI -- systems that can plan, sequence, and execute multistep tasks with minimal human oversight -- could add between $32.5 billion and $60 billion to the data-center CPU market by 2030. That market already exceeds $100 billion, meaning the bank is projecting growth of roughly 30 to 60 percent on top of an already massive base.

"As AI transitions from generation to autonomous action, the computing bottleneck is shifting towards CPU and memory, driving a step-change in general-purpose compute intensity," Morgan Stanley wrote, adding that demand for graphics processing units remains strong.

The thesis represents a meaningful reframing of the AI infrastructure trade. For the past three years, capital spending on AI has been overwhelmingly concentrated in GPU accelerators, primarily from Nvidia. Morgan Stanley is not disputing that demand. Instead, the bank argues that agentic workloads introduce an entirely new layer of compute requirements -- one that GPUs alone cannot satisfy.

From Computation to Orchestration

The distinction hinges on how agentic AI systems operate. Unlike conventional large language models that process a single prompt and return a response, agentic systems coordinate across multiple tools, datasets, and sub-tasks. They retain context over longer durations, store intermediate states, and repeatedly access large volumes of data. That architecture demands persistent inference and continuous orchestration -- functions that fall squarely on CPUs and memory, not GPUs.

Morgan Stanley describes CPUs as increasingly functioning as the "control layer" in these environments, responsible for orchestrating task execution, managing dependencies between processes, and coordinating interactions between models and external systems. Analyst Shawn Kim, Morgan Stanley's head of Asia technology research, has framed it as a "structural shift from computation to orchestration."

The memory implications are equally significant. The bank projects an additional 15 to 45 exabytes of DRAM demand from agent workloads by 2030. To put that in perspective, a single exabyte equals one billion gigabytes. Kim has previously noted that "memory access increasingly determines the performance of longer text, image/video and agentic AI workflows," requiring more advanced server DRAM and enterprise NAND to support context-heavy, continuously learning systems.

Memory as the Durable Bottleneck

Perhaps the most actionable element of the report is Morgan Stanley's call on memory stocks. The bank flagged memory as a "durable choke point," with supply constrained through at least 2027. DRAM slack, the analysts wrote, is effectively gone.

"Our favorite way to play CPU strength is through memory stocks," the note stated. Morgan Stanley named Micron Technology and SanDisk as its preferred plays, maintaining Overweight ratings on both. The bank set a price target of $520 for Micron and $690 for SanDisk, citing tight data-center supply conditions and structural tailwinds from the explosion in high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and enterprise SSD demand.

The logic is straightforward: as CPU throughput rises in data centers, memory bandwidth must keep pace. Memory makers sit at the intersection of every AI workload -- training, inference, and now orchestration -- giving them exposure to the full breadth of the spending cycle.

A Wider Beneficiary List

Beyond memory, Morgan Stanley identified a broad roster of potential winners. In CPUs and accelerators, the bank pointed to Nvidia, AMD, Intel, and Arm Holdings. Intel's entrenched position in server CPUs aligns directly with the anticipated surge in general-purpose compute demand, while Arm's power-efficient architecture continues gaining traction in data centers where persistent, complex workloads make energy consumption a growing concern.

In memory, Samsung Electronics and SK hynix join Micron and SanDisk as beneficiaries. Upstream, TSMC and ASML stand to gain as chipmakers race to meet AI's expanding needs across multiple product categories.

What It Means for the AI Trade

Morgan Stanley's note arrives at an inflection point. After two years of near-singular focus on GPU procurement, hyperscale cloud providers are beginning to build out the broader infrastructure needed to support always-on, multi-agent systems. If the bank's projections hold, the AI capital expenditure cycle is not narrowing -- it is widening into a full-stack infrastructure trade that touches every layer of the semiconductor supply chain.

For investors, the takeaway is that the AI hardware story is no longer just about who makes the fastest accelerator. It is about who controls the bottlenecks. And right now, Morgan Stanley is betting those bottlenecks are in memory.
