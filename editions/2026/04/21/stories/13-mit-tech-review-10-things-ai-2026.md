# MIT Technology Review Unveils 10 Things That Matter in AI Right Now for 2026

**The publication's new annual list goes beyond breakthrough technologies to map the forces, fears, and frontiers reshaping artificial intelligence today.**

MIT Technology Review has never lacked ambition when it comes to cataloging the future, but on April 21 the publication acknowledged something its editors could no longer ignore: artificial intelligence has grown too large, too fast, and too consequential to share a single list with quantum sensors and synthetic biology. The result is a brand-new annual franchise--"10 Things That Matter in AI Right Now"--unveiled at the EmTech AI conference on MIT's campus and designed to complement, not replace, the venerable 10 Breakthrough Technologies list that has run for over two decades.

"We had so many worthy AI candidates that we couldn't fit them all in," executive editors Niall Firth and Amy Nordrum wrote in the announcement. Rather than restricting themselves to nominating only technologies, they explained, the team wanted to spotlight "the biggest ideas, topics, and research directions in AI" along with the societal tremors those advances are triggering. "These are the things that our reporters will be watching this year."

The ten entries read less like a product catalog and more like a geopolitical mood board. They span humanoid robot training data, the continued evolution of large language models, supercharged scams and weaponized deepfakes, world models that push AI beyond text boxes, generative AI in military war rooms, multi-agent orchestration, China's open-source model strategy, AI co-scientists, and a mounting global resistance movement against unfettered AI development.

## China's Open-Weight Gamble

Perhaps the most strategically significant entry is China's open-source bet. While American labs largely keep their most capable models behind APIs, Chinese labs--DeepSeek, Alibaba's Qwen, MiniMax, and others--are shipping frontier systems as downloadable open-weight packages that anyone can adapt and run on their own hardware.

The numbers tell a striking story. Chinese open-weight models accounted for 17.1 percent of global AI model downloads in the year ending August 2025, surpassing the US share of 15.86 percent--the first time China led this metric. Alibaba's Qwen models now have more user-generated variants than models from Google and Meta combined. Governments are taking notice: Singapore's state-backed AI program chose Qwen over Meta's Llama for its regional model, and Malaysia announced its sovereign AI ecosystem would run on DeepSeek technology.

"Keep the secret sauce behind an API, and charge for every drop. China's leading AI labs are playing a different game," wrote MIT Technology Review's Caiwei Chen. The strategy went mainstream after DeepSeek open-sourced its R1 reasoning model in January 2025, which matched the performance of the best American systems reportedly at a fraction of the cost.

## The Capabilities Surge--and Its Shadow

The list arrives against a backdrop of staggering capability gains. According to MIT Technology Review's companion data analysis, AI now meets or exceeds human expert performance on PhD-level science, math, and language benchmarks. Software engineering scores on the SWE-bench Verified leaderboard surged from roughly 60 percent in 2024 to nearly 100 percent in 2025. Over half of the global population now uses AI tools--88 percent of organizations and 80 percent of university students have adopted the technology--a pace of uptake faster than PCs or the internet.

But capability and anxiety are rising in lockstep. The list's inclusion of "Resistance" as a standalone entry signals that the backlash is no longer background noise. US states passed 150 AI-related bills in 2025 alone. The EU AI Act's prohibitions took effect, and Japan, South Korea, and Italy enacted national AI laws. Public opinion data shows 52 percent of people now report feeling nervous about AI, even as 59 percent believe it will ultimately provide more benefits than drawbacks.

The human cost is already visible in certain sectors. Software developer employment among 22-to-25-year-olds has declined nearly 20 percent since 2022, even as AI boosts productivity 26 percent in the same field--a paradox that helps explain why the backlash unites constituencies as diverse as artists, labor unions, conservatives, and civil libertarians.

## Scams, Deepfakes, and the Battlefield

Two entries--supercharged scams and weaponized deepfakes--address the darker applications head-on. AI is making phishing attempts faster, cheaper, and more convincing, while synthetic media tools enable nonconsensual imagery and state-level disinformation at scale. A separate entry on "the new war room" examines how generative AI is reshaping military intelligence-sharing and lethal decision-making, a topic that has drawn scrutiny from defense analysts and human rights organizations alike.

## Looking Ahead

The list's breadth is the point. By yoking humanoid robot training centers--where gig workers overseas puppet bipedal machines--to the quiet revolution in mechanistic interpretability, and by placing AI co-scientists alongside the global resistance movement, MIT Technology Review is arguing that no single lens can capture what AI means in 2026.

"I am stunned that this technology continues to improve" without plateauing, noted Yolanda Gil, a prominent AI researcher, in the publication's companion data report. With global AI data centers now consuming 29.6 gigawatts--equivalent to New York City's peak electricity demand--and OpenAI's GPT-4o alone requiring enough water annually to supply drinking needs for 1.2 million people, the resources flowing into this field show no sign of slowing down.

Whether that momentum produces Nobel Prize-worthy science or a regulatory reckoning may depend on which of these ten things the world decides to pay attention to first.
