# Chinese Scientists Achieve 1,000x Faster 2D Chip Growth

Chinese researchers have developed a method to manufacture two-dimensional semiconductor films at speeds roughly 1,000 times faster than previous techniques. The breakthrough, published in the National Science Review, addresses one of the most critical bottlenecks in next-generation chip production.

The team, led by Zhu Mengjian at the National University of Defence Technology, reimagined the chemical vapor deposition process by introducing a liquid gold and tungsten bilayer as the growth substrate. The new method achieves a growth rate of approximately 20 micrometers per minute.

"The lack of p-type materials has become a critical bottleneck for the development of sub-5-nanometer node 2D semiconductors," Zhu Mengjian noted. Their method enables tungsten silicon nitride films with superior hole mobility and high on-state current density, opening applications in LEDs, photodetectors, and bioelectronic interfaces.

China's advance comes as traditional silicon transistors reach physical limits at sub-3-nanometer scales. Two-dimensional materials, substances just a few atoms thick, represent one of the most credible pathways forward for continued transistor scaling while reducing power consumption.

The path from laboratory success to commercial production remains uncertain, but if results prove reproducible, manufacturers could begin transitioning to commercial viability within five to ten years, fundamentally reshaping competitive dynamics in chip production.
